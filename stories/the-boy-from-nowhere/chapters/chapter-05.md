# Chapter 5: Leather and Willow

The storage room was eight feet by ten. Vipin knew this because he paced it on the first morning — heel-to-toe, two passes, one horizontal and one vertical, the numbers consistent across both passes — and then he counted the ceiling cracks.

Twenty-three.

He lay on the cot and assigned each one a name, which was not something he'd done before with ceiling cracks and not something he could explain except that this was his ceiling now, for whatever time it lasted, and his things deserved names. The long one running north to south between the second and third planks he called the River. The three smaller ones clustered near the bulb fixture he called the Sisters. The one that had been repaired badly, with some kind of filler that had then re-cracked along its own seam, he called the Argument.

He named all twenty-three.

This took forty-five minutes, which was forty-five minutes less sleep than he would otherwise have gotten, which was probably fine because his body was doing something that night that had nothing to do with sleep. It was checking whether the room was safe. This was a thing it did in new places — a slow autonomous scan, listening for threats that hadn't arrived yet. The pandit's temple had taken a week. The construction site had taken three nights. The chai stall overhang had been outside, so the scan had never fully resolved.

The room had a door that locked. A padlock, heavy, functional. Keshav had given him the key without ceremony — dropped it into his palm and walked away, and that was the entirety of the conversation. Vipin put the key on the hook inside the door and stood for a moment, looking at it. A key you could leave on a hook, because you were coming back, because the room was yours.

His body started relaxing around the second week. Not sleep, exactly — more like a supervised rest, like something that was keeping watch decided it could let its attention wander slightly without catastrophic consequences. The cracks were the same every night. The River was where he'd left it. The Argument had not changed positions.

The linseed oil smell was, as Rao had warned, not going anywhere.

---

The morning routine arrived with the kind of inevitability Vipin associated with good things: it didn't feel chosen, it felt installed. Five-thirty, without an alarm because his body had always been better at time than clocks. Run two laps of the park — roughly two and a half kilometers, concrete paths, one section where the grass crossed the path and the footing was unreliable in rain. Then back to the room to collect the bat and the rubber ball. Net practice at six-thirty, which meant Rao would already be there — already had been there, always, every time Vipin arrived, as if the man didn't require the same physics of sleep and commuting that other people required.

Breakfast came from the cricket club: idli, or vada pav, or sometimes both. Chai. Eaten at the concrete bench near the south boundary, alone or with whichever of the other junior players came through. Nobody asked Vipin where he lived. In Mumbai, the city had an agreement with its own people not to ask certain questions.

He ate everything. This was the first consistent thing in his life that surprised him — not that food was available, but that it arrived with regularity, at the same times, and that the regularity itself had a physical effect. His body responded to scheduled meals the way a parched thing responds to rain, not gradually but immediately, as if it had been waiting with clenched patience for exactly this.

He ate more than the other junior players. He was hungry in a way that had nothing to do with the previous meal.

---

"Your grip," Rao said, on the third morning.

"Works," Vipin said.

"Sit down."

He sat on the ground. Rao crouched beside him — the left knee was the bad one, the one from the district cricket injury in '87, and he lowered himself with the careful economy of someone who has calculated exactly how far a joint will go and learned not to ask it for more. He took Vipin's right hand, the top hand, and repositioned the bat handle in it.

"Like this. Three knuckles visible. The V between thumb and forefinger pointing here." He indicated the back edge. "Not the face."

Vipin looked at the adjusted grip. It felt wrong — not painful, just unfamiliar, like wearing someone else's shoes. His muscle memory was pulling the hand back to the position it had always used.

"Play a forward defense."

He played a forward defense. The ball went somewhere incorrect. He felt it in the wrists — the wrong resistance, the bat face coming through at an angle that was not natural, not calibrated. He tried again. Still wrong.

"Again."

He tried eleven times. On the twelfth attempt, the bat face came through correctly by accident — pure randomness, the body producing the right thing by variation — and the imaginary ball would have gone somewhere good.

"There," Rao said.

"That was luck," Vipin said.

"Yes. Keep doing it until it isn't."

The next two weeks were a specific kind of punishment. Rao had him bat for an hour each morning with only his top hand. Just the top hand, the proper one, the one that should be guiding rather than forcing. The bottom hand wrapped around his own forearm and kept there. He looked ridiculous, he knew he looked ridiculous, and two of the other juniors walking past the net on the second day stopped and stared in the specific way of people finding something very funny that they have decided not to comment on.

His shots looked worse. His drives went to midwicket. His cuts went behind square. His pulls — the instinctive, violent, bottom-hand pulls that had carried him through every piece of gully cricket he'd ever played — disappeared entirely, replaced by a weak, top-hand gesture that barely moved the ball.

"Uncle," he said, on the eighth day of this, "I was better when I was bad."

Rao looked at him from behind the net. He was chewing something — he always had something to chew in the mornings, dried ginger or cloves or something with a particular medicinal smell that Vipin had identified but never named. He chewed. He didn't respond.

"I had six boundaries in eight balls before I came here," Vipin said. "Now I can barely hit the ball."

"Correct."

"That's not—that's not coaching, uncle, that's undoing."

"Yes."

He took his glasses off his forehead and looked through them, examined them, put them back. "The six boundaries you hit at Shivaji Park that morning, against that fifteen-year-old — how many of them came off the front foot?"

Vipin thought about this. "Two."

"The back-foot ones. Which hand were you using?"

He thought about it more carefully. "Both. Mostly bottom."

"At district level, against a bowler who can move the ball at 130, the bottom-hand pull leaves you here." Rao indicated a specific position — chest on, weight back, face open to the off side. "One ball swings back, you nick it. One ball stays straight, it's your stumps. You've batted your way to fifty. Against boys this age. Against men who've been coached, you'll get out at eight." He looked at Vipin. "The six boundaries will go from six to two. And the two will be better than any of the six."

Vipin sat with this.

He didn't say anything.

He picked up the bat and went back into the net and played a forward defense with only the top hand, badly, and then played it again.

---

The stance was the other thing.

His natural stance was too open — body facing the bowler instead of the incoming ball, right shoulder dropped, feet wider than textbook. It had worked in gully cricket because in gully cricket there was no bowler with a plan, just bowlers throwing as hard as they could in the hope of hitting the stumps. Against a bowler with a plan, the open stance was an advertisement: *here is my off stump, please use it.*

Rao adjusted his feet daily. Moved the back foot. Squared the shoulder. Vipin's body moved back to the old stance within three balls, like a memory foam pillow, restoring the familiar shape. Rao adjusted again. Vipin's body moved back. This happened every session for six weeks, until eventually — slowly, resistance decreasing, the new muscle memory accumulating like sediment — his body began, in isolated moments, to choose the new stance on its own.

The leaving was the hardest.

In gully cricket, you hit everything. The concept of leaving a ball outside off stump was not just foreign, it was philosophically incompatible. The ball was the point. The ball was why you were here. The ball arrived and you hit it, and if you didn't hit it, you had failed at the only objective.

Rao set up a drill: a line of orange cones one foot outside the off stump, running from just back of a length to the popping crease. Any ball that would have pitched outside that line was out of bounds. Vipin must let it pass.

First day: he let three out of ten pass. He swung at the other seven, made decent contact with five of them. Rao reset and bowled again.

Second day: five out of ten.

By the end of the third week, it was eight out of ten. On the days he was tired, it dropped to five again. On the days something was bothering him — Bhai, a delivery route he'd had to cover, the ongoing low-frequency worry about what happened when the money ran out, the days he hadn't heard from the man with the limp before a session and thought maybe this was the day the man stopped showing up — it dropped lower.

Rao said nothing on the bad days. He bowled the same ball. He waited for Vipin to leave it. If Vipin swung, he reset and bowled again.

Patient in a way that felt, at first, like indifference, and then gradually revealed itself as the most consistent thing Vipin had encountered in an adult who wasn't dead.

---

There was one day he didn't come to the nets.

This was in February, three months in. Rao had told him, the day before, that his bowling action would need to be remodeled if he wanted to play representative cricket. Not a suggestion — a statement of fact. The slinging, wide-arm action was effective and unorthodox, and it would work in gully cricket and money matches and perhaps even age-group cricket where batsmen weren't yet sophisticated enough to use it against him. But against batsmen who were batsmen, who watched video, who had coaches who watched video, the release point was too low and too consistent and too wide and they would play the angle and he would hit everything and take nobody out.

Vipin had argued. Rao had not argued back. He'd walked away mid-session, which was his way — the cold silence that said more than argument would have. Vipin spent the rest of that session bowling his natural action at the cones and doing everything right and feeling like he was proving a point that nobody was watching him prove.

The next morning he woke at five-thirty and lay in the dark and did not get up.

The River crack was in its usual place. The Sisters. The Argument.

He had built his bowling from nothing. From watching cricket on chai-stall TVs and absorbing what bodies did and trying it himself in the lane behind the dhaba and adjusting by result, day after day, until he had seventeen named deliveries that he could call on command. His action was his. It was the thing he had made that nobody had given him. Rao could take his batting apart and put it back together better because there was a grammar of batting that existed outside any individual's version of it, and Vipin could feel the improvement happening even through the frustration.

But the bowling was his. The action was his.

He lay there until the sun came through the gap under the door and made a line of light on the floor that moved as he watched it, and then he got up and went to the dhaba for chai because the breakfast window at the cricket club didn't open until eight and he had nothing else to do with his hands.

He came back the next day. Not because he'd decided anything — he hadn't, or he had and the decision was below the level of decisions that get made consciously. He came back because the alternative was to not come back, and not coming back was a thing that his body, that sleeping-place-scanner, had decided somewhere around the third night that it wasn't going to do.

Rao was already there. He had set up the cones for the leaving drill, which was the session they would have been running had yesterday's argument not happened. He did not look up when Vipin came in. He said nothing. He placed a ball on the tee and adjusted the cone by two inches and stepped back.

Vipin picked up the bat.

He played the leaving drill. He left nine out of ten, which was his best ratio. Rao wrote something in his notebook. Neither of them said anything for the next ninety minutes.

On the walk back to the storage room, Vipin noted that the bowling action conversation had not been re-started. He was not sure whether this meant Rao had dropped it, was waiting for a better time, or had made some other calculation. The conversation was in the file — open, unresolved. He would find out eventually which kind it was.

He squeezed the rubber ball twice and went to breakfast.

---

The transformation was not gradual.

Six months of regular meals, the two-lap morning run, the net sessions, the specific Rao-designed strength work — bar from a construction site, bodyweight, something involving a resistance band he'd produced from a bag one morning without explanation — and the Jaat frame that had been quietly waiting for inputs started making the most of them.

He grew two inches between June and December. Not the polite half-inch of normal adolescent growth — a visible change, the kind that made people do the double-look. His shoulders widened. The forearms, already thick from years of gripping bats and balls and rails and the edges of buildings, developed further. His hands, which had always been large for his body, now made sense with the body.

He walked into nets in October and the junior assistant coach, who had recruited him as a fielder from the boundary eight months ago and hadn't paid close attention since, looked at him with an expression that crossed multiple registers simultaneously.

"Who's this?" he asked someone nearby.

"That's Vipin. He's been here since March."

The junior assistant stared.

The pace came with the body. Rao had a radar gun — borrowed, not owned, from a friend who ran a proper academy and owed him something. He set it up one afternoon and told Vipin to bowl. Vipin bowled. The gun read 125 kph. Then 124. Then 128 on a delivery he'd put a little more into. At fourteen, in October of 2003. The gun reported it the same way it reported everything else — numerically, without commentary, without the weight that the number deserved.

The coaches from the other groups at Shivaji Park had started watching his net sessions. Not conspicuously — they had their own sessions, their own students, their own concerns. But there was a pattern that Vipin had noticed and catalogued: when he was batting or bowling, the peripheral traffic in his net's vicinity increased. Coaches who had reason to be at the far end of the park were discovering reasons to be slightly less far. This was data.

He squeezed the rubber ball in his pocket and said nothing.

---

The rubber ball had appeared in his pocket in September without a considered decision being made about it.

He'd had a red rubber ball — cracked, soft, bounce almost gone — that he'd been using for wall practice when he couldn't get to nets. It was in his jacket pocket. He went to the nets. Came back. The ball was still there. He went to the dhaba for dinner. The ball was still there. He squeezed it twice while waiting for his dal and not thinking about anything specific, just running through the morning's session in the background of his mind the way he ran through everything.

The squeezing helped. Not with anything he could name. It occupied his hands and left his mind free to run in the background. He was the kind of person whose hands needed occupation — the counting habit was a version of this, giving his brain something repetitive to process in the foreground while his real thinking happened in the layers underneath.

He started replacing the ball every three weeks, when it lost its bounce and the squeeze became too easy. Ravi Deshmukh — who he'd met once by then, briefly, at a combined session where boys from different groups had been put together for a fielding drill, and who had memorized Vipin's face immediately because Ravi memorized everything immediately — would later say that he'd known Vipin was extraordinary the first time because of the sound. The specific sound of a rubber ball being squeezed in a fist, one-two, one-two, while waiting to bat.

The MCA selectors came to Shivaji Park for the first time in November.

They were there for another boy — a middle-order batsman from a senior coaching group, locally celebrated, the son of a former Ranji player. But there was a moment in the afternoon session where the focus drifted, the way focus at coaching days sometimes drifts toward the loudest thing in the room.

What was happening in Vipin's net was the loudest thing in the room.

Rao saw the selectors watching. He saw Vipin see the selectors watching. He saw Vipin's face do nothing — no conscious reaction, no performance, no glancing over to check the effect. Vipin hit the next ball to the boundary rope with the new grip, the one he'd been refusing for eight months, the top-hand grip that had come back from the dead in October and was now producing sounds from the bat that Rao had not heard from a fourteen-year-old before. He put the reading glasses on his forehead and drank his chai.

After the selectors left, one of the Shivaji Park senior coaches — a heavy-set man named Pathak who had never spoken to Rao directly but was known to be politically connected in the MCA — walked past Rao's net and said, almost to himself: "Who arranged his documentation?"

Rao looked at him. "He's registered with the club."

"Where's he from? Which school?"

"He attends school," Rao said, which was technically true.

Pathak looked at him for a moment, then walked on.

Rao turned back to the net and watched Vipin leave a ball outside off stump without swinging at it. The ball passed the off stump by eight inches. Vipin didn't watch it go. He was already resetting his stance for the next delivery.

---

The bat arrived on a Sunday in December, which was Rao's day off from Shivaji Park, which meant he had not appeared at the nets that morning, which meant Vipin had run his laps and done his solo shadow-batting and eaten his breakfast and gone through the leaving drill by himself using a cone and his memory and found the whole thing three percent less useful without Rao's steady presence at the boundary rope.

At four in the afternoon, there was a knock on the storage room door.

Rao stood there with a bat in his hand. Not wrapped. Not in a bag. Just the bat — an SG, not the top model, mid-range, factory-fresh. He held it out handle-first without preamble.

"Your old one's done. Use this."

The old bat was a hand-me-down that one of the senior club players had left behind when he'd retired, a beat-up Kookaburra with two major scuffs and a repaired toe. It had been adequate. It was also, Vipin realized now, nothing like this.

He took the bat. He weighed it in his hand — a specific heft, a particular balance point, nothing fancy but honest. He turned it face-up and looked at the grain. The willow was not the best — it was a production bat, not a hand-selected blade, not the kind that gets picked individually — but the grain was tight and even. He counted the grains he could see along the face.

Seventeen.

Not fine willow. Fine willow was eight, nine, ten. This was seventeen. A workmanlike blade, built for purpose rather than prestige. He thought, for a reason he could not have explained, that this was exactly right for where they were. Something about the honesty of it.

He looked at Rao and there was a moment — brief, not planned, coming from somewhere he didn't have architectural control over — where the humor that lived in his chest, the reflexive deflection machine that had been running since he was six years old, didn't load. Just a half-second where it couldn't load because there was something in the way.

Rao saw it. It was three seconds, or one second. Whatever it was, it happened.

Vipin made a joke. The armor came up. Rao turned away and pretended to examine something on the doorframe that didn't need examining.

"Sunday is your day off," Vipin said.

"I was in the area."

"Dadar is two kilometers away."

"I walk fast," Rao said, and limped slightly on the left leg, and Vipin nearly said something about that but stopped because some things were better left where they were.

Rao went back to Dadar. Vipin went into the room and sat on the cot with the new bat across his lap and ran his fingers along the grain, counting the seventeen lines again, and then sat with his hands on the blade and looked at the ceiling.

The Argument crack. The River. The Sisters and their fourteen smaller cousins.

He thought about the pandit, which he had not done in weeks. The dry hands. The four o'clock bell. The resident cat — Shankara, the original one with the torn left ear, who had been there when his mother had been there, who had observed without comment and found most things unremarkable. He wondered if Shankara was still alive. Cats could live to fifteen, twenty years. The math was possible.

He touched the pendant, which was cool under his shirt.

Then he stood up, because the storage room was eight feet by ten and he had a bat and a body and a technique that was getting better whether he was ready for it or not, and he shadow-batted in the dark until the groundskeeper banged once on the wall.

---

Keshav the groundskeeper left a newspaper outside Vipin's door most mornings.

Not every morning. Not with any visible system. Just sometimes the newspaper was there and sometimes it wasn't. Vipin had never asked for it and Keshav had never mentioned it. The first time it appeared, Vipin had spent ten minutes trying to figure out whether it was intended for him or had been placed there by mistake, and then he'd read it, and then he'd taken it inside.

He read everything. Cricket scores and classifieds and the political news and the births and deaths and the listings for cultural events he would never attend. The deaths column had a particular pull — he read it with the focused attention of someone who is not looking for anything specific and is therefore more likely to find it. He read about the municipality's ongoing dispute with a group of Dharavi residents over a road widening project. He read about a Bollywood premiere. He read about a factory in Thane that had been cited for violations involving chemical storage.

He did not know why he was reading any of this. He read it anyway. His head filled with mid-2000s Mumbai municipal knowledge the way the storage room filled with the smell of linseed oil — not by choice, not by plan, just by extended proximity.

The newspaper was outside his door the morning Rao came with a clipboard and sat on the concrete bench and told him about the paperwork problem.

"District trials are in February," Rao said. "Three months. The MCA requires a birth certificate, school enrollment, residential address, guardian signature. Standard for under-fifteen registration."

"I have a birth certificate," Vipin said.

"No," Rao said. "You don't. You have a name on a pendant. That's not the same thing."

Vipin looked at the newspaper in his hands. He folded it. He set it on the bench.

"My cricket registration—"

"Is valid for the club. Not for district. District is MCA directly. Different standards." Rao looked at the clipboard. "I'm going to sort it. I have people I can ask. Pathak knows a man at the district office who can process a late-registered birth certificate. Nair at St. Xavier's has been enrolling players part-time for twenty years — he'll backdate enrollment if I ask him to. The club address works for residential."

"And guardian signature?"

A pause.

"I'll sign it," Rao said.

Neither of them said anything for a moment.

"You don't have to—"

"I'll sign it," Rao said again. The same tone he used to say *bowl it again* and *your grip is wrong* and *the leaving drill starts at the yellow cone* — the tone that closed debate without dismissing the person. He looked at the clipboard. "February, Vipin. The trials are in February."

Vipin nodded.

Rao stood up, took his clipboard, and walked back toward the nets. The morning fog was still on the grass. The park was beginning to fill up.

Vipin sat for a moment with the folded newspaper. Then he unfolded it, found the cricket scores, and checked whether the Ranji match had been reported yet.

It hadn't. He read the municipal news instead, something about a water pipe, and somewhere behind the ribs the catastrophe model added *paperwork* to its list of things that could end this, and he filed it alongside the others, and the River crack was still in its place, and the morning session was in forty-five minutes.

He went to run his laps.
