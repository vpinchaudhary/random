# Chapter 33: Dark Night

**Timeline**: Early 2017 (age 27)

---

The selectors dropped him in February.

The language was *rest period,* which was the polite form of the thing, the form that left room for return, that didn't permanently assign a player to the category of finished. He'd watched the language applied to other players over the years. *Rest period* meant: we think you may come back. *Left out of the squad* meant: we're not sure. *Not considered* meant: the conversation about you in the selection meeting was brief. He received *rest period.* He held this.

The batting had recovered — the 61 at Delhi, then a 78 and a 44 in subsequent matches, the presence fully restored, the temporal stretch doing its work. But the press had found its narrative by then, and the narrative had its own momentum. The form slump of 12-8-23 had been written about. The comeback of 61 and 78 had been written about less. The equation the media arrived at was: *inconsistency.* Vipin Chaudhary, one of the most consistent performers in Indian cricket for eight years, was now inconsistent, and the narrative about a player who was inconsistent had a specific gravity that pulled new events into its orbit. His next score of 34 against the West Indies — a 34 that was tactically sound but undramatic — was reported as further evidence. His bowling, which remained excellent, was reported as the only remaining reliable element. *Chaudhary's bowling saves a middling batting display.*

The selectors read the press the way selectors did, which was to say they read it while telling themselves they didn't. The *rest period* announced itself in the squad list for the three-match ODI series against Australia, his name simply absent where it had been present for four years. The explanation given to the public was rotation and managing player workloads. The explanation given to Vipin, from the chairman of selectors in a five-minute call, was that they'd be watching his IPL form and expected to bring him back in the Test series later in the year. He said *yes sir* at the appropriate moments. He thanked the man, which required a specific kind of internal management. He put the phone down.

He sat in his flat in Bandra. Early February, which was Mumbai's best season — the months between the cold-not-cold of December and the building pressure of pre-monsoon March, when the city was dry and mild and the light had a particular quality that the rest of the year didn't achieve. Six floors up, the Arabian Sea was visible between buildings in the gap that he'd bought this flat partly for, the strip of blue that confirmed you were still in Mumbai, still near the edge. Shraddha was not there. This was the state of things: she was at her parents' house, technically, and they were in a separation that had the careful quality of a thing that was not formally decided. They talked. He called, she called. But the distance was real and they both knew it. He had not fixed the pattern — he'd described it, he'd named it, he'd had the kitchen conversation at Delhi and the return of form and the three texts from her across the innings. But the fundamental thing had not shifted. The fatal flaw was named but not dismantled. And naming a thing without dismantling it was a specific kind of lie.

She was generous with him in a way that worried him more than anger would have. Anger would have been easier to manage — a problem with a clear surface, something he could be precise against. Her generosity was a thing he was spending down, and he could feel the account balance without knowing how to stop drawing on it.

---

The flat was fine. He'd learned to be alone — had always been alone, in the essential sense, the self that had been alone since the pandit died was still the load-bearing self. But fine and good were different categories. The cooking was better now: the dal was right, reliably, the kind of right that doesn't require consulting a YouTube tutorial but proceeds from the body's accumulated knowledge of the process. The rice was dry. The sabzi had improved. He'd started making a south Indian sambar with dal and tamarind that he'd seen the hotel kitchen produce in Chennai and then found a recipe for and spent three weeks refining, the adjustment of the tartness being a problem of calibration that he'd approached with the same focused attention he brought to analyzing a bowler's hand position. The sambar was now good.

The structure of his days was the sambar, and the dal, and the IPL net sessions that would ramp up in March, and the mornings at Shivaji Park running laps and watching Rao's sessions and not playing. Cricket was still present — it was still the planet his life orbited — but the light it gave was lower this winter.

He counted the cracks in his bedroom ceiling. Eleven. He'd counted them before — had been counting them since the injury recovery — but now the count had an aggressive quality it hadn't possessed then. During the injury recovery, the counting had been boredom. Now it had an edge. He counted and recounted the same eleven cracks and arrived at eleven each time and the arrival at the correct number brought no satisfaction.

He cooked a lentil soup from a recipe he'd found on a food blog run by a Punjabi woman in Canada who explained her grandmother's cooking in the style of someone who had learned to describe the thing she loved for people who didn't share her reference. He followed it exactly. The soup was good. He ate it alone, standing at the kitchen counter, looking at the strip of sea between the buildings, and thought about the grandmother he'd found in a Haryana village in June and the report that was coming and the woman who was at her parents' house rather than here and the India squad list with his name absent from it.

*This is the bottom.* He thought this in a neutral way, not dramatically, the way a navigator notes coordinates rather than the way a person announces a crisis. *This is what the bottom feels like. You've been lower. The street was lower. The pandit dying was lower. But this has a specific quality of its own: everything at once.*

The report was coming in the morning.

---

Krishnamurthy arrived at nine, which was the time they'd agreed. He had a folder. He always had a folder, and the folder was the physical embodiment of the investigation rather than the investigation itself — the investigation was Krishnamurthy's mind, the folder was the record of it.

He sat across from Vipin at the kitchen table with the folder and a cup of tea that Vipin had made and that was the correct temperature. Krishnamurthy opened the folder. He had, over the months of this investigation, developed a method of delivering information — not dramatic, not building toward revelation, but sequential and clear, each piece given time to be registered before the next arrived. Vipin had come to rely on this method. It was the correct method for this kind of information.

"Hospital records from Mumbai, 1990," Krishnamurthy said. "I had to go through three separate institutions and two charitable hospital networks. The records are incomplete — 1990 was before digital, and many of the physical records have been damaged or lost. But I found a partial admission record."

Vipin waited.

"Rajveer Chaudhary. Admitted to a charitable hospital in the Dharavi corridor, June 1990. Age: twenty-five. Cause: acute dengue hemorrhagic fever, complicated by secondary infection." Krishnamurthy paused the appropriate length of time. "The record shows a death entry five days after admission. Rajveer Chaudhary died on the fourteenth of June, 1990."

The kitchen was quiet. The ceiling fan turned. Outside, Mumbai maintained its constant sound, the city's ambient frequency that existed regardless of what happened inside its rooms.

"Kamla Chaudhary," Krishnamurthy continued, in the same even tone. "Female. Same address on the admission record — a rented room in the Dharavi area. She is listed as next of kin on Rajveer's record. But there is no subsequent death record for Kamla in the same hospital, or in any hospital I've searched in Mumbai in 1990 or 1991 or 1992."

Vipin made himself ask: "What does that mean?"

"Several possibilities." Krishnamurthy had his folder open but wasn't reading from it now — he was speaking from memory, the information absorbed. "One: she survived, left Mumbai, and is alive somewhere that I haven't traced. Two: she died elsewhere — in a hospital not captured by my search, or outside Mumbai, or without formal record. Three: she changed her name or returned to a different identity, making her untraceable. In 1990 Mumbai, a young woman from a rural area, recently widowed, with a newborn, facing the city alone — she would have been in considerable difficulty." He paused. "She may have left the child because she had no capacity to raise him. She may have left the city afterward. She may not have survived."

Vipin held the cup in both hands. Cold now — he'd stopped drinking it at some point without noticing.

"The money that was left with you," Krishnamurthy said. "The amount in the pandit's account, from his record of the day — it was approximately four hundred rupees. That's a significant amount for someone in that economic position in 1990. If Rajveer had been earning in Mumbai and had savings when he died, it's possible Kamla used the savings to leave you with what she could. Four hundred rupees was not trivial." A pause. "I think she was a mother trying to do the possible thing in an impossible situation."

Vipin sat with this for a long time.

*Your father died before you were born.* Or essentially — a few weeks before, June fourteenth. He'd been born in June, the municipal estimate, which matched the date in Krishnamurthy's record with a specificity that had the quality of something becoming real rather than being confirmed. His father had been twenty-five years old. He was twenty-seven now. Two years older than his father had lived to be.

*Your mother may be dead. May be alive. There is no record. The search ends here unless new information arrives.*

"Can you continue looking?" Vipin said.

"For Kamla, yes. I'll continue. But I want to be honest: the trail after 1990 is very cold. If she survived and left the city, she could be anywhere. If she's alive, she's in her late fifties. If she changed her name or returned to a different community, she may be essentially unfindable without a specific lead I don't currently have."

"Continue," Vipin said.

"I will." Krishnamurthy closed the folder. "There's one more thing. Small. May be nothing." He opened the folder again to the last page. "In the rented room in Dharavi, at the time of Rajveer's admission — the admission record lists the contents of the room as recorded by the hospital's intake process. Routine. This was a charitable hospital, so there was a process of recording possessions. The list is partial. Clothes, some utensils. And: a photograph. Described in the record as 'one photograph, personal.' No further detail."

Vipin looked up.

"It may be nothing," Krishnamurthy said. "Photos were routinely catalogued and then returned to next of kin, which in this case would have been Kamla. Or they were stored and lost. The photograph you have — the one you showed me with the cut edge — may or may not be related. But the timing suggests it could be from that room. It could be that Kamla took the photograph when she left. That it was cut from a larger image before she gave it to you." He paused. "Or that someone else cut it. I don't know."

One photograph. The young couple in the late-1980s street setting. The right edge cut clean with scissors, something removed from the frame before it was sent to him.

"What was in the part that was cut off?" Vipin said.

"I don't know. I have no way of knowing. If Kamla is alive and found, she would know. Otherwise it's an open question."

He left the flat with the folder and his even voice and his filter-coffee manner, and Vipin sat at the kitchen table for a long time after the door closed.

---

Three things were true simultaneously, and all three things were very loud.

His father was dead. Had been dead since before Vipin's first breath, essentially — dying of dengue in a Dharavi hospital room while Kamla had a newborn in a rented room in the same corridor. Twenty-five years old. Rajveer Chaudhary, who had left his village with ambition and a love marriage and the specific hunger of a man who needed to do more than this, had lasted one year in Mumbai before the city's infrastructure — the mosquitoes, the water, the absence of healthcare for people without money — had done what it did to the poor people of 1990 Mumbai. He'd died and there had been a woman and a newborn and four hundred rupees and a gold pendant engraved with the child's name.

*She may not have survived.* Or she might be alive, fifty-eight years old, somewhere in a country of a billion people, a woman named Kamla Chaudhary or whatever she was named now, who had put a pendant on a child on temple stairs and walked away from the worst moment of her life and then lived the rest of it.

Or she had died too. And there was nobody left to explain the cut photograph.

Both possibilities devastated him in different ways that he couldn't organize into hierarchy. His father was dead before he was born — this was the thing that was. His mother was a mystery with the specific quality of a mystery that might never resolve, which was worse, somehow, than a confirmed absence. A confirmed absence was a ground you could stand on. An uncertainty was a floor that might or might not be there depending on information you didn't have.

He was not going to cry. He'd done the crying in August — the ugly, ragged August crying that had emptied something in him and left it clean. This was a different quality of pain. This was the thing that settles at the base of a body and stays there. Not acute. Chronic. The specific weight of understanding that the question had been answered as much as it would ever be answered, and the answer was: *your father was twenty-five years old and died of dengue, and your mother did what she could, and then nothing.*

He was dropped from the India squad and his girlfriend was at her parents' house and his father had been dead since 1990 and his mother might be anywhere or nowhere.

He went to the temple.

---

His temple was in Dadar, fifteen minutes from his flat by auto. Not a famous temple, not a tourist destination — a neighborhood temple, the kind that had existed on that street for eighty years and would exist for eighty more, that served the people of that specific area and had no interest in being known beyond it. He'd gone there irregularly across the years since Rao had helped him find his footing in Mumbai, always the way you go to a specific place in a city when you need the city to give you something you can't articulate. He'd gone there at seventeen, nervous and young. He'd gone at twenty-one after the World Cup. He'd gone at twenty-four when the spot-fixing approach had happened and he'd sat with the weight of it for a night.

He sat in the back, on the stone floor. Different pujari from the one he'd known — this one younger, efficient, had the air of a man who knew his job's practical and spiritual dimensions equally well and was comfortable with both. There was a cat, which surprised him — large, grey, occupying a corner of the back area with the possessive ease of an animal that has concluded it owns the place. Not the same cat from before. But a cat. This pleased him in a way he couldn't explain and didn't try to.

He didn't pray. He'd never been consistently devout — the pandit had taught him the forms, the mechanics of temple devotion, but the inner experience of faith had arrived and departed in the irregular rhythm of a person who had too much reason to doubt the ordering of things to commit fully to the idea of a benevolent ordering. He sat and watched others pray. An old woman with a plastic bag of offerings, moving through the ritual with the practiced ease of someone who had done this every day for decades, for whom the prayers had the quality of conversation with a very old friend — not performed, just ongoing. A young couple, probably in their twenties, the woman praying earnestly, the man praying with the cooperative attention of a person who isn't sure he believes but loves the person who does. A child who sat next to her grandfather and picked up a fallen flower from the temple floor and handed it back to the priest without being asked.

He watched this and thought about Rajveer Chaudhary, who had been twenty-five, and who had presumably done things that twenty-five-year-old men do, and who had brought his wife from a Haryana village to a rented room in Dharavi and died of dengue and left a question that his son would spend twenty-seven years trying to answer. He thought about Kamla, who had been described as having certainty and force, a kind of knowing who she was — and who had put a pendant on a baby on temple stairs and walked away, or been taken, or disappeared into whatever the rest of her life was.

He thought about the possibility that Kamla was alive. Fifty-eight years old. Somewhere. Perhaps she'd seen his name in print at some point — the name on the pendant, the name of the IPL player from Mumbai. Perhaps she hadn't.

He thought about the cut photograph and what was in the part that had been removed.

He sat with all of this and did not arrive at any resolution, which was correct. The temple didn't resolve things. The temple held them. That was what temples were for: the holding of the unresolvable, the space in which you could set a thing down and let it be what it was without you needing to fix it.

What broke open in him was not release — the release had happened in August, in his flat, with the objects spread on the bed. This was something else. The dark kind of acceptance, the acceptance that doesn't feel like peace but feels like the ground. *This might be all there is.* His father, dead at twenty-five. His mother, unknown. A partial answer, a cut photograph, a thread that Krishnamurthy would follow for as long as there was thread. And that might be all there was. Life continuing anyway.

*Your father had a name.* Rao had said: *whatever you find, you're still you.* He was Vipin Chaudhary, who had grown up on Mumbai's streets and found cricket and found Rao and played for India and loved a woman who could see him clearly and was currently at her parents' house because he kept shutting doors he didn't mean to shut. He was the sum of everything that had happened to him, including the things he'd never know fully.

The dark acceptance arrived without announcing itself. He sat with the grey cat in the back of the Dadar temple and it was enough, not in the way of *I am satisfied* but in the way of *the ground is here, I am standing on it, tomorrow exists.*

The old woman with the offerings left. The young couple left. The child and grandfather left. The pujari did the last aarti of the evening, the lamp moving in its circular pattern, the bell ringing, and the sound of the bell was the same bell, the same frequency, the same metal note that the pandit had rung every evening in the temple where Vipin had grown up and slept and learned to read and eaten dal that tasted right because the person who made it loved you.

He sat until the pujari was finished. Then he got up, touched his forehead to the threshold on his way out — the pandit's habit, absorbed, unkillable — and stood on the street.

Mumbai in February at nine PM. The city at its best: warm but not hot, the crowds thinned to comfortable, the lights of Dadar's streets in the specific arrangement he'd known for fifteen years. An auto went past. A vegetable vendor was closing his cart, wrapping the remaining produce in cloth. A group of college-aged boys stood outside a chai stall in the manner of people who have nowhere to be and are content about it.

He stood on the street and took out his phone.

He didn't text. He called.

Two rings.

"Vipin." Her voice: concerned, not performing concern. Shraddha didn't perform concern — she'd told him once that the acting she did was about finding the truth in a character, not putting something external over it. Her concern was always the real kind.

"I need to tell you something," he said. His voice was steadier than he'd expected, which was the wrong kind of steadiness — the kind that came from having used up the sharp end of the feeling rather than from being okay. "Not the facts version. The full version."

"Okay." He heard her moving, the sound of her finding a place to sit, the specific quality of a person rearranging themselves to be fully present.

"Krishnamurthy came this morning. He found records from 1990." He stopped. "My father died before I was born. Dengue. He was twenty-five years old." He stopped again. "My mother — they don't know. She might be alive. She might be anywhere. She might be dead. Krishnamurthy is still looking but he doesn't think —" He stopped.

"Vipin." Just his name, in the voice that meant: *I'm here, I'm listening, nothing you say will make me leave.*

"I went to the temple after. The Dadar temple. I sat in the back. There was a cat." He heard how this sounded. "I don't know why I'm telling you the cat."

"Because you're telling me everything this time," she said. Not gently — truly.

Something moved in his chest. The thing that had been sealed since the pandit died, the wall that was load-bearing, the independence he'd built into the architecture because the architecture had required it — he felt it move. Not crumble. Move. Shift by millimeters. The movement of something very old, very fixed, being asked to be less than it was.

"I'm scared," he said.

The word sat on the pavement in front of him, visible, three syllables he'd never said to another person in this context. Not about cricket, not about his family, not about the drop or the form or the question that had defined him. He'd told Rao he was nervous before the India debut. He'd told Ravi he was worried after the spot-fixing approach. But *scared* — the specific word, the word that meant *I don't know if I can hold this alone* — he had not said this to anyone.

"I know," Shraddha said. "I've known for a while. I've been waiting for you to know it too."

He laughed. The laugh surprised him — not the forced kind, not the armor kind. The genuine laugh that came from being seen and having the seeing be accurate. "You're very patient," he said.

"I'm extremely patient," she said. "I'm also very tired."

"I know."

A pause. Then: "I need you," he said. The three words. The fatal flaw, named, broken, offered. "Not — not to fix it. There isn't a fix. I just need you to know that I need you. Because I've been acting like I don't and that's not true."

The pause that followed was long enough that he counted his own breaths. Three.

"I'm already in the car," she said.

He stood on the Dadar street with his phone at his ear and listened to her start the engine, and the sound of it — the particular sound of Shraddha's car starting, the engine he knew from six years of her presence — was the most specific and grounding thing he'd heard in months.

"I'm at the Dadar temple," he said. "Come here. I'll wait outside."

"Ten minutes," she said.

"I'll be outside."

He put the phone away. He stood on the pavement and the auto went past and the vegetable vendor finished closing his cart and the boys at the chai stall were still there, still with nowhere to be, still content about it. He touched the pendant through his shirt — the habit, unkillable.

His father had been twenty-five. He was twenty-seven. He had two more years than his father had gotten, and however many more years after that, and whatever they contained.

He counted the lights in the building opposite. Fourteen floors. On the sixth floor, a window with the blue light of a television. On the ninth, a child visible at the window, looking out at the street at the exact moment Vipin looked up. The child looked at him. He looked at the child. Then the child moved away from the window, back into whatever the room contained.

He waited. The city moved around him. In ten minutes, her car would turn onto this street.

The pendant was warm against his sternum, warm from his body, which had been carrying it for twenty-seven years. Not evidence anymore. Not proof of existence. Just a name, and a beginning, and whatever came after.

He was going to be okay. Not tonight — tonight was going to require the sitting and the talking and the ugly work of two people deciding not to let the space between them become permanent. But tomorrow, or the day after, or eventually. He was going to be okay.

The car turned onto the street. Her headlights swept the pavement. He raised a hand.

She stopped. The engine idled.

He opened the door and got in.

The car was warm. She'd been driving for ten minutes and the heater had been on and it was the warmth of a specific person's specific car, familiar and particular and his. She looked at him with the look that she had — the one that didn't require anything from him except his presence.

He put his seatbelt on. She put the car in gear.

"The cat at the temple," she said. "What color?"

He looked at her. The corner of her mouth moved. He understood: she was going to talk him through this. Not the big things immediately, not the father and mother and the questions that had no answers. The grey cat first. The small true thing. And then the rest.

"Grey," he said. "Large. Owned the place."

"Okay," she said. She drove.

Somewhere in Mumbai's February darkness, Krishnamurthy was making notes and the search for Kamla continued, and the photograph in the drawer still had its cut right edge, and the rest of the picture was somewhere.

But tonight there was a grey cat and a woman who'd driven toward him when he'd asked, and that was enough of a beginning.

Tomorrow, everything else.
