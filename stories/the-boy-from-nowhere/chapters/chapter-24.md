# Chapter 24: Aashiqui

**Timeline**: 2013 (age 22-23) — Shraddha POV

---

The film had been a gamble.

Shraddha knew it the moment she read the script — not a calculated gamble, not the kind of risk that came with a safety net of franchise attachment or a director's established track record. A genuine gamble: an unknown director, a remake of a beloved 1990 original that the industry had largely decided was untouchable, and a brief so specific in its emotional requirements that two of the other actresses who'd been considered had declined on the grounds that the role was too exposed.

She'd said yes in the room. Not as a calculated decision — she'd processed the script three times before the meeting and had felt something that she knew better than to analyze too closely before saying out loud. She'd told her father about it afterward, and Shakti had looked at the title and then at her and then at the title again and then called three industry contacts for an anonymous assessment of the director, all of whom had said some version of *talented but unproven*, and Shakti had said: "Your risk."

It was her risk. She'd taken it.

She'd told Vipin the same night, over the phone. He'd been somewhere in Rajasthan, a Ranji match, hot — he'd described the pitch to her when she called as if this was necessary context. She'd said "I took the role" and there was a brief pause and then he'd said "Good" in the flat, certain way he said things he actually meant. She'd said "You haven't even heard what the project is." He'd said "Doesn't matter. You took it, which means you read it and felt something. That's enough."

She'd hung up the call and stood in her room for a moment thinking about what it meant that he'd been the first person she'd told.

The first day of shooting, April 2012. She drove herself to the set in Filmistan — not the studio car, her own car, which was a thing she did at the start of a project that mattered because the drive in was part of the preparation. Forty-two minutes from Juhu at that hour. She counted three petrol stations, a school with a crooked gate, a billboard for a mobile phone plan featuring a cricketer she recognized and smiled at involuntarily.

The role was Aarohi — a singer, a woman whose love becomes both her sustaining force and her undoing. The emotional range was large. The music was the vehicle, and the music, once she'd heard the final recordings, was extraordinary. She played the first song three times in the car on the Filmistan drive and felt it settle in her chest in the way the best film music settles — not as decoration but as structural load-bearing.

She was twenty-two. She understood, in the abstract way you understand things before they become real, that this could be the film that defined the next five years. She had the specific energy of someone who has decided not to be careful about something important.

There were days in the shoot when this energy was its own problem — when she was so inside the scene that coming out of it took forty minutes and a cup of chai and Priya the makeup artist saying something completely mundane about the catering. There were days when it felt like the right engine. The trick was learning to tell the difference.

The shoot was eleven months. She sang live for the playback recording sessions — not standard for most actors, a decision the director had proposed and she'd accepted because the alternative felt false. She'd spent three months before filming with a classical vocal coach, two hours every morning, learning to produce the sound of someone whose voice was their whole emotional vocabulary. The coach was from Bhopal, had trained vocalists for twenty years, and told her in the second week: "You are not a singer. But you know what you want to say. That is more than half of singing."

Vipin had asked her once about the preparation — not the technical side, the other side. "Are you afraid of it?"

"The role?"

"The exposure. It sounds like the role requires—"

"It requires me to be visible in a way I usually—" She'd paused. "I usually manage what people see. This role doesn't allow that. The camera sees what it sees."

He'd been quiet for a moment, in the way that meant he was processing rather than waiting for a turn to speak. "I think that's why you took it."

She'd looked at him. "Explain."

"You don't like not being in control of your own presentation. So you took the role that required you to give up that control, because you knew you needed to practice it."

She'd sat with this for a moment. "That's either very perceptive or very annoying."

"Probably both," he'd said. She'd thought about it for a week before deciding: both, but she'd taken the role anyway.

---

The film released in April 2013.

What happened next was something she had not prepared for and could not have — not because she hadn't thought about success, but because the specific texture of this success was different from anything she'd planned for. The film had been tracking well in advance of release. The music had already broken through: *Tum Hi Ho* in every auto-rickshaw, every corner shop, every phone ringtone she'd heard in three months. She'd heard it playing from Vipin's flat when she'd arrived unannounced one Saturday, faintly from the neighbor's flat above, and he'd answered the door looking slightly confused and she'd said "your neighbor has good taste" and he'd said "I genuinely have no idea who my neighbors are" which was information about Vipin that was both entirely unsurprising and, she found, rather endearing.

The release day, she watched from a distance. Her team had organized a morning screening at a multiplex in Andheri — the kind of event where the director and producers sit in the back row and watch the audience rather than the film, which they've already seen forty-seven times. She sat in the middle. She watched the couple to her left, who were clearly on a date and were at the point of the date where they were both sitting forward slightly, leaning toward the screen without knowing they were doing it. She watched a group of college girls in the third row who were already singing along to the songs they'd heard a hundred times in the last month. She watched a middle-aged man sitting alone who had come to a 10 AM show on a Friday, which meant he'd taken a half-day from work or had no work that day, which meant this was a deliberate choice.

She did not watch the film.

She watched the audience watching the film.

She'd learned this from Vipin without him teaching it directly. He watched crowds. He watched what crowds did with information. She'd absorbed the method.

By the interval, something had shifted in the multiplex's atmosphere — the specific quality of air in a cinema where a film has worked. It was not a subtle thing. It was the opposite of subtle. Three women behind her were crying from the second act, not quietly. The man sitting alone had not moved since the interval began.

She went to the bathroom, stood at the mirror, and said to her own reflection: *okay then.*

But the film's first week — the response to the film itself, not just the music — was of a scale she had no reference for.

The opening weekend numbers: highest for a Hindi film that year. First week collections: record-setting. The reviews were divided on the screenplay and unanimous on the performances. The line that her publicist sent her at 7 AM on the Monday after release, from a review she hadn't read yet: *Shraddha Kapoor steps out of her father's shadow and into her own light.* She read this line three times. The first time she felt it land. The second time she felt the shadow part. The third time she understood that both parts were true and that this was apparently her relationship with accurate descriptions of herself.

Her father called at nine.

"You saw the numbers," she said, before he could.

"I saw the numbers," he said.

A silence.

"Baba—"

"You were—" He paused. A long pause. Shakti Kapoor with a long pause was either about to say something genuinely important or was deploying the theatrical version of a long pause. She'd spent twenty-three years learning to tell the difference. This was the genuine one.

"You were," he said again, and she heard something in his voice that she could count on one hand the number of times she'd heard it. "You were extraordinary."

She sat down. On the floor, with her back against the sofa — the floor-sitting position she'd picked up from Vipin without noticing it until he'd pointed it out, laughing, the previous year. "You sit like someone who grew up without chairs," he'd said. She'd said "I sit like someone who finds floors clearer," which he'd accepted as an explanation while clearly filing something about it.

"Thank you, Baba," she said.

"I have opinions about the third act," Shakti said, because he was Shakti and he always had opinions about the third act.

"I know."

"The director should have—"

"I know."

"But the scene in the rain. With the song. That was—" Another pause. Shorter. "That was yours. It didn't come from anywhere else."

She sat on the floor with the phone and didn't say anything for a moment. The morning light was coming through the gap in her curtains that she'd never gotten fixed, a gold stripe across the floor that moved as the day moved. She'd woken in this room on the day of the shoot's first day and had lain in exactly this light thinking: *this could change everything or nothing, and you chose it, so*. And then she'd gotten up.

It had changed something. She didn't know yet all of what.

"Come for dinner," her mother said, from somewhere in the background of her father's phone. She hadn't known she was there. "Saturday. Bring Vipin."

---

Vipin came on Saturday.

He arrived with a box of pedha from a specific sweet shop in Dadar that had been making pedha from the same recipe since 1968 and had a two-hour queue on Saturdays, which he'd apparently stood in because Ravi had told him that showing up to someone's parents' house for a celebratory dinner required bringing something and Vipin had said "I'll bring sweets" and Ravi had said "not ladoo" and Vipin had said "why not ladoo" and Ravi had said "trust me" and so he'd brought pedha.

He'd called her that morning to ask about dietary restrictions. She'd said "my father eats everything, my mother is light on sweets but polite about them, bring something good." He'd called back twenty minutes later to ask the name of the sweet shop he'd been recommended because it was in Dadar and he wasn't sure if she knew it. She'd said she didn't know it specifically. He'd called Ravi again, confirmed, and then apparently stood in the queue for two hours with the application of a man on a training run.

She'd heard about the queue from her mother, who'd heard it from Shivangi who'd seen him mention it to Shakti at the door. She'd heard about it and then thought about it. A man who stood two hours in a sweet shop queue on a Saturday because he'd been told it was the correct thing to bring. Filed under: *takes preparation seriously*. All preparation, not just cricket.

Which she already knew. She was still accumulating evidence.

Shakti received the pedha with the specific expression of a man who had been brought sweets of varying appropriateness for thirty years and had developed a taxonomy.

"Pedha," Shakti said.

"From Anand in Dadar," Vipin said. "They've been making it since 1968."

Shakti looked at the box. "I know Anand's. There's a queue on Saturdays."

"Two hours today. They were doing a Satyanarayan puja in the back and the kitchen was moving slowly."

"You waited two hours."

"I was early anyway."

Shakti looked at him. Something shifted in his expression — not full warmth yet, but something being recalibrated, an assessment that had been in progress for eighteen months arriving at a new interim conclusion.

"Sit," Shakti said, and this time the word had a different quality than the earlier sittings. More natural. Less evaluation.

Shivangi had made the dinner. Vipin ate with the focused appreciation of a man who understood good cooking and was not embarrassed about expressing this opinion through the medium of a second helping. Shakti watched this.

The pedha was eaten after dinner. Shakti had one. Looked at the texture. "This is the real recipe. A lot of places cut the ghee now." He ate it. "Good."

From Shakti, *good* and *extraordinary* occupied adjacent shelves in the vocabulary of approval, separated only by context. *Good* for the pedha was approximately *extraordinary* from anyone else.

Vipin filed this. Shraddha, watching him file it, filed that he had filed it.

Her mother had set out the chai and then left the three of them alone at the table, which was also a specific signal — Shivangi could read rooms with the precision of someone who'd spent thirty years as the quiet operator behind Shakti Kapoor's louder presence. She knew when to stay and when the conversation required fewer people.

The pedha box sat open on the table between them. Vipin took a second piece. Shakti noticed this, nodded once like a judge issuing a favorable ruling, and also took a second piece.

"You followed the domestic circuit," Shakti said to Vipin. Not a question.

"I follow all the cricket I can."

"Not just MI."

"MI is where I play. The cricket is everywhere. I watch the Ranji, the Duleep, the Under-19. I watch state-level when there's tape available." He paused. "I watch a lot of tape."

"Rao taught you that."

"Rao taught me the principle. I built the library."

Shakti was quiet for a moment, eating his pedha. "In the industry, we say the best directors watch everything. Not just prestige films. B-films, regional, whatever's running. They say that's where you see what's possible before anyone has decided what's allowed." He paused. "I didn't expect to see that in a cricketer."

"With respect," Vipin said, "that's because most people in your industry don't watch much cricket."

Shakti's mouth corner moved. The family expression — Shraddha had it too. "Fair point," he said.

---

The dinner conversation moved, as it always eventually moved around Shakti, toward stories. The Bollywood decade of the 1990s, the productions, the directors, the specific texture of an industry in transition. Vipin listened with the quality of attention she recognized — the full-attention mode, where he was not performing listening but actually cataloguing. He asked two questions in the course of forty minutes. Both were good questions — specific, contextual, the kind that opened the story further rather than redirecting it.

After the second question, Shakti looked at him for a moment with an expression she'd seen rarely: the one that meant *this person is worth talking to.*

"You watched films growing up?" Shakti asked.

"The groundskeeper at Shivaji Park had a small television. He left it on in the evenings. I watched whatever was on."

"What do you remember?"

Vipin considered this. "The music. I remember the music more than the stories. There was a song — from the early nineties, I don't know which film — that played every evening for about a month one year. I couldn't have been more than nine or ten. It was on at the time I was usually outside. I learned the words without trying."

Shakti leaned slightly forward. "Do you remember the tune?"

"I remember the melody." He paused. "I don't know what it's from."

"Hum the beginning," Shakti said, which was — Shraddha noticed — the least Shakti thing he'd said in two and a half years of dinners. He was asking. Not performing.

Vipin hummed seven notes. It was brief and slightly uncertain — he was not a hummer, not a singer, he lived in a world of specific and numerical rather than melodic — but the seven notes were clearly something.

Shakti sat back. His expression had moved entirely out of assessment and into something more private.

"Dil Hai Ki Manta Nahin," he said. "1991. Aamir Khan. The song is *Dil Hai Ki Manta Nahin.*"

Vipin waited.

"That film," Shakti said, looking at the table rather than at either of them, "was the year I moved to Mumbai. From Connaught Place. I was twenty-one. I had eight hundred rupees and a letter of introduction to a producer who never called me back." He picked up his chai. "That song — every wedding, every shop, everywhere that year. I know exactly the month you mean." He paused. "I was also outside, that year. In a different way."

The table was quiet.

Vipin said: "I didn't know that."

"Why would you?" Shakti looked at him now. "My father sold clothes. I came from nothing, you know. Just a different kind of nothing."

It was the most honest thing he'd said at a dinner table in Shraddha's memory. She'd heard versions of the Connaught Place story. She hadn't heard it said like this: as an equation, not an anecdote. As recognition.

"Different kind of nothing," Vipin agreed. Not dismissing the difference. Acknowledging the shape.

Shakti drank his chai. Nodded.

"Good pedha," he said again, and this time it meant something larger than the pedha.

---

She excused herself from the dinner table briefly when her phone rang — a work call she'd been waiting for, a scheduling conflict for the next shoot. She took it in the entrance hallway, standing among the eleven photographs, keeping her voice low. Through the dining room doorway, she could hear her father telling Vipin about the 1991 film industry as if they'd been discussing it for years rather than this being only the second time they'd had an unguarded conversation. She could also hear Vipin asking the right question — not the surface question, the one underneath it — and her father's voice changing register when he answered.

She ended her call. Stood in the hallway for a moment. The gold-framed photograph of Shakti from a 1994 film watched from the wall. The younger version of her father looked like himself but brighter, like a more saturated version — less considered, more combustible. She'd grown up with that photograph; she'd stood under it since she was four years old waiting for him to come home late from sets. Tonight it looked like a document rather than a decoration. Evidence of a man before he knew what he would become.

She went back into the dining room.

In the car home — Vipin driving her, a thing that had begun happening organically after dinners and had become unremarkable — she looked out the window and felt the specific exhaustion of a night that had required full emotional attention from everyone in the room.

"How are you feeling?" Vipin asked.

"Glad. And also tired from being glad."

"Is that a thing?"

"It's a very specific thing. When something you've wanted for a long time actually happens, there's a cost to holding it. You've been tense about it for so long that the relief has its own weight."

He was quiet for a moment. Then: "Yes. I know that one."

She looked at him. The city outside, the night, the way his hands held the steering wheel with the specific economy of someone who had been driving for three years and had strong opinions about where his hands should be.

"The film," she said.

"The film."

"I think — I think I made something real." She paused. "I'm scared to say it out loud."

"Why."

"Because saying it out loud lets other people disagree with it."

"They're going to disagree whether you say it or not," he said. "That's not connected to your saying it."

She absorbed this.

"Right," she said. "Yes." She paused. "You always do that."

"Do what."

"Remove the variable that isn't actually variable." She smiled, looking out the window. "It's very annoying."

"You like it."

"I do, na. That's why it's annoying."

They drove. She watched the city. At a signal, an auto-rickshaw pulled up alongside them with the radio on — a song she'd been hearing everywhere for three months, the song from her film, the one that had migrated into every corner shop and auto speaker and morning routine across a country of a billion people.

She said nothing.

He glanced at her, then at the source of the music, then back at the road.

"Tum Hi Ho," he said.

"Yes."

"I've heard it approximately a hundred and twelve times since April."

"I know." She looked out the window. "Do you like it?"

A pause. "It's a very good song," he said. "Structurally, the second verse does something — the melody drops when you'd expect it to climb. It goes somewhere unexpected."

"The composer explained that to me. He called it 'the wrong move that's right.'"

"That's a good description of several things."

The signal changed. The auto pulled away. The song faded.

"My neighbor's been playing it every evening," he said. "I've learned the words without trying."

She turned to look at him.

"That's what you said about the song from 1991," she said. "The one your father's generation listened to."

He was quiet for a moment.

"Yes," he said. "I suppose it is."

She looked back out the window. Something in her chest did the thing it sometimes did — the thing she'd been not naming for approximately two and a half years, accumulating evidence about, filing carefully. She let it exist without naming it. There would be time.

Not yet.

---

The week of the film's release, she gave fourteen press interviews in three days. She'd been doing press since her debut, understood the mechanics of it, had developed a mental inventory of the questions she'd be asked and the answers that were truthful enough to give and would not feed a narrative she didn't want fed. She answered well. She said the right things. She was charming and articulate and accessible without being overexposed.

On the second evening, she called Vipin from the car between a TV segment and a photoshoot.

"Tell me something that has nothing to do with film," she said.

A pause. "Rao told me today that the most important delivery in cricket is the one before the one that gets the wicket. Because if you bowl the delivery that gets the wicket from the previous over, the batsman might have reset. The pressure accumulates. You can't skip ahead."

She sat with this. "That's very applicable to where I am right now."

"I know. It works for most things." A pause. "How's the press?"

"Functional. I've answered the shadow question eleven times."

"Did you have a good answer?"

"Different ones. Still finding the right one." She looked out the car window at a Mumbai evening in full voice. "How was Shivaji Park?"

"Good. Rao's shoulder is bothering him. He won't mention it."

"Did you mention it?"

"I left the nets early so he didn't have to tell me he was tired."

She'd sat with this for a moment. The car moved through traffic. She held the phone.

"You look after him," she said.

"He looks after me," Vipin said. "I just try not to make it cost him more than it needs to."

This, she'd filed. Not for later — immediately, directly, into the permanent inventory.

A week after the film's release, a journalist asked her in a press interview what it had felt like to step out of her father's shadow.

She said: "I've always been in my own light. Sometimes it's adjacent to his. That's not a shadow — that's just how light works when two people are in the same room."

It was the right answer. She knew it was the right answer when she said it. She also knew it was the kind of answer that would travel — it had the shape of something that would be shared, quoted, turned into a headline. She felt this without feeling cynical about it. The truth wasn't less true for being quotable. It wasn't less hers for being useful to someone else.

She thought about Anjali Mehta's article about Vipin, from the previous year. The piece that had used him as a lens. He'd said: I'm tired of being the useful story. She understood that differently now than she had when he'd first said it. Not because she disagreed — because she'd thought about it. The useful story was a cage when it was the only story. When you had other stories alongside it, it became something you could choose to offer or not.

Vipin hadn't had that choice yet. His story had been someone else's discovery, someone else's lens. She'd been shaped from birth in an industry where you learned early that your story was always being read by people with purposes you hadn't agreed to. She had, in a sense, more practice at being useful. The question was whether you could be useful and still be whole.

She was still working on that question.

Driving home from the press event, she called Vipin. He answered on the third ring.

"I said something good today," she said.

"More context."

She told him. He was quiet for a moment.

"That's very good," he said. "Did you come up with it in the moment?"

"Yes."

"Hm." A pause. "I've been thinking about a version of that for the temple questions. For three years. You said it in one press junket."

She laughed. "The principle translates."

"Yes," he said. "I think it does." Another pause. "Are you done for the night?"

"Three more interviews tomorrow."

"Do you want me to come over."

She looked out the window. The city in the dark, the specific Mumbai dark that was never quite dark, the light coming from everywhere. The question, asked in that voice, meant exactly what it said and nothing more and also something a little more than that. She'd been accumulating evidence about this for two and a half years.

"Yes," she said.

"I'll be there in forty minutes," he said. "I'll bring something to eat."

"Nothing from a queue this time."

"The queue was twenty minutes," he said. "The other twenty I spent trying to find parking."

She laughed. The full version, in the back of the car, alone, in the Mumbai dark.

"Yes," she said again. "Come over."
