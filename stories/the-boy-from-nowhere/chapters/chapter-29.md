# Chapter 29: The Hollow Season

**Timeline**: 2015 (age 25)

---

The first thing he saw when he came up from the anesthesia was the pendant.

It was around her neck, which was wrong — it was always around his — but his brain was still processing at half its usual speed, the way a phone works after it falls in water, sluggish and literal and stripped of its normal processing capacity. He stared at it. The gold against the hollow of her throat. Her hair was loose, the way she only wore it when she wasn't going anywhere, and she'd been here long enough that the mascara she'd put on in the morning was gone now, not dramatically — just dissolved away over the hours of waiting, the ordinary entropy of a face that had been pressed into worry for too long.

"Suits you," he said.

The voice came out wrong. Grained, scraped. General anesthesia left your voice somewhere behind you, like luggage the trolley had failed to collect. He'd sounded better with a fever. He'd sounded better after getting punched in the throat by a wild elbow in a packed dressing room in Kolkata.

Shraddha laughed and cried at the same time, which was a sound he'd never heard before, and it surprised him — not the fact of it, but the quality of it. He'd expected one or the other. The simultaneous thing was its own genre. She squeezed his right hand, the good side, and he squeezed back, which required a kind of focus that didn't make sense until he registered that his left arm was not producing sensory information in its normal way. Not pain — not yet. Just a profound, managed absence. Like a sentence with a word blacked out.

"That's the first thing you say," she said. Not a complaint. Something between a complaint and the kind of gratitude that can only arrive after long waiting.

"I was going to ask for water," he said. "But then I saw — where did you — can I have water?"

She handed him the cup without releasing his right hand, which required a contortion of her upper body that she performed without seeming to notice it. He drank. The water was room temperature and tasted like nothing and was the best thing he'd ever had.

The surgeon came in thirty minutes later, a man named Dr. Krishnaswamy who had the careful, pleasant manner of someone who spent his days delivering news that could break people and had developed extensive emotional insulation against the weight of it. Rotator cuff repair, full: the supraspinatus had been the primary issue, the infraspinatus showing secondary wear, the whole structure having been operating under conditions of sustained overload for longer than Dr. Krishnaswamy considered wise. He used the word "wise" as if it were a medical term. Vipin, still blunted by post-op medication, found himself liking the man for it.

"Timeline?" Vipin said.

"Conservative estimate: nine to twelve months full recovery."

"Non-conservative."

Dr. Krishnaswamy looked at him with the expression of a man who'd anticipated this question. "Six to eight. If your rehabilitation is exceptional."

"It'll be exceptional."

"Then say eight," Shraddha said, not looking up from where she'd taken his hand again, "and he'll be back in six."

Dr. Krishnaswamy smiled, which was not reassuring in the medical sense and was entirely reassuring in every other sense.

What happened next, across the weeks that followed, surprised the physiotherapists more visibly than they intended. Vipin noticed them doing it — the exchanged glances when he hit a milestone two weeks before the expected date, the recalibration of the laminated recovery charts that lived on the physio room wall, the way Shetty would type something into his laptop after a session with the focused brevity of a man updating a document he was increasingly uncertain about. The shoulder that should have needed four weeks to manage basic elevation movements managed them in eleven days. The range-of-motion benchmarks — not strength, that was different — but range, the raw mechanical question of how far the rebuilt thing would move — arrived ten days early. Then twelve days early. Then three weeks early.

"Your biology is unusual," Shetty said, in the careful tone of a man who was not willing to commit to a stronger word.

"I know," Vipin said.

"I'm not saying it's a problem."

"I know that too."

Shetty entered something into his laptop. Vipin watched him do it and thought: *write down whatever you want, I can't tell you anything you'd put in the notes.* He had no mechanism for explaining the way his body worked. He'd never been able to explain it. He just knew it did, the same way the pandit had known which incense affected different worshippers without being able to explain the chemistry behind it. Knowledge without mechanism. You accept it or you exhaust yourself trying to.

But fast — even extraordinarily fast — was still geological by the standard he'd operated at. Not the standard of injured recovery time. The standard of himself, fully operational, moving through a day at the speed of instinct, every hour organized around the game, the body's main purpose being the game, every food and sleep and rest decision in the service of the game.

Without that architecture, the days had no skeleton.

He'd wake at six-fifteen, which was when he'd always woken, and the morning would be physio and ice and the specific ache of a shoulder being coaxed back into usefulness. That took two hours. Then: nothing. Then: nothing. Lunch — he was cooking now, which was new and which he'd explain to himself as practical and which he would not examine too closely. Then: nothing. An attempt at watching something on his laptop that collapsed into staring at the ceiling. Then: nothing. Sleep.

He was twenty-five years old. He had been in organized cricket since he was fourteen. For eleven years the game had been the skeleton on which he'd hung everything else — the schedule, the geography, the relationships, the daily metrics of his own competence. Remove it, and you were left with a man-shaped space. The space that was left, it turned out, was also shaped a little like an orphan. He noticed this without being able to articulate it. He didn't try.

The humor got harder. Not absent — Vipin without humor was a different organism, unrecognizable, which both he and the people around him understood. He still cracked jokes about the sling. ("This is a fashion choice. I'm very confident about it.") Still made the physio — a woman named Dr. Priya Bhat who had the professional commitment of a general and the bedside manner of a general's more efficient assistant — the subject of extensive speculation about her motivations. ("She says 'three more' and then when I do three more she says 'three more' again. This is a technique. This is a technique they teach.") Still produced, in public and in the MI team meetings he attended as a watching passenger, the kind of humor that made rooms stop.

But Shraddha could hear the difference. He knew she could hear it. The jokes were armored now in a way they hadn't been before — before, the humor had been a genuine response to the world, the way some people's first instinct was to sing or shout, his was to find the angle at which something was funny. Now the angle was found, yes, but the finding required a step of conscious effort that it hadn't before. The jokes arrived a fraction slower. Built a fraction differently. Like a man who'd always cooked from instinct now consulting a recipe and producing the same dish, but if you knew the original you could taste the deliberation.

She said nothing about this for three weeks, which was one of the things he would remember: that she saw it that early and said nothing. Did not press. Did not arrive with the targeted question that he both feared and sometimes needed. Just sat next to him on the sofa while he watched a match he wasn't playing in and held the pendant around her own neck and did not tell him that she could see the empty.

---

IPL Season 8 happened in April and May 2015 without him.

He watched it from his flat, which had become a museum to inactivity. He'd started measuring the distance from the sofa to the kitchen — not deliberately, just a habit that had intensified without the counting-frames that cricket provided. Thirty-seven steps from the bedroom to the kitchen. Eight tiles across the bathroom. The street outside had a sequence of seven potholes in the forty meters visible from his window. He knew this because he'd counted them eleven times.

MI went on to win their second title under Rohit, which was correct — Rohit was an exceptional captain and the team was strong — and he watched the trophy lift with something that he categorized at the time as pride and only later, with more honesty than comfort, recognized as corrosive. The kind of pride that has envy metabolized into it. The kind that says *they are winning and I am here and I should be there and I should be proud and I am proud and —*

He turned it off. Made dal. The dal was too thin again.

The commentators were talking about new names. A young fast bowler from Mumbai — Jasprit Bumrah, twenty-one years old, the slingy action, the yorkers, the threat. The Pandya brothers, Hardik and Krunal, big and aggressive and hungry with the specific hunger of talent that has not yet been told what it can't do. A nineteen-year-old from Hyderabad debuting for Sunrisers, a pace bowler who'd clocked 148 on the speed gun in his first IPL over and had the commentators reaching for superlatives about the "next big thing" and the "future of Indian pace bowling." Vipin watched this nineteen-year-old bowl and thought: *he's rough, the length is inconsistent, the slower ball doesn't exist yet*. And then thought: *in two years that won't matter. In two years he'll be fine. In two years he'll be twenty-one and I'll be twenty-seven and the camera will find me in the stands.*

The game didn't stop.

He'd known this intellectually. He'd known it the way you know something you've never had to apply — the knowledge sitting unused in a drawer somewhere, waiting for circumstances to make it relevant. The game did not pause for injury. The game did not arrange itself around his timeline. He'd watched other players come back from injury before, watched them slot back in or fail to slot back in, and he'd absorbed some abstract understanding of what that must have felt like, the way you absorb the idea of grief before you grieve. The abstraction and the fact were different creatures.

He went to Shivaji Park one Wednesday in May.

Not for rehabilitation. He couldn't bowl, couldn't bat in any meaningful sense, the shoulder still producing a complaint at full extension that Shetty had told him to respect. He went because he'd been in his flat for three days without leaving it, which was its own signal, and the park was what the park was — the place where Rao had found him, the place where Mumbai cricket breathed. He stood near the boundary of the main ground in his regular clothes, right arm in the sling, and watched Rao run a session with a group of sixteen-year-olds. He'd been sixteen once, or thereabouts — the official age documentation was always provisional, the birthday a municipal guess. He'd been sixteen and done what these boys were doing, except worse. His technique had been raw clay, and Rao had sat with him for two years shaping it.

Rao saw him and gave him a single nod. Continued the session. Did not come over to check on him, did not perform any of the concerned attention that Vipin would have found suffocating. The nod said: *you're here, good, stand there, it helps.*

It did help. Marginally. In the specific way that the familiar helps — not by solving the thing, but by providing a reference point for the thing. This is what it looks like when people are fine. This is cricket when cricket is normal.

He left after forty minutes. The park didn't miss him.

---

Ravi came on a Thursday in June, arriving at the flat with a bag of food from a specific vada pav stall on Linking Road that he knew Vipin had a precise and somewhat embarrassing attachment to, and with the specific energy of a man who was playing, doing well, and deeply uncomfortable about visiting a friend who was not doing either of those things.

"You look terrible," Ravi said.

"You look healthy," Vipin said. "It's offensive."

Ravi put the food on the kitchen table and looked around the flat with the expression of a man cataloguing something. Not critically — just registering. The laptop that had clearly been left mid-program. The physio resistance bands looped over a chair. The notebook on the coffee table with what appeared to be a tally count of something in the margins.

"How's the shoulder?" Ravi said.

"Following the program. Shetty is optimistic in a way that makes me think he's hiding something."

"He's probably not hiding something."

"He enters things into his laptop with a very specific typing speed that suggests he's processing information he doesn't know how to classify."

"Or he's writing his notes."

"Have you considered that you're wrong about this?"

Ravi sat on the sofa and ate his own vada pav and said: "I got a thirty-two against Kolkata last week. The press gave me about two paragraphs. The same press gave Dhoni seven hundred words for a four that went to the boundary off the edge."

"He's Dhoni."

"I know. I'm just saying. The press loves a name."

There was a pause in which they both ate, and in which the thing that was not being said hung in the room. Ravi was playing. Vipin was not. Ravi was doing thirty-two against Kolkata and getting two paragraphs, which was a real thing that frustrated him legitimately. Vipin was here in his flat counting potholes.

"I'm glad you're playing," Vipin said.

Ravi looked at him. "I know you are."

"And."

"And I know it's also not simple."

"No," Vipin said. "It's not simple."

They ate. The vada pav was from the right stall and tasted exactly like it should, which was a small relief in the way that only food can provide — not resolution, not even comfort exactly, but the straightforward confirmation that some things remain what they were.

The missed physio appointments started in that same week. He missed one, which he attributed to a scheduling confusion that he mostly believed. He missed the second, which he attributed to traffic that he had not actually experienced because he had not left the flat. He missed the third, which he attributed to nothing, because by the third one he'd stopped constructing rationales. He simply didn't go.

He was not trying to punish his shoulder. He understood this with a clarity that arrived only in retrospect: it was not the shoulder he was withholding treatment from. The shoulder was healing on schedule and ahead of schedule and would keep healing because his body had its own agenda that was independent of his emotional state. He was punishing himself — the self that had let this happen, the self that had bowled through the precursor sound in December, the self that had known and not spoken. He was punishing the specific stubbornness that had walked him into the surgery. If he missed the physio appointments, there was no progress to report. No progress meant no questions about return dates. No return dates meant no hope to manage the possible collapse of.

The physio called MI management on a Friday. MI management called Rao on a Saturday. Rao called Vipin on a Sunday morning.

"Hello," Vipin said.

Rao said nothing for twenty seconds.

The twenty seconds passed. Vipin counted them, which was unavoidable, and stared at his kitchen ceiling, and the count reached twenty-one and twenty-two and he waited for the lecture, the concern, the structured concern that Rao had perfected over twelve years of dealing with him — the approach that said *I see what's happening* in a voice that made it impossible to deflect.

"You're not punishing your shoulder," Rao said. "You're punishing yourself."

Pause. Longer than twenty seconds this time.

"I know," Vipin said.

"The shoulder will heal whether you feel like going or not. You know this. You're not confused about this."

"I know."

"Dr. Bhat has a Tuesday slot open."

"I'll call her."

"You'll call her today."

"I'll call her today."

He called her that afternoon. Made the appointment. Went on Tuesday. The physio room was white-walled, with resistance bands looped on hooks along one wall in the progression of colors — red, yellow, green, blue, black — that corresponded to increasing resistance. The bench smelled of sanitizer. The window faced west and in the afternoons admitted a particular quality of Mumbai light that turned everything slightly golden, which was the wrong aesthetic for a space that was fundamentally about clinical measurement and tolerated pain. He'd spent fifty hours in this room since the surgery and knew its inventory the way he knew cricket grounds — the position of the clock, the specific squeak of the third resistance band hook when you pulled it, the sound his own breathing made during the resistance exercises.

That last thing was the strangest. In cricket — at the crease, in the field, during his run-up — there was always ambient sound. The crowd, the fielders, the opponents, the ground itself making its cricket-ground sounds. In physio there was nothing between him and the sound of his own effort. He breathed during the exercises and heard himself breathing and it was a sound that was both familiar and alien, the sound of the thing that was left when you stripped everything away: just the body doing what the body did, in a white room, without an audience.

Dr. Bhat said "three more" and he did three more.

---

He started cooking in July.

Not cooking cooking — he'd made dal before, simple things, rice and dal on his small gas stove in the way that you do when the alternative is going out and you don't want to go out. He'd always been capable of the basic thing. This was different. This was following YouTube tutorials for recipes with the level of focus he'd brought to studying a new bowler, taking notes in the margin of a small notebook, tracking ratios of dal to water, spice quantities, timing. Vegetarian, always — the pandit's inheritance, as much a part of him as his bowling action. But now a project.

The results were mediocre for a month. The dal too thin, the seasoning inconsistent, the rice sticky because he couldn't get the water ratio right. He watched four different tutorials on plain rice. Four. He got better by the end of July. Not good — better. The dal thickened correctly. The tadka arrived at the right moment. The rice dried out.

Nobody who knew him knew about this. He was not a person who cooked; his identity was already full. But in the empty scaffolding of these months, the chopping and measuring and counting of ingredients — three tablespoons, seven minutes, two pinches — gave the day a micro-structure that was not cricket but was at least structure. You can't count potholes forever. At some point you need to count something that turns into food.

---

The pendant came back on a Wednesday afternoon in August.

Shraddha arrived unannounced, which she almost never did — she had a performer's awareness of the importance of staging, of entering a space at the right moment rather than the convenient one. She arrived at his flat with a bag from his particular preferred bakery in Bandra, the one that did the masala bread he liked, and she handed him the bag and then reached around her own neck and unclasped the pendant.

"I've been keeping it safe," she said. "But it's yours. You need it back."

He held it for a moment. The chain pooled in his palm. The pendant was warm from her skin, which felt wrong and right simultaneously. He put it on. Clasped it at the back — awkward with one fully functional arm, but manageable. The weight against his chest reset something he'd been unaware was not in its right position.

"Thank you," he said.

"Don't thank me for something that's yours."

She stayed for dinner. He made dal, his current best version, and she ate it with the particular diplomatic appreciation of someone who was being honest about finding it adequate rather than exceptional. He appreciated this. She'd always done it — told him the truth sideways, not the truth you had to brace yourself for but the truth that emerged from being herself in your company.

After she left, he didn't put the drawer away. He'd been thinking about the drawer for three days, in the way that a specific thing occupies a corner of your mind without you formally assigning it attention. He'd known the drawer would assert itself when the pendant went back around his neck. He didn't know why. He just knew.

He pulled the drawer open.

The collection, spread on the bed: the newspaper clipping — "Boy from nowhere takes IPL by storm," the headline, though that wasn't the headline he remembered, the actual headline was shorter, he'd just reconstructed it that way. A small piece of paper, folded twice, on which he'd written at age eighteen in the handwriting of someone who'd taught himself to write without formal instruction: *I want them to say my name.* Three match balls from specific innings — he didn't need to read the dates to know which games, the balls had their own texture to his fingers, the worn seam of the IPL debut, the raised stitches of the India debut. A boarding pass to South Africa. An ATM receipt for a withdrawal of twelve thousand rupees on the night of his first major contract signing, kept because he'd checked the balance twelve times after the deposit arrived and this particular receipt showed the number. An unsigned letter, three sentences, handwriting he didn't recognize. A photograph: a young couple, anonymous, taken in what might have been a Mumbai street in the late 1980s, printed on paper stock that had been at the margins of photographic reproduction. His India cap, not the cap itself but a photograph of him wearing it, taken by Ravi in the dressing room. A leather tennis ball with its seam mostly unwound — Bhai's ball, from the day on the street when Bhai was just a man who watched cricket. Anjali's card: *Anjali Mehta, Feature Writer, Cricinfo.*

He laid them out on the bed. Looked at the full array.

He'd never done this before — never arranged them. They lived in the drawer in the order he'd put them in, and he'd add to the collection and sometimes open the drawer and see them all but never with the intent of examination, never with the purpose of seeing what they added up to. Now he arranged them in a line and looked at the line and understood, in the way understanding sometimes arrives not as a thought but as a physical sensation somewhere below the chest, what the line was.

Evidence. Every object was evidence that he had existed. That he had mattered to moments. The ATM receipt proved he'd had money. The boarding pass proved he'd gone somewhere. The piece of paper proved he'd wanted something. The balls proved he'd played.

The boy who had been left on temple stairs was still checking, from the inside of an adult's body, that he was real. Still collecting proof. Still performing the audit of his own existence.

He sat on the edge of the bed with the objects surrounding him and the pendant warm against his chest and the truth of this settling into his body the way cold water settles into stone — not quickly, not gently, but completely. The crying that started was not the kind he'd produced in any crowd, not the kind you perform even privately, not the specific grief of a bad match or a passed moment. It was the older kind. The kind that comes from the eight-year-old who woke up one morning and the pandit was dead and the world became a place you moved through alone, and who had been carrying the audit of his own existence for seventeen years, and who had just, on an August Wednesday in his own flat with dal thickening on the stove, looked at the audit and understood what it was for.

Nobody saw it. He was alone. Some things happen alone because they have to. There's no one who should see you like that, not from the outside, not looking in. The only person who can survive the view is the one who's doing it.

He cried for twenty minutes, approximately. Ugly and ragged and private, the ugly kind that sounds wrong and involves breathing through the mouth. Then it stopped, the way that kind of crying stops — suddenly, as though a valve had closed. He sat with the objects. Pendant against his chest.

He picked up the photograph. The young couple, anonymous. He turned it over. Nothing. He turned it back and looked at the edges.

He'd looked at this photograph dozens of times. He'd never noticed — or rather, he'd registered and not processed, the way you register peripheral information without it surfacing. The right edge. The paper was cut. Not torn — cut. With scissors, or a blade, cleanly along the edge. The image had been cropped. There had been more photograph once, more of the frame, and someone had removed it. Not damaged — removed. Someone had decided, deliberately, what this photograph would show and what it would not.

There was more to the image.

Someone had chosen what to send him and what to keep.

He held this for a long time. Long enough that the dal started to catch at the bottom of the pot and the smell drifted into the bedroom, and he got up slowly and turned down the gas, and then stood in his kitchen with the photograph in his right hand and the pendant against his sternum and the very specific understanding that the archive in the drawer was incomplete.

Somewhere, there was the rest of this picture.

---

*He put the photograph back in the drawer. Closed it. Ate the dal. The dal was right this time — right temperature, right consistency, the tadka sitting on top where it should. He ate the whole pot. Then he went to bed, and lay on his back, and stared at the ceiling, and counted the cracks in the plaster until the number stopped mattering and he slept.*

*He dreamed of a photograph. Wide. Full frame. A young man and woman with the specific look of people who have recently survived something and don't yet know if what comes next is better. There was more of the frame. On the right. Something he couldn't quite see, because he'd never seen it, because someone had cut it away before it reached him.*

*He didn't remember the dream in the morning. But the feeling of it stayed: the specific frustration of an image that stops before you want it to.*
