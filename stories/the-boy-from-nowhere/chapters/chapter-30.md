# Chapter 30: The Search

**Timeline**: Late 2015 – Early 2016 (age 25–26)

---

The PI's name was Sunder Krishnamurthy, and he worked out of an office in Andheri that smelled of strong filter coffee and the accumulated paper of thirty years of finding people. The office walls had no sports memorabilia, no celebrity photographs — just a city map of Mumbai with clusters of yellow pins marking nothing Vipin could read, and a framed print of something Tamil that he couldn't translate. Krishnamurthy himself was sixty or thereabouts, with the bearing of a man who had been a senior police officer and had retained the posture and the economy of movement long after the rank had become a pension. He didn't stand when Vipin came in. Didn't look up for three seconds. Read the last line of the document he was reading, set it down, looked up.

"Sit," he said.

Vipin sat.

"You're a cricketer," Krishnamurthy said, not as a compliment, not as the beginning of a conversation about cricket — as a data point being registered.

"Yes."

"I find people. Celebrities, construction workers, missing children, absconding husbands — same process for all. You being a celebrity means that I'll charge the standard rate plus a privacy adjustment because there will be press risk I'll have to manage. It doesn't mean I'll find your people faster or slower."

"I'm not looking for faster. I'm looking for thorough."

Krishnamurthy wrote something. "Tell me what you know."

He told him everything. The pendant. The temple stairs. The year — 1990, June approximately, which was the municipal estimate. The name: Vipin Chaudhary, engraved. Money left with him. The pandit's account of the morning, which Rao had recorded from him when Vipin was fifteen and the pandit's memory was going and they'd both understood that the record needed to happen before the memory completed its departure. He hadn't told Rao why he'd asked for that conversation. Rao had arranged it and asked nothing.

Krishnamurthy asked questions that were specific and uncomfortable and had no performance in them, which Vipin found he appreciated more than he'd anticipated. What denomination was the money. Did the pandit describe the person who left him or only the pendant. Were there other children left at that temple. Was Vipin's date of birth a medical determination or a municipal guess. He asked these questions in the same flat tone he'd use to ask for a glass of water, and Vipin answered them with the same tone, and it felt like the most honest conversation he'd had about this in years, possibly because neither of them had any investment in the answers being palatable.

"Brief," Krishnamurthy said when Vipin was done.

"Male infant left on temple steps, Mumbai, approximately June 1990. Gold pendant engraved 'Vipin Chaudhary.' Money. Find who left him."

Krishnamurthy wrote this down as well, though Vipin was fairly certain he didn't need to write it down — the notebook seemed to be for Vipin's benefit, the visible act of recording being part of the service. "I'll start with temple records and municipal birth records. If those are clean I'll go to the police side — abandoned children cases from 1990. It'll take time. Mumbai in 1990 was a different city. Records are patchy or missing entirely."

"I know."

"You understand there may be no answer. I want to say that first. The most likely outcome in cases like this is partial information. You find a thread and you follow it and it ends somewhere before the full picture."

"I understand."

"Then we're clear," Krishnamurthy said, and reached across his desk to hand Vipin a card, which was redundant since they were already in his office, but which seemed like the correct gesture to close this opening of the conversation.

---

He told Shraddha first. Not Rao, not Ravi — Shraddha, that same evening, over the phone because she was on a shoot in Pune for three more days and he didn't want to wait.

"I'm looking for them," he said. No preamble. No framing. "My parents. I hired a PI."

A pause. Not a surprised pause — a pause that had the texture of something being set down carefully. "I know," she said.

"You know?"

"I've been waiting for you to be ready." She said it without drama, the way she said things that were true and important and didn't need staging. "The drawer. The photograph. I've watched you with that drawer for two years. I knew you'd get there eventually."

He held his phone and thought about the drawer and the cut photograph and the eight-year-old who'd been performing an audit of his own existence for seventeen years. "You didn't say anything."

"It wasn't my thing to say. It was yours." A pause. "Are you scared?"

"No," he said immediately, which was the wrong word. "Yes. I don't know what it is. It's not exactly scared."

"The thing where you might find something worse than not knowing."

"That's it. Yes."

"You still did it."

"I still did it."

She said: "Good. Tell me everything."

He told her everything. Krishnamurthy's office, the filter coffee smell, the city map with the yellow pins. The questions about the denomination of the money. The pendant. She listened without interrupting, which was rare — she was a person who thought while speaking, who processed by talking — and when he was done she said: "He sounds methodical."

"He's the most methodical person I've met outside of Rao."

"Then he's the right person."

He told Rao the next day, at Shivaji Park, at the end of a session he wasn't participating in but had come to observe. Rao listened with his glasses on his forehead, which was their default position when he wasn't reading, and looked at the net session in progress rather than at Vipin, which was Rao's method of giving people space to talk without the pressure of being watched.

"Whatever you find," Rao said, when Vipin was done, "you're still you."

Vipin thought about this. "What if what I find changes how I understand me?"

"Then you're still you," Rao said. "With more information. The you part doesn't move."

This was the simplest possible formulation of a thing Vipin had been unable to articulate, which was why Rao was Rao. He said it and it was correct and it didn't need anything added to it. Vipin stood next to him for twenty minutes after, watching the sixteen-year-olds in the net, not talking, and the pendant sat against his sternum and he let the simple true thing do what it needed to do.

---

The shoulder cleared him in the third week of October.

Eight months post-surgery. The rehabilitation had been everything Dr. Krishnaswamy had meant by "exceptional" — consistently ahead of the benchmark dates, consistently past what the conservative estimate had predicted, the enhanced body doing what it had always done which was refuse to cooperate with the idea that things took the time they were supposed to take. Shetty ran his final battery of assessments and typed his notes at a speed that suggested satisfaction rather than the diagnostic uncertainty Vipin had attributed to his earlier typing. Dr. Bhat, who had said "three more" an estimated four hundred times over eight months, said "cleared" in the same tone she said "three more," which was no tone at all, just the information.

The MI nets were at Wankhede, late October morning. The other net was occupied by two of the U-22 boys doing throw-downs. Vipin stood at the top of his mark with a cricket ball in his right hand.

He hadn't held a cricket ball in competition or practice since the surgery. He'd held other things. Resistance bands. Physio rollers. The rubbery grips of rehab equipment. But a cricket ball is a specific object — the weight of 155.9 grams, the leather that has a smell like nothing else, the hard core that communicates through the palm with a different language than anything else you put in your hand. He turned it twice. The seam was raised on one side, worn on the other. Practice ball. The red had gone brown on the used hemisphere. He'd held thousands of cricket balls. He'd been holding them since he was six years old, when the pandit had found him a taped tennis ball and then, a year later, a real one from the temple donation box — someone had left it, which even then had struck him as an odd thing to leave at a temple, though the pandit said God received all offerings and cricket balls were no different.

Nine months. The hands remembered but the body had a caution built into it that wasn't fear — it was biology, the joint's memory of damage, the automatic conservatism of rebuilt tissue. He felt it as a slight hesitation in the run-up, not physical, just a question his body was asking itself before each foot fall.

He bowled.

The ball came out clean. Not fast — 126 on the speed gun — which was correct, which was what the body did after nine months of not, the pace temporarily buried under the caution the body imposed without being asked to. But the arm was right. The action was sound. The shoulder reported nothing irregular. He stood at the end of his follow-through and listened to the shoulder's report and it said: *this is fine, this is what this is for.*

He walked back to his mark. Measured the steps with his eyes. Stood at the top. The Wankhede wicket at the far end of the net, its stump visible from here at the distance that always looked shorter than it was. He bowled again.

128.

The session had a rhythm after that — the run-up, the bound, the leap, the delivery, the follow-through, the walk back. The rhythm was familiar in the way that home is familiar, which is not a comfortable familiarity but a deep one, the kind that operates below the level of thought. He stopped counting the deliveries consciously after the twelfth. Somewhere around the twentieth ball his body stopped asking the question. The caution dissolved and what was left was just the action, the act of bowling, the shoulder turning over like a mechanism that had been correctly reassembled.

The sequence on the speed gun went: 126, 128, 131, 131, 134, 130, 136, 138 — a progress report in digits, the body unlocking from the inside. By the thirty-fifth ball he was at 141. The fortieth: 145. The fiftieth: 143, with the slight dip at the end of a long set that was effort rather than damage. He stopped and rotated the shoulder in its diagnostic motion and it said the same thing: fine, this is what this is for.

He stood in the net for a moment after the fiftieth ball. Shetty was behind him with the speed gun and a clipboard but had the professional wisdom not to speak. The U-22 boys in the adjacent net had stopped their drill at some point in the last fifteen minutes, which he registered now, which meant they'd been watching, which he chose not to acknowledge. He turned the ball in his hand. The leather was warm from fifty deliveries. He put it in the ball bag and unstrapped his wrist brace.

"How does it feel," Shetty said.

"Correct," Vipin said. Which was the most precise answer he could give: not good, not great, not better-than-expected. Correct. The body doing what the body was made to do.

He came back the next day and bowled to 148.

Two weeks later, Shetty was standing with the speed gun and looking at the reading in the way a man looks at a number he was not expecting. "153," Shetty said.

"I heard it," Vipin said.

"Your pace is entirely back."

"I know."

"Maybe more. The comparison data from pre-injury has you at a median of 149. You've hit 153 four times in this session."

Vipin looked at the net. The practice stump at the far end, standing intact, which meant his line had been imprecise. He adjusted in his mind. "The pace was always going to come back. The body heals what it heals." A pause. "Tell me the exit velocity on the slower one. The off-cutter I bowled last Tuesday."

Shetty consulted his data. "118."

"Thirty-five kph differential on successive balls from the same action."

"Yes."

"That's what I'm more interested in," Vipin said.

---

Rao watched him for two weeks before he said anything. This was Rao's method: watch, process, speak when the speech had something in it that the watching couldn't contain. Two weeks of net sessions, Vipin mixing the 150-plus thunderbolts with the cutters and the slower deliveries and the genuine variation that came not from limited pace but from a specific strategic intelligence that had been absent from his bowling before and was now, startlingly, present.

The injury had done this, which was the perverse logic of bodies that have time to think. Eight months in rehabilitation rooms, no cricket, only the mechanics of recovery — and the mind had gone somewhere the game hadn't let it go before. He'd been thinking about bowling the way a chess player thinks about position: not what can I do to a batsman right now, but what do I want the batsman to believe, and what does that belief make him do, and what do I do to the belief. The pace was a fact. The variation was a vocabulary. The sequence was a sentence. He'd had nine months to learn how to construct sentences.

"You could just bowl fast," Rao said one morning, which was the opening line of the conversation rather than a suggestion — Rao never said a thing to a player without knowing the response he expected to receive.

"I could," Vipin said. "But anyone can be fast. There are five people in the country right now who bowl at 150 or better. I want to be smart AND fast."

Rao's reading glasses — which had migrated from his forehead to the bridge of his nose during the session, an unusual position, suggesting he'd been reading something and forgotten to return them — slid off the bridge and dropped. He didn't catch them. They hung by the arm lodged on his shirt pocket. He left them there.

"Where did the chess player come from?" Rao said.

"He was always there," Vipin said. "I just had enough pace that I didn't need him."

Rao looked at him for a long moment. His expression was the expression of a man receiving confirmation of something he had suspected for a decade but had not been able to demonstrate. "You know what you just told me?"

"That I wasted eight years bowling too straight."

"No." Rao picked up the fallen glasses, folded them, held them. "You told me the injury was useful. I'm not going to say I'm glad it happened. But you just told me it was useful."

Vipin considered this. He thought about the physio room and the white walls and the eight months of not-cricket, the thing that had always felt like punishment becoming a different thing in retrospect. "Maybe."

"Not maybe."

"All right. Not maybe."

The bowling coach — a man named Venkat who had played four Tests in the nineties and had the cheerful belligerence of someone who'd survived a career in professional cricket — had not been at this specific conversation but was present three days later in the match simulation where Vipin played against MI's own batting line-up. Their best batsman in the simulation — a young opener from Karnataka named Suresh with the particular technique of someone who'd been coached into correctness — had been hooking anything short all session. Vipin had given him three bouncers and watched him hook them, registered the length of the pull shot, the early commitment. The fourth short ball: 153. The batsman braced, pulled early. The fifth: same run-up, same body angle, same arm speed. 118, off-cutter. The batsman was already through it. The ball hit the off stump with the modest, clarifying sound of a thing done correctly.

Vipin said nothing. Small smile, the private kind.

Venkat turned to Rao. "He just chose craft over ego with 153 in the tank."

Rao watched Vipin walk back to his mark. "That's not a bowler. That's a cricketer."

Krishnamurthy's first report arrived in December, four months into the search.

The executive summary was brief. Temple records from 1990: incomplete. The pujari from that time was dead; his successor knew nothing of 1990 cases. Municipal birth records: nothing corresponding to Vipin Chaudhary as a registered birth, which was expected since illegitimate or abandoned infants in 1990 Mumbai were often not formally registered. Police records for abandoned children cases in 1990: three entries. None matched the pendant detail.

Then: a thread.

A woman named Sunita Devi had filed a missing-person report in 1991 at the Haryana police station serving a village near Hisar. Her husband: disappeared from the village in 1989, headed for Mumbai, seeking work. Surname Chaudhary. Jaat community. Husband's first name: Rajveer. Rajveer Chaudhary. The report was filed, investigated cursorily — a Haryana village man going to Mumbai in 1989 was a common enough pattern that the investigation didn't last long — and eventually marked as inactive.

Krishnamurthy's analysis: "If Rajveer Chaudhary came to Mumbai in 1989, possibly with a companion or acquiring one in the city, and had a child in 1990, the pendant with the Chaudhary surname and the Jaat community connection align with circumstantial probability. The naming pattern — Vipin being a northern Hindi name, not a Mumbai name — also suggests North Indian parentage. This is circumstantial, not evidential. But it is a thread. Sunita Devi may still be alive — she would be in her late fifties or early sixties. Address on the 1991 report is a village near Hisar, Haryana. Recommend deciding whether to pursue this lead."

Vipin read the report at his kitchen table. Dal on the stove — his improved version, the version that worked now. The report was six pages. He read it twice. Then he read the single paragraph about Rajveer Chaudhary three more times.

*Rajveer Chaudhary.*

He put the report down on the table. Outside the window his street was doing its evening thing, the auto-rickshaws and the fruit seller and the specific pitch of a city that doesn't fully understand the word quiet. A man's name. An actual man's name. A person who had existed in this world, not as a shape in a cut photograph, not as an engraving on a pendant, but as a person — someone who had filed a missing-person report as part of, or been the subject of, one. He'd been twenty-four or twenty-eight or somewhere in that range when he left Haryana. He'd had ambition, the way young men who leave villages for cities have ambition, the particular hunger of it.

*His father had a name.*

He read the paragraph again. The dal bubbled on the stove; he turned it down without getting up, the instinct for the stove's sounds now automatic, a domestic literacy that had arrived without announcement during the nine months of recovery. Then he read it a fifth time.

What he was looking for each time was the same thing: evidence that this was real. The mind did this with information that contradicted its operating assumptions — ran the data again, and again, waiting for the reading to change, waiting for the paragraph to say something different or less true. He read it a sixth time and it said what it had always said. He put it face-down on the table.

*Rajveer Chaudhary.* A name. A life. A person who had gone to Mumbai from Haryana in 1989 and disappeared into the city. Who might have been twenty-two or twenty-five or twenty-eight. Who was Jaat, which was what the pendant said, which was what the community said. Who had a mother who had filed a missing-person report in 1991 and then presumably resumed her life in a village outside Hisar and had never heard from her son again.

He held this like a lit match — the particular way of holding something that can do damage if you're not careful, in which the care is not protecting yourself from it so much as not dropping it before it shows you what it can illuminate.

He didn't act on it. He put the report in the drawer — not with the other objects, those were a different taxonomy, but in the drawer in an envelope. Closed the drawer. Finished the dal. Ate it.

He turned Krishnamurthy's fee structure over in his mind and thought: the money is the least of it. The question is what you do when you know there might be a sixty-year-old woman in a Haryana village who filed a missing-person report in 1991 and never found her answer and might be the only living person who can tell you about a man named Rajveer Chaudhary.

He didn't know yet. He put that question in the drawer too, metaphorically, and told it to wait.

IPL Season 9 in 2016 was headlined, in the narrative architecture of sports journalism, as "Chaudhary Returns After Injury." The narrative machine loved this structure — the comeback, the question of whether the body was the same, the implicit drama of a player who'd been exceptional now needing to prove it again. He'd watched this machine work on other players before. He understood its logic. He gave it the material it needed in the first match.

The dressing room at Wankhede before that first match had a quality he hadn't anticipated — not nervousness exactly, because he'd been in this dressing room before sixty-odd matches across eight seasons and he knew its specific acoustics, the way the ventilation hummed at a pitch slightly below conversation and the way the locker door at the far end stuck and required a particular shoulder-first method. He knew it. But the body didn't care what the mind knew. The body knew it had been away for nine months and was registering that absence in the form of a slight elevation in his resting pulse that the physio had noted and logged and he'd waved away.

He sat on his bench — his corner, the same corner he'd occupied since his first season — and changed his boots with the deliberateness of someone buying time. The laces. The tension of the laces. He had a specific method for boot lacing that he'd developed at sixteen and had never varied. He did it now, consciously, the familiarity of the method doing what the method had always done which was communicate to the body that things were in their correct order.

The pendants were out of his shirt, visible. He didn't do this in nets or in practice. He did it for matches — had done it since his first senior match at nineteen, an old habit that had somewhere become a ritual and was now simply a fact of his match-day preparation. The pendant caught the dressing room light. He tucked it back under his shirt, thought about it, took it back out.

Out.

He left them visible for the first match back.

The match was at Wankhede, which helped. His ground, his crowd, the knowledge of every part of its geography that he'd accumulated over eight seasons. His batting in the first innings — coming in at three, needing to rebuild after a collapse — produced 44 off 30 balls that wasn't vintage chaos, wasn't the pure pre-injury aggression, but was more controlled than anything he'd produced at this stage previously. He picked the gaps. Played the slow ball in the gap rather than over the top. Went over the top when the calculation said over the top. The innings was not spectacular. It was correct.

The bowling: two overs in the powerplay. First over: 153-pace first ball, which the batsman — a competent opener from Chennai — survived wide of off stump. Second ball: 121 off-cutter, three-quarter pace. The batsman was too early. Edged. Caught behind. Second wicket: a full 153-pace yorker that the number three batsman could only toe-end to cover. One for sixteen.

The crowd understood something they couldn't have explained, which was that this was a different kind of dangerous from the previous kind. The previous kind had been the danger of raw speed and confidence — the danger of a physical presence that might hurt you. This was the danger of being uncertain about what was coming, of facing a bowler who had two distinct weapons and chose between them by criteria you couldn't reliably predict.

He fielded in the deep and took a diving catch at the boundary in the sixteenth over that required a commitment to the ground that should have worried him given his shoulder's history and did not produce any worry at all, because the shoulder had long since passed into normal and his body had made the decision before his mind formed the question. The crowd roared. He came up from the grass with the ball, stood, and thought: *all of it. Everything is back.*

After the match, alone in the dressing room — the team had moved to the reception and the press area — he sat on the bench and held the match ball. The season-used pink ball, the Kookaburra with its specific compressed resilience. He turned it in his hands.

He didn't put it in the drawer. He held it for perhaps two minutes and then put it in the bin with the other match equipment. He sat for another minute on the bench, in the empty dressing room with its smell of deodorant and damp kit and old leather, and thought about the museum in the drawer.

Maybe he didn't need to keep collecting.

Maybe that was the thing he'd understood on the August night with all the objects spread on his bed: that the collection had served its purpose, which was to prove to the eight-year-old on the temple stairs that he was real. The proof existed. The proof was real. The museum could stay closed now. He could stop adding to it.

He pulled out his phone and texted Shraddha: *I played. It was different. I was different. But I played.*

Three minutes passed. Then her reply: *Different isn't less.*

He read this four times, which was the right number, and then got up and went to find his teammates.

Three days later, a follow-up email from Krishnamurthy arrived at eleven-seventeen at night. He read it and then locked his phone and put it on his nightstand. Then picked it up and unlocked it and read it again.

Sunita Devi was alive. Age 62. Still in the same village outside Hisar, Haryana. Same house she'd filed the missing-person report from, or near enough — the village had grown and the house had changed, but the address from 1991 was traceable to a woman of that name and age. PI had not made contact. Awaiting instruction.

He read the email. Then he read it again, which brought the count to three. He turned his phone face-down, lay on his back, looked at the ceiling. Turned the phone back over and read it. Four. He put the phone on the pillow next to him, which was not where he put his phone, and lay with it there, and after a time he picked it up and read it again.

The thing he was looking for on each reading was different. First reading: the fact, received cold. Second reading: testing whether the fact had changed. Third reading: absorbing the word *alive.* She was alive. A woman who had filed a missing-person report in 1991 for a son who had walked toward Mumbai and not returned was still alive, in the same house, twenty-five years later. Fourth and fifth readings: the particular terror of that, and the particular responsibility of it. She had been waiting for an answer, not waiting in the sense of sitting by a window — waiting in the sense that the absence had never resolved, had simply been added to the architecture of a life, fitted around and built over but not sealed. Sixth reading: the instruction clause. *PI had not made contact.* The question was deferred to him. Seventh and eighth: what it meant that he was the one who had to decide this. Ninth: whether he was ready. Tenth, eleventh: whether ready was even a concept that applied. Twelfth: what going there would feel like. Thirteenth: what not going would feel like. Fourteenth: the same as the first, the fact received fresh, which was the way of things you couldn't fully absorb in a single sitting.

On the third day he opened the laptop and began composing a reply to Krishnamurthy.

He typed: *Do not make contact.*

Deleted it.

Typed: *I will handle this personally.*

Saved the draft. Closed the laptop.

The woman in the village had been waiting for an answer since 1991. She had been waiting twenty-five years without one. Whatever came next was not a thing you could delegate.

He got up and went to the kitchen and made tea, which he never made at midnight, which was why the tea came out wrong — too strong, too sweet, the milk added at the wrong point in the process. He drank it anyway. Stood at the window. The street below had six of its seven potholes illuminated by the streetlight, the seventh in shadow. He counted them — six, then guessed the seventh, then confirmed it when the light from a passing auto caught it.

Seven. Seven potholes. Same as always.

*All right,* he thought. *All right. I'll go.*
