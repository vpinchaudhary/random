# Chapter 34: Rebuilding

**Timeline**: Mid-2017 (age 27)

---

She stayed.

This was the first fact of the new arrangement: Shraddha stayed. She went back to her parents' flat in Juhu the next morning to get clothes and toiletries and the particular face cream she used that smelled like cardamom and something floral he couldn't identify, and she returned by noon, and she stayed. Not forever-stayed — there was no conversation about forever, no decision made — but she stayed for that night and the night after and the week after, and after a while the question stopped presenting itself and the staying became the condition of things.

His flat adjusted to her. This was surprising. He'd lived in it for four years and it had taken his shape and his shape only, the way a man's space does when no other permanent body inhabits it — the kitchen arranged for one, the bathroom shelf with a specific quantity of things, the silences of the flat calibrated to one person's silence. But Shraddha had a presence that occupied space without imposing, and within a few days the flat had adjusted the way a body adjusts to temperature: without announcement, through some mechanism below consciousness.

Her face cream on the bathroom shelf. A book on the kitchen table that she was halfway through — a biography of Indira Gandhi that she kept returning to in ten-minute increments and losing her place in and having to flip back through. A second toothbrush. These were small things. He noticed each one separately, without deciding what to do with the noticing.

---

The cooking was a problem.

Not for him — for her. She was genuinely, comprehensively bad at it in a way that had a certain purity, the badness of someone who had not practiced a thing at all rather than the badness of someone who had tried and failed in incremental ways. The first morning, she attempted to make tea and burned the milk by walking away from the stove to find her phone and not returning for seven minutes. The milk skin had welded itself to the bottom of the saucepan with the adhesive authority of something that had decided to become part of the pan permanently.

"That's — that's a smell," he said.

"I'll clean it."

"The pan might be gone."

"I'll buy a new pan."

"You don't need to buy a new pan."

"I burned the milk, Vipin."

"You didn't burn the milk. You converted it." He looked at the pan with the expression of a man making a diagnosis. "This is milk in a different form now. You've created something."

She looked at him. "Are you always like this in the morning?"

"No. Usually I'm funnier."

She took the pan to the sink. The process of getting the milk residue off took twenty minutes and a level of physical effort that seemed disproportionate. He watched from the kitchen doorway and said nothing useful. Later, when she was in the shower, he put the pan to soak with baking soda.

He taught her to cook in the way of someone who'd learned without a teacher — not by explaining technique but by standing next to her and doing the same thing in parallel, so she could see without being instructed. Dal first, because dal was forgiving. He showed her how to temper the tadka without burning the mustard seeds — the two or three seconds between them releasing their smell and the moment they tipped into acrid.

She burned it on the first attempt. Looked at her phone. On the second attempt she didn't, and the mustard seeds popped correctly and the smell that rose from the pot was the right smell — the small domestic announcement of a thing being done properly.

"That's it," he said.

"This is actually good," she said, tasting from the spoon.

"Yes."

"You could have told me this was easy."

"It's not easy. You just got better at it."

She looked at him over the pot with the expression of a person recognizing the person they are standing next to. He recognized it because he was feeling the same thing: *this is who this person is, specifically, and the specifically is the part that matters.*

---

The telling happened over several days, not all at once. He'd tried all at once before — the version of himself that delivered information efficiently and completely and then closed the subject — and had watched it fail, had felt it fail, the way you feel a wall going up even as you're putting the bricks down yourself. So this time it happened in pieces, the pieces arriving when they arrived, in the kitchen and the evenings and once at two in the morning when neither of them was sleeping and the Arabian Sea had its monsoon sound outside the window, the low continuous percussion of the pre-season pressure building.

He told her about Haryana. The drive from Delhi, the four hours on the NH8 with his driver and the specific quality of the landscape flattening out, becoming its own kind of vast. The village. The lanes that required the car to stop and him to walk. The woman who came to the door — Sunita Devi, his grandmother, seventy-three years old, with a face that was unmistakably a face that had produced his father's face which had produced some version of his own face. The particular way she moved her hands when she spoke, the arthritis making them imprecise, the writing on the envelope she'd eventually pressed into his hands: his name, in handwriting that shook at the corners.

He told her about the photograph. The young couple on a street, 1988 or thereabouts, the right edge cut clean and something — someone — missing from the frame. He got up and brought it out from the drawer, put it on the kitchen table between them. Shraddha looked at it for a long time without saying anything, which was the correct response.

"That's your mother," she said finally. Not a question.

"Probably."

"She looks like she knows what she wants."

He looked at the photograph. "Sunita said she had certainty. A kind of — knowing who she was."

Shraddha was quiet a moment. "What was removed from the photo?"

"I don't know."

"Does Krishnamurthy —"

"He doesn't know either. It's an open question."

She looked at the photo again. He watched her looking at it — watched her doing the thing she did, which was reading a situation at depth without rushing to fill the depth with speech. This was one of the things he'd understood about her in the six years he'd known her: she was not afraid of what she didn't know, the way many people were, the way he had been. She let the not-knowing sit.

He told her about the PI's report. His father's name confirmed: Rajveer Chaudhary. Dengue, June 1990, a charitable hospital in the Dharavi corridor. Twenty-five years old. The admission record listing a woman as next of kin — Kamla — and then nothing after that, no death record, no trace, the city having simply absorbed her or released her somewhere unnamed.

"He was twenty-five," he said. "I'm twenty-seven."

"Yes."

"I've been two years older than my father for two years now." He said this in a flat way, a factual way, the way you state a thing that doesn't have appropriate language. "I keep thinking about that. He was twenty-five in a Dharavi hospital. And I've been —" He gestured vaguely at the flat, the six floors, the strip of sea. "All of this."

Shraddha said: "He'd have been proud."

"You don't know that."

"No. But I'd bet on it."

He was quiet for a while. The pendant was warm against his sternum. He'd been touching it without noticing — the habit, unkillable, the thumb's reflex toward the chain whenever the topic turned toward the origin. "My mother might still be alive," he said. "Somewhere. Not finding me. Or finding me and —" He stopped.

"And choosing not to."

"Yes."

"That's the harder possibility," Shraddha said. Not with judgment. Just accurately.

"I know."

"Have you —"

"I think about it," he said. "Not constantly. But when I wake up sometimes. Like my brain is running the scenarios while I'm asleep and serving me the results in the morning." He touched the pendant again. Stopped when he noticed. "Krishnamurthy is still looking. He doesn't think he'll find anything, but he's still looking."

"Let him look."

"I am."

She reached across the table and put her hand over his. Not saying anything with it, not performing comfort — just the weight of her hand, the warmth of it, the simple fact of contact. He let it stay. This was the new thing: he let things stay that he would previously have moved away from, the wall that had been load-bearing now asked to carry less weight, the architecture adjusting.

He told her about Bhai. The full version — not the edited version he'd given before, with the details smoothed and the debt-guilt managed, but the complete account: the street childhood and the way Bhai had functioned as infrastructure, how a kid with no documents and no people needed someone who knew how to operate outside the legible city. The spot-fixing approach at the Wankhede. The anti-corruption interview where he'd told the true story and omitted Bhai's name and then reported Bhai separately afterward, and the specific quality of that decision — not cowardice, not entirely, but the attempt to square two debts simultaneously and the way it had ended up not squaring either.

"You lied to them," she said.

"I told the truth and kept one thing back."

"That's a kind of lying."

"Yes," he said. "I know."

"Does Rao know?"

He was quiet for a beat too long. "No."

Shraddha took her hand off his. Not in anger — she sat back and looked at him with the expression of someone doing arithmetic. "Are you going to tell him?"

"I don't know."

"That's a yes or a no, Vipin. Not an I-don't-know."

"It's an I-don't-know. I don't know if it helps Rao to know. He'll be — he'll feel —"

"He'll feel like you trusted him enough to tell him. Which is apparently difficult for you."

This landed. He felt it land. Not defensively — it was accurate, which was the reason it landed the way it did, the specific sting of a thing that's true. "I'm working on that," he said.

"I know." She picked up her chai cup. "I'm watching you work on it."

---

She opened up in return, not in a trade — not tit-for-tat, you tell me yours — but in the organic way of two people who have created the conditions for honesty and find, once those conditions exist, that honesty prefers to expand. It happened over the same several days, in the same kitchen and evening spaces.

She told him about the mind-reading problem. The way she'd learned to read people precisely and early — the particular adaptation of a person raised in a family with a large personality at the center of it, who learned to map moods and anticipate reactions the way a ship's navigator maps weather — and how that precision had become a distortion. She knew what people wanted from her before they asked. And once you know what someone wants, the path of least resistance is to give it to them, and she'd spent years giving people what they wanted and then being surprised when they loved the given-thing rather than the actual her.

"I people-please," she said. It was not a confession, exactly — more a factual statement, the way you state a physical characteristic. "I do it automatically. A new person in the room and some part of me is already calculating what they need from me and how to provide it. It's fast. I don't always know I'm doing it."

"You don't do it with me," he said.

"I do it with you too. Less than I used to." She looked at her hands. "When I first started getting known — after Aashiqui — people stopped seeing me as a person and started seeing me as a symbol of something. The daughter. The star. The face from the poster. And I'd spent my whole life being good at knowing what people wanted, so I gave them the symbol. I was very good at being what they needed." She paused. "But nobody was loving me. They were loving the curated version."

"Your father does," he said. "Love you. The actual you."

"My father loves me the way a man loves something he's afraid of losing. Which is its own kind of distortion." She said this without bitterness — analytically, the way of a person who has spent time with the fact. "He also loves the idea of who I should be. And I spent a long time trying to be that idea because the people-pleasing —" She stopped. "It's automatic. You can't turn it off. You just learn to notice when you're doing it."

"Are you doing it with me now?"

She looked at him steadily. "No."

"How do I know?"

"Because I would never have told you any of this if I were doing it with you." A pause. "You don't want me to be what you need. You're too honest for that."

He thought about this. "I'm not always honest."

"You're always honest about the things that matter. The things you hide, you hide because you can't bear them — not because you're trying to manage my impression of you."

"I'm afraid of being loved for my last name," she said. "Or my poster. Or the version of me that's been curated for consumption." She said the last word with a lightness that indicated she'd said it before, had landed on it after many iterations of trying to describe the thing. "You've never given a damn about my last name. You've given me — you've been more annoyed by my last name than impressed by it."

"Your father is terrifying," he said.

"My father is not terrifying."

"From the outside, to a street kid from Mumbai who has never been inside a home like yours — your father is terrifying."

She considered this. "That's fair," she said. "He was trying to be."

"He succeeded."

---

The cricket started slowly and got precise.

Rao met him at Shivaji Park the third week of February, when the morning had its best quality — the 7 AM light coming in low and warm, the ground crew rolling the pitch, the smell of the dew burning off the outfield in the first hour of sun. Rao was already there when Vipin arrived, which was Rao's way: always there first, always positioned in the specific spot at the boundary where he'd stood for thirty years of mornings, a spot that now had the quality of a landmark. Reading glasses on his forehead. Plastic chair from somewhere.

They didn't talk about the drop. Rao knew about the drop; Vipin knew that Rao knew; there was nothing to discuss that discussing would improve. They talked about the bowling. Rao had watched the footage from the last twelve months, which was Rao's constant practice — film study was not a modern innovation to him, it was just part of the work. He'd made notes. Actual notes, in a small notebook with a blue cover that he'd been using since at least 2009. He opened the notebook and read from it with the briskness of someone who had thought through what they were going to say and was now simply transmitting it.

"The pace is there," Rao said. "Always was there. I know you know that. What I want to talk about is what you do with it."

"Okay."

"You've started bowling cutters. Good. The slower ball you tried in the West Indies series — the off-cutter that held up off a length — that was the right idea, wrong execution. Too wide, the batsman was able to wait." He made a mark in the notebook. "And the length. Your natural length is fuller than most fast bowlers — you've always been an lbw bowler, not a caught-behind bowler. Don't change that. What I want to add is variety in the short ball. Not as aggression — as information. You show them the bouncer once, early, you make them think about it for the rest of the spell. Then you don't bowl it again for six overs. And when you do —"

"They've forgotten about it," Vipin said.

Rao looked at him over his reading glasses. "They think they've forgotten about it. That's different. The body doesn't forget. The body is still expecting it. And then when you bowl it again, the body jumps before the brain can tell it not to."

They started with basics. Genuinely basic — the run-up rebuilt from its foundation, every element stripped back. At twenty-seven, with eighteen months of disruption behind him and the drop still recent, the coaching felt like ground. At twenty-two and fast it had felt like interference. The difference was not the coaching.

He ran in for the first time since October and the body knew. The recovery was complete: shoulder healed, arm working, the run-up the return to a language spoken natively. The ball left his hand at 147. By the third morning it was 153 and the shoulder took the load without complaint.

He didn't try to bowl at 153 every ball. That was the new understanding: the pace was an option, not an obligation. You didn't deploy artillery before you'd done reconnaissance.

He bowled a cutter at 128 kph, then a fuller delivery at 136, then stepped up to 148. The academy boy batting against him had moved back by a step without knowing — the body shifting in response to accumulated information. Bowling was a conversation in which you established premises and the batsman responded. If you'd set the premises correctly, the response was always a fraction late.

"Now bowl the one-fifty-three," Rao said.

He ran in full and the boy got an outside edge that flew to where third slip would have been. Rao wrote in his notebook.

---

The Ranji match was in April. Maharashtra versus Baroda, at the BKC ground — the pitch was a good one, slightly green in the first morning but easing, the kind of pitch that rewarded patience.

He batted at number five. He knew this dressing room — the creak of the second locker, the plastic chair with the wobbly leg, the tiffin-wallah near the gate who'd been adding a small banana to his poha order since he was twenty without being asked. The banana was still there. The gesture so consistent it had become ceremonial.

He came in at 187 for 4, second session. Baroda's left-arm seamer was awkward — wide-of-crease angle, the ball sliding across, geometry rather than pace. Vipin spent the first three overs not scoring, doing the thing he'd spent a career resisting: watching. Actual functional watching, the information arriving before he acted on it. He scored his first run off the fourth over. A single, worked to square leg.

Rao was at the boundary in his plastic chair, reading glasses on his forehead, chai cup cooling in his hand.

By stumps Vipin was 79 not out. Four hours forty minutes. The next morning he resumed and batted through the first session and into the second, the focus narrowing as time passed, the world contracting to twenty-two yards. When he felt the old pull toward aggression — *open up, you've earned it* — he let it arrive and didn't act on it. The patience a choice, not an absence.

He scored his century off the first ball after lunch. Touched his helmet, touched the pendant. The second century came forty minutes later, a four through extra cover, and he stood at the wicket and thought: *this is what it feels like to bat like a man who has decided who he is.*

167. Out in the second session of the third day, caught at mid-on reaching for a shot he'd earned — the innings ending the way proper innings end, not to a good ball but to the innings running its natural course.

He walked off and Rao was there at the gate, which was not the usual place Rao stood, but he'd walked from his spot at the boundary to the gate.

Rao's chai cup was in his hand and it had gone cold, he could tell by the way Rao was holding it — absent, the cup having stopped being a functional object at some point and become simply a thing he was carrying. The reading glasses were still on his forehead. He looked at Vipin as Vipin walked through the gate and he said nothing, which was Rao's version of everything.

Vipin sat down on the bench inside the gate. Rao sat next to him.

"Six hours twelve," Vipin said.

"I know. I counted."

"Chai's cold."

Rao looked at the cup. "Hasn't been hot for about four hours."

Vipin laughed. The laugh came from somewhere clean, somewhere that had been used up in the months before and was now refilling. He pulled off his gloves. His hands were sweating and the red of the ball had transferred faintly to the palm of the right glove, the way it did after a long innings, the dye releasing in the grip.

"You've changed," Rao said.

"The bowling you taught me. The patience —"

"Not the technique," Rao said. "That's improved. I mean you've changed. You're different from the boy I had in this ground." He paused. "Different from the player I had in this ground three years ago." He turned the cold chai cup in his hands. "You found something."

Vipin thought about how to answer this. He thought about the Haryana drive and Sunita Devi's shaking handwriting and the hospital record with Rajveer's name and Shraddha staying and the flat adjusting and the dal that was now reliably right. "I found some things," he said. "I lost some things. It's not — I didn't arrive anywhere."

Rao nodded slowly. "No. You started from somewhere different. That's not the same as arriving."

A silence. The kind of silence between two people who have known each other for sixteen years, which has its own quality — not empty, not requiring fill.

"Coach," Vipin said.

"Yes."

"I need to tell you something."

Rao waited. He had always been good at waiting — the specific patience of a man who had spent decades watching young cricketers take the time they needed to say the thing they needed to say, and had learned that the waiting was most of the work.

"When I spoke to the anti-corruption people. About the spot-fixing." He stopped. "I told them the true story. But I left something out. Bhai had come to me first — that's the thing I left out. I reported him separately afterward, but in the interview I —" He stopped again. "I lied by omission. I've been carrying that."

Rao didn't say anything for a moment.

"Why are you telling me now?"

"Because I should have told you then. And because — because Shraddha told me the things I hide, I hide because I can't bear them. Not because I'm managing people. And I think that's — I think that's right. And I could bear this now."

Rao turned the cold chai cup.

"You reported him," Rao said finally.

"Yes."

"And Bhai is done."

"Yes."

"Then the outcome was right. The path was —" He pressed his lips together. "Not what I would have wanted. But you were twenty-three and scared and you had a debt." He looked at Vipin. "I understand it. Don't do it again."

"I won't."

"The selectors will bring you back. West Indies series in November. Your form is right." Rao pushed his reading glasses up another fraction — a nervous tic he probably didn't know he had. "You're bowling like a bowler now, not just a fast bowler. Don't tell anyone I said that. Fast bowlers are sensitive."

Vipin looked at him. "You're the one who made me bowl fast for fifteen years."

"And now you're a bowler. The fast was the foundation. Don't mistake it for the building."

---

Shakti Kapoor called in May.

Not through Shraddha — directly to Vipin's number. Shakti did not as a practice call Vipin directly. He called Shraddha, who relayed things, the infrastructure of a relationship between two men in the same family who hadn't fully worked out what to do with each other.

"Dinner. Thursday. Kiran has a thing. Bring Shraddha."

"Of course, sir."

"Stop calling me sir. You've been with my daughter for six years. You can call me —" A pause in which *Papa* was visibly considered and abandoned. "Shakti. For now."

The Juhu home bore forty years of full habitation — the furniture shaped by people who'd sat in it, the walls arranged by Kiran Kapoor's specific aesthetic rather than a decorator's. Even in her absence the home felt like her.

The dinner was Punjabi: dal makhani, two paneer preparations, roti. Shakti ate with the attention of a man who loves food concretely. He gave the dal credit before speaking, which Vipin found he respected.

"You went to Haryana," Shakti said. He said it not as an accusation and not as a question — as a statement, the way you state a thing you've thought about and have come to a conclusion about.

"Yes," Vipin said.

"Shraddha told me. Not the details — she told me you were looking for your parents. That you hired someone." He tore a piece of roti. "Did you find what you were looking for?"

"Some of it." He gave Shakti the factual core: his father's name and death, Sunita Devi, the hospital record, Kamla's uncertain fate. No affect — Shakti responded to directness.

Shakti listened without interrupting, which was notable for him.

"She might be alive," he said when Vipin finished. "Your mother."

"She might be."

"That's a hard thing to carry."

Shakti put down his roti. The look he turned on Vipin was the same direct look from their first meeting in 2011, but the question behind it had changed — from *sizing up a risk* to something else entirely. "A man who looks for his family," he said slowly, finding the sentence as he said it, "is a man who values family. I didn't understand that. I saw it as instability. Mystery." A pause. "I misjudged you."

"You were protecting your daughter," Vipin said.

"Yes. And protecting a picture. A man without a history is a man you can't fully trust — that was my reasoning. The anchor is the people you come from." He looked at his chai. "I was wrong. The anchor isn't where you come from. It's what you're willing to go and find."

"I don't know if the full question will ever be answered," Vipin said. "My mother —"

"Let him keep looking." Shakti made a small gesture, the shorthand for *things beyond human management.* "Shraddha loves you. The actual way, not the performed version." He looked at Vipin. "Don't make her wait forever."

Vipin held his chai. The sentence had the weight of a man communicating what he was now willing to approve. What had been conditional was becoming unconditional — not by announcement but by accumulation.

"I'm not planning to," he said.

In June the monsoon arrived. The streets ran, the sea went grey-green, and the air had its particular compound of earth and wet stone and relief. His flat was dry — the building well-sealed, the rain staying outside where it belonged.

He sat at the kitchen table on the third day of the monsoon with his tea and Shraddha's half-abandoned Indira Gandhi biography, which he'd picked up from curiosity and found himself reading. She was on the phone with her manager in the other room — the logistics kind of call, not roles, the kind requiring sustained attention he'd learned to give space to.

The pendant was warm against his sternum. He was at the Emergency chapter — 1975, the suspension of elections — and the prose was better than the jacket design had suggested. Shraddha had good instincts about books.

She came in when her call was done. "You're reading it."

"I'm at Emergency."

"I kept falling asleep at Emergency."

"It's good."

"You have no loyalty," she said.

She sat down across from him — her chair, which had become her chair — and found the pencil she'd been marking passages with. Small checkmarks in the margin, not underlines. A system whose logic was her own.

"Shraddha."

"Mm."

"I wanted to say something."

She looked up. Patient expression.

"I know I've been harder to be with than I needed to be. The withdrawal. The going alone to Haryana. The things I didn't say." He stopped. "I'm not asking for credit for trying. I just —" The independence was still present; it didn't disappear. It had been asked to carry less. "I'm glad you stayed."

She looked at him for a moment. Then she looked back at the book and picked up the pencil.

"I know," she said.

"That's all?"

"What else do you want?"

"I don't know. Something."

She made a checkmark in the margin. "You told me you were scared. Outside the Dadar temple, on the phone. You hadn't said that to anyone before." She looked up. "That was the something. Everything since is the building on top of it. Don't undo the foundation by needing applause for the building."

He looked at her.

"I have a tendency to explain wisdom immediately after I've delivered it," she said. "Ignore that part."

He took the first bit.

---

Outside, the monsoon ran down the windows in the way it always did — not in sheets but in each pane's own pattern, the water finding its channel, different every day but somehow always the same. He'd watched those channels for four years. He knew where the one on the left window jogged right near the bottom, caught by an imperfection in the glass.

The trail for Kamla was cold and Krishnamurthy was still looking and the cut photograph in the drawer still had its missing edge. That thread sat where it sat, open, the floor of a question that might or might not ever have an answer.

He was twenty-seven years old. He had the years his father hadn't, and the woman across the table making checkmarks in margins, and the pendant, and Rao's notebook, and the dal he'd finally got right. A career stripped back and rebuilt differently — not with aggression as its foundation now, but with patience. The patience a choice, not an absence.

Tomorrow Rao wanted to work on the slower ball. The off-cutter at 128, the release point a half-degree different, twenty minutes of focused practice to ingrain. Early morning, before the ground got busy, in the best light.

He looked forward to it.

He noticed he looked forward to it. This was the new thing, the thing that had been absent in the months of the bottom: the presence of forward. Tomorrow existing not as a threat but as a continuation.

Shraddha made a small sound — the note of someone finding a sentence worth marking — and turned the page.

The monsoon ran its channels down the glass. He held his tea, which had ten minutes left before it went cold.

The morning would come.
