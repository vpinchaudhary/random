# Chapter 1: Temple Stairs

The rains arrived on a Tuesday in June. Not the polite kind — the Mumbai kind, the kind that doesn't announce itself, just appears at three in the morning and begins, matter-of-fact, to drown the city.

The woman moved through the streets like she knew where she was going, which she did. She had walked this route before. Perhaps many times. Perhaps only once, rehearsing.

The temple was small by Mumbai standards — no towering gopuram, no crowds of pilgrims, just a squat building on a lane in Matunga with a modest covered porch and a brass bell that the pujari rang at four in the morning, eight in the morning, and six in the evening, regular as a government clock. The kind of temple that collects the neighborhood's daily prayers and asks nothing extraordinary in return. The kind of place you could leave something precious and trust it would be found.

She came at 3:45.

She was precise about this. Later — much later — a man who made a career of reading crowds and picking up on things a fraction before they happened would inherit this quality and not know where it came from.

The stone steps were slick with rain. She sat on the third one from the bottom, which had a small overhang from the porch roof above, and she unwrapped the cloth she had been carrying close to her chest the entire walk from wherever she had started.

The baby was asleep.

He had been asleep for most of the journey, which she was grateful for, because she had not been certain what she would do if he cried. She had thought about this. She had thought about all of it — the timing, the temple, the step — and she had not been able to think past the crying, so she had simply hoped.

He didn't cry.

She worked quickly. The pendant was gold — not ornate, not expensive, just solid, the kind that comes from a jeweler's shop in a small town where jewelry is utilitarian rather than decorative — and the chain was long enough that it would not tighten around his neck as he grew. She had measured. She had thought of this. She looped it twice and fastened the clasp, and the pendant came to rest against his chest, which rose and fell with the small reliable rhythm of someone who has not yet learned to be afraid of anything.

The pendant was engraved. Two words: *Vipin Chaudhary*.

She had chosen the name. The surname told him what he was — Jaat, northern, from people who worked with their hands and held grudges and loved with the ferocity of those who have never had too much to lose. The first name she had chosen because it meant *conqueror* in Sanskrit, and she was either hoping for something or asking for something, and she was not, from the evidence of her actions, a woman who asked for much.

She placed money on the step beside him. Not a handful of coins — a deliberate amount, folded inside a small cloth. Enough that whoever found him would understand this was not desperation. This was a decision.

The rain came down steadily. The porch overhang held. He stayed dry.

She stood.

The lane was empty at this hour. Somewhere down the street a dog barked twice and went quiet. The temple cat — a thin orange thing with a torn left ear that had been a temple fixture longer than any of the current neighborhood could remember — watched from the top of the outer wall with the expression of someone who has seen everything and found most of it unremarkable.

She did not look back. Or if she did, the story does not follow her, because she walked out of it the moment she placed him on the stone, and everything that came after was his.

At four in the morning, the pandit arrived for the first prayers of the day.

His name was Govind Shastri and he was sixty-three years old and he had been waking at 3:45 every morning for forty years, which meant that by now his body no longer required an alarm and had, in fact, stopped consulting him about the matter. He climbed the steps in the dark with the automatic confidence of a man who has walked the same path ten thousand times and expects no surprises, and he nearly stepped on the baby.

He did not shout. He stopped. He bent down, slowly, because his knees had been a source of ongoing grievance for the past decade, and he looked.

The baby looked back. He had woken at the sound of footsteps and was now considering the old man above him with the calm, absolute attention of someone who has recently arrived in a place and has not yet decided what to make of it.

The pandit picked him up. He was lighter than expected. He had a full head of dark hair and a gold pendant against his chest, which the pandit examined with one dry-skinned finger. *Vipin Chaudhary.* He turned the pendant over. Just the name, nothing else — no address, no prayer, no explanation. He considered it for a moment, then tucked the baby against his chest, picked up the folded cloth with the money in it, and carried both of them inside to ring the morning bell.

He was four minutes late, which in forty years had never happened before.

He lit the oil lamp. He rang the bell. He placed Vipin on the clean cloth beside the small brass statue of Ganesh — which regarded the new arrival with the benign equanimity of a deity who has seen stranger things — and said, in the conversational tone he used for the deity and for the temple cat and, now apparently, for this: "Well. Here you are."

---

The temple had a logic to it, and Vipin grew up inside that logic the way most children grow up inside a city — absorbing its rhythms until they become indistinguishable from your own heartbeat.

Wake before four. The bells. Incense coming alive in the dark. Marigold garlands placed fresh each morning, their smell thick and orange in the air before the sun arrived. The floor still cool underfoot because the stone had shed the previous day's heat overnight and not yet received the new day's. The pandit's first chai, made in a small steel pot on the one-burner stove in the room behind the sanctum, taken in silence before the first worshippers arrived with their small, daily needs.

They ate rice, dal, roti. The pandit was vegetarian the way the temple was spiritual — not as a discipline, not as a practice, but as the only universe that existed. Vipin ate what was placed in front of him and did not know there was anything else. This would stay with him long after everything else the temple gave him had been transformed by circumstance into something unrecognizable.

He was fast. Not in the way you notice and remark on, not like a trick. Simply: fast. A brass cup sliding off the edge of the low table, and his hand was already there before the thought had completed its circuit. A flower garland unspooling from a hook on the wall, and he caught the trailing end before it touched the floor. A bird landing on the porch railing and then startling — he could tell, half a second before it happened, from the way the wings shifted. The pandit noticed these things. He did not comment on them. He observed them the way he observed the weather or the quality of the morning light, as information about the world worth storing.

At five, Vipin began counting things.

Tiles on the temple floor: forty-seven, with one cracked one near the east wall where a flood season a decade back had pushed the foundation a fraction sideways and nobody had fixed it. Cracks in the outer boundary wall: eleven, though one of them depended on whether you considered a hairline fracture a crack or simply stone being stone. Steps from the inner sanctum to the street: nine, which included the landing. Marigold petals that fell during an average morning puja: between forty and sixty, depending on how enthusiastically the pandit was feeling about the day.

The pandit didn't ask about the counting. He seemed to understand, in the incurious way of old people who have lived long enough to stop being surprised by children, that the boy was building a framework. A way of measuring a world that had not, so far, offered much stability. Numbers were reliable. Numbers stayed where you put them. Numbers did not, without warning, stop being there.

The reading lessons happened in the afternoons, in the low heat of the three o'clock lull when the temple was quietest and the neighborhood was inside avoiding the worst of the sun. The pandit scratched Hindi letters in chalk on the flat expanse of the floor. *Ka. Kha. Ga. Gha.* Patient, deliberate, his voice the same whether he was teaching for the first time or the fifth. He had been a schoolteacher in Nasik before he became a pujari, which explained the methodology. He'd taught the same alphabet to hundreds of children over fifteen years, in a classroom above a hardware shop, before his wife died and the city felt simultaneously too large and too small and the only sensible thing seemed to be taking a quiet posting and living very simply.

Vipin was a better student than most. He had the particular focus of a child with nothing to be distracted by — no television, no other children demanding attention, no mother calling from another room. Just the letter and the chalk and the old man's finger pointing. He learned quickly, the way hungry things often do. By six he was reading the Hindi newspapers that worshippers left behind on the porch bench, scanning columns about politics and cricket and Bollywood with the same neutral attention he brought to counting tiles: not for meaning exactly, but for the pleasure of decoding the system.

The joke happened on a Tuesday in March, when he was six.

A woman came for her regular morning puja and discovered that the specific flowers she had ordered for the offering — a particular yellow variety, important for reasons she had not explained — had not arrived. She was large and wore a great deal of gold jewelry and she expressed her displeasure at length and with enthusiasm. The pandit received this without visible reaction. When the woman finally exhausted herself, Vipin — who had been sitting in the corner supposedly practicing letters — said, in a perfectly reasonable tone: "The flowers are probably counting how much gold you're wearing and feeling inadequate."

A silence of some seconds.

The pandit's face did something it almost never did. Something moved in it — not quite a smile, more like the fault line of one, a controlled shift below the surface. He turned away and made a careful adjustment to something near the oil lamp that did not require adjusting.

The woman stared at Vipin for a long moment, then laughed. The kind of laugh that takes over the body, that requires sitting down. She laughed for what felt like a full minute.

He filed this away. He didn't have words for it yet — he was six. But something in the back of his brain, the part that counted tiles and memorized license plates for no reason, noted the transaction: a joke had moved through a room and changed the temperature of it. That was useful. He didn't know yet how useful. He didn't know it would become the thing he reached for first, always, before he reached for anything else.

The pendant he wore always. Tucked under his shirt — he had started doing this himself at four without being asked, because it seemed right that it should be against his skin rather than visible. He did not know yet the full weight of *Vipin Chaudhary*. He knew it was his name. He knew someone had fixed it around his neck before he was old enough to carry it himself. He did not have the language to think about what that meant, so he simply carried it the way you carry something whose purpose you haven't worked out but which feels too important to set down.

The pandit talked to the temple cat.

Not casually — full conversations, one-sided, about things that had nothing to do with cats. Shankara — the orange temple cat with the torn ear, who was replaced after a few years by a grey cat the pandit also called Shankara, which suggested the name was less a name than a role — received lengthy one-sided discussions throughout the day. Not simple domestic murmuring. Full philosophical conversations. The difficulty of interpreting certain passages of the Gita in modern life. Whether routine devotion retained its value if conducted without genuine feeling. The question of whether a man who had made a mistake could become someone who had not made that mistake, or whether he was always, in some permanent way, the man who had made it.

Shankara sat on the wall and absorbed these observations with the impassive attention of a listener who has heard it all before and found none of it particularly surprising.

Vipin started doing it too. Mostly to pigeons, since he encountered them more frequently on the temple steps. He reported to them: license plates he had memorized from passing cars, the number of blue shirts he had counted that week, what the morning's worshippers had said and what he thought they had actually meant. The pigeons, like Shankara, received this without complaint. He would do this, unconsciously, for the rest of his life — talking to animals in quiet moments, when no one was watching, working out some thought that needed to be said to something that would not respond with an opinion.

The second habit was the pink soap.

The temple bathroom had a particular institutional soap — flat, rectangular, cheap, the color of a washed-out rose — which the pandit ordered in bulk from a supplier in Dharavi. It smelled of nothing and everything, the precise scent of institutional cleanliness that belongs to its category: temples, municipal schools, government hospitals, places that need to be clean without making a statement about it. Vipin washed his hands fifty times a day and the smell lived in him like a second breath. Years later, in hotel bathrooms that cost more per night than the pandit had spent in a month, he would reach for a bottle of something imported and expensive and notice, with the precision of a man who had learned to track things, the absence — the pink soap smell not there — and feel something he had no name for, a gap in a thing he hadn't known was continuous.

---

It was a Wednesday in October, three weeks into the post-monsoon cool, which in Mumbai means the air has become merely warm rather than the previous months' actively hostile.

Vipin was seven.

He knew something was different that morning because the pandit made chai at ten instead of waiting for the noon quiet. He made two cups. He gave one to Vipin.

This had not happened before. Chai was a morning thing and a singular thing. Two cups at ten was an event without precedent, which meant it was worth paying attention to.

They sat on the temple steps in the October sun. The lane in front of them went about its business: a vegetable cart creaking past, two scooters, a woman carrying laundry stacked improbably high on her head, a dog investigating something behind a parked auto-rickshaw with the focused urgency of someone doing genuinely important work.

The pandit drank his chai. Vipin drank his, which was sweeter than usual — the pandit had made it the way Vipin liked it, which he had learned without being told, which Vipin noted as a second unusual thing.

"Do you know what your name means?" the pandit said.

"Conqueror," Vipin said. "And Chaudhary is Jaat."

"Who told you."

"I read it. In a library book about Indian surnames."

The pandit was quiet for a moment, looking at the lane. His hands, wrapped around the steel cup, had a small tremor in them. Vipin had been noticing this for several months — cataloguing it, adding it to the running count of things that were changing. The tremor. The slowness in the morning, the extra time between intention and action. The way he occasionally stopped mid-sentence and looked at the middle distance for a beat too long. New things, all of them. New data.

"The person who left you," the pandit said, "knew what they were doing."

Vipin looked at his chai.

"They gave you a name before you could know what to do with it," the pandit said. "Not a description. Not a wish. A name. That means they saw something before there was anything to see."

"What did they see?"

"I don't know. Perhaps they were asking. Perhaps they were telling." He turned the cup in his hands. "Names can be both. Instructions dressed up as identity."

They sat with this for a moment. A pigeon landed three steps below them, looked at Vipin, and left with the air of someone who had made a wrong turn.

Vipin should have said something thoughtful. He didn't. What happened was this: the pandit had mentioned that morning prayers the following week would begin at four-thirty to accommodate a festival schedule, and Vipin had been quietly furious about this since the announcement, because four-thirty was categorically worse than four — not by thirty minutes in any meaningful sense, but because four was a known time and four-thirty was an intrusion into a different, previously peaceful part of the night. He had been sitting with this grievance throughout the chai conversation, and it chose this moment to find its voice.

"Do we have to do the evening prayers today?" he said. "There's a cricket match at the maidan."

The pandit looked at him. Not with anger. With something that had no quick name — the expression of a man receiving information he already had.

"Yes," the pandit said.

"But it's only—"

The pandit's reading glasses, which had been resting on his forehead, came down. He looked at Vipin over the rims. He said nothing.

Vipin made the specific sound of a child for whom the world is being profoundly unreasonable. The pandit had heard this sound before and had found it unpersuasive on every previous occasion. This occasion was not different.

They did the evening prayers.

Afterward, while Vipin swept the floor — his assigned closing task, which he performed with the efficiency of someone trying to get it finished before being assigned something else — he heard a sound from the back room. Something dropped. A cup, from the acoustic quality of it.

He went to look. The pandit was crouched down collecting the pieces, his hands moving with care, not hurry. He had not called for help. He would not have.

Vipin got down on the floor without comment and picked up the pieces he could reach. The pandit received this without acknowledgment, which was the same as receiving it warmly.

When they had collected everything, the pandit remained still for a moment, and looked at Vipin in a way that Vipin would not be able to categorize for years — not instructional, not supervisory, not any of the usual registers. The way you look at something you are trying to remember accurately.

"Eat properly," the pandit said. "Whenever food is offered. It will not always be offered."

Vipin said okay and went to put the broom away.

He would think about the evening prayer complaint for the rest of his life. Not in the way you think about real wrongs — the self-examination of someone who has done something genuinely harmful. More in the low-frequency way of a thing that arrived at exactly the wrong time to be ordinary. The brattiness of a seven-year-old. The perfectly normal selfishness of a child who wants to watch a cricket match. He would carry it the way you carry a small stone in a pocket — not enough weight to slow you down, but always there when your hand goes in.

---

The pandit died on a Thursday in November. Vipin was not there for it.

He had been sent to the market for vegetables — the pandit's request, which Vipin would later understand was the considered act of a man who had decided what kind of departure he wanted and had made arrangements accordingly. He came back to find a woman from the adjacent lane sitting on the temple porch, crying into her dupatta, who looked up at him and found she had nothing to say.

He put the vegetables down. He looked at the temple — oil lamp, brass Ganesh, marigolds, the smell of incense — and understood that everything was the same and nothing was.

He counted the tiles. Forty-seven. Cracked one near the east wall.

The cremation happened two days later. The neighborhood people took these things on as a collective responsibility — someone always did. Vipin stood at the edge of the gathering in the position of someone who is not sure if they belong at the center of something. Nobody told him to stand elsewhere. Nobody asked him to come closer. He stood at the edge and watched the proceedings with the careful attention of someone filing information they do not yet know the use of.

The new pujari arrived the following week.

He was younger, with a wife and two small children who came with furniture and noise and the particular energy of a family making a new space their own. He was not unkind to Vipin. He fed him twice in the first week and asked questions about family that Vipin answered without decoration — *No parents, no relatives, the pandit raised me* — and the man nodded and asked no follow-up questions, which was itself a kind of answer.

Over the days that followed, the new pujari did not throw Vipin out. He did not ask him to leave. He simply began, with the careful, non-deliberate precision of a man managing logistics, to not quite see him. To step around him. To organize the temple's routines without including a seven-year-old in them. Vipin was furniture from the previous tenancy — not unwelcome, not welcome, simply present in a way that would resolve itself.

He was not a villain. He was a man who had taken a job and found it included complications he had not been quoted on. Vipin recognized this with a clarity that had nothing to do with wisdom and everything to do with having learned early to read the gap between what people say and what they mean. He understood the situation. He was simply deciding what to do with it.

His last night, he lay on the sleeping cloth and looked up at the ceiling. Forty-seven tiles. The cracked one. He had counted them almost nine hundred times. He knew them the way you know the streets of your own neighborhood — not by looking, but by some mapping in the body that doesn't require the eyes.

He took the pendant from under his shirt. *Vipin Chaudhary.* He read it with his thumb, as he had thousands of times, the edges of the letters worn smooth from the years of touch. Someone had fixed it around his neck before he could walk, before he could talk, before he was anything more than the thing they were leaving. The pandit had carried him before he could carry himself. Now neither was here.

He put the pendant back on.

He was up before sunrise. He took nothing. The pendant and whatever he was wearing. He had the sense, which was accurate, that the things the pandit had given him were not the kind of things you could pack. He left the cloth. He left the cup. He left the pink soap in the bathroom, which was not his to take.

He walked out at six in the morning.

The lane was already moving: a vegetable cart, two boys running for a school bus, a woman opening the shutters of a small shop across the street. The city received his departure the way it receives everything — without comment, without ceremony, already moving on to the next thing.

He walked for several minutes in no direction in particular. Then he chose one and walked it.

His first night he slept under the overhang of a chai stall on a lane near Matunga station. The stall was shut. The night was warm. He lay on his back on the concrete, which still held the day's heat in it like a stored grudge, and looked up through the gap between the two buildings above him at the sky. Somewhere to his left a tap was dripping. He counted the drips for a while — irregular, not useful, not a pattern he could do anything with. He stopped.

He counted the stars visible between the buildings and the city's orange glow.

Fourteen.

He knew there were more — that the light was eating the sky and that out beyond the buildings and the sodium lamps the stars continued without him. But between these two buildings, at this hour, in this specific slice of sky, there were fourteen. Fourteen was the number. He filed it next to the forty-seven tiles and the eleven cracks and the nine steps and everything else he had measured, everything that had stayed where he put it.

A stray dog had followed him for the last three blocks of the walk — a medium-sized brown thing with ears that could not decide on a direction, maintaining about four meters of distance with the dignity of someone making clear this was their own choice and not proximity for its own sake. When he stopped at the chai stall, the dog stopped. When he lay down on the concrete, the dog sat.

He looked at it. It looked at him.

After some time — he didn't know how long — it stood up, turned around, and walked back the way it had come. He watched it until it rounded the corner and was gone. It didn't look back. He waited to see if it would, then stopped waiting.

He looked back up at his fourteen stars.

The city stretched in every direction — enormous, indifferent, full of people who had arrived here from somewhere else and made themselves a place in it through some combination of luck and stubbornness and refusing to leave. He had nowhere to go and nothing to carry and no one expecting him in any direction.

He was seven years old. The pendant was warm against his chest.

In the morning he would figure out what came next.
