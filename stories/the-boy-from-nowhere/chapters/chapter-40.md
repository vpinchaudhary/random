# Chapter 40: The Boy From Somewhere

**Timeline**: 2023 (age 33)

---

The waiting room had forty-one floor tiles.

He had counted them three times — once from the door, once from the window, once seated in the plastic chair closest to the vending machine, because position affected the count, because he needed the count to be right. Forty-one was a prime number. He was not sure what to do with this. He sat with it, rotating the fact like a stone in his palm, and when it stopped being useful he stood up and walked to the window and counted the ceiling panels instead.

Thirty-one.

The corridor outside was standard hospital quiet — not the silence of nothing happening but the silence of many things happening behind closed doors, the suppressed industry of a building whose main work was invisible to the people in the waiting room whose only job was to wait. A nurse passed the doorway at intervals he couldn't predict, which was the most unsettling thing. He had gotten through thirty-three years largely on the basis of patterns. This was not a pattern situation.

His phone was in his hand. He had been putting it down and picking it up for — he checked — forty-seven minutes.

---

Ravi called at nine-twelve.

"Are you pacing?"

"I'm standing at the window."

"That's pacing with fewer steps." A pause, the quality of someone eating something. "Priya says to tell you she's on standby if Shraddha needs anything. She also says to tell you to breathe."

"I'm breathing."

"You say that in the voice of someone who has not considered whether it's true."

He looked out the window. The hospital courtyard below had a large neem tree — old, its bark in long vertical ridges, the kind of tree that had been there before the building and would be there after it. Under it, a man in a checked lungi was talking on the phone, pacing the exact elliptical path that Ravi had correctly identified. Vipin watched him.

"She's been in there three hours," Vipin said.

"Three hours is normal. Priya—"

"I know it's normal. I know."

"You're still going to tell me it doesn't feel normal."

"It doesn't feel normal."

Ravi made the sound he made when something was both funny and not funny. "You once bowled a hat-trick in the Centurion Test at one hundred and fifty-four kilometers per hour in front of thirty-two thousand people and then gave the most boring press conference in the history of cricket because you didn't want to show anything."

"That's different."

"Yes. It's less important."

He sat down in the plastic chair again. The chair had a small crack in the right armrest that he had catalogued on his first circuit of the room — a hairline fracture, the plastic grey underneath the cream surface. He ran his thumb along it. "Tell Priya thank you."

"I will. Now actually breathe."

"I'll call you when—"

"I know. Go. I'm here."

He put the phone down. He looked at the forty-one floor tiles. Thought about thirty-one ceiling panels. He was counting things the way he had always counted things, the way the pandit's temple had trained him and the streets had refined and Rao's arithmetic of cricket — the runs per over, the degree of the crease, the number of steps in a bowling run-up before the jump — had given a professional home. Counting was not anxiety. Counting was attention. He was paying attention because this was the thing that required the most of it.

His phone buzzed.

A text from Rao.

*Congratulations.*

He stared at it for a moment. Then he checked the time: nine-twenty-three. Then he checked the last time the nurse had passed: nine-eighteen. He had not been called. Shraddha had not sent word. The birth had not, to his knowledge, happened yet.

He typed back: *He isn't here yet.*

Thirty seconds passed. Then: *He will be.*

He put the phone in his pocket. He looked at the ceiling. Thirty-one panels. Outside, the man under the neem tree was still pacing his ellipse. The ellipse was consistent — the same points, the same arc, repeated. Vipin thought that this was probably not a conscious decision. Some things you did without choosing them.

---

The nurse came at ten-forty-one.

She was a practical woman with efficient hands and the expression of someone who had delivered this particular species of news — the good kind — many hundreds of times and found it still worth delivering well.

"Mr. Chaudhary. Your son is here."

He stood up from the chair so quickly that he knocked the plastic armrest with his elbow and had to catch it before it tipped. The nurse observed this without comment.

"He's healthy. Your wife is asking for you."

He followed her down the corridor. The hospital quiet was different now — or he was different in it, the same acoustic texture arriving differently because the thing he was carrying had changed. He noticed: the strip lighting was the particular temperature of all hospital strip lighting everywhere, institutional and non-committal. There was a small handwritten sign on one door: *Please knock before entering.* Someone had drawn a small star next to the word *please* and he had no theory about this.

---

She was in bed, the position of someone who has recently done the hardest physical thing and is now in the recovery end of it — tired in the way that has no complaint in it, the tiredness that comes on the other side of effort that was worth the effort. Her hair was damp at the edges. The reading glasses were on the bedside cabinet, which was where they ended up when she was not using them, ten degrees clockwise from where she'd set them, because she always set them down at a slight angle.

He adjusted them automatically, which was a reflex he did not register making.

She looked at him. The expression she had, at this moment, in this light, was one he did not have a word for. Not joy exactly — joy was too light a word, too decorative. Something more foundational. The expression of someone who has built a thing and is now seeing it is real.

In her arms: a bundle. Small. Wrapped in hospital cotton, the white-and-blue stripe of every public hospital in India since before either of them were born.

"Come here," she said.

---

He sat on the edge of the bed.

The bundle was — he did not have the vocabulary for this. He had seen newborns before. He had held Ravi's nephew at six weeks. He had seen the photographs his teammates sent, the *it's a boy / girl* pictures in the team group chat. He had processed these intellectually, the way you process data about a thing you know is significant but have not yet directly experienced.

This was not processing.

This was a face. Specific, individual, not generic-baby-face but a face with its own particular arrangements — the slightly pursed mouth, the closed eyes with their fine lashes already defined, the color of him in the hospital light, the hands, the improbable smallness of the hands. He had fingernails. This fact struck Vipin as somehow the most staggering thing — the fingernails, already formed, curved correctly, each one the size of a match head.

Shraddha transferred him with the careful competence of someone who had been doing it for forty minutes now and had learned the weight distribution, the correct angle, the way the head needed support. He received the bundle and adjusted and the baby's eyes opened.

He did not know if newborns could focus. He knew this was probably documented somewhere, that there was pediatric research on the visual capacity of thirty-minute-old humans, and that whatever the research said it was probably not *your son looked directly at you and knew you.* He knew this.

The baby looked directly at him.

He felt the pendant shift against his chest as he leaned forward slightly, and then — without intention, without ceremony — the pendant came free of his collar and touched the baby's forehead. The metal was warm. He felt it happen before he saw it, and when he saw it he stayed still, not lifting it away, holding the position.

The baby's expression did not change. He was considering something, with the focused attention of someone who has recently arrived in a place and has not yet decided what to make of it.

*Here you are*, the pandit had said, thirty-three years ago, to another newborn on another cold surface in another context entirely.

Vipin looked at his son's face and thought: here you are.

---

"Arjun," Shraddha said, from behind him. Her voice was different — tiredness and something else, the quality of a woman saying a name she has been carrying for eight months and is now setting down in the correct place.

"Arjun," he said.

The name landed in the room.

It had taken them a long time to find it. He had, early in the pregnancy, when they had just told people and the name conversations had started happening the way they always happened — at dinners, in text threads, in the particular social form of *have you thought about* — rejected his own name without having to think about it. Too complicated. Too much to give a child before the child could consent to what it meant. He'd rejected Rajveer without saying this aloud, because Shraddha had offered it gently and he'd gone quiet and she had understood and withdrawn it, because she always understood the shape of his silences.

He had not wanted a name that was a monument. He had wanted a name that was a door.

Arjun: the warrior who had stood on the battlefield unable to fight, because he could not find the line between the right fight and the wrong one. Who had been shown the difference. Not a perfect hero — a specific one. A man who asked the real questions and had to learn the answers the hard way. A name that gave the child something to grow into, rather than a weight to grow under.

Arjun Chaudhary. His son.

The pendant had touched both of them now — thirty-three years apart, one newborn left on temple steps in the rain, one newborn in a hospital bed in Mumbai, 2023, the chain from a Haryana jeweler's shop on a woman's last night of making the choice she had made. The pendant that had survived everything: temple floor, storage room, gully cricket, the injury, the worst year, the long road to a village near Hisar and back.

He lifted it away from Arjun's forehead with one hand. The baby's expression remained unchanged — still considering.

---

He stepped out to make the call at eleven-fifteen, finding the corridor outside with the forty-one-tile waiting room visible at the far end, the neem tree man gone from the courtyard below.

The call took three rings. Then a voice, hoarse with sleep and age — Sunita Devi did not phone well after ten in the evening, her hearing was less reliable after dark, she had explained this to him as a practical matter of fact on his last visit.

"Who is this?"

"It's Vipin. I'm sorry to call late."

A pause. The quality of the pause changed. "Vipin." Her voice shifted — not more awake, but more present, the way a person becomes more present when the thing they have been waiting for arrives. "Is it—"

"A son. He's healthy. Shraddha is fine."

On the other end, a long, uneven exhale. He listened to it. A breath that had been held for eight months and was now releasing. He thought of her hands, the arthritis that had been progressing since his first visit in 2016 — she would wake tomorrow and her knuckles would ache in the cold of a Haryana March and she would know that her grandson's son had arrived.

"A great-grandson," she said. Her voice had changed to something that was neither crying nor not-crying, occupying the register in between. "Rajveer would have—" She stopped. He could hear her composing herself, the brief silence of a woman of eighty relearning control. "He would have—"

"He would have been proud," Vipin said. "He would have."

A silence. Not the silence of nothing — the silence of two people sitting with the same image, the same man they were both holding in their separate hands, the same absent figure in the photograph with the cut edge. The man who had gone to Mumbai at twenty-two, who had played cricket badly and then not played it at all, who had loved a woman named Kamla and made a child he could not stay for and died at thirty-one of a liver he had treated unkindly, who had filed a missing-person report with his own name before the end.

Who had given Vipin, without meaning to, the stubbornness. The persistence. The particular Jaat refusal to stop when stopping seemed sensible.

"What is his name?" Sunita asked.

"Arjun."

She said the name in the way old people say names — fully, considering it. "*Arjun.*" A beat. "Your Rajveer will like it. A warrior's name."

He did not correct her: *he's gone*, or *Rajveer is not here to like it.* He understood what she meant. The Rajveer she was talking about was not dead. He was the Rajveer she carried internally, the young version, the one before Mumbai, the one who had laughed at her kitchen table and eaten roti off the same charpoy and helped with the water pump. That Rajveer was present to her in a way that transcended chronology. He did not correct that.

"Get some sleep," he said. "I'll bring him to see you."

"I'll make something," she said immediately, the reflex of a woman who had expressed everything important through food for sixty years. "I'll make—" A pause. "What does Shraddha like?"

"She'll eat anything you make."

"Tell her that from me." A beat. "Tell her the grandmother says so."

He smiled. The corridor light was the same institutional temperature as all the others. Outside the courtyard window, the neem tree was dark, its leaves still in the March night. He stood in the corridor light with the phone warm in his hand and the conversation at its natural end and the hospital quiet around him.

"Good night, Amma," he said.

He was not sure when he had started using that word with her. Some point in the last seven years, the third or fourth visit to Hisar, it had arrived without announcement. She had not commented on it. She had received it the way she received most things — directly, without ceremony.

"Good night," she said. "Go be with your family."

He put the phone in his pocket. He stood in the corridor for a moment. He was not counting tiles. He was not counting anything. He was simply standing in the middle of a Tuesday night in March 2023, thirty-three years old, with a wife down the hall and a son in a cotton blanket and the pendant warm under his shirt and an eighty-year-old woman in Haryana already planning what to cook.

He walked back.

---

It was nearly two in the morning when they moved Shraddha to the recovery room and the hospital went to its quietest version of itself.

She was asleep. He was supposed to sleep. There was a chair that the nurse had pointed to with the confidence of someone who did not believe anyone actually slept in it but felt obligated to offer. He sat in it. He did not sleep.

Arjun was asleep too, in the small wheeled crib beside the bed, on his back in the way they'd been shown, the striped hospital cotton rising and falling with the reliable rhythm of someone who has not yet learned to be afraid of anything. His fists were closed. His face was composed in the specific peaceful absence of a person with nothing yet to resolve.

Vipin sat in the chair that was not for sleeping and watched his son breathe.

He was trying to identify what he was feeling, the way he identified deliveries — early, completely, before the ball arrived. What he felt was not what he had expected to feel. He had expected something large and climactic. The swelling of it. The specific emotional crescendo that the moment seemed to call for. He had seen the social media posts from teammates, the *I can't describe it* and the *life will never be the same* and the *every other thing falls away.*

This was different. This was quiet.

It was the quiet of something being completed — not spectacularly, not with fireworks, but in the way that a thing that has been building for a long time reaches its natural state. The way an innings that begins on a difficult pitch and requires patience for two sessions eventually arrives, if you hold, at the boundary of something good. The way the city outside had been moving for all of this — all thirty-three years — and was still moving, and he had arrived at a particular Tuesday night in March with his wife asleep and his son breathing and the pendant warm under his shirt, and the feeling was not joy exactly or triumph exactly. It was: *here.*

He was here.

He thought — not dramatically, without any sense of it needing to be said aloud — *You will always know where you come from. I'll make sure of it.*

The thought arrived and sat in him and became a fact. Not a promise made to be dramatic. A decision, the kind that doesn't require ceremony, that happens once and then is simply part of the architecture.

Arjun would know the village near Hisar. He would know the photograph on the bedside table — his great-grandparents, their faces, a woman named Kamla who had died before he was born but whose eyes were in the photograph with a specificity that portraits of the living sometimes had. He would know the pendant and what it meant and where it came from, when he was old enough to carry the story. He would know Sunita Devi's kitchen and her roti and the tulsi plant in the painted pot in the courtyard.

He would know Rao's park on Saturday afternoons. He would know Ravi, who cried at everything and called it sinusitis. He would know his mother's reading glasses always ended up ten degrees clockwise and his father always straightened them without thinking.

He would grow up knowing where he was from.

That was the thing Vipin had not had. The thing the pandit had given him in parts — the names, the pendant, the instruction to eat properly whenever food was offered, which was advice about more than food — but could not give him completely. He had spent twenty-six years of his life not knowing. He had found his way to knowing through a detective named Krishnamurthy, an envelope in a cricket federation office, a flight to Delhi at 6 AM, a green gate outside a village temple, an old woman's arthritis hands and her metal box of stored things.

His son would not have to take that journey. The journey was done. The map was made.

He sat with this for a while. The crib's wheels had a small scratch in the chrome, a thin curved line that caught the light when the crib moved. He noticed this without meaning to, the way he noticed everything — force of habit, the pandit's first training, the street's refinement of it, forty-seven tiles and twenty-three ceiling cracks and thirty-one panels and forty-one floor tiles in a waiting room.

Arjun slept.

---

At three in the morning Vipin went to the bag he'd packed — the bag Shraddha had asked him to pack three weeks ago, calmly, as if planning a cricket tour, with the specific list she'd sent him on WhatsApp that he'd screenshot and referred to four times to make sure he hadn't missed anything. In the bottom of the bag, in the inner pocket with the zipper, he'd put the drawer.

Not the actual drawer. A selection of it.

He had understood, packing the bag, that there were things he wanted to bring. Not for any practical reason — the hospital was not requiring him to present a tennis ball or an old Anjali Mehta article from 2008. But he had carried the drawer as a concept for twenty years and he had understood, packing on Saturday night while Shraddha watched him from the doorway, that Arjun's first night in the world was the night the drawer needed to be present. She had not asked what he was doing. She had watched him put the items in the inner pocket and said nothing, which was the response that meant she understood.

He laid them out on the windowsill, in the hospital room with its institutional strip lighting turned low and his wife asleep and his son in the crib.

The tennis ball first. Old, the red worn from one hemisphere to lighter pink, the seam raised on one side. Gully cricket in the lanes near Matunga station, 1999, the year he had discovered you could swing a tennis ball with wet hands and the lanes had a particular bounce off the wall that a cricket pitch would never replicate. He had kept it for twenty-four years and never adequately explained why, even to himself.

Rajveer's batting gloves. Old leather, the palm padding flattened from use, the velcro closure on the left glove slightly stiff from age. Sunita had given them from the metal box — wrapped in cotton, the way important things were wrapped. He had put them in the drawer the night he came back from Hisar in 2016 and had not taken them out since except to look at them occasionally, the way you look at things that are too significant to handle casually.

The photograph. Rajveer and Kamla: the cut-edge photograph, the careful trim where someone had been removed — he still did not know who, had never asked, had understood on the third visit to Hisar that this was information Sunita was keeping for her own reasons, which she was entitled to. Rajveer at twenty-something, Kamla beside him, both of them in the compressed format of a photograph from the late 1980s, the colors slightly warm-shifted the way photographs from that decade were. Kamla's eyes were looking directly at the camera. Rajveer's were looking sideways at something outside the frame, which was in character for a man who had, apparently, spent most of his short life looking toward the edge of wherever he was.

The debut match ball. The red Dukes ball from his first Test, 2010 at Mohali, signed by both teams, the signatures faded now to pale blue on the dark leather. He had kept it in the drawer because it was the evidence that the thing had happened. Not the cap, not the scorecard — the ball, because the ball was the instrument, the thing his hands had held, the object that had done the thing.

Three Anjali Mehta articles. Printed, folded, the printer ink slightly smeared on one because the Dadar flat's printer had always leaked on the left margin. The first was from 2008, when he was eighteen — the one she'd written after his debut season with Mumbai, called *The Boy From Nowhere*, which was the article that had introduced that phrase into his life, and which he had received with the complicated feeling of someone being correctly named for the first time and not knowing whether to thank the namer. The second was from 2014, after the injury announcement. The third was from 2018, the Test century in Cape Town.

He arranged them on the windowsill. It looked, in the low hospital light, like the altar of a religion with very specific deities.

He stood and looked at it for a moment. Then he took the photograph of Rajveer and Kamla and walked to the bedside table beside the crib. He set it there, facing the crib, so the faces looked toward the sleeping baby. He stepped back and considered the positioning. He moved it two centimeters left, so the angle was directly toward where Arjun's face would be when he woke.

Arjun's eyes were closed. The crib was between the bed and the window, and in the low light from the corridor his son's face had the quality of things that are being looked at while asleep — the specific openness of the unconscious face, the absence of the usual minor arrangements people make when being observed.

Rajveer and Kamla looked at him from the photograph.

He stood between his son and his parents. This was not a dramatic thought. It was a spatial fact. He stood in the room his son had just entered and in the frame of the photograph two people he had never met and had found late in a village near Hisar, and the three of them were here, in a hospital room on a Tuesday night in March, and for the first time in his life the word *family* described something in all directions — forward and back both.

He had been the boy from nowhere.

He was not that anymore.

His son would be from somewhere from the first day. He would have names for things: this hospital, this city, the pendant, the cricket gloves in the drawer, the village near Hisar where the woman with the arthritis hands would make roti and call herself his great-grandmother. He would have the building in Bandra with the lift that clunked on the third floor. He would have Shivaji Park on Saturday afternoons, and Rao's tea, and the particular quality of October light at the park when the humidity retreated and the practice session carried the specific cleanness of a sport being played for its own sake.

Vipin sat back down in the chair that was not for sleeping.

He looked at his son.

His son slept.

---

At four-fifteen, Arjun woke.

Not dramatically — not crying, not distressed. He simply opened his eyes and returned to the project of considering things. He had done this twice already in the three hours since the birth: eyes open, the focused attention of a person with a great deal of new data and no particular system for sorting it. Vipin was there each time. He was there now.

He reached in and picked him up. He had learned the weight already — the particular heft of him, the way his head required the supporting hand — and his body remembered it from the last time, the adjustment automatic.

He walked slowly to the window. Below, the courtyard: the neem tree, the hospital paths empty at this hour, a security guard at a far gate doing what security guards did at 4 AM, which was to stand in a way that suggested the possibility of action without committing to it. The city beyond the courtyard walls was in its pre-dawn state, the sodium lamps still on, the sky not yet having committed to any particular color, the specific hour that Vipin had been alive in thousands of times from a different angle — the pandit's morning bell, Shivaji Park nets before the light, hotel rooms in Johannesburg and Colombo and Leeds, the quiet that arrives between when the night finishes and when the day begins.

He had never been in this hour with someone he'd brought into it.

He stood at the window with his son and looked at the city that had raised him. Mumbai in the dark. The salt water somewhere beyond the buildings. The trains, which had already started — the first suburban local would be running by 4:30, because the city did not wait for anything, had never waited for anything, had been receiving people from everywhere and absorbing them and making them Mumbai since before anyone's recorded history of it. It had absorbed a seven-year-old off a temple step in Matunga in 1997 and it would absorb this one too. But he would know, when it absorbed him. He would arrive in the city knowing where he was from. That was the difference. That was everything.

Arjun made a sound. Not crying — something preliminary, the exploratory sound of a person testing a system. Vipin adjusted his grip. The pendant hung forward and Arjun's hand — tiny, the fist the size of a table-tennis ball — opened and closed once near it, not reaching for it, just moving.

"That's yours eventually," Vipin said, very quietly, not in a whisper — just quiet, the room being quiet. "When you're ready for the story."

The baby did not respond to this, which was the correct response.

Outside, in the courtyard, a bird started up. He didn't know what kind — not a crow, too early for the crows, something smaller. A single call, repeated at intervals, the way birds in the dark established that they were there, that the night had an end, that something was coming that was worth announcing.

He stood at the window and let the bird establish it.

The sky, very slowly, changed. Not dramatically — just the first intimation of a gradient, the dark becoming fractionally less absolute at the horizon line, the shift that preceded all the other shifts. He had watched this transition from more windows in more cities than he could count. He watched it now.

His son, warm against his chest, went back to sleep.

---

In the morning, two hours later, he would call Rao.

Rao would answer on the second ring, which was unusual — Rao was not a fast phone-answerer — and Vipin would understand, from the speed, that Rao had been awake already, which meant Rao had been waiting, which was the closest Rao had ever come to saying *I was thinking about you.*

He would say, "He's here. Arjun."

Rao would say: "Arjun Chaudhary."

And then there would be a silence that was not empty — a silence that had in it everything that had accumulated over twenty-five years: the storage room at Shivaji Park, the rubber ball, the daily nets sessions, the cold chai that had been intentional, the sessions where Rao stood at the boundary and watched and said nothing because nothing needed saying. Twenty-five years of a man who had found a boy on the street and given him, not kindness exactly, not warmth exactly — something better, more durable: *craft. Discipline. The knowledge that you have something, and here is how to use it.*

"Good," Rao would say.

That would be all. But it would be enough.

For now, it was still the early dark of a Tuesday becoming Wednesday in March, and a city finding its first light, and a man at a window holding his son, with a photograph facing the crib and a pendant warm against his chest and forty-one years of forward ahead of him, all of it unknown, all of it beginning.

*End of Chapter 40*
