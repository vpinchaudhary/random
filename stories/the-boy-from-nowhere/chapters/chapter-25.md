# Chapter 25: Clean Hands

**Timeline**: May–August 2013 (age 23)

---

The trophy was on the coffee table.

Not displayed — just there, the way something lands when you carry it home from a long night and set it down in the first available surface and don't go back for it. The IPL trophy. Mumbai Indians, first title. Three weeks old now, still in the same spot, and Vipin had developed a habit of sitting on the couch with his feet on the edge of the table, not touching it, just next to it, which Ravi had pointed out was the behavior of someone who didn't fully believe the object was real and was testing whether proximity would clarify anything.

Ravi was often right about things Vipin didn't want to hear.

It was a Wednesday morning. May 16. He was eating curd rice — the chef at the team hotel had given him the recipe two seasons ago and he'd been making it badly ever since, adding too much mustard, never getting the consistency right — when his phone started doing the thing it does when multiple people are trying to reach you simultaneously. The sound of a phone in full riot.

He looked at it.

Whatsapp. Group chats layering on top of each other: MI squad, Ranji squad, his India callup group, the general chaos group where Ravi and four others sent each other cricket memes and increasingly unhinged takes on current events. The messages were moving so fast the preview text kept replacing itself before he could read it. He put the curd rice down.

Delhi Police. Sreesanth. Chandila. Spot-fixing. Rajasthan Royals.

He read the news alert first. Then the second alert. Then he put the phone face-down on the table and looked at the wall for perhaps forty seconds.

The number of tiles from the window to the edge of the kitchen counter: eleven and a half. He'd counted before.

He picked the phone back up.

---

The MI management meeting was called the same afternoon. Full squad, support staff, the franchise owner's people. Conference room in the hotel they used for home games — the same room where contracts were explained, where physios gave injury updates, where selections were announced. The AC was set to a temperature that somebody in facilities management believed was ideal for productivity, which meant it was approximately three degrees too cold and everyone sat in training gear looking vaguely annoyed.

Rohit was already there when Vipin arrived. Captain face — the particular version Rohit wore when processing something in real time, which was an expression of almost prenatural calm that people who didn't know him misread as indifference. Vipin had played alongside Rohit for five IPL seasons and understood that the calm was the processing, not the absence of it.

"Bhai, yeh sach hai?" Vipin said, dropping into the chair beside him.

Rohit looked at him. "You've read the same things I've read."

"Yeah, but—" He stopped. There was no but. The arrests were real. The charges were real. The police had been surveilling for weeks, apparently. Three players. A system. Money moving in shapes that cricket fans had always suspected existed and authorities had always officially denied.

The franchise people spoke for thirty-five minutes about the BCCI investigation, the process, cooperation, what cooperation would look like, what not cooperating would look like. Legal language wrapped around a cricketing core. The essential message: everyone would be interviewed. Phone records, financial records, communication logs. Standard procedure. Nothing to worry about if you have nothing to hide.

Vipin looked at Rohit's face during this part. Rohit was writing something in his notebook — not notes, exactly, more like the absent doodling of someone whose hands need to be doing something while their brain does the real work. Small circles, then a straight line, then something that might have been a cricket bat or might have been a sword.

The players who'd been arrested — Vipin knew Sreesanth the way you know people in the circuit. Not friends. Opponents who become familiar through repetition. They'd played against each other, shaken hands at the toss, the way you shake hands with someone at the toss when you're both professionals doing a job. Sreesanth had a temper that made him interesting to watch and occasionally difficult to play against — big personality, the kind of cricketer who took wickets and celebrated loudly and made cricket feel slightly larger than it was.

Vipin had thought he was just that. A man who felt everything loudly.

He'd been wrong, apparently. Or he'd been right and that had nothing to do with this.

The meeting ended. People filed out. Rohit stayed seated, writing more doodles, and Vipin stayed because staying felt like the right thing to do.

"You knew anyone?" Rohit asked eventually. Not accusingly. Genuinely.

"Not like that. You know how it is — you know everyone in the circuit, you don't know anyone."

Rohit nodded. "That's what I thought. But they're going to ask."

"I know."

"Just tell them exactly what you know and don't say anything you're not sure of."

Simple. Correct. Vipin tucked it away.

He drove home in traffic that took fifty-three minutes to cover a distance that should have taken eighteen. He sat in the crawl on the Western Express Highway and looked at the trophy through the gap in the back seat where he'd left it this morning — he'd been meaning to find a proper shelf for it — and thought about Sreesanth's face at the toss before a match last season. Handshake. Good luck. The performed civility of the competitive circle.

What had Sreesanth been thinking about at that moment? Whether the handshake was a cover. Whether Vipin was clean or dirty, another node in a network that apparently touched everything.

He didn't know. That was the thing. You could shake hands with someone for five years and not know.

He'd grown up in Bhai's world. He knew what that kind of not-knowing felt like. Had lived inside it and called it normal.

The traffic moved six car-lengths forward. He let the car roll.

The IPL was supposed to be different. He'd believed that, he realized. Not consciously — not in a way he'd ever have said out loud, which would've sounded naive. But somewhere beneath the skepticism and the street knowledge, he'd believed that organized cricket at this level was clean in the ways that mattered. Governed by rules. Watched over. Too exposed to risk.

He'd been wrong the way you're wrong when you want something to be clean.

There was also the other thing that kept surfacing and that he kept pushing back under: the BCCI investigation was going through financial records. Scrutinizing. Men in suits — he'd been told they wouldn't be in suits, would be in polo shirts, but the mental picture refused to update — going through his accounts looking for irregular deposits, offshore connections, the financial architecture of someone who'd been bought. And his records were clean. Completely clean. The boy who'd spent five years fighting to get documentation, chasing notarized certificates through government offices that seemed designed to lose paperwork, who'd stood in more queues than he could remember just to prove on paper that he existed — that boy's records were impeccably clean. The irony sat in his chest alongside everything else.

He called Shraddha when he got home. She picked up on the second ring, already in makeup from whatever shoot she had that day — he could tell from the particular kind of ambient noise in the background, the set's specific frequency.

"You saw," she said.

"Yeah."

"Are you—"

"I'm fine. It's a lot. The whole thing is—" He stopped. Sat on the couch. "It's a lot."

A pause. The set noise in the background. "Do you need me to come over after?"

The question landed carefully, which was how she asked careful questions — with the lightness that disguised the seriousness. She'd learned that direct offers felt like pressure to him, so she framed them as options.

"I'll be okay. Call me when you're done?"

"I'll call you when I'm done," she said, and he could hear in the phrasing that she'd accepted the answer and would call and they'd figure it out from there.

He sat on the couch after the call and looked at the trophy and thought about how strange it was to have won the thing you'd wanted and to be unable to locate the feeling of it because another feeling had moved in and taken all the available space.

---

The intermediary's name was Deepak. Not a bad man — the category of person who exists in the spaces between clearly good and clearly bad, who runs small businesses, knows everyone in a neighborhood, is useful and used and doesn't ask too many questions about why. They'd known each other since Vipin's early street years, not closely, but the way streets make their own social fabric: you know the threads even when you don't know the whole cloth.

Deepak called on a Tuesday. Said he had a mutual friend who wanted to say hello. Said Vipin should come by the dhabha near his old neighborhood — the one with the blue sign, yes, that one — Thursday evening. Said it casually, the way you say something that is not casual at all when you've had time to think about how to say it.

Vipin knew. He knew the moment he heard the framing.

He should have said no on the phone. He said he'd come.

He drove there himself, no driver. Parked three streets away because the old habit was to never make your car visible in a place where you weren't sure of the exits. The dhabha smelled of dal tadka and cigarette smoke and the specific quality of old cooking oil that accumulates in places that have been frying the same things since before he was born. A ceiling fan moved slowly overhead. Three other customers, all eating with the focused silence of men eating alone.

Bhai was at a corner table.

He'd evolved. Vipin had been half-expecting the same man from the streets — the t-shirt and the territorial confidence and the roll of cash that signified position in that particular hierarchy. But Bhai was in a collared shirt now, actual fabric, not synthetic. Phone on the table with a leather case. The posture of a man who'd moved up through some series of grey-area escalations and had arrived somewhere that looked, from a particular angle, like legitimacy.

Still the same eyes. That had not changed. Eyes that processed everything at a profit.

"Vipin bhai," Bhai said, and the warmth in it was either real or had become indistinguishable from real through years of practice. "You look good. The IPL agreed with you."

Vipin sat.

The dhabha owner brought chai without being asked — Vipin's glass chipped at the rim, Bhai's glass intact. Small things. The dhabha remembered who the regular was.

"Main sun raha hoon," Vipin said.

Bhai smiled. He always smiled when someone was being efficient with him. Appreciated the reciprocal efficiency.

He laid it out. Not clumsily — the years had refined whatever pitch instincts he'd always had. Didn't ask for anything fixed. Didn't mention names. Framed it as information sharing. Vipin knew things — pitch conditions, team selections, injury status, dressing room weather — that Vipin knew naturally by being inside a system that generated this information like weather. Bhai knew people for whom this information was worth more than Vipin probably imagined. The word "arrangements" never appeared. The phrase "nothing wrong" appeared twice.

Vipin held his chai glass. The chip on the rim was small and sharp, a rough quarter-circle where the glaze had broken. He ran his thumbnail along it, back and forth, as Bhai spoke. A small anchoring motion. Six times.

The grey zone. He understood the grey zone. He'd lived in the grey zone from age nine to thirteen, eating because Bhai fed him, sleeping under roofs Bhai controlled. He'd always known that Bhai's grey had edges — places where it turned into something harder. He'd just been young and hungry enough not to look at those edges too carefully.

"No," Vipin said.

Bhai had expected this. His face didn't change. "I'm not asking you to do anything wrong, Vips. The way the world runs—"

"No."

"—everyone in cricket knows this exists. The bookmakers, the players, the administrators. You think the people above you are clean? You think the money that built these stadiums—"

"Bhai." Quiet. The voice he used when the jokes had stopped.

Bhai stopped.

"I heard you. The answer is no."

A silence. The ceiling fan made three revolutions. Then Bhai leaned back slightly, and his expression changed — not anger, something that sat at an angle to hurt. Genuine or performed: still unreadable. That had always been the thing about Bhai. You could never find the floor.

"Chaudhary." The name. The Dadar street inflection. "Do you remember what you ate the summer you were nine? Because I do. I remember because I'm the one who fed you."

Vipin set the chai glass down.

"You were a kid with nothing. No name, no papers, no family. A stray. You know what happened to strays in this city? I don't have to tell you — you know what happened to the ones I didn't take in. You know their names. The ones who made it and the ones who didn't."

This was accurate. This was all accurate.

"I'm not saying this to be cruel. I'm saying it because I need you to remember that the world you're in now — the stadiums, the jersey, the five-star hotels — that world didn't build you. You came from somewhere else. I was part of that somewhere."

Vipin sat with this for a moment. He'd always known this conversation would happen eventually, in some version. He'd just assumed it would arrive differently — more threat, less grief.

He stood.

"I said no, Bhai. Don't ask again."

He left without looking back. The dhabha door was a push, not a pull — he always got this wrong, pushed when he should pull, a small continuous failure he'd never corrected. Got it wrong again. Pushed, felt the resistance, pulled.

Outside, the evening was the specific thick quality of a Mumbai evening in early summer, before the monsoon, when the heat sits on your skin like something with weight. He walked the three streets to the car. Got in. Sat.

His hand was shaking. Not fear — that wasn't it. It was the physical evidence of something being cut. Not cleanly. The way you'd sever something that had been attached for long enough to become structural.

He called Rao.

Three rings. Rao picked up with the background sounds of what was probably the Shivaji Park pavilion — wind, a distant ball on bat.

Vipin told him. Abbreviated but accurate. The intermediary, the meeting, the pitch, the no.

A silence from Rao. Then: "You need to report this to the anti-corruption unit."

"I know."

"Today or tomorrow, not later. Don't let this sit. You report what happened, your name is clean."

"I know."

He was looking at the building opposite. An old residential building, six stories, the kind with the exposed water tanks on the roof and the paint a different shade on different floors where different residents had painted their own sections at different times. He counted the water tanks from left to right. One, two, three, four, five.

"Vipin."

"I'll report it."

He didn't.

---

The BCCI anti-corruption unit interview was in a conference room at a hotel in BKC. The table was long enough to seat twelve but there were only three chairs in use: Vipin, and two officers in polo shirts — not suits, which he'd somehow expected. One officer was older, methodical, the kind of person who asked questions and wrote the answers without inflection. The other was younger, had a ballpoint pen shaped like a cricket bat — miniature, souvenir quality, the kind sold at stadium shops — and clicked it at irregular intervals.

Vipin counted the clicks.

The recording device on the table had a red light. Steady, not pulsing.

The questions were standard and thorough: contact with bookmakers, approach by anyone known or unknown, unusual financial offers, conversations about team selection or pitch conditions. The format was designed to make lying difficult — they circled back to the same territory from different angles, the way you'd test a material for weaknesses.

There was also a section about his personal background. Not adversarial — framed as routine. But the older officer had clearly done reading before this. He asked about Vipin's childhood, his early years in Mumbai, whether Vipin had known anyone in the informal economy of the city. The phrase "informal economy" — Vipin had to work not to react to this, the bureaucratic softening of a world he'd actually lived in, the way institutional language put a glass case around things that had been real and immediate and capable of hurting you.

Vipin answered. The pandit. The streets. Rao and Shivaji Park. He answered the edges of the questions without answering the center. He was aware that this was the shape of a deliberate evasion and that the officers might be too. The older one wrote. The younger one clicked his pen and his face was entirely neutral.

He answered everything honestly.

Except the one thing.

No, nobody had approached him. No, he had no information about approach attempts by anyone in the circuit. No, he was not aware of any irregular contact.

The lie sat in his mouth like something he'd swallowed wrong and would feel for hours after — not painful exactly, but present, something displaced.

The younger officer clicked the pen. Thirty-seven times, total, over the course of the interview. Vipin counted without deciding to count — the habit engaged itself, something to do with one part of his brain while the rest performed.

The officers looked at their notes. Cross-referenced something. Asked him one follow-up about a player from a different franchise he'd played golf with twice — he answered truthfully, it was just golf — and then the older officer said: "Thank you, Mr. Chaudhary. You're clear to go."

Clear to go.

He thanked them. Collected his phone from the tray where he'd placed it at the door. Walked through the hotel lobby, past the arrangement of fresh flowers that hotels in BKC kept in their lobbies as a performance of quality, past the doorman who pulled the door open with practiced efficiency.

Outside.

He should have felt relieved. Clean. Cleared.

The stone in his chest stayed. It had arrived somewhere between the dhabha and this hotel lobby and it had no intention of going anywhere just because an officer in a polo shirt had said *clear to go*. The stone didn't know what the officer knew. The stone was tracking a different ledger.

He stood in the hotel's portico for a moment and touched the pendant through his shirt.

---

Shivaji Park after training. Three weeks later.

August, the sky doing the pre-monsoon version of overcast: not quite threatening rain, just withholding light. The park smelled of cut grass and damp earth and the specific sweat-and-leather smell of cricket bags left open. Most of the squad had gone. Just the two of them — Rao collecting the training equipment with the systematic thoroughness of a man who'd been collecting training equipment for thirty years and had opinions about how it was done.

Vipin was sitting on the boundary with a rubber ball — not a cricket ball, the old pink rubber kind, superball texture — rolling it between his palm and the ground. He'd had it in his pocket for years. Not for any reason. The rubber was smooth where it should be rough, worn past the pebbled surface into something between wax and skin.

He waited.

Rao finished with the bags. Walked over. Did not sit. Stood at the boundary beside him, looking at the pitch, which was being rolled by one of the groundstaff in lazy passes.

"You didn't report it."

Not a question. Thirty years of coaching. The same way he'd always known things Vipin hadn't said — the injury Vipin was playing through, the night before a match he'd spent outside in the Shivaji Park pavilion because the team hotel felt suffocating, the time at fifteen when he'd stolen a bike not maliciously but because the hunger that week had been worse than usual. Rao had known that without being told, and had not said anything directly, and had instead made sure Vipin's meal allowance increased that month without explanation.

He knew the same way he'd always known.

"No," Vipin said.

The syllable dropped into the afternoon like a stone into still water. It sat there.

Rao looked at the pitch. His jaw moved slightly — the thing it did when he was thinking through something he didn't want to say imprecisely.

Then he turned and walked away.

Vipin watched him go. He crossed the outfield at the diagonal — the angle that took him toward the pavilion, past the practice nets, toward the gate. He disappeared behind the netting.

The groundstaff kept rolling the pitch.

Vipin rolled the rubber ball. Seventeen times, palm to ground, slow turns.

Rao came back twelve minutes later. Vipin had not moved. When you are the kind of child who has learned not to leave a space you've been given because spaces can close — this was a thing he knew about himself, had known for years, the staying-put as survival instinct — you don't move until you're told to.

Rao stood again at the boundary.

"The man you're protecting would destroy you if he could no longer use you." Not loud. Not a sermon. Just fact, delivered with the economy of someone who had spent decades removing unnecessary words. "If the anti-corruption unit finds out you lied, your career is over. Not suspended. Not investigated. Over. A fix on your record. Everything you built becomes evidence of what it was built despite."

Vipin didn't answer.

"I know what he was to you," Rao said. "I've always known. I'm not asking you to pretend he wasn't. I'm asking you to understand the difference between gratitude and liability."

"And if I go back? If I tell them now, that I lied?"

"You accept whatever comes. You walk in, you tell the truth, you accept whatever comes. That's the only version of this that leaves you standing."

"My name in the papers. His name in the papers. IPL star's criminal background. The whole thing—"

"Yes. Probably." Rao said it without flinching. Rao had never been the kind of coach who softened truths because they were hard to hear. "That might happen. And your career survives it because you told the truth. The other version — the version where they find out some other way, and they will find out — that version your career does not survive."

Vipin looked at the pitch.

The groundstaff had finished rolling and left. The pitch was empty now — the specific emptiness of a cricket pitch at the end of a training day, all the action gone out of it, just the marks where feet had been.

"What do I do?" he said.

He'd asked this question of Rao before. Different moments, different problems. The answer was always the same structural shape: go toward the thing, not away from it.

"You go back. You tell them the truth. You accept whatever comes."

Rao left before the conversation could become something else. He did that — understood that there were conversations that needed to end before they became negotiations, where the other person starts looking for alternatives to the only correct answer. He didn't give Vipin the time to do that.

The park was quiet after he left.

Vipin sat. The rubber ball moved in his palm — slow, three-fingered rotation, the unconscious kind. He was aware, distantly, that he'd done this all his life. The counting, the rolling, the small physical anchors. A therapist — the team psychologist had suggested he see one, twice, he hadn't — would probably call it something. He called it staying in the body when the mind was trying to leave.

He thought about the drawer in his flat. The photograph that had been there since the beginning — the one the researcher had found, his name written in a hand he'd never traced. The match ball from his Ranji debut, signed by Rao without ceremony. The boarding pass from the first India tour to Sri Lanka, which he'd saved without knowing why and had stopped questioning. These things meant something, had weight, were the receipts of a life that had been built.

He thought about the officers. The polo shirts. The thirty-seven clicks of a bat-shaped pen. *Clear to go.*

He thought about the stone in his chest, which was still there, which had not moved.

---

The package arrived Thursday morning.

No return address. His building's watchman brought it up — a small parcel, padded envelope, his name and address handwritten. He knew before he opened it. The handwriting was the kind that doesn't need to announce itself.

Inside: a tennis ball. The old rubber-coated kind, the gully cricket standard, slightly deflated, taped at the seam with the pale yellow electrical tape that was the unofficial currency of street cricket — the kind you bought from the hardware shop when the ball started to lose its feel and needed weight redistribution. He'd taped hundreds of balls this way, at nine, at ten, at eleven, in the lanes near the park where Bhai's boys played and losing was never just losing.

No note.

Just the ball.

He held it in his palm. The tape had the texture of something that had been outside in Mumbai weather for a season or more — faintly gritty, the adhesive edge lifting at one corner. Someone had found this or kept this. Had held onto it long enough to send it.

*You were a kid who ate because I fed you.*

Accurate. Undeniable. Carrying all the weight Bhai had intended it to carry and a weight Bhai hadn't intended too, which was the weight of everything Vipin had become after the feeding stopped. As if the feeding was the whole transaction and everything after was balance owed.

He carried the ball to the drawer. Opened it: the photograph, the match ball, the boarding pass, the folded newspaper from his Wankhede debut with the photo of him and Rohit with the trophy. He held the tennis ball over the drawer for a moment, this stupid deflated piece of rubber that cost forty rupees on the street, this artifact of a particular kind of debt.

He put it in the drawer.

Closed it.

The drawer: a museum of debts. He'd never thought of it that way before and now couldn't think of it any other way.

He sat on the couch. The IPL trophy was still on the coffee table. He'd never found the shelf.

Outside his window: a crow on the building opposite's water tank, doing the thing crows do in Mumbai heat, standing very still with its beak open slightly, waiting for the temperature to decide something. Three floors down, someone was frying something with cumin — he could smell it from here, this specific combination of cumin in hot oil that meant someone's mother or grandmother was making dal the long way, not the quick way, the way that took forty minutes because you tempered the spices separately and added them last.

The pendant was cold against his skin, which meant his hand had found it again without him noticing.

He thought about tomorrow. About the BKC hotel. About the man in the polo shirt and the bat-shaped pen.

The stone in his chest.

He'd read once — somewhere, a magazine in a team hotel waiting room, the kind of article that gets read and half-remembered — that the physical sensation of guilt and the physical sensation of indigestion were processed by the same nerve pathways. That the gut and the conscience shared infrastructure. He'd thought this was interesting and had not thought about it again for three years.

He thought about it now.

He wasn't going tomorrow. He wasn't ready to go tomorrow.

He might be ready by the end of the week. He might not be. He'd told Rao he'd go and he would go — that was not the question, the question was when he could carry the weight of Bhai's name in his mouth in a room with a recording device and a red light without something in him breaking that he couldn't put back. The gratitude and the liability. The debt and the drawer.

The crow on the water tank hadn't moved.

Vipin picked up the rubber ball — the old pink one from his pocket, not the tennis ball from the drawer — and turned it in his hand. The smooth place where the pebble texture had worn away. He'd been doing this for years and the ball hadn't noticed, didn't know it had been touched a thousand thousand times, didn't know it was part of something. Objects didn't know what they meant.

He'd been placed on temple stairs with a name and a gold pendant and some money by hands he'd never seen. The pandit had called it care. He'd called it a debt he'd never be able to repay because he didn't know who to repay it to. The pendant was cold and heavy against his sternum, familiar as his own heartbeat.

He counted the tiles from the window to the kitchen counter again. Eleven and a half. They hadn't changed.

He ate. Bad curd rice. Too much mustard.

He waited for morning.

---

*The drawer now held: one photograph (his name written in an unknown hand), one match ball (Ranji debut, signed without ceremony), one boarding pass (first India tour, Sri Lanka, seat 14C), one newspaper (folded, the photo of him and Rohit, Wankhede), and one tennis ball, taped at the seam with yellow electrical tape, deflated, weightless, heavier than everything else.*
