# Chapter 12: First Blood

The number was 47.

He lay on the hotel bed looking at the jersey hung on the wardrobe door and did the arithmetic he'd been doing since the number was assigned: four plus seven, eleven. Eleven players in a cricket team. He had decided this was significant at the time and he was now, three hours into the night before his debut, revising this opinion. It wasn't significant. A shirt number was a shirt number. The MCA had given him 31 in age-group cricket because it was the next available number. Nobody had made arithmetic out of 31.

He did it again. 4+7=11. He lay on the bed with his hands folded on his chest and looked at the jersey and tried to stop doing cricket arithmetic with his shirt number.

He tried counting the ceiling tiles instead. Forty-eight. He had counted them the first night and the number hadn't changed, which was both consistent and expected, but counting them again gave his hands something to do even though his hands weren't the part of him that needed occupying.

The jersey: Mumbai Indians blue, gold trim, CHAUDHARY in white across the back. He had held it when they gave it to him, read his own name across the fabric. He had seen his name on things before — on a registration form, on a newspaper clipping, on a birth certificate where it appeared twice in the same document in different contexts. This was different. This was his name on something that went on his body and was visible from fifty meters away. This was his name announced.

He got up. Walked to the window. The city below in its nighttime configuration. He found Shivaji Park in the dark — the green patch was invisible at night, just a shape among shapes — and registered its location, south-east, twelve kilometers, the specific coordinates of the place that had made him. Then he found the direction of DY Patil Stadium, east-northeast, across the creek. Twenty-four kilometers. Tomorrow.

He had called Rao at nine. "I'm in the playing XI. Tomorrow."

A pause on the line — not the thinking-pause, the receiving-pause. "Which ground?"

"DY Patil."

"The toss will favor the team batting first. The pitch plays true in the first ten overs." Not: congratulations. Not: I'm proud. The cricket information was the congratulations. Vipin understood this.

"I know."

"Leather ball still comes out of the bowler's hand," Rao said. "Same as it always has."

He hung up feeling the steadiest he'd felt in three weeks.

He went back to the bed. Lay on top of the covers — still on top, the habit not yet broken. The jersey on the door. The rubber ball in his hand.

He did not sleep much, but when he slept it was clean — not the grinding pre-auction insomnia, not the post-auction stillness. This was the sleep of someone whose body was conserving itself for something it knew was coming.

At 5:15 AM his eyes opened. He was ready.

---

The warmup was at nine.

DY Patil Stadium in Navi Mumbai was newer than Wankhede, the pitch flatter, the boundary shorter in a way that rewarded big hitting and punished length bowling if you weren't precise. Vipin had read the ground's statistics and knew its history: two international matches, fifteen IPL fixtures, the precise measurements in meters from the pitch to each boundary rope. He knew this because in the two weeks since selection he had been reading ground profiles the way he used to read cricket footage — analytically, building a physical understanding from data.

The crowd was already filling by the time warmups started. Not full — matches didn't fill until the first ball — but full enough. Thirty thousand, approximately. He estimated from the density of color in the stands. Then corrected: the capacity was thirty-five. They were at about twenty thousand. Which meant twenty thousand people could see him fielding in the warmup.

He fielded in the warmup. He caught three catches — routine, high ones from the fielding coach's launcher, the kind you caught with eyes open and nothing to think about. He caught them. Clean. Between the second and third he counted the advertising boards along his boundary section: nine total, four empty, three mobile phones, one insurance company, one paint brand. He noted the paint brand was peeling at the corner. The crowd made sounds when the ball went to a player they knew — Sachin drawing a sustained reaction when he caught one at deep midwicket — and made nothing in particular when Vipin caught his. He noted this. Filed it under: baseline. Acceptable starting position.

MI won the toss. They batted.

Vipin, batting at seven, padded up and watched from the dugout.

The dugout was better and worse than he'd imagined. Better because you were on the ground, inside the match, close enough to feel the vibration of the crowd when a boundary was hit. Worse because you were also not playing. You watched the match happen and your hands had nothing to do. He had the rubber ball — tucked in his trouser pocket under the pads, pressing slightly against his thigh. He squeezed it between overs when nobody was looking.

The MI innings built. The top order fired. Sachin made 62 — the Wankhede crowd (or DY Patil crowd; either crowd treated a Sachin innings the same way, a kind of collective held-breath punctuated by roars at boundaries) treating each shot as an event. The run rate climbed. The target accumulated. MI posted 183/5.

Vipin did not bat. The match had not required him to bat.

He had known this was possible. He had prepared for this — had told himself, on the night before, that the debut might be partial, might be one half of his skills and not the other. Cricket was not a guaranteed canvas. You showed what the match gave you room to show.

He went out to field.

The ground under his spikes was different from any ground he'd stood on. The DY Patil outfield was manicured in a way that cricket grounds in the Shivaji Park ecosystem were not — soft, even, the grass a specific shade of prepared. Floodlights made the colours slightly different at this hour — the blue of the MI jerseys was more intense, the red of the ball more vivid. The crowd noise was a physical presence. Not just sound. Pressure.

He walked to third man. His position for the first three overs. He stood at the boundary rope and looked at the twenty-something thousand faces and did not feel afraid. He felt: awake. Hyperalert. Every detail of his peripheral vision was sharp and reporting. He was operating on the frequency that only cricket activated, the one where time worked differently.

The third ball of the first over came toward him. A mis-hit off a failed swing, rolling softly to the boundary rope, a certain four unless a fielder got there. Vipin was moving before the ball completed its trajectory — anticipating the line, covering ground. He reached it two feet from the rope, scooped it clean, turned, threw. Flat. Hard. Direct to the keeper's gloves over the stumps. The keeper took it standing up.

The batsmen had run three. Saved one. The throw was perfect — not spectacular, nothing worth a highlight, but perfect in the technical sense: the right hands, the angle tight, the pace exact. The keeper gave him a nod. He walked back to his position.

His first touch in professional cricket: routine, unnoticed, exactly right.

---

The 12th over.

The opposition was 94/2, the required rate edging toward uncomfortable. The captain — the MI captain, senior, a Test batsman turned franchise leader — walked to Vipin with the ball.

"Two overs. Bowl your lengths."

Vipin took the ball. Felt the seam. The same seam that had been the organizing principle of his physical existence since he was nine years old — the ridge where the leather was stitched, the axis around which everything turned. This was a Dukes ball, not an SG, the seam felt marginally different under his fingers but his fingers adjusted without instruction.

He walked to the top of his mark.

This was the longest run-up he'd ever used in a match. Twenty-two meters to the crease, four more than his Shivaji Park run. His body had recalibrated in the nets over three weeks — the feet knew the distance now, the acceleration was set to arrive at maximum at exactly the delivery stride. But standing at the top of the mark in an IPL match, with the opposition batsman settling at the crease and the field arranged by someone else's captain and twenty thousand people watching, he stood for one second that felt longer.

He ran.

The first ball: 138 kph, good length, outside off. The batsman — a Kenyan-born Zimbabwean international who had played eighty ODIs and was not intimidated by young fast bowlers from unknown backgrounds — played and missed. The ball swung away late, an inch outside off, and the keeper took it to the right of his body.

Dot ball. Good ball. Crowd noise, quickly subsiding.

He walked back to his mark. Ran in again. 140 kph, same line, same length. The batsman pushed at it — a firm push, committing to the line — and got an outside edge. The ball flew. Fast. Regulation catch for the slip, about chest height, a ball that arrives with time to move.

The slip dropped it.

A senior fielder, international experience, a man who had caught a hundred such edges in his career, shelled it. The ball went to the ground. The batsman ran a single. The dropped catch was audible in the silence that followed — not the drop itself, but the crowd's intake of breath, the thing that should have happened not happening.

Vipin did not look at the slip. He walked back to his mark. He looked at nothing in particular — the ground, his run-up area. He organized his face into neutral and kept it there. Rao's voice: *control what you control.* The dropped catch was a fact. The next ball was a decision.

Third ball. The batsman had survived and was emboldened by the dropped chance. His front foot moved, a shuffle forward — Vipin tracked it in the early-ball reading that his processing speed made possible. The shuffle committed him: he was going to drive. He was set for a drive.

Vipin did something he hadn't been told to do. He bowled the bouncer.

Not because it was in the plan — the plan was good length, outside off. Because he saw the weight going forward and his hands knew before the brain confirmed what the counter was. 143 kph. The fastest ball of the match. The ball arrived at shoulder height before the batsman's weight had shifted back, before his arms had any purchase on the pull shot. The bat came through hurried — a reflex more than a shot, the hands windmilling at a ball that was already past its ideal contact point.

The ball hit the top edge of the bat. A thick deflection, not a clean nick — the ball traveled upward at a steep angle, less than a meter and a half off the ground, the pace killing most of the trajectory. Going forward. Toward mid-on.

Vipin was already going for it.

He was three strides into his follow-through when he started the dive. The distance was four meters — too far for a comfortable reach, possible for someone willing to be wrong about the dive and hit the ground regardless. His hands went out ahead of him. The ball was still traveling. He read the landing point and adjusted the left hand slightly inward, a correction that happened in the last quarter-second, and his fingers closed around the ball two centimeters from the ground as his chest hit the turf.

He held it. He knew he'd held it. He looked at the ball in his hands, verified, got up.

The umpire's finger went up.

The crowd erupted. Not the polite appreciation-crowd sound, not the four-boundary sound — the roar that came from a dismissal nobody expected, from a young bowler who had created something from nothing, from a catch that had no right to be held. Twenty thousand people, all of them making the same noise simultaneously, and Vipin was standing on the DY Patil outfield holding the ball and the sound hit him in the chest like a physical thing.

Harbhajan reached him first — the bear hug arrived at speed, off the ground, both feet. Vipin had not been picked up by another human being since he was four years old, probably, and the sensation was alarming and then was not alarming because Harbhajan was laughing so loudly the sound cut through the crowd roar. The rest of the team arrived in the pile. Gloves, helmets, slaps on the back that rocked him forward. Sachin was at the edge of the group — he didn't pile in, that wasn't Sachin — but his hand came forward and gripped Vipin's forearm once.

Vipin grinned. The armor was completely down for five seconds. He grinned like a seventeen-year-old — eighteen, actually, his birthday had been two weeks ago and nobody had noticed and he hadn't told anyone — who had just taken his first wicket in professional cricket and was held off the ground by Harbhajan Singh. Somewhere under the grin, a part of him that had been running calculations since he was seven noted the fact of the happiness and assigned it an expiration date. He didn't argue. He just didn't look at it right now.

He had earned this five seconds.

He finished the over. Yorker next ball — dot. Final ball: full toss, the adrenaline wobble he'd told himself he wouldn't have, the ball arriving hip-high, the batsman hitting it for four. First IPL boundary conceded. He filed this under: correctable. Walked off at 1-12 from the over.

Second over, four overs later. 0-8. Dot, single, dot, dot, two, dot. He beat the bat three times. No wicket. Correct lines, correct lengths, the rhythm that comes after the adrenaline has been metabolised. Final figures: 2-0-20-1. The stat line that showed he'd been expensive in moments but disciplined overall. The bowling coach's clipboard had several lines on it.

MI won by 31 runs. The match was not close.

---

In the dressing room after, he sat in the corner with the match ball.

The room smelled of sweat and the antiseptic soap from the physio's kit — he could identify it without looking, the same flat chemical-pink variety stocked in every institutional space he'd spent time in since the age of six. He didn't think about that. He sat in the corner.

The keeper had saved it — pulled it from his bag in the celebrations and pressed it into Vipin's hands. A ball from his first wicket. It was scuffed and grass-stained and one side had started to lose its sheen. He turned it in his hands and felt the seam and looked at the stitching.

The captain's post-match words: "Good team effort. Young Chaudhary, solid first outing." That was it. Twelve words. He had heard more feedback from Rao after a bad training session. But it was in the dressing room, from the captain, in front of Sachin Tendulkar and Harbhajan Singh and Lasith Malinga. Twelve words in that room were a different currency.

Sachin walked past him on the way to the showers. Didn't stop. Put his hand on Vipin's shoulder, a brief pressure, and kept going.

He ate the team dinner. Laughed at the right moments. When the subject of the dropped catch came up — the senior fielder wanting to apologize, visibly uncomfortable — Vipin saw the man's mouth open and moved before the words could start.

"Slip saab, don't worry." He picked up his water glass, kept his voice easy. "Your hands are older than my entire career. You've held twice as many as you've dropped. One for me." He said it with the timing of something thought about, though he hadn't thought about it — it arrived complete, the way his best deliveries did. The fielder laughed, the relief visible in the way his shoulders changed. The table moved on. The moment defused.

He stored this. The humor working two jobs simultaneously — his own armor, team currency, the thing that bought him inclusion at a price he could afford.

He walked back to his room through the hotel corridor, and a television in a hospitality suite had the highlight package on. He stopped at the door. Watched the feed through the glass.

The bouncer, first. The batsman's weight going forward, the ball arriving early, the hurried bat. The top edge. The trajectory. Then him — running, diving, hands going out, the catch. His own back, his own arm angle, his own body from the outside, doing the thing that from the inside had been pure instinct.

On television, it looked deliberate. Calculated. Like he'd planned it.

He watched it again. The replay looped. On television, the Vipin Chaudhary who caught that ball was someone who had done this before, who did this kind of thing, who lived in a world where caught-and-bowled off your own bouncer was a reasonable expected outcome. The person watching through the glass had caught it because he'd read a shuffle and his hands had known before his brain confirmed it and he'd hit the ground regardless of whether the dive was right.

Both were true, somehow.

He went to his room. Put the match ball in his kit bag. Lay on the bed.

Under the covers.

He pulled the duvet over himself. The mattress did its thing. He closed his eyes.

Tomorrow the cricket internet would discover his name. He didn't know this yet. He was asleep before eleven for the first time since moving into the hotel.
