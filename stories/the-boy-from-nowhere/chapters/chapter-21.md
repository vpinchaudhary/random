# Chapter 21: Crime Master Gogo's Daughter

**Timeline**: April–May 2011 (age 20-21)

---

The photograph appeared on a Tuesday.

Shraddha saw it at 8:14 AM, from her phone, while she was still in bed in the Juhu house, the curtains doing their daily battle with the morning light and losing. Her assistant Pooja had sent a WhatsApp message at 7:52 saying *you should probably see this* with three links and a crying-laughing emoji, which was either reassuring or the opposite.

The photograph was blurry. Shot from two rows behind, low-light, a phone camera not built for distance. But it was clear enough. She knew her own jersey. She knew the back of his head. She knew the specific angle of his jaw when he turned slightly to the left, which was what he did when he was concentrating on something and not performing concentration. She also knew her own hand, placed over his, on an armrest at Wankhede Stadium during the World Cup final on April 2nd, the night India won.

She sat with the phone for a moment.

The blog post was titled: *Did anyone else see Vipin Chaudhary at the final?* Four hundred and twelve comments. Eleven news outlets had run the photo. The captions ranged from professional (*Chaudhary and actress Shraddha Kapoor, reportedly attending together*) to something less professional (*B'wood beauty spotted with cricketer beau at WC Final — wedding bells?*).

The word *beau* was doing a lot of heavy lifting in a sentence that contained only nineteen words.

She got up. Washed her face. Made tea (badly — the cook arrived at nine, and Shraddha's relationship with a tea-stove before nine AM was, at best, adversarial). Sat down with the tea and the phone and thought about what this meant and what she was going to do about it.

By 8:25 her phone had forty-three new messages. She didn't open them. She looked out the window instead — the garden, the mango tree, the bougainvillea wall. Something her mother said once: *when you don't know what to do, look at the same thing for sixty seconds and stop making it a problem that has to be solved immediately.* She'd been seventeen when she'd heard this. She'd rejected it then and retrieved it now.

The thing about the photograph was this: it was not wrong. A blurry phone shot could not be wrong. It had captured something that was true. Two people at a public event, close enough to touch. Both of them crying at the same moment. The hand was visible and the hand had been deliberate and she was not sorry about the hand, and she was not going to perform sorrow about it for anyone, including the eleven news outlets currently deploying the phrase *reportedly attending together*.

What she was doing, she recognized by 8:38, was being afraid. Not of the photo. Of the specific next ten minutes — the conversation with Vipin, the question of what they both thought the photo meant, the thing she'd been carefully not naming since Wankhede while also thinking about it fairly constantly.

What she was going to do about it, she decided by eight forty-five, was: call him, tell him, assess what he wanted to do, and tell Pooja to send a *no comment* if anyone called. She'd been through media cycles before. This was a minor one. A blurry photograph was not evidence of anything except that two people had attended a public event.

She called Vipin.

He picked up on the second ring.

"Good morning," she said.

"Morning." His voice had the texture of a man who had been awake for some time. Cricket players woke early. "You saw the photo."

She paused. "You know."

"My manager called at six. He sent me seven links. He wanted to know if we were dating."

"What did you tell him?"

"I told him we were friends and that I'd attended a public event."

"Good."

"He said that wasn't going to be enough."

"Managers never think enough is enough. It's a professional hazard." She sipped her tea. "How are you?"

A brief silence. "Fine. It's a blurry photo."

"It is a blurry photo."

"Nobody can see anything."

She thought about whether to say this. Decided yes. "Vipin. They can see my hand."

Another silence. This one had a different quality — the kind of silence he produced when he was processing something that required genuine processing, not the one-second pause before a deflection.

"I know," he said.

"I'm not—" She stopped. Started again. "I need to tell you something. My father saw it."

---

Shakti Kapoor had seen the photograph at six-thirty in the morning. His assistant had shown him on a tablet. He'd looked at it for thirty-seven seconds by the kitchen clock — Shraddha's mother Shivangi had been watching from the doorway — and then he'd put the tablet down and gone back to his chai and said nothing.

Shivangi reported this to Shraddha at nine-fifteen, via a separate call, in a tone that communicated approximately fourteen different things at once.

"He saw it, he said nothing, and then he asked me to call the Mehta family about their summer party," Shivangi said. "That's either very good or very bad."

"It's very him," Shraddha said.

"Shraddu. Your father can go from charmed to catastrophic in thirty seconds. I know you know this."

She knew this. She had known this since she was seven years old and had brought home a painting she'd made of a film scene and her father had first praised it loudly for ten minutes and then asked why she hadn't made it bigger.

"I'll come over," she said.

"Bring the cricketer," her mother said, which was either extremely helpful or the opposite, and the call ended before Shraddha could determine which.

She called Vipin back.

"I'm going to tell you something," she said, "and I'm telling you as a courtesy, not asking permission."

A brief pause. "Okay."

"I think you should come to my parents' house tonight. For dinner. My father has seen the photograph. If we don't address it directly, he will spend the next three weeks constructing his own narrative, which will be worse than whatever he imagines. I know him. The unknown is more dangerous than the known."

A longer pause.

"When you say address it directly—"

"I mean we eat dinner at my parents' house and let my father form an opinion of you. That's it. That's the whole plan."

"And if he forms a negative opinion."

"He will. Initially. He always does. And then he revises it." She paused. "Or not. But that's his choice, not mine."

She heard him breathe — not a sigh, just the specific breath of a man recalibrating.

"What should I wear," he said.

She almost laughed. "Not the jersey."

---

The Kapoor house in Juhu occupied a lot that had been purchased in 1997, when Shakti Kapoor was still making the kind of films that required a house to be purchased. The bungalow had grown in that way that successful Mumbai houses grow — outward when possible, upward when not — until it had become the kind of property that made sense only if you understood it had been added to rather than planned. There were eleven framed photographs in the entrance hallway alone. Shraddha had counted them once; Vipin counted them when he arrived.

The auto from Dadar had taken him through Bandra and out along the coastal road, and Vipin had spent the journey doing what he always did in the approach to something unknown — cataloguing the information available. Shraddha had told him her father had opinions and revised them. Her mother had told her to bring him, which was either an invitation or a provocation. He'd worn a clean shirt that Ravi had once called "the serious shirt" because it was the only one without a hole.

He counted other things when he arrived.

Eight pairs of shoes near the entrance. Three of them Shakti's, identifiable by the width of the foot. Four lamps in the living room, only two on. The Ganeshotsav calendar on the wall still showing March — nobody had changed it. A photograph of Shakti with an actor whose name Vipin knew but whose work he'd never seen. Another with someone he didn't recognize. A third — tucked slightly behind the others, smaller, the quality of a different decade — that appeared to be Shakti as a much younger man, standing outside a shop, wearing a shirt that was not the expensive kind. He filed that last one and moved on.

Shraddha's mother, Shivangi, met him in the living room. She was small, quick-moving, with a warmth that announced itself before she spoke. She shook his hand and told him she'd seen his innings in the Deodhar Trophy and had the specific credibility of someone who had actually watched it.

"The pull shot," she said. "In the second innings. Sit down, please."

"The ball was slightly short," he said, sitting. "I got lucky with the placement."

She gave him the look of a woman who had heard *I got lucky* from someone who clearly hadn't, and had opinions about it that she was choosing not to deploy. Offered chai instead. He accepted. It was very good chai — better than almost anything he'd had since Rao's Sunday mornings, and he had consumed enough chai at this point in his life to have strong opinions. He noted that Shraddha had previously described her mother's chai as outstanding while describing her own as inadvisable, and filed that this was accurate on both counts.

"You've got good hands. Shakti!" She raised her voice toward the deeper part of the house. "He's here."

A pause. From somewhere deeper in the house, the sound of a door.

Shakti Kapoor entered the living room the way he entered all rooms — which was to say loudly, and before you were fully ready. He was larger than he appeared in photographs, with a physical presence that seemed to expand slightly to fill whatever space he was in. He wore a kurta that probably cost more than Vipin's first month's Ranji salary. He did not appear to notice this and it probably had never occurred to him that it was worth noticing.

He looked at Vipin.

"So," he said. "You're the cricketer."

Vipin stood. Extended his hand. "Sir. I'm Vipin Chaudhary."

Shakti looked at the extended hand. At Vipin's face. At the hand again. Shook it.

"Sit," he said, and sat himself in the larger chair.

---

There was a moment during the introductions when Shivangi offered him chai and Shakti said *he can eat, Shivangi* and that was actually an invitation to sit at the table, which Vipin recognized as a specific kind of test — whether he would wait to be told more precisely or whether he would read the room. He sat.

The dinner was long.

Shakti dominated the first forty minutes — film stories, industry anecdotes, a long account of a production meeting in the early 2000s that went wrong in a way that was apparently hilarious if you'd been present and somewhat less hilarious if you hadn't. Vipin listened. He was good at listening. He was also good at cataloguing: Shakti used the word "beta" eleven times in the first forty minutes, deployed as both affection and gentle assertion of hierarchy. He shifted to "young man" when the story required him to be the voice of authority. He used Shraddha's name as punctuation, a check-in — *na, Shraddu?* — to confirm the room was still with him.

What he didn't do was ask Vipin anything directly.

Shraddha watched her father the way Vipin watched batsmen — alert, patient, tracking the signals that meant something was about to happen.

The signal came over the dal: Shakti picked up his glass, set it down, looked at Vipin with a directness that had been notably absent from the previous forty minutes, and said:

"The photograph."

Vipin waited.

"My daughter," Shakti said, "is a public figure. Her face is worth money to certain people. When she is seen with someone — when there is a photograph of her, in a public place, with a specific someone — that has consequences. You understand this."

"Yes," Vipin said.

"Do you." Not a question. An assessment.

"I understand the mechanics of it. I've been on the other side of it — smaller version."

"Smaller version." Shakti repeated this. "Chaudhary, there is no smaller version of this. There is Shraddha Kapoor's name attached to something, and there is the absence of Shraddha Kapoor's name. Those are the only two categories." He picked up his glass again. "I'm not saying you're a bad person. I don't know you. I know what I can find out, which is not the same as knowing."

"That's fair," Vipin said.

"It's not fair. Nothing about this is fair. But we are in it now."

Shraddha, very quietly, said: "Baba."

Her father looked at her. A long look.

"You know what I'm telling you as a courtesy, not asking permission," he said.

She didn't react. Only Vipin, who'd heard that phrase once before in a different context, caught the echo.

"I know," she said. "And I'm telling you that I invited him to the match. I asked him to come here tonight. That's my choice. He's not something that happened to me."

Shakti looked at his daughter.

He looked at Vipin.

He ate a spoonful of dal.

"Where are you from?" he said.

"Mumbai," Vipin said.

"Originally."

"I don't know originally. I was left at a temple in Matunga when I was a few weeks old. I don't have parents. My coach's name is on my emergency contact form."

The table went quiet in the way tables go quiet when someone has said something that reorients the room's understanding of what kind of conversation this is.

Shakti put his spoon down. He looked at Vipin with a different kind of attention than he'd been deploying — the theatrical assessment was gone, replaced by something that processed rather than performed.

"That's not in the papers," Shakti said.

"Some of it is. I don't read them."

A silence. Shivangi had gone quite still. Shraddha was looking at her food.

"And the cricket," Shakti said, carefully. "That was — you found this yourself?"

"A coach found me. Prakash Rao. He was at Shivaji Park when I started playing there." Vipin paused. "He's the one who noticed. I just hit the balls."

"Rao." Shakti seemed to be computing something. "Dadar? Used to coach the Mumbai Under-15 side?"

"Yes."

Shakti nodded once. Something in this calculation had resolved.

"The pull shot," he said. "In the Deodhar Trophy second innings."

"Shivangi said the same thing," Vipin said. "I got lucky with the placement."

"You didn't get lucky. You played it early." Shakti picked up his dal again. "I'm not entirely ignorant about cricket, despite what Shraddha may have told you."

"She said nothing about your cricket knowledge," Vipin said. "She said you have opinions about jersey sizes."

Shraddha made a strangled sound.

Shakti looked at him. A long beat.

The corner of his mouth moved.

---

After dinner, Shraddha showed Vipin the garden. It was Juhu — not a large garden, but a deliberate one, the kind of garden that had been planned by someone who knew what they were doing and maintained by someone who did it without attribution. A mango tree. Bougainvillea on the east wall. A small water feature near the back fence that made a sound Vipin found immediately calming.

"Your father counts things too," he said.

"Does he?"

"Eleven photographs in the entrance. The way he tracks the room — he knows exactly how many people are in it at any given moment. It's how he knows when someone has left."

Shraddha turned to look at him. "I never noticed that."

"He also calculates before he speaks. The pause before he says something important — it's not theatrical, even though everything else is. That one is real."

She was quiet for a moment. The garden made its sounds. Traffic somewhere, muffled. A parakeet landed on the mango tree, looked at nothing for three seconds with the specific contempt parakeets deploy toward everything, and left.

"He didn't ask you to leave," she said.

"Is that the bar."

"For my father, with someone new? Yes. That's the bar."

He picked up a fallen bougainvillea petal from the garden path. Deep magenta, dry at the edges. He put it down.

"He'll have questions," he said. "About where I come from. The temple, the streets. The full thing."

"Probably."

"He should. It's a legitimate question. I don't have answers to all of it."

"I know."

"And he's going to find the parts of it—" He stopped. "There are parts of my history that aren't — clean."

She looked at him steadily.

"You don't have to tell me now," she said. "You don't have to tell me on a timeline that isn't yours."

"I know."

"But eventually you're going to have to trust that the messy parts aren't disqualifying." She paused. "At least not to me."

He looked at the water feature. The sound it made.

"In the car, after the match," he said, not looking at her. "You said I should have been out there."

"I meant it."

"I know." He paused. "But what I said was true. It hurts less than I thought. Because—" He stopped. Started again. "Cricket is the thing I do. It's not all of what I am. I used to think it was. I don't know exactly when that changed."

She didn't say anything for a moment. When she spoke, her voice had the quality of the slow version — each word chosen.

"You're allowed to be more than the cricket, na."

"That's a recent understanding," he said. "I'm still—" He paused. "I'm still learning it."

From inside the house came the sound of Shakti's voice on the phone, theatrical volume restored. The garden was quiet around it.

"He's going to call me tomorrow," she said. "With his assessment."

"And?"

"And I'm going to listen and not be moved by his assessment, because it's mine to make." She turned toward him briefly, then back to the garden. "For what it's worth. What I think."

"What do you think," he said. It came out quieter than he'd intended.

"I think you're the most interesting person I've ever sat in traffic with."

He almost laughed. The surprised version — the kind that came out before he decided whether it was appropriate.

"That's a very low bar," he said.

"You'd be surprised how many people can't clear it."

Shakti appeared at the garden door ten minutes after they'd come out. He stood in the doorframe with a glass of water and looked at them — at the garden, at the two of them — and then he said:

"Chaudhary. The innings you played in the Duleep Trophy North Zone semifinal, 2009-10. The lower-order partnership."

"With Vyas," Vipin said. "He batted for forty-two balls."

"You scored 61 off those same forty-two balls. Three fours, a six, and a lot of very irritating singles."

"The singles were deliberate. They had a left-arm spinner who was turning it. I didn't want Vyas on strike."

Shakti's eyebrows moved slightly upward.

"You were protecting the lower order," he said.

"Vyas is a better batsman than his record shows. I was just giving him the right deliveries to face."

A long silence.

"Hm," Shakti said. He took a sip of water. Went back inside.

Shraddha looked at Vipin. Vipin looked at where Shakti had been.

"He just tested you," Shraddha said.

"I know."

"He does that. The obscure domestic question. He looks these things up specifically."

"I know." Vipin picked up a fallen bougainvillea petal. "He wanted to see if I'd get defensive about the smaller innings. Whether I'd say 'the 61 was what mattered' instead of 'I was protecting Vyas.'"

"Which is the right answer?"

"Both are true." He set the petal down. "But one of them is about ego and one isn't."

---

On the auto back to Dadar — Shraddha had offered the car, he'd declined, habit — he sat with his hands in his lap and watched Mumbai do what Mumbai did on spring evenings, which was everything simultaneously and at volume. A food cart on the corner. A group of boys playing cricket under a streetlamp, the ball a tennis ball, the bat homemade. He watched them until the auto turned and they were gone.

He replayed the dinner. Not the whole of it — the operational parts. The things he'd said, the things he'd withheld, the moment the table had gone quiet when he'd mentioned the temple. He did this with every social situation that mattered — the same forensic examination he brought to reviewing his own bowling. What had worked, what hadn't, what he'd said because it was true vs. what he'd said because it was safe.

He'd said: temple, Matunga, no parents, Rao's name on the emergency contact form. He had not said: Bhai's streets, the packages, Dongri, the things that needed further explanation than a dinner table allowed. The lie to anti-corruption would come later — it always would have to come later. These were the facts about himself that required context before they could be facts rather than indictments.

He was aware that withholding them was its own kind of risk. Shakti Kapoor had said *I don't know where someone comes from, you don't know where they're going.* He'd said this as a warning. But there was something in it that Vipin turned over in the dark of the auto — the word *comes from.* He didn't know where he came from. That was literally true. The pendant had a name on it. The photograph in the drawer had two faces he'd never heard speak. The grandmother in Haryana was years away yet, a fact he didn't know was waiting.

Where he came from was unfinished. The coming from was still happening.

He decided this was information he could hold and still move.

A group of boys playing cricket under a streetlamp, the ball a tennis ball, the bat homemade. He'd already watched them and they were gone. He thought about them anyway — specifically the boy facing, who'd had his left foot across his body, wrong stance, and had been hitting everything anyway, the way children do before someone teaches them the correct method and takes something out.

He touched the pendant.

His father's hands had matched his in the photograph. His mother's face was turned away, laughing at something outside the frame. He didn't know yet what her voice sounded like. He didn't know what she'd been laughing at.

Shakti Kapoor had asked where he was from.

He'd told the truth — or part of it. The temple part. Not the streets part, not the Bhai part, not the things that weren't clean. He filed this: there was more to say. He'd said less than the whole, which was different from lying, but only by degree.

The city moved past him in its nighttime incarnation. Someone's television through an open window. The specific smell of a dhaba at nine PM — the accumulated heat of a day's cooking released into the evening air.

He'd spent enough of his life avoiding rooms. Avoiding the specific vulnerability of letting someone look at the full picture — the picture that included the streets, the Dongri time, the packages he'd carried, the mango he'd stolen and felt nothing about. The parts that didn't read as origin story. The parts that read as choices.

Shraddha had said: the messy parts aren't disqualifying.

He'd told her that cricket was not all of what he was.

Both things were true.

He was still deciding what they meant in combination.

The auto turned onto his road. The city around him was the same city it had always been — the one he'd arrived in on temple stairs, the one he'd navigated at eight years old, the one that had named him a cricketer and then an IPL player and then something the newspapers had an opinion about. It had absorbed him, the city. Not lovingly, not cruelly. Just — the way Mumbai absorbed everything. As a fact.

He was still here.

He got out of the auto. Paid. Stood in the street for a moment.

Counted the lights on in the building opposite: seventeen, down from twenty-three at dinner time. People going to sleep. The city folding itself.

He went upstairs.

---

Shakti Kapoor called Shraddha the next morning at nine.

"He's from nowhere," her father said.

"Baba—"

"I'm not saying it as an insult. I'm saying it as a fact. He's from nowhere. No family, no background, no — nothing on paper that goes back further than Rao at Shivaji Park." A pause. "I'm from Connaught Place. My father sold clothes. You know this story."

She knew this story. She knew it the way you know a story that has been told often enough to become furniture.

"You came from nothing," her father said. "Is that what you're going to tell me? I know. I know where I came from. But I also had a father who sold clothes. I had a mother who cooked. I had a *place*. This boy—" He paused again. "This boy has nothing. Nowhere. Literally. And I'm telling you, beta, that's not a small thing. You don't know where someone comes from, you don't know where they're going."

She was quiet for a moment.

"I don't know where he came from," she said. "You're right. He doesn't either. But I know where he is now. And I know what he said yesterday in that garden when he thought he was being careful." She paused. "And I know you."

"You know me."

"You came from nothing. You said so. You made yourself something. You respect that in other people, when you let yourself." She paused. "He made himself from nothing too. Different kind of nothing. But the same kind of building."

A long silence.

"I'm not saying he's a bad person," Shakti said. His voice had changed slightly. "I don't know enough to say that. I'm saying I don't know."

"Neither do I, completely. That's rather the point of getting to know someone." She paused. "And knowing is a process, na. It's not a form you fill in."

Another silence.

"He knew about Rao," Shakti said. "Deodhar-era Rao. That's a man who followed the domestic circuit before people followed the domestic circuit."

"He notices things."

"Yes." A pause. "He notices things." A different tone — not full approval, but something shifting in that direction. "Tell him to eat properly. Does he eat properly?"

"He eats dal every day and runs ten kilometers and has opinions about protein content. He eats properly."

"Good," Shakti said. "Then that's settled."

She almost said *that's Kavita Aunty's line*, but her father had already hung up.
