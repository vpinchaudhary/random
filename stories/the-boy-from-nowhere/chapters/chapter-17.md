# Chapter 17: The Girl in the Third Row

The cap weighed almost nothing. He had not expected this.

He was standing in the dressing room at Wankhede Stadium on a Thursday in February 2010, twenty years old, holding the India cap that had just been handed to him by the team manager, and the first thing he registered was the weight. Or the lack of it. Less than a cricket ball. Less than a glove. A thin piece of blue fabric and some stitching and a badge, and he stood there with it in his hands trying to reconcile the weight of the physical thing with the weight of the concept it represented.

He had watched men receive this cap on television. The faces they made. The whole ceremony of it — the senior player presenting it, the handshake, the moment of looking at it for the first time. He had categorized this, at various points over the previous decade, as performance. Cricketer discovers he has emotions. Unusual.

Standing here with the cap in his hands, he did not revise this opinion. He just understood that the performance was also true.

He put it on. He looked in the mirror on the far wall of the dressing room. The blue was the blue he'd watched on screens since age eleven. The badge was the BCCI badge he'd seen on Sachin's chest, on Dravid's chest, on Kumble's chest, on every chest he'd ever studied for form and technique and the specific information of how greatness held itself. His chest.

For one second, he didn't make a joke.

Then: "Not bad for a guy with no birth certificate." He said it to the mirror. The mirror didn't laugh. His own face didn't laugh. He took the cap off and put it on the bench. He picked up his bat. He went to warm up.

---

He didn't have a fairytale debut. He had a competent one, which was better.

Wankhede, first over, last ball: the West Indian opener had come at him hard. He'd bowled three deliveries that went for 13 runs — a no-ball wide, a full toss dispatched over mid-on, a short ball pulled through square leg — and the coaching staff's collective body language had done the thing that coaching staff body language did when a debut was going wrong, which was: become very still. The fourth ball he'd corrected the length, gone back to the Malinga lesson, straight and hard at 143. The seam had held. The opener had played down the wrong line. The appeal had gone up. The finger had gone up. He'd completed his follow-through and turned and walked back to his mark without any visible celebration, because the ball had done what balls at that length were supposed to do and celebrating the ball doing its job seemed premature.

His over: 1-12. The remaining two overs he'd tightened — 1-19 total, the wicket a batsman who'd stepped outside off and miscued a cut shot. Economy rate: 10.3 for the evening overall. Not clean, but international cricket was not clean. International batsmen were not the level he'd faced in IPL. They were better, and they proved it with the half-tracker in his first over, and he filed the proof and adjusted.

28 off 19 balls: 3 fours, 1 six, the rest running between wickets with the precision that came from a lifetime of treating every run as something that could not be assumed. He was out to a delivery he should have left — a ball that held its line longer than he read it, his reach slightly short, the edge taken cleanly. He walked off the way he walked off every dismissal: face forward, bat tucked under his arm, the score already filed for later analysis.

The coaching staff used the word "controlled." He preferred "learning." Both were true.

The blue jersey was in his bag on the bus home. He held the bag, not the jersey itself — but he was aware of where in the bag the jersey was, the way you were aware of a new object in a familiar space. The pendant was against his chest. He counted the streetlights through the window. He stopped at one hundred and let the rest go.

He thought about the groundskeeper, who had sat next to Rao at Shivaji Park to watch the match on the television in the groundskeeper's shed. He knew this because Rao had mentioned it in the morning conversation — not directly, because Rao did not mention things directly, but as a contained reference: "The groundskeeper's television has a good reception for the channel." Vipin had processed this in real time. The old man had carried his television out of the shed or adjusted its position to get the right channel and had sat through a T20 international for the first time in his life to watch a boy he'd let sleep in his storage room for four years play for India.

He held the bag.

When his wicket fell in the match, Rao had told him, the groundskeeper had knocked once on the arm of his chair. Rao had looked at him. The groundskeeper had shrugged. They had continued watching.

He counted streetlights. He stopped when he felt ready to stop.

At Shivaji Park the next morning, Rao was at the nets. The groundskeeper was rolling the pitch. Rao looked up when Vipin came through the gate. Looked back at the nets. The reading glasses were on his nose, which meant this was a real session.

"Bowling was functional," Rao said. This was their language for: something to work on, but not a crisis.

"I know."

"The second over had two length errors."

"I know."

"Fix them."

He went to get his kit.

Rao watched the nets for a moment. The cold chai sat on the boundary cot — he hadn't touched it, which meant he'd been thinking. About the session, presumably. About the length errors. About something.

"The batting was good." He said this once, in the same tone he used for technical observations, and then he walked to the nets and called a boy in from the boundary.

Vipin stood there for a second longer than necessary. The batting was good. Four words, Rao-standard, delivered to the back of a retreating figure who had already moved on to the next item of business.

He had known, from the moment Rao started with the bowling rather than the batting, that the bowling note was the framing and the batting was the content. In Rao's language, the direct comment always came last and meant the most. The cold chai was evidence of thought. The thought had produced: four words.

He picked up his kit and went to the crease. He bowled at the stumps for ninety minutes. He fixed the length errors.

He wasn't ready to tell anyone how much those four words mattered. He wasn't sure he'd ever be.

The party was in April.

IPL Season 3 had started — back in India after South Africa, back in stadiums he recognized, back in the city that was his. MI hosted a sponsor event at a hotel in Colaba: post-match celebration, the particular kind of gathering that existed at the intersection of cricket and Bollywood and money, where everyone in the room had access to things everyone else wanted and the evening's purpose was the trading of that access. He attended because there were contractual obligations attached to certain sponsor events and this was one of them.

He wore a suit. His first — bought with endorsement money, navy blue, made at a shop on Hill Road that Shetty had recommended. The tailor had asked his measurements, had taken them twice because the first set seemed wrong, and had produced a suit that fit his chest and waist but was tight across the shoulders in a way that communicated: you were not designed for formal occasions. Vipin wore it anyway. The suit was a costume. The pads were real. He knew the difference and so did anyone paying attention.

The room was everything rooms like this were: chandeliers, trays of things he didn't eat, a level of noise that existed specifically to signal importance. He recognized two actors from film posters he'd seen on the way in. He recognized three cricketers from other franchises. He did not recognize the remaining one hundred and eighty-three people, which he'd estimated from the table count (nineteen round tables, nine people per table on average, plus the people standing at the bar — approximately twenty-three), and was grateful for this.

He orbited the edges. This was his natural position in a room: the periphery, where you had a view of the whole and a route to the door. When people approached him — and they did, the India debut had added a new quality to the attention — he was funny. Quick, deflecting, leaving them with something worth repeating at the next party so they would remember the conversation as better than it was. He was good at this. He had been good at it since he was nine years old and discovered that making someone laugh bought him thirty seconds of goodwill.

The problem with being good at something for long enough is that you notice when you're doing it.

He was calculating his third deflection of the evening — a film producer was in the middle of a sentence about cricket's "relatability challenge" — when he saw her.

She was at the far side of the room, near the window, with a group of three people he didn't recognize. Twenty-something, dressed simply in what looked like actual clothes rather than event clothes — jeans, a good top, earrings that caught the chandelier light. She was nodding at something someone said, and her head-tilt had the quality of someone who was genuinely listening rather than performing listening, which was remarkable in a room of approximately two hundred people performing listening. Then she laughed. Loudly, completely, the kind of laugh that doesn't ask permission — and then she caught herself, covered it slightly with her hand, and then laughed again because the impulse was stronger than the self-consciousness. The laugh cycled through this loop twice in three seconds.

He watched this happen with the same attention he brought to a spinner's run-up.

What he noticed, in the time he spent watching: the way she tilted her head when the next person spoke, the small adjustments she made to the conversation's direction that nobody at her end of the room probably registered she was making. Her body language said "comfortable here" in the practiced way of someone who had learned to be comfortable in rooms like this. But there was something underneath it — a slight economy of expression, a brief moment between laughs where her eyes scanned the room with the automatic assessment of someone who'd been reading rooms since childhood. He recognized the scan because he did it too. He'd been doing it since he was nine years old and the question of which direction to move in was a practical one.

He'd never seen anyone else do it.

The film producer was still talking about relatability. Vipin made his deflection. Laughed at the right moment. The producer moved on. He tracked back to the far window.

She was gone. He had lost her in approximately forty seconds of conversation he hadn't been paying attention to.

He spent four minutes cataloguing the room systematically, the way you catalogued a fielding position — where are the gaps, where is the threat, where has the ball gone — before deciding this was disproportionate behavior for a person he had watched for thirty seconds. He stopped cataloguing.

He found the kitchen entrance instead.

He'd done this before at events — there was a reliable quality to service corridors and catering kitchens that was absent from event rooms. The staff had specific tasks and moved toward them without the directional uncertainty of a social occasion. He talked to a waiter near the service door for seven minutes about whether MI would make the knockout stage (the waiter was a Mumbai fan with specific opinions). He helped pick up a tray that another waiter dropped — his reflexes moved before his brain decided whether a cricketer in a suit picking up canapés was appropriate, and then the moment was over and he was holding a tray of canapés in a room full of people with endorsement deals, and it was a little late to pretend it hadn't happened.

Across the room, a filmmaker said something quietly to the person beside him. He didn't hear it. Later, through Ravi, he would learn it had been: "That cricketer is either very humble or very lost."

Neither description was accurate. He was just more comfortable here.

Three weeks later, a smaller event: a cricket-film crossover charity dinner at a hotel in Bandra. Round tables, forced mingling, fewer people, the particular atmosphere of a room where everyone is doing something good and wants it known that they're doing something good. He was there because Ravi had told him it was mandatory and then turned out not to be there himself for the first forty minutes because of a franchise call that ran over.

He was at a table of mixed cricket and film people, third from the wall, eating the vegetarian option (which was paneer in a sauce that had been negotiated with, not created) and maintaining the appropriate level of social engagement while conserving energy for the walk to the parking garage, when he told a story.

It was a story about a net session earlier that week — the kind of session where everything that could go wrong with a bowling machine had gone wrong, including the machine changing its own settings mid-session to deliver wides at 140 kph — and he told it the way he told training stories: without setup, already mid-event, his face carrying the punchline three sentences before he arrived there. His table laughed when they should. The table adjacent also laughed, because the story had carried.

He looked up.

She was at the adjacent table. The woman from the party. He placed her in approximately two seconds — the laugh was identifiable at any distance.

Their eyes met for the duration of a delivery — half a second, long enough for both of them to register that the look was happening, not long enough for either of them to do anything about it. Then someone at her table asked her something and she turned.

He went back to the paneer.

Twenty minutes later, Ravi appeared at his shoulder. This was the thing about Ravi: he arrived when useful without announcing it, as if he had a passive radar for moments that needed social engineering.

"Chaudhary." Ravi's voice was at the specific pitch of someone doing a thing while also pretending they're doing something else. "Come, I want to introduce you to someone."

Vipin followed him to the adjacent table.

"Shraddha, this is Vipin Chaudhary. Chaudhary, Shraddha Kapoor. Her dad is Shakti Kapoor." Ravi delivered this with the practiced efficiency of someone who'd made many introductions. "Don't make a Crime Master Gogo joke. She's heard them all."

"I wasn't going to make a Gogo joke," Vipin said. "I was going to make a Gogo *reference*. There's a difference."

Shraddha laughed — surprised, genuine, the real version of the laugh he'd identified from across a crowded room. Ravi groaned. "I specifically said don't."

"You said don't make a joke. A reference is an homage."

"An homage," Shraddha said. She was assessing him, not rudely but openly — the way someone assessed a person they'd just discovered was a different shape than expected. "From a cricketer."

"I have many sides. Most of them are very fast."

She laughed again. The laugh had the quality he'd observed — it arrived completely and then she noticed it and then it arrived again. He noticed her notice it, which meant she was aware of the laugh in a self-conscious way but couldn't fully manage it. He found this particular. It was difficult to look away from.

They talked for fifteen minutes that neither of them planned. Ravi took himself elsewhere at some point in the first five — the bridge-building instinct, creating the distance that allows connection. Shraddha asked about his cricket with the specificity that separated genuine interest from networking interest: not "how's the IPL going" but "I heard you bowl slower balls differently now — is that the Malinga thing, or is that separate?" He stared at her. She had watched an interview he'd given three months ago, in Cape Town, that he'd assumed nobody would find.

He asked about her film. She deflected — "It wasn't great, it's fine" — with the ease of someone who'd been deflecting this specific question for months.

"I watched the trailer," he said.

She looked at him with the expression of someone who knew what was coming — the polite compliment, the obligatory acknowledgment.

"You were the only person in it who looked like a real human."

A beat.

"That's not nothing," he added.

She was quiet for a moment. Not the quiet of someone formulating a response — the quiet of someone recalibrating. He had said something specific. He had said something he meant without wrapping it in a joke first, which was — he noticed this at the same moment she did — unusual for him.

"My director would be devastated to hear that," she said finally. "In the best way." She smiled. "Thank you. Seriously."

"The batata vada," he said, deflecting now, the joke arriving as cover. "You mentioned it at — somewhere. You mentioned a place in Versova."

"I said you should go. The owner is rude but the food is genuine."

"Rude and genuine sounds like my career."

She laughed, the real one, twice. The second time she didn't catch herself.

The conversation continued. She asked where he lived (Dadar flat, four years). What he ate (everything without a mother). She told him she was vegetarian — her mother's Kolhapuri family background — and he lit up with the specific warmth of someone who has spent years being the outlier in a room of people eating meat and recognizing suddenly that they are not the only one.

"The batata vada in Versova is actually—" she started.

"You're going to describe it to me."

"The oil is right. There are places where the oil is wrong and you know it from the first bite. This is right oil."

"Right oil is everything," he said. He had eaten enough bad oil in enough years of bad kitchens to have opinions about this that predated any knowledge of Versova.

She said she'd been making batata vada with her mother since she was six. She made a hand gesture demonstrating the precise thumb motion for the patty. He watched her hands for a second longer than was neutral.

Something crossed his face that he didn't manage. She noticed it — he saw her notice it, and in the same moment thought about his mother, who he did not know, who was perhaps the kind of woman who made batata vada with a thumb motion, or perhaps was not, and who would never have the chance to teach him either way. The thought arrived and he caught himself and his expression went neutral again, but the half-second was there and she'd seen it and both of them knew she'd seen it.

She did not push. She moved the conversation to something else — smoothly, without acknowledgment, the way someone moved a conversation when they understood that the tender place existed and could wait.

When the conversation moved to food — which it did, as it apparently moved with Shraddha to food the way his moved to cricket — she talked about it the way he talked about batting: with specificity and genuine preference, not performing enthusiasm. She had opinions about the oil temperature for batata vada. She had opinions about which Versova spots had changed since 2007 and which had stayed honest. She had a theory about why South Mumbai restaurants could never replicate the Bandra food even with the same recipes, which was about the water pressure in the kitchen, which she acknowledged was possibly irrational.

"The water pressure theory," he said. "You've thought about this."

"I have a lot of theories about food." She said this without apology. "My family thinks it's strange. My mother encourages it. My dad wants to know why I'm not eating more."

"What does your dad eat?"

"Everything. He's Punjabi. There's nothing he won't eat and he wants everyone to try it."

He thought about Rao, who ate whatever Kavita put in front of him without commentary. He thought about the pandit, who had made dal with a specific pattern of adding the tadka — ghee first, then mustard seeds, then hing — and had never varied this in Vipin's memory. He thought about the groundskeeper, who had asked *khana khaya* every time he left.

"Food is where people show you who they are," he said. It came out sounding more considered than he'd intended.

She looked at him. The assessment again — not the party version, the actual version. "Yeah. It is." A beat. "What do you show?"

"Paneer. Every time. No exceptions. I've had paneer in five countries."

"Five countries! Which countries?"

"India, South Africa, UAE once for a tournament, and England." A pause. "The English paneer was a mistake."

She laughed — the real version, both loops. "What did they do to it?"

"I'm not sure it was legal." He leaned back. "I think they boiled it first. And then apologized."

She was still laughing when someone across the room called her name. She half-turned, held up a finger — *one second* — and turned back. The turn-back was quick. He noticed the turn-back.

"I have to go say hello to someone. But—" She picked up the small card from the table that had her place name on it and turned it over. She wrote something on the back and slid it across. Her number. "The batata vada place. You should go. Not from Ravi's description. From your own."

He looked at the card. He looked at her. She was already half-turned to leave.

"The water pressure theory," he said.

She stopped. Turned fully back.

"Tell me more about the water pressure theory," he said.

The corner of her mouth went up — not the full laugh, just the precursor to it. "Next time," she said.

The dinner ended. They went to separate cars. He had her number on the back of a place card. He held it in his hand in the taxi home and thought about whether this was the kind of thing you could put in the drawer.

It wasn't. You couldn't put a person in a drawer.

In the car home, he replayed the fifteen minutes. Specifically: *you were the only person in it who looked like a real human.* He had said that. He had meant it. The honesty had come out without the armor around it and he had not noticed until after.

He counted the streetlights. He stopped at one hundred and let the rest go.

---

Ravi texted the next morning.

*Chaudhary. I have something for you. It's a phone number. Don't be weird about it.*

He stared at this for a while. Then: *I'm not weird.*

*Your last four apologies all came out wrong. You ARE weird. She asked for yours but I gave you hers so you have the power. You're welcome.*

He saved the number. He did not call it. He put the phone face down on the kitchen table and went to training and came back and ate dinner and watched half an episode of MasterChef Australia and did not call it.

The second day: nothing. He went to Shivaji Park. He ate at the dhaba. He watched two episodes of MasterChef Australia and noted that the contestants were again failing to respect oil temperature, which he considered a systemic problem. He did not call the number.

The third day, the catastrophizing did its work. She was Shakti Kapoor's daughter. The family had been in the film industry since Shakti Kapoor arrived from nothing and made himself into an institution — but the nothing he'd come from was a different kind of nothing, the kind that had a family and a hometown behind it. She had grown up in a bungalow in Juhu. She had aunts who were film stars. Her mother's family had been in the industry for decades before Shraddha was born. He had a pendant and a storage room and a drawer full of objects that constituted his entire personal history.

The distance between a gold pendant on temple stairs and a Juhu bungalow was not a distance you crossed with a phone call. He had no evidence for this belief and no intention of testing it.

The fourth day, she texted first.

A link. A map pin. A restaurant in Versova. And: *The batata vada place. You should go. The owner is rude but the food is genuine.*

He looked at the message for a long time. Then: *Rude and genuine sounds like my career.*

Three dots appeared. A laughing emoji. He sent one back. He had never sent an emoji before in his life and he sat there for a moment feeling ridiculous and not caring, which was a new combination.

He put the phone down. He went to the drawer.

He wasn't sure why. He didn't examine the impulse — the drawer opened before he'd decided to open it. He took out the photograph. The young couple. The man with the jaw. The woman half-turned, laughing at something he'd said, caught in the moment before the laugh fully arrived.

He looked at the woman's face. She was mid-motion, not posed. Her expression was the expression of someone who had been surprised into amusement, who hadn't expected whatever he'd said to be funny, and who was discovering that it was. He had seen this expression forty minutes ago, in a restaurant in Bandra, on a different face.

He put the photograph back. He put everything back in the order it lived in. The boarding pass, the team photo, the SA match ball, the anonymous letter, the ATM receipt, the first-wicket ball, the MI practice ball, the paper that said *I want them to say my name*, the newspaper clipping from the auction. The photograph on top, face down.

He closed the drawer.

He picked up his phone and looked at the laughing emoji on the screen.

He did not know what the two things had to do with each other. He was pretty sure the answer was nothing. He held this certainty the way you held a bad bowling delivery — complete, in both hands, aware of what it was. He put the phone on the table, face up this time, and went to make chai.
