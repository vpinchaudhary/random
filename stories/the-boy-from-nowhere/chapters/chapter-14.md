# Chapter 14: New Money

Rajasthan Royals won IPL Season 1.

Shane Warne's team, the tournament underdogs, won five games in a row at the end including the final, and the cricket internet — which had spent the first half of the season writing about Vipin Chaudhary — spent the second half writing about this instead. Vipin had watched the final on a hotel television with four other MI players who hadn't made the squad for the last three matches, eating dal and watching Warne celebrate with his players and feeling something he identified, after sitting with it for a few minutes, as relief. Not at MI's loss — at the reduction of attention. For three weeks, his face had been the thing the cricket internet was looking at. Now someone else's face was the thing.

His final season stats: 8 matches, 234 runs at a strike rate of 148, 9 wickets at an economy of 7.2. The bowling coach had shown him the comparison table: these numbers placed him in the top five for combined batting and bowling contribution among debutants in the tournament. He had looked at the table for a moment and then handed it back, not because the numbers didn't matter — they did, they mattered precisely — but because looking at a table of your own statistics for too long felt like something that happened to people who were satisfied, and he was not satisfied.

The bank statement arrived in paper form — the bank manager had insisted on this, something about formality, which Vipin had taken as useful because it meant he had a physical object in his hands rather than a number on a phone screen that could be scrolled past. He read it in the hotel lobby. He read the number three times. Then he folded the statement along its original crease and put it in his wallet, which was itself a new possession: a leather wallet, brown, bought at a stall near the stadium three weeks ago, fourteen centimeters by nine, capacity for eight cards though he currently had two.

He had never had a bank account before the previous month. Rao had taken him to the bank personally, walked him through the documentation, sat across from the branch manager and said nothing but was present in the room in the way he was always present in rooms when he wanted something done correctly. The account number was now part of the information set that constituted Vipin Chaudhary's official existence. A birth certificate. A cricket registration. A bank account. Three items that said, in the language of institutions: this person is real.

The number in the account.

He stood in the lobby. The number stayed the same no matter how many times he read it, which was consistent with how numbers worked but still felt like it required verifying. He read it a fourth time. He folded the statement. He put it in the wallet next to the receipt from the bat shop. He stood there for a moment with the wallet in his hand.

The part of him that assigned expiration dates noted the number and filed it under: temporary. Not a permanent condition. A current state. He didn't engage with this. The number was real and it was in his account and the part that assigned expiration dates was not wrong about the world — things did end — it was just not useful right now.

He worked through the comparison. He knew what a pandit earned, broadly — the Matunga temple had a modest trust, the regular puja fees, the festival contributions, comfortable but not abundant, a life that required no money to be meaningful. He knew what Bhai's best month had looked like from the outside, in the years when Bhai's territory was productive: the car, the phones, the meals. He knew what the groundskeeper earned because he had once, at fourteen, seen the pay packet left on the desk and noted the number without meaning to. He had noted everything without meaning to.

The number in his account was larger than all of those. Significantly larger. And this was not the whole contract — the $100,000 auction amount was distributed across the years of the contract, minus taxes, minus the agent fee Rao had negotiated with a cricket management firm, minus the deductions he hadn't fully understood when signing but would understand later. This was the first tranche. The first payment for existing in a world where his particular gifts had a market price.

His hand was not trembling exactly. It was doing something that was the precursor to trembling — a heightened state of fine-motor awareness where he could feel each individual tendon's small adjustments. He folded the statement again. He put it in the wallet. He walked to the elevator.

---

He bought Rao a bat the following week.

Top-of-the-line SG, the kind that came in a cloth case rather than a plastic bag, the kind that Rao had spent his coaching career buying for other people's boys. Vipin had known exactly what to buy — he had watched Rao examine bats for seventeen years, knew the grain count he preferred, the weight he favored, the specific way the old man's thumb ran along the shoulder of the blade when he was assessing a good piece of willow. The bat shop owner in Dadar had recognized him, which was a strange experience that lasted three seconds and then resolved into a normal transaction.

He didn't wrap it. He carried it on the bus to Shivaji Park in its cloth case, upright beside him like a traveling companion.

Rao was at the nets when he arrived. The usual: a boy hitting into the catching cradle while Rao stood at mid-off watching the swing of the bat. Rao looked up when Vipin walked through the boundary gate, noted the cloth case, said nothing. Let the session end. Waited until the boy had gone.

Vipin held out the case.

"For the nets," he said.

Rao took it. Opened the case. Ran his thumb along the shoulder of the blade. The reading glasses slipped off his forehead and he didn't fix them — they hung there for a moment on his nose before he pushed them back up. He turned the bat over. Checked the back. Weighed it in both hands.

"Good willow," he said.

He put the bat back in the case, closed the zip, and carried it to the equipment shelf along the boundary wall. He set it down among the spare balls and old pads. He did not look at Vipin again. He walked back to the nets and called the next boy in from the boundary.

Vipin sat on the cot bench along the boundary and watched for an hour. It was not, he understood, that Rao was unaffected. It was that Rao and he had learned to transact in the currency of cricket — and the bat said what the words would have been too much of, so the bat was enough, and the next session was how Rao said: received.

He took the bus back to the hotel with his wallet in his pocket, the bat shop receipt folded inside the bank statement inside it.

---

He was coming out of the hotel's side entrance after a team breakfast — not a grand exit, the kind of exit that involved sunglasses and preparation; just an eighteen-year-old walking through a door with a phone in his hand — when he saw Bhai.

Bhai was leaning against the railing of the hotel's car park entrance, smoking. He was older than the last time Vipin had seen him — heavier in the face, the same flat eyes, the same quality of attention that absorbed the room without revealing what it concluded. He was wearing a clean kurta. He had dressed for this.

"Chaudhary," Bhai said.

Not the street name. Not the childhood name. The cricket name, which was now his name. There was a message in the choice.

They walked. Not toward Bhai's territory — neutral streets, the hotel district, the kind of anonymous urban space that belonged to neither of them particularly. Bhai walked at his own pace, which was the pace of someone who had nowhere to be while being exactly where he intended. He asked about the IPL. He congratulated Vipin on the season, on the article, on the innings he had apparently watched on television. He asked about MI's plans for next year.

Vipin answered. Short answers. Correct answers. Watching the conversation the way he watched a spinner's run-up — for the tell, for the moment when the plan revealed itself.

It came gradually, as it always did with Bhai. Not a sudden ask — a suggestion. A business opportunity. A club in South Mumbai, music venue, good location, needed an investor and a face. Nothing illegal, technically. The licensing was in order. The money was clean, or Bhai said it was clean, and Bhai believed in making the clean version visible because it was useful for exactly these conversations.

Vipin listened. He let Bhai finish. He was aware of what was happening in himself as Bhai talked — the two separate things that had never fully resolved. The street kid who had been raised partly by Bhai's economy, who knew what it was to be cold and find warmth in Bhai's orbit, who had learned to read rooms and spot exits and when to run in exactly those streets where they were now walking — that part of him heard Bhai and felt the pull of the old obligation, the weight of the years, the specificity of the debt. The other part — the cricketer, the one Rao had built, the one that had read a spinner's grip from forty meters and translated it into a six — that part heard Bhai and read the tell. The business was not the point. The name on the paper was not the point. The point was the precedent: once the name was on one piece of paper, it could be on another. Once the claim was activated, it was activated.

He let Bhai finish.

"I can't, Bhai," he said. Quiet. No joke.

Bhai nodded once. The slow nod of someone for whom this answer had been one of two possibilities, and who had planned for both.

Bhai drew on the cigarette. He had been smoking the same one since they started walking, which meant either it was his second or he smoked very slowly. "I'm not asking for much." He exhaled, looked at the street rather than Vipin. "Just your name. On a piece of paper." A pause. "You don't need to do anything."

"I know what it means to put my name on something now," Vipin said. "That's why I can't."

Silence. A motorcycle passed on the street. A vendor on the corner was selling coconuts and had not looked up once.

"Remember, Chaudhary." Bhai's voice had not changed — still flat, still even, the tone that was identical between conversations about cricket and conversations about things that were not cricket. "I knew you before anyone knew your name. Don't forget the people who were there before the money."

It was almost exactly what he had said on the day Vipin had left for Shivaji Park. Six years earlier, the same claim: *remember who was here.* The repetition was not accidental. Bhai did not repeat himself accidentally.

"I remember," Vipin said. "I don't forget."

He walked back to the hotel. His chest was tight in the way it went tight when he couldn't name what he was feeling — not fear, not guilt, something that belonged in the space between them. Bhai had fed him when nobody else had. Bhai had given him work and safety and the specific education of a street kid in a city that did not care if you survived. These facts did not change. They had not become smaller because of the money. The debt existed. He could not pay it with a name on a piece of paper.

He came through the lobby. A staff member at the concierge desk said, "Sir, can I help you with anything?"

Vipin looked at him. He was young — twenty, maybe. Clean jacket, name badge, the professional smile of someone for whom the professional smile was load-bearing.

"No," Vipin said, more sharply than he'd meant to. Not angry at the staff member. Angry at the texture of the afternoon, which had lodged in his chest and had nowhere to go. He walked past. He got three steps and stopped.

He went back.

"Sorry," he said. "That was — sorry." He paused, because he was not good at this, not good at the formal acknowledgment of his own failures, and the apology came out at the wrong speed with the wrong rhythm. "I had a hard afternoon. Not your fault."

The staff member smiled politely, professionally, with the practiced equanimity of someone who processed other people's moods as part of the job. "No problem, sir."

Vipin stood there for one more second, aware that the moment was worse now than if he hadn't apologized, that there was no recovery available, and walked to the elevator.

He carried the moment for days.

The endorsement meetings arrived in a cluster in the second week of June.

Shetty, the commercial director, had scheduled five conversations in two days — calls and meetings and one in-person session in a glass-fronted office in BKC where the air conditioning was set to the specific cold of spaces where a great deal of money changed hands. Sports drink. Mobile phone brand. A clothing company. A protein supplement. An insurance provider who wanted "the resilience narrative," which Shetty explained by referencing Anjali's article and Vipin understood immediately as: *they want to buy the orphan story and put a logo on it.*

He sat in the meetings. He had not known, before this week, that meetings about money were themselves conducted in a specialized language, a vocabulary of terms that sounded like English but meant something more precise. *Brand alignment. Exclusivity window. Deliverables. Social activation.* He took notes on his phone, which was a new phone, which was itself an object he was still calibrating to. The meetings lasted between forty minutes and two hours each. He signed two deals through MI's management agency — the sports drink and the clothing brand. He did not sign the protein supplement (they wanted him to say he used it, which he didn't and wouldn't), and he did not sign the insurance company (something about the resilience-narrative framing that he could not quite articulate but which sat badly enough that he said no, and Shetty noted the no and moved on without argument).

The first ad shoot was three days later.

A studio in Andheri, the kind of building that looked industrial from outside and opened into a white space full of equipment and people with very specific jobs. Vipin arrived at 8 AM. Someone took his bag. Someone else led him to a chair in front of a mirror surrounded by light bulbs — makeup, he was told, and he sat there for forty minutes while a woman applied things to his face that he could not see the effect of and was told not to touch. He did not touch them. He watched the mirror and tried not to count the light bulbs. There were thirty-two.

The shoot was for the sports drink. He was required to hold a bottle and look fierce. Then look approachable. Then look fierce-but-approachable, which was, the photographer explained through his interpreter, a specific mood. Vipin held the bottle. He looked at the camera. The photographer said something. The interpreter said: "More alive. Like you just won."

He was alive. He had just won. He looked at the camera.

"Better," the photographer said. "But relax the jaw."

He had spent eighteen years developing his jaw for situations that required it. He relaxed it. He held the bottle. He looked at the camera. The photographer took several hundred pictures in the next three hours, and afterward the creative director said the session had gone well, and Shetty said the same thing, and Vipin looked at the mood board of the final selects on the art director's laptop and saw a person who looked approximately like him but whose eyes were doing something his eyes did not normally do.

In the break between the morning session and the afternoon session, he was standing near the lighting rigs eating the vegetarian sandwich the catering had provided when one of the lighting assistants — a young guy about his age, wearing the company's crew shirt, managing a diffuser panel — came over and said: "Bhai, mast innings tha. IPL wala." *Brother, that was a great innings. The IPL one.*

He had a Mumbai accent. Working-class, the specific rhythm of someone who had grown up north of the city. His name badge said DEEPAK.

"Thanks," Vipin said.

"I bowl a bit," Deepak said. He was clearly deciding whether the conversation was welcome and proceeding with modest confidence, which Vipin respected as a calibration. "Off-spin. Nothing like you, obviously. But I saw on YouTube — this grip adjustment for off-spinners?" He demonstrated on thin air, his fingers moving into a modified position. "Have you seen this?"

Vipin looked at the grip. He had not seen it. He tried it with his own hand, the bottle still in the other. It didn't feel right — wrong axis for what an off-spinner needed, probably worked better for specific conditions. "I don't think this works for most pitches," he said. "The seam position would—" He thought about how to explain it. "You'd lose carry. The ball would skid through."

Deepak nodded. "I thought something was off about it. The guy on YouTube made it look easy."

"YouTube guys always make it look easy."

Deepak laughed. The assistant director called Deepak's name from across the studio. He went. The afternoon session started. They didn't speak again. Vipin watched him work the diffuser panels with the efficiency of someone who knew exactly what his job required and did it without requiring it to be more. There was a directness to this that he found restful after the morning of being directed to be fierce-but-approachable.

At the end of the day, Deepak was packing up equipment cases when Vipin walked past. He nodded. Deepak nodded back. Vipin noticed the name again — Deepak, crew shirt, doing the work of the "before" side of the life, the side where you were the person who made things happen for other people rather than the person things happened to.

He got Deepak's number from the production coordinator. He never called it. But he remembered the name.

---

He went back to Shivaji Park at the end of the month.

Not for training. Just to go. He took the bus rather than a car — habit, or something that still felt like his own life rather than the life that was being organized around him. The bus was crowded in the late afternoon. Two aunties discussed a wedding at length. A school boy slept against the window. An older man read a newspaper with MI's season statistics on the back page, did not look up, did not recognize the eighteen-year-old standing in the aisle three feet away.

Vipin stood in the aisle and watched the city go past the windows. He counted the stops from memory. Fourteen to Shivaji Park.

The groundskeeper was rolling the center strip when Vipin came through the gate. He looked up. Nodded once, the nod of someone who had been expecting this and was not surprised by the timing. He kept rolling.

The storage room was as Vipin had left it. The cot was there, the blanket folded on the end. The peg on the wall where the bat had leaned. The shelf where the practice balls lived. The equipment manager's boxes along one wall. The smell: linseed oil and stored leather and the particular quality of dust that accumulated in rooms where people slept.

Twenty-three cracks in the ceiling. Same as always.

He sat on the cot. The springs adjusted under him — a familiar resistance, one his body remembered. He sat without counting anything else. He just sat. This room was the last place where nobody had wanted anything from him — not his face, not his name, not his brand-alignment, not his claim on the debt Bhai was owed. The groundskeeper had left newspapers at his door for four years without asking for a thing. The room had held him without requiring him to perform.

He was aware, sitting here, that this was a thing he might be losing. Not the room — the room was still here, nothing had changed. But the ability to be in a room where nobody wanted something from him was increasingly a condition he had to manufacture rather than find. You could be alone in a hotel room. But that was not the same as this.

He took out his wallet. He took out the envelope he'd prepared at the hotel — cash, an amount he'd calculated as useful without being an insult, folded inside the kind of envelope that came in hotel stationery sets. He walked to the groundskeeper's shed and put the envelope on the table.

The groundskeeper came in from the pitch. He looked at Vipin. He looked at the envelope. He shook his head. He pushed the envelope back across the table.

"Tere paisa nahi chahiye." His voice was the same as always — not particularly warm, not cold, simply the voice of a man who said what he meant. *I don't want your money.* "Tu kuch bana, woh kaafi hai." *You becoming something — that's enough.*

"Please, uncle," Vipin said.

The groundskeeper looked at him for a moment. Something crossed his face that was not quite expression — the careful consideration of a man who did not accept things easily and who understood that refusing this would be its own kind of cost. He picked up the envelope. He did not count it. He folded it once and put it in his shirt pocket, which had a button. He buttoned the button. He patted the pocket once. Then he turned back to the door.

"Khana khaya?" he said on his way out. *Did you eat?*

"Haan, uncle."

"Good." He was already at the pitch.

Vipin sat in the storage room for an hour after. He did not sleep. He was not tired. He sat with the linseed oil smell and the twenty-three cracks and the sound of the groundskeeper's roller on the outfield, the same rhythm he had fallen asleep to for four years.

At some point he noticed the letters on the wall.

They were behind the headboard of the cot, in the lower corner — he wouldn't have seen them if he hadn't been sitting in the exact posture he was sitting in, and even now he had to lean and angle to make them out. Old letters, scratched into the concrete with a nail or a stone, the kind of scratching that required patience and repetition. Faded from time. Two letters and a year.

*R.S. 1987.*

Someone had been here before him. Twenty-one years before, someone had slept in this room and scratched their initials into the concrete of the wall behind where their head had been. A cricket boy, probably. Another one who needed a place to be. He had left his mark in a room where nobody was supposed to mark things. *R.S. 1987.* Just that. No message. No explanation.

Vipin traced the letters with his fingertip. The concrete was rough, the letters slightly deeper than the surrounding surface from however many nights of scratching it had taken. *R.S.* He tried to construct the person: also from Mumbai, since this was Shivaji Park. Also under Rao's care, probably. Also sleeping here, under a cricket club's provisional hospitality, with the next step still uncertain.

He hoped R.S. had made it. He had no way of knowing.

He sat with this for a while.

Then he got up, straightened the blanket on the cot — unnecessary, since nobody was sleeping here anymore, but the habit — and walked out. He touched the doorframe on the way out. He had touched it going in and out for four years without knowing he was touching it. His fingers found the spot without looking.

He walked back through the park. The groundskeeper was at the far end of the outfield, working on the boundary rope with wire ties. He didn't look up as Vipin left. Vipin didn't wave. This was the correct way to end it.

He counted the stops back on the bus. Fourteen. The city did its evening thing around him — the orange of late afternoon on the buildings, the auto-rickshaws finding their evening rhythm, a chai stall on a corner he recognized from years of this same route. He had taken this bus when he had nothing. He was taking it now with money in his wallet and his face on a sports drink billboard two stops earlier. Both versions of the journey felt the same from inside it. This was either reassuring or the most important thing he'd noticed all month. He couldn't decide which.

He put his hand in his pocket and found the wallet, felt the folded bank statement inside it. The number that shouldn't exist next to his life.

Tomorrow: off-season training. Domestic cricket next month. India ambitions that he had not said aloud to anyone because saying them aloud did something to their weight. The IPL season was over. Something else was beginning.

What was still missing, he did not answer yet. But he knew the shape of it — the shape the room had, the shape the groundskeeper's silence had, the shape that thirty thousand people saying your name could not fill. R.S. 1987 had scratched their initials into a wall and left, and it was possible R.S. had found the shape of it. It was also possible they had been looking for it the same way he was, in the spaces between the things that were supposed to matter.

He folded the ATM receipt from that morning's hotel lobby machine into the wallet alongside the bank statement. He would add it to the drawer later. The collection: a newspaper clipping, a piece of paper, a practice ball, a match ball, an unsigned letter, and now an ATM receipt. The drawer was becoming a museum of his own life.

This was, somehow, okay.
