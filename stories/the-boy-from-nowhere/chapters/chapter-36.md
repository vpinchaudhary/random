# Chapter 36: The Captain's Armband

**Timeline**: 2018-2019 (age 28-29)

---

The armband was a strip of cloth with velcro at one end.

Vipin turned it over in his hands for perhaps thirty seconds before putting it on, standing at the bathroom mirror in the dressing room annexe at the Wankhede, November 2018, one hour before MI's first home fixture of the Syed Mushtaq Ali Trophy where they were trying him out in the vice-captain role ahead of the full IPL transition. The armband was sky blue with the MI logo in gold. It fastened around the left arm just above the elbow. It had been worn by Rohit for six years and then laundered and folded and handed to Vipin by the team manager, Suresh Pradhan, who had done this with the neutral expression of a man distributing mail.

"Just put it on," Suresh had said. "It doesn't bite."

It did not bite. It was a strip of cloth with velcro.

He fastened it, checked it in the mirror, then noticed it was slightly crooked. He adjusted it. It became slightly crooked in the other direction. He adjusted again, got it approximately level, and decided approximately level was what the universe was offering today.

Later he would score 67 and take two wickets and lead MI to a win by eight runs and the post-match write-up in three separate outlets would describe his captaincy as "instinctive" and "tactically sharp" and one of them, the pink paper, would call it "the natural extension of a cricket brain that was always going to lead." He would read the pink paper piece in Shraddha's kitchen at six in the morning two days later with a cup of chai and think: *these people weren't watching me fix the armband for forty-five seconds.*

He kept this to himself. There were things that stayed private.

---

The India recall had come in February 2018.

Not a phone call — he'd half-expected a phone call, the way he'd imagined it during the long eighteen months of Ranji cricket and IPL seasons without the blue cap. What came instead was a text from Rao at seven in the morning: *check your email.* The email was from the BCCI, the official selection announcement, which he was on because Vikram Batra had pulled his hamstring in training and they needed a fast bowling allrounder for the South Africa tour. The text of the email was formal and impersonal and he read it twice standing in his flat in his kitchen in the January cold of an air-conditioned Mumbai morning.

Then he put the phone down and made chai.

The chai took four minutes. He stood over the pot watching it come to the boil, the specific activity of the surface as the heat built from below. The milk separating slightly at the edges and then homogenizing back. The chai leaves expanding. Normal chai behavior. He watched it with the complete attention of a man who needs something ordinary to do.

Shraddha called while the chai was still hot enough to need blowing.

"I know," she said, when he picked up. "Rao called Ravi called me. Are you—"

"Making chai."

"I know you're making chai. Are you okay?"

He considered this. Outside his window, the Bandra street below was doing its morning thing — auto-rickshaws, a vendor with a cart of oranges positioned near the signal, a school bus that made the same stop every day at the same time, which he knew because he'd noted the schedule over the years without ever consciously deciding to track it. Ordinary morning. Ordinary street.

"Yeah," he said.

"That's it?"

"I don't know what else there is. I'm in the squad. I'm going to South Africa. I need to pack the right gear."

"Chaudhary." A pause. He could hear the shift in her register — not the teasing one, not the one she used when she was about to make a point, but the quiet one, the one she'd developed over the years for the moments he closed the doors. "It's okay to feel it."

"I am feeling it," he said. "It doesn't have a name yet."

This was true. It sat in him as pressure without direction — not joy exactly, not relief exactly, not the vindication he'd fantasized about in the low months when he'd told Rao he was fine and been lying. Something larger and less legible. Something that would take time to locate.

He drank the chai. It was still slightly too hot. He drank it anyway.

---

He went to the nets at Shivaji Park that afternoon. Rao was already there — he was always already there, the old man operating on a schedule that preceded Vipin's own existence and would presumably outlast it.

Rao had reading glasses on his forehead. He had a cup of chai on the bench behind him that had been going cold since the previous hour, which was also always true. He looked up when Vipin walked in through the side gate and said nothing. The look was enough.

They worked for two hours. The session had no specific agenda — bowling at the batting machine, then facing it himself, then bowling again. At one point Rao stopped him mid-run-up and said: "You're rushing the crease. You were doing it in the Karnataka match in November too. You want to bowl fast, you have to be slow getting there." Vipin stood at the top of his mark. He ran in again. Rao watched. He didn't say anything the second time, which was the commentary.

At the end, on the bench with chai that was genuinely cold but Rao drank regardless, the old man said: "The selectors see what I've been watching for two years."

"They see what's convenient," Vipin said. "Batra got hurt. They needed a body."

"They needed a specific body. There were other bodies. They picked yours." He paused. "Don't be ungrateful."

"I'm not ungrateful."

"You're making it smaller than it is because you're frightened of making it big."

Vipin didn't answer this. A boy of about eleven was doing throwdowns at the far net, trying to impress whoever was watching him, which was nobody except Vipin from thirty meters away. Decent balance. Front foot moving too far across. Very fixable.

"You taught me that," Vipin said, eventually. "Make it small so it can't hurt you."

"I know I did." Rao looked at his cold chai. "I was sometimes wrong about things."

---

South Africa was sharp winter and altitude and pitches that offered pace bowling what Mumbai pitches refused to — lateral movement, carry through to the keeper, the ball doing things off the surface that made batsmen genuinely uncertain. He took seven wickets in the ODI series. He scored 41 and 63 in the first two games, composed innings, nothing like the explosive batting of his early years — that mode was still there, but he'd learned what Gambhir had known at Wankhede: sometimes the knock worth having is the one that doesn't make the highlight reel.

On the team bus in Johannesburg, Bumrah sat beside him — Vipin playing the Sudoku app he'd had for three years and played obsessively despite finding it genuinely not relaxing, which was perhaps the point — and Bumrah watching the city pass outside.

"You bowl differently now," Bumrah said. Not accusation. Observation.

"I'm older."

"Not that differently from 2014. Technically. But the selection — when you bowl which ball — that's changed."

Vipin looked at the Sudoku. He'd made a error in the fourth column, which meant the top-right box was unsolvable and he'd need to restart. He put the phone away.

"Tell me what you're seeing," he said.

They talked through the overs, the selection process, reading a batsman's stance and weight distribution over the first twelve balls to understand the sixteenth. Bumrah was younger by six years and had things to learn and knew it, which was the quality that made him extraordinary. Vipin had things to teach and found, in the teaching, that he understood them more precisely than when he'd only been doing them. The conversation lasted until Johannesburg thinned to a suburban expressway and then an airport hotel approach road.

"Why do you bowl the off-cutter when they're expecting the bouncer?" Bumrah asked. "They're back on the crease. The bouncer's the percentage ball."

"The off-cutter at 132 kph when they're set for 153 is more disruptive than the bouncer at 153 when they're expecting pace. You're not just bowling a delivery. You're breaking a rhythm."

Bumrah sat with this. Outside, the hotel's palm trees were visible — the planted kind, too regular, the kind that look like cricket grounds when you've been playing cricket too long.

"Bhai taught you that," Bumrah said. "Rao, I mean."

"Rao taught me to think. The rest I learned slowly." He paused. "And some of it I learned from getting hit."

---

Rohit told him about the MI captaincy in October 2018, over the phone, from Dubai where Rohit was finishing an interview circuit.

"I want you to have it," Rohit said. "I've spoken to the management. They agree."

"You don't have to—"

"I know I don't have to. I want to." His voice had the particular warmth of a man who had thought about this for longer than the conversation. "You read the game better than anyone I've played with, Chaudhary. Not just the ball — the people. The room." A pause. "MI needs that. I need to focus on the Test calendar. The workload is—"

"I know." He did know. He'd watched Rohit manage the MI captaincy alongside India duties for years, the double-taxation of leadership at both levels, the way it built specific lines in a person's face.

"Publicly, we say it's a transition," Rohit said. "We don't say I'm stepping back. We say MI is a young captain's team and this is the moment. Which is true."

"Okay."

"You're going to be good at this."

"How do you know?"

A pause that was almost a laugh. "Because you've been captaining since 2008. You just didn't have the armband."

After the call, Vipin stood in his flat for a moment doing nothing in particular. Then he went to the kitchen and found Shraddha at the counter reading something on her tablet, her glasses on, a half-eaten bowl of fruit next to her that she'd started and then forgotten about, which was a thing she did. She looked up.

"I heard," she said. "The call was on speaker." A pause. "Sorry. Habit."

"No, it's fine." He pulled out a stool. Sat. "He said I've been captaining since 2008."

"He's right."

"I know he's right. I'm just—" He stopped. "It's different when it's official. When the armband is yours and the decisions are yours and if it goes wrong there's nowhere to point except—"

"At yourself."

"Yeah."

Shraddha put the tablet down. She took off her glasses in the way she took them off when she was going to say something direct — one arm, folded, set on the counter with the glasses perched. "You know what I noticed, the first time I saw you in the field? Not the first time I noticed you playing. The first time I was actually watching."

"When was that?"

"2012. The Wankhede IPL game against Bangalore. You made a fielding change in the sixteenth over that baffled everyone in the stand, including the commentators, and then the batsman hit exactly to that fielder and was caught. And the camera cut to the commentator looking at the monitor like he was being professionally embarrassed." She picked up the bowl of fruit and ate a piece of melon. "You were twenty-two and you already knew. The only thing you were missing was everyone else admitting you knew."

He looked at her. "You remember the specific over."

"I have a good memory."

"You remember the specific over of a 2012 IPL game."

"It was the sixteenth over. I said that."

He shook his head, which was the thing he did when he had no adequate response and smiling felt too easy.

---

He told Rao in person, the following Monday morning, at Shivaji Park. Rao listened without interrupting, which was how he listened to most things that mattered. Then he picked up his chai — cold, as always — and drank some of it and set it back.

"Finally," he said.

"That's all?"

"What do you want? A speech?" Rao adjusted his reading glasses on his forehead. They were new glasses — same position, new frames, slightly thicker, the concession to the fact that seventy-one was different from sixty-seven. "You understand what it means?"

"Managing a batting order. Bowling changes. The team meetings."

"No." Rao shook his head slowly. "It means you'll carry the results of twenty-five young men in your stomach every night. Wins and losses both. The ones you made the right call and lost anyway are the worst ones. Those sit differently than the wrong calls." A pause. "You also need to know which players need a word and which ones need to be left alone. You already know that. But now it has consequences."

"I've always read people."

"You've read them to survive. Now you read them to lead." He looked at Vipin with the quality he had when he was deciding something. "Same skill. Different purpose. Bigger cost when you're wrong."

The sparrows at Shivaji Park were doing what sparrows did at that hour — navigating the grass near the pitch in irregular trajectories, picking at something, startling at nothing, relocating. He'd watched these sparrows, or their predecessors, or their predecessors' predecessors, for fifteen years at this ground. The ground hadn't changed. The sparrows hadn't changed. He had.

"Rao," he said.

"Mm."

"Thank you." He hadn't said it plainly before. Not without deflection, not without the joke that followed immediately behind. He said it plain this time and let it sit.

Rao looked at him. The reading glasses caught the morning light and threw a brief reflection. "Twelve years," he said, "and you thank me now."

"I thanked you before."

"You thanked me with results. Which is also thanks." He picked up the cold chai. "But this is better."

---

The 2019 IPL season began in late March, and what Vipin discovered about captaincy in the first two weeks was that Rohit had been right and Rao had been right and both of them had told him different true things about the same thing.

Rohit's version: he'd been doing this informally for years. Senior players had been coming to him with tactical questions since 2015, the younger ones since 2017, and the meetings where the plans were made had included his input in ways that weren't about the armband. The formal captaincy was a change in protocol, not in function.

Rao's version: the cost was different. In the fifth game of the season against Kolkata, MI defending 162, he bowled one bad ball — a full toss in the seventeenth, the kind that happens when your mind is in the next over before the current ball is complete — and it went for six. They compensated. MI won by four. But the full toss sat in him for three days in the way bad decisions sit when you're the one making them — not just the result, but the cause, the specific lapse that produced it.

He ran harder in the nets that week. Rao watched without commenting.

---

Team meetings were the part nobody wrote about.

He'd been in other captains' team meetings — Rohit's, which were organized and direct; MS Dhoni's, which were brief and assumed competence; one overseas captain whose name he'd decided not to remember, who managed meetings by reading from notes and looking at no one, which produced the particular team atmosphere of a room where everyone was thinking about something else. He'd formed views.

His meetings were shorter than they should have been according to coaching theory and longer than they needed to be according to his own preference, which averaged to about right. He said what he knew. He left space for what the players knew. He did not lecture people about their strengths. He did sometimes, unpredictably, make a joke at his own expense at the moment when the seriousness in a room had been going long enough to start eating itself.

In the meeting before the qualifier against Delhi, he stood at the board for eight minutes, then said: "Karthik, you're going to play the reverse sweep in the first over and I can see it in your face and I'm telling you now — if you do it before ball fifteen, I am personally driving to your house after the match and removing the reverse sweep from your body." Karthik — twenty-three, technically brilliant, dangerously attached to his reverse sweep — started laughing before the sentence was finished. The room temperature dropped to something usable. They won by twenty-two runs.

Leadership is partly tactics. Partly it's knowing when the room is holding its breath and needs permission to exhale.

---

He scored 450 runs in the 2019 league stage. Took twelve wickets. The runs came in different registers: a 56 that was patient and ugly and necessary when MI were four down for nothing against Bangalore; an 89 off 47 against Punjab when the calculation demanded the big-swing mode. The wickets were more satisfying because they required more thinking. A 153 kph bouncer that broke a batsman's resolve early. A 118 kph off-cutter in the seventeenth that looked exactly like the bouncer until it wasn't. Variation wasn't the goal; uncertainty in a batsman's head was the goal, and variation was the instrument.

They reached the final against CSK in Hyderabad with a fourteen-run win over Delhi in the qualifier, and the night before the final Vipin sat in his hotel room at ten-thirty going through the CSK batting lineup in his head — not notes, he never trusted notes in match prep. Batsman by batsman, the tendencies, the things they'd done in three league-stage meetings that season.

His phone lit up. Rao. He picked up.

"You should be sleeping," Rao said.

"I am sleeping. This is me sleeping."

"I know what you sound like when you're thinking about cricket." A pause — the weight-bearing kind. "I wanted to say, before tomorrow — whatever happens — this was always going to happen. From the first time I saw you at Shivaji Park hitting a ball you had no business hitting. A final. An armband. The whole thing."

Outside the hotel window the Hyderabad night was doing its version of the same business as Mumbai's — honking, far-off music, a city that doesn't fully stop.

"You couldn't have known that," Vipin said.

"I didn't know it. I saw it." Seventy-one, still holding. "Sleep. You'll need the reflexes."

He lay in the dark for another forty minutes. Then he slept.

---

The final was at Rajiv Gandhi International Stadium, Hyderabad — the ground with the slope that deceives you if you haven't played there before, measuring your yorker run-up from the wrong end. He'd played there twice. He knew the slope. He'd briefed his bowlers on it once, without elaboration, trusting the information to land.

CSK batted first and posted 162. Dhoni doing what he did — cool accumulation, footwork against pace so good it bordered on unfair, the shuffle across that made you recalibrate your yorker line in real time. The third CSK wicket fell to Vipin's off-cutter in the twelfth — batsman reading pace, getting nothing, top edge to fine leg. He carried the ball back to his mark with the internal quiet that had replaced the early-career aggression. Not calm exactly. Focused differently.

MI chased 163.

Finals are close when both teams have the quality to make every run earned. The partnership that mattered — his and Karthik's, overs eleven to seventeen — was built on ones and twos and one boundary off a Deepak Chahar length ball through mid-wicket, the weight transfer Rao had spent two years correcting. He was 38 off 29 when Karthik was out: twelve needed from twelve, four wickets in hand. Doable. Not comfortable.

The last over. Shardul Thakur bowling. MI needed seven.

Ball one: dot ball, outside off, left alone. Seven needed from five.
Ball two: he came down the track and missed a legbreak — the spin wasn't there, it was the variation, the slower one, and he was through it and the keeper gathered and didn't miss and he was already back in his crease. Somehow. His feet making a decision before his brain caught up. Five from five.
Ball three: a single, sliced to third man. Five from four.
Ball four: a wide. Five from four, with the wide as bonus: four from four.
Ball five: Karan Sharma hit it hard and it was going — it was going to the rope, definitely to the rope — and the fielder at long-on, Dhoni's placement, was too deep and too far right — and it stopped eight feet short of the rope. Two runs. Two from two.

Ball six: he was on strike.

He stood at the crease and looked at the field. Dhoni had moved deep cover to sweeper cover, which told him one thing: Dhoni expected him to hit straight. Dhoni was right. He was going to hit straight. The question was whether he was going to hit straight where Dhoni put the fielder or slightly left of where Dhoni put the fielder.

Shardul ran in. The ball was a yorker, well directed, aimed at the base of off stump.

He jammed his front foot down the pitch — too far, slightly — and dug it out. Not clean, not beautiful, the bottom edge of the bat taking most of it, which sent it not to the rope but to the gap between mid-on and long-on. The ball rolled. They ran two and then he was already looking at deep cover and it was going to be close — the throw was coming — and the second run was tight and the fielder's throw hit the keeper's gloves as his bat reached the crease.

The umpire consulted. Waited. The field went quiet in the particular way of fifty thousand people deciding whether to breathe.

Not out.

Two runs. Total tied.

Ball seven — the super ball, because the umpire had called a no ball on the previous one, which he had not heard in the noise.

He did not hear it. He would not learn about the no ball until the post-match review. He stood at the crease with the belief that MI needed zero from one, which was different from the reality of zero from one and a free hit — less pressure, somehow, though the arithmetic was identical.

Shardul's free hit was full, straight. He hit it. His arms knew what to do with full balls before his mind made a decision. The crack of bat on ball was the specific sound of a well-timed straight drive — the center of the face, the seam not catching the edge — and the ball went past the bowler and ran to the boundary.

Four.

MI won by two runs.

---

The dressing room after was the particular chaos of twenty-five people having the same large feeling in a small space with different ways of expressing it. Someone had a bucket of water that appeared from nowhere and was deployed on the nearest three people, which happened to be two of the support staff and the physio who had not been expecting to become wet. Karthik was crying — openly, no shame in it, the release of a young man who had needed the win for reasons the newspaper wouldn't cover. The rest of them were variously loud or silent or somewhere between.

Vipin stood near the wall for a moment. He had the armband in his hand — he'd taken it off when the last ball was hit, which he hadn't planned to do and didn't know why he'd done. He looked at it. Velcro. Sky blue. Gold logo.

Rohit found him. Rohit had been in the stands — watching, not playing, the MI patron rather than the captain now. He came in through the dressing room door and crossed to Vipin and didn't say anything, just put his hand on his shoulder for a moment, and Vipin looked at him and nodded, and that was the whole of it, which was enough.

The trophy ceremony happened on the ground. He stood at the podium and someone put the trophy in his hands — the real IPL trophy, which was heavier than it looked in photographs, the gold plate at the top thicker than the design suggested — and the cameras flashed and the crowd noise came from somewhere above and behind him and he lifted it.

He'd lifted batting trophies. Match awards. The ball he'd carried off after his first IPL wicket. He'd held things that mattered. This was different in a way he didn't have clean language for — not just the weight of it, but the weight of what it represented, which was twelve years of work and forty-three early mornings at Shivaji Park and the specific longing of the night at Wankhede in 2011 when he'd watched someone else lift something while he was in the stands.

He held it for the cameras. And then, without planning it — the same way he'd taken the armband off, the same instinct — he looked up at the stands.

They were in the fourth row, left side, where Suresh had placed them. Shraddha in the MI blue that she still wore with the studied casualness of someone who has reconciled herself to liking cricket, even though she would never fully admit to liking cricket. Shakti beside her — he'd come, flown from Mumbai, said nothing about it except: "I want to watch." Rao next to Shakti, the reading glasses on his forehead at this hour even with no reading material in sight, as if the glasses had stopped being about reading years ago. Ravi at the end of the row — big, loud, already standing, already doing the thing with his fist that he'd done since they were fifteen years old winning under-17 matches.

He pointed at them with the trophy. A brief, unmistakable gesture: this one is yours.

Shraddha saw it. Her hand went to her mouth.

Shakti saw it. The man sat very still for a moment, then nodded once — slow, deliberate — and looked at the pitch. Later, Shraddha would tell him that Shakti had said nothing on the drive back from the stadium. That he'd looked out the car window the whole way and when they got to the hotel had sat in the lobby for twenty minutes drinking tea. That when she'd asked him what he was thinking, he'd said: "That boy."

Just that. That boy.

---

He found a bathroom in the corridor between the dressing room and the players' tunnel, the functional kind with fluorescent lights and the specific smell of cricket ground facilities — disinfectant and something underneath it, the clay of pitches and the sweat of people who had worked. He ran cold water over his face and stood with his hands on the edge of the basin.

He was alone. He could hear the dressing room through the wall — Karthik's laugh specifically audible above everything. He had maybe four minutes.

He reached inside his shirt and took out the pendant.

"Rajveer," he said. Aloud. Not the way he'd done it at Wankhede in 2011 — that had been a first time, pendant held, silence let do the work. This was different. He knew how to do this now. "Kamla." His mother's name. He still could not say it without the specific sensation in his chest that was not grief and not recognition but something in between that had no available word.

"Your son won something tonight." He looked at himself in the mirror. The sweat still on his face from the chase, the jersey with the faint salt marks of an evening's work. A thirty-nine-year-old who had been left on temple stairs with a name and nothing else. "He was captain." He paused. "He read the field."

The fluorescent light above the mirror had a hum to it, the particular frequency of old fittings — forty-hertz, somewhere in there, the frequency that doesn't bother you until you're paying attention and then becomes the loudest thing in the room.

"Amma says he looked like you," he said to Rajveer. The image of the photograph on Sunita's steel cupboard — the boy in the field, hand up against the sun, the same jaw he saw in the mirror. "The way he stood. The hands." He looked at his own hands on the basin. Large. The specific width of Jaat hands that Sunita had described when she told him about the gloves. Too big for the gloves. "The hands are the same."

He held the pendant. It was warm — it was always warm from being against his skin, and this had once seemed strange to him and now seemed like the ordinary thing, the temperature of something that had been carried long enough to become part of the body.

The dressing room noise swelled briefly through the wall. Someone had opened a door.

"I'll tell you about the rest of it," he said. "When there's more to tell."

He tucked the pendant back. Ran the cold water one more time. Dried his face.

He was almost out the door when he noticed, in the fluorescent light, that there was a small cricket badge on the floor — the kind that falls off kit bags, the MI logo, gold-and-blue — pushed against the base of the wall. He stopped. Looked at it. Someone had dropped it and not known and might never know.

He left it there. He didn't know whose it was. Some things don't get resolved.

He went back to his team.

---

They flew back to Mumbai the next morning, the eleven o'clock flight, the kind of team travel that has a specific quality — the come-down from the night before, people sleeping in odd positions across the seats, the trophy in a padded case in the overhead, Karthik still intermittently emotional every four or five hours when it hit him again. Vipin had the window seat by choice.

Somewhere over the Western Ghats, when the land below had become the brown-and-green pattern of the Deccan plateau giving way to the coastal incline, he took out his phone and opened the Sudoku app. The same app. Three years, same app, same obsession with a logic puzzle he played while finding it insufficiently relaxing to count as rest.

He stared at the grid.

He was captain. He'd won. He was twenty-nine and he'd won the IPL as captain and the India squad was in his future and the armband was in his kit bag and Shraddha was on the flight three rows ahead of him with the window seat — she liked watching the cloud formation at this altitude, which she'd told him about at length on a different flight to a different city at the beginning of something.

Twenty-nine. The number sat in him like a room that was bigger than it looked from the outside.

He closed the Sudoku app without making a move.

Below, the Deccan plateau gave way to the coastal escarpment and then to the silver-grey line of the sea, and then to Mumbai — the city coming up from below the way it always came up when you flew in from the east, the congested miracle of it, the vertical ambitions and the water on three sides and the specific morning light Mumbai made when it was trying. The city that had left him on its temple stairs and then, when he refused to disappear, had given him everything it had.

He put his forehead against the window.

He thought about Shraddha. About the ring he'd been looking at on and off for three months in the window of a jeweler on Hill Road — the same ring, the one with the specific setting that reminded him of nothing he could name except rightness. He had not said the question out loud to anyone, not even Ravi, because saying it made it real and real things could fail.

He thought about the drawer in his flat. The gloves, wrapped in cotton. The pendant against his chest. The eleven-year-old note that said *I want them to say my name.*

They said his name last night. Fifty thousand of them.

The plane descended toward the airport, the runway appearing over the water, the approach so low the sea seemed close enough to touch.

*After this,* he thought. *There's something else after this.*

He didn't know exactly what. But he was beginning to think he knew the shape of it.

*End of Chapter 36*
