# Chapter 11: The Dressing Room

The shower turned on wrong and hit him with cold water at full pressure from four different directions at once.

He stood in it for two full seconds — the shock was so total it bypassed reaction — and then stepped backward into the glass door, which he hadn't realized was behind him, which was a sliding door, which slid open and deposited him onto the bathroom tiles in a wet heap.

He sat on the tiles and looked up at the rain shower, which was now producing a fine mist across the entire bathroom. There were four showerheads. He had turned what he thought was the temperature knob and it was, in fact, the pressure knob. The temperature knob was on the other side. He had not found this intuitive. He found very little about this bathroom intuitive. The taps were lever-style, which he'd seen before, but there were seven of them, and he'd been working on a system of hypotheses about which did what.

He sat on the tiles and laughed.

It came out of him the same way it had with Keshav and the River crack — before the decision to laugh, already in the room, a real laugh with full sound that echoed in the marble bathroom. He was alone in a bathroom that cost more per night than the construction site overhang where he'd slept at eight years old, and he had fallen out of a shower, and he was laughing on expensive tiles in an expensive hotel belonging to the Mumbai Indians cricket franchise, which had paid forty lakh rupees for the right to have him on this bathroom floor.

He got up. Found the temperature knob. Adjusted to warm. Got back in.

The shower was, with the correct settings, the best thing he had experienced in years. He stood under it for fifteen minutes, which was twelve minutes longer than any shower he'd taken in his life. When he finally turned it off, the bathroom was filled with steam and the mirror was completely fogged and the towels on the heated rack were warm in a way that suggested the rack had been running since before he arrived, which meant someone had turned it on for him, which meant someone had been thinking about whether he would want warm towels, which he had never in his life had occasion to think about as a thing that happened.

He dried off. Put on clean clothes. Stood at the window.

Mumbai spread below him like a map of itself — not a city anymore but a model, the distances reduced and made legible from this height. He could see Shivaji Park from the seventeenth floor. He was fairly sure he could see it — the green, a bit south and east, the familiar shape of the grounds, reduced to a patch. He put his hand on the glass. Seventeen floors. He had stood at the boundary rope of that park at thirteen years old and watched organized cricket from the outside and thought: that's where the game actually is. The game was seventeen floors up now, apparently. Or maybe it was still there, in the green patch, and he was here by mistake.

He counted the threads in the bedsheet: somewhere over three hundred. He made a note to look up what thread count meant and why it mattered, then decided this was not important information and deleted the note from his mental list.

He put the rubber ball on the windowsill. Sat on the bed. Tried lying down, found the mattress was doing something to his lower back that wasn't pain exactly but was a kind of excess of comfort he didn't have the vocabulary for. He sat back up.

He explored the room the way he explored all new spaces — systematically, from left to right, noting what was where. The writing desk with hotel stationery, the pen branded with the Taj logo. The television with a remote control that had thirty-two buttons, of which he would probably use four. A room service menu in a leather folder: dal makhani, two hundred and eighty rupees. He put the menu down.

The wardrobe had a safe. He read the instructions, found them simple, set a code that was the number of cracks in the storage room ceiling — 23 — and immediately thought this was the most unnecessary use of a four-digit code since the concept of codes was invented. He had nothing to put in a safe. He closed the safe. Left it there.

The minibar: small bottles arranged in rows, chocolate bars, a can of imported juice from a country he'd never been to. He read the price list on the inside of the door: the juice was four hundred rupees. He closed the minibar.

He stood at the window again. The hotel soap was in the bathroom — white, expensive, odorless in the way of things that had had the character removed from them to make them suitable for everyone. He had picked it up in the bathroom, earlier. Held it. The smell was nothing. The pink temple soap had smelled like everything — institutional cleaning fluid and cheap perfume and the specific association of every morning for seven years. He had put the hotel soap down.

He sat on the bed. Tried lying down again. Still the thing with the mattress. He sat back up.

He put the rubber ball on the windowsill and looked at the sea.

Outside the window, the sea.

---

The team meeting was in a conference room on the third floor.

Vipin arrived two minutes early, which he did everywhere. The room was already filling — coaches, support staff, franchise officials, and then the players. The players entered with the particular energy of people who had been in team rooms before, who had done this circuit of franchise cricket and international tours, who understood where to sit and who to greet and how the room worked. Vipin stood near the wall and watched them assemble, because watching a room was the first thing you did before you knew your place in it.

He knew the faces from television, from newspapers, from the websites he'd been reading obsessively since the auction. He matched each face to its information — height, handedness, preferred shot, pace (for the fast bowlers), recent form. This was what he did with all new information: built a database, organized it by relevance.

Then Sachin Tendulkar walked in.

The problem was that Vipin's database had an entry for Sachin Tendulkar that was enormous. It occupied more space in his cricket understanding than any other player — fifteen years of watching, from a chai-stall TV screen to a VCD player in the equipment room to newspaper clippings to the first time he'd understood what footwork meant by watching someone else's. His entire technical vocabulary of batting had been assembled in part by watching Sachin Tendulkar and building reverse-engineered explanations for why things worked the way they did.

And then Sachin Tendulkar was across the room, shorter than Vipin had imagined — five foot five, the same height as Rao, which was somehow the most startling fact — sitting down with a glass of water, talking quietly to the team manager. Just a man. Just: sitting there. The most famous cricketer alive was across a conference table and he was just sitting there.

Two seconds. Vipin's body was completely still for two seconds, which had not happened at the auction, which had not happened at the Ranji camp, which had not happened in any situation he could recall since age nine when Bhai had first spoken to him and the weight of the moment registered before the brain could classify it.

It broke when Sachin looked up. Across the room. Their eyes met. And Sachin Tendulkar gave him a nod — a small, precise nod, the nod of someone who acknowledged new people in rooms without making the acknowledgment a performance.

The world resumed. Vipin nodded back. Found a seat.

The players assembled. Harbhajan Singh dropped into the seat two down — Vipin had seen Harbhajan on TV for years and the TV version had not prepared him for the physical version, which was louder and larger and somehow warmer than the broadcast image suggested. Harbhajan looked at Vipin with the specific attention of someone assessing a new person's potential usefulness and entertainment value simultaneously.

"Young one," Harbhajan said. Not a question.

"Seventeen," Vipin said.

"Chaudhary, right? Mumbai kid?"

"Yes."

"Good. Local boy. Team needs local boys." Harbhajan turned to say something to the player on his other side. The assessment apparently complete. Vipin had passed or been filed for later.

Lasith Malinga was at the far end of the table — the slingy-armed Sri Lankan fast bowler who was, by Vipin's calculations, the best death bowler in limited-overs cricket alive. He was sitting quietly, watching the room with the focused stillness of someone who had found a resting state that was also alertness. He met Vipin's glance once and looked away. Not unfriendly. Just: not yet interested.

Shaun Pollock came in next — the South African veteran, tall, unhurried, the look of someone for whom team rooms had long since lost the quality of being new. He sat and opened a notebook. He was taking notes at a team meeting, which meant he was still treating each situation as information to be captured, which explained in part why he was still playing at thirty-four. Vipin noted this. Put it in the category of things he intended to still be doing at thirty-four.

A young bowler from Karnataka — one of the other uncapped picks, name of Suresh — sat down next to Vipin and introduced himself with the slightly excessive friendliness of someone who was also new and also looking for structural allies. They were in the same situation. Vipin was friendly back. Noted that Suresh's eyes went to Sachin three times in the first two minutes, which Vipin's own eyes had also done but he'd stopped after the second time because Sachin was just sitting there and you couldn't learn anything from watching someone sit.

The coach opened the meeting. Schedule, structure, the philosophy of the franchise. Training began at eight-thirty, which was earlier than the previous MI coaching staff had scheduled. There would be video analysis sessions three times a week. Diet and fitness protocols were attached in a printed document distributed by the trainer — Vipin took one and read it under the table during the subsequent budget presentation, because the budget presentation was not going to teach him anything he could use but the nutrition document was.

Vipin listened to everything and took mental notes — not just the cricket information, but the dynamics. Who deferred to whom. Who interrupted whom and whether the interruption was accepted. Who the coach addressed when making a general point, which was usually Sachin or Harbhajan — the informal poles of the room. Malinga, he noticed, was never addressed directly; the coaching staff spoke near him and let him absorb. He suspected Malinga spoke limited Hindi and the coaching staff spoke limited English and the resulting protocol was a respectful mutual pretense.

The landscape of authority in a team was not the same as the hierarchy on paper: there were informal currents, the real power structures, and he was learning them from the ground floor.

When the ice-breaker segment came — team-building, each player to share something about themselves — Vipin waited until his turn and then said: "I'm from Mumbai. I bowl fast. I'm vegetarian, so if we're sharing rooms, your chicken is safe."

The room laughed. Harbhajan laughed the loudest, a bark of a laugh that Vipin would come to learn was his natural frequency. Malinga looked up, and something at the corner of his mouth moved — not a smile exactly, a registration. Sachin Tendulkar, at the far end of the room, did not laugh. But the corner of his mouth — the same corner — moved.

Vipin filed this. The corner of Sachin Tendulkar's mouth, moving. He was going to replay this for the next week.

---

The nets were at the DY Patil Stadium facility — better than anything Vipin had trained in, better than the MCA facility, better than the Wankhede practice grounds. The wickets were prepared daily. The bowling machines were calibrated by an analyst who had a laptop and a specific face for when the numbers were wrong. The throwdown specialist — a compact man named Jadhav, forty, who had been doing this for fifteen years — greeted the players with the flat professionalism of someone who had seen every level of talent and had opinions about none of it.

Vipin bowled first. The run-up felt different on a proper surface — not the Shivaji Park ground, not the age-group pitches, a surface with genuine pace and bounce engineered into it. He ran in and released and the ball came out at 140 kph, good length, the natural line he used when he wasn't thinking about it.

The senior batsman at the other end defended. Well. No drama.

Vipin ran in again. This time he thought about the last four strides — the acceleration technique Kedar had shown him at the Ranji camp, the specific kinetic chain. 145 kph, moving in late. The batsman was beaten for pace and the ball thudded into the keeper's gloves.

Third ball: he went short. 148 kph. The ball hit the surface and lifted — not sharply, it wasn't a lethal delivery, but it forced the batsman back and high and the pull was hurried, a mis-hit to fine leg. The batting coach, sitting with a clipboard, made a note.

Then Jadhav set up to throw. He was loading for 145 kph — Vipin could tell from the way he was preparing his run, the angle of approach. The first ball came in at 144, full, just outside off. Vipin stepped forward and drove. The net shook.

"Again," Vipin said.

Jadhav sent another at 146. Vipin drove it harder. Same spot. The net had a dent now, a slight pocket in the fabric where the balls had impacted.

He settled into it — the temporal perception doing what it did when he was properly loaded, the ball arriving early in his perception, the information coming before the instinct required. He hit eight consecutive balls to the same area of the net. Seven to the covers, one straight, all with the kind of timing that made a sound different from a mis-hit — the clean sound, the sound Vipin had known since the leather ball at the maidan when he was eleven.

He stopped because Jadhav stopped, not because he wanted to.

The bowling coach — a man named Ramesh Dalvi, who had played 14 Tests for India in the early nineties as a fast-medium bowler before a knee ended the career — had been watching from the side. He was a compact man with the economical movement of former fast bowlers, the ones whose bodies had learned to conserve energy after years of the most physically demanding action in cricket. He had a clipboard and he had not written on it in the last five minutes.

"How old?" he asked, to nobody in particular.

The MI batting coach, beside him: "Seventeen."

Dalvi made a sound. Not approval — processing. He wrote something on the clipboard. Put it under his arm. Watched Jadhav set up for the throwdown sequence. Made another note when the first drive hit the net.

He didn't speak to Vipin. He didn't need to — the notes were the speech. Vipin understood this. You didn't tell a talent you'd noticed it. You wrote it down and changed the plan. The changed plan was the compliment.

He was toweling off his hands when he became aware of someone standing nearby. The sound of another net session still going. He looked up.

Sachin Tendulkar was padding up at the adjacent net. He had been there — Vipin wasn't sure for how long. He had watched three of those eight balls. Vipin had not known, because when he was batting in that state he didn't know anything outside the space between his feet and the end of the pitch.

Sachin finished adjusting his pad. Picked up his bat. Made to turn back to his own net.

Then stopped. Looked at Vipin.

"Good hands, young man."

Four words. Said the way Sachin said things — precisely, without performance, as if the words were exactly the right words and no others were available. Then he walked to his net and took guard and the throwdown specialist moved into position and the session continued and Sachin Tendulkar was facing 150 kph throwdowns with the focused quiet of a man doing ordinary work.

Vipin stood with his towel and heard the four words again. And again. He had read, once, that Sachin had told someone their technique was good, and the someone had said it was the most meaningful compliment of his career. He had thought this was hyperbole. He now understood it was not. When someone who had done what Sachin had done with a cricket bat said *good hands* to you, the words carried a weight proportional to the source. This was not a certificate of excellence. It was a direction.

He picked up his bat. Went back to the net. Asked Jadhav for more.

Later, in the lunch break, he sat alone with a glass of water and went over the session in his head — not the good things, those were accounted for, but the gaps. Two deliveries where he'd been slightly early, weight transfer fractionally forward before he'd read the line. His hands had compensated. Above a certain pace threshold, the feet needed to be right or the hands weren't fast enough to cover it.

He needed footwork coaching. Not Rao's expertise — Rao was form, placement, shot selection. This was footwork against extreme pace, a different specialization. He filed this as a question for Dalvi, then filed whether it was appropriate to ask Dalvi as a question for Chetan.

A practice ball rolled past his feet. He picked it up. Mumbai Indians logo in gold on one hemisphere. He turned it in his hands, studying the seam. Then, when the lunch session broke and people moved to the changing room, he put the ball in his kit bag.

---

The team dinner that evening was in a private room at the hotel — a long table, covered dishes, the specific excess of franchise hospitality. The players ate and talked in the way of people who knew each other or were finding the path to it. Vipin sat next to a domestic player from UP whose name was Chetan and who was not famous and who was in the same position Vipin was, which made them natural allies in the geography of the table.

The food arrived. Vipin looked at the spread — a buffet of a kind he had never encountered, which was not because the food was unusual but because there was so much of it and all of it was good and none of it had a price visible. He took a plate.

He loaded it. The vegetarian section alone: two kinds of dal, a paneer dish, mixed vegetables with cashews, rice, three types of bread, raita, something he didn't recognize that turned out to be a saag preparation with fenugreek. He put some of each on his plate.

He ate the plate. It was excellent — the fenugreek in the saag had been handled correctly, which he knew because the pandit had cooked with fenugreek and the pandit had been precise about its bitterness requiring patience. He registered this fact and then registered that he'd registered it, which was the kind of recursive observation that usually preceded a thought he wasn't ready to have. He set the observation aside and went back.

He was halfway through the second plate when he became aware of what he was doing.

He had been loading plates since he was eight years old. When food was available, you loaded plates. When food was going to not be available, you loaded plates preemptively. You ate what was there while it was there. This was not a philosophy — it was a physical habit built over years into his hands and his body's relationship with the act of taking food. You didn't pause to assess abundance. You took.

He looked at the second plate. Then looked around the table. Harbhajan was eating with appetite but without excess — a normal amount, the amount of a man who ate regularly. Malinga was picking precisely at a small plate. Chetan, next to him, had one plate and was about two-thirds through it. Nobody else had gone back.

He put the second plate down.

The food was still there. It was going to still be there. If he wanted it after he finished this plate, he could get more. The hotel was not going to run out of paneer at nine PM. He understood this, had understood it since walking through the lobby, but the understanding was in his head and the plate-loading habit was in his hands and the two had not yet been introduced.

He ate the second plate slowly. Made himself eat slowly. Tasted it this time — the fenugreek in the saag, the specific quality of the cashews in the mixed vegetables. Food that deserved tasting.

He left a small amount of rice on the plate. An act of deliberate practice. Chetan noticed nothing. Harbhajan was telling a story at the other end of the table and the room was laughing and Vipin ate the rest of his dinner and told nobody about any of it.

---

He called Rao at ten.

"Sachin said I have good hands," he said, instead of hello. He had not planned to lead with this. It came out.

Rao was quiet for a moment. "When."

"In the nets. After the session. He was watching. He said: good hands, young man."

Another silence. Longer. Vipin waited it out, which was what you did with Rao's silences.

"He's right," Rao said. "You do."

His voice — Vipin had heard Rao's voice in hundreds of conversations, in training, in committee hearings, in the car, over the phone. He had heard it measured and careful and flat and dry and occasionally cold. He had heard it say important things in the same register it said ordinary things. He had heard it break, exactly once, when Rao arrived at Shivaji Park after the MCA registration hearing and stood on the boundary rope for five minutes without speaking before he said anything at all.

He heard it now with something in it he didn't have a name for. Not pride — the word was too simple. Something more structural than that. Like: a load-bearing wall, and the load it was bearing had shifted into a different distribution, and the wall was still there, doing its job, but the mathematics of it had changed.

"Uncle," Vipin said.

"Get some sleep," Rao said. "Camp starts early."

"I know."

"Chaudhary."

"Yes."

"...Good."

He said it like he'd been collecting it for a while. Like it had been sitting in a specific drawer in his head, waiting for the correct moment. Vipin heard the weight of those four years in that one word — the dawn sessions, the enrollment forms, the committee hearings, the cold chai on the boundary bench, the driving of a borrowed car with a kid who was learning what it meant to belong somewhere.

"Theek hai," Vipin said. Fine.

He hung up. Sat on the bed. Took out the pendant and held it. The window was dark now, the city lit below in its nighttime configuration. The sea was invisible from here — just the darkness where it had been during the day.

He thought: she left me a name and she left me gold and somewhere in the thirty-four years between then and now she has a life that continued. Does she know tonight? Is there a version of tonight where she knows? Is there a newspaper on a table in a flat in Mumbai or Haryana or anywhere, open to the back page where a small article says *Vipin Chaudhary — MI*, and someone who looks like me is reading it and saying nothing?

He put the pendant down.

He didn't know. He had never known. He filed it the way he filed the things that were true but not actionable — the file was full by now, had been for years, the file was a significant portion of the person he was.

He opened the minibar. Looked at the contents: small bottles, chocolate bars, a can of imported juice. He looked at the price list on the inside of the door. The juice cost four hundred rupees. He closed the minibar.

Drank water from the tap. It tasted like hotel water — filtered, slightly chemical, not unpleasant. He drank a full glass standing at the sink.

He went to bed. On top of the covers, fully dressed. His body didn't trust the comfort yet — the bed was too much, the room was too much, the seventeen floors of altitude was too much. He lay on top of the covers and closed his eyes and the air conditioning ran and somewhere in the hotel a door closed and the sound was different from the park sounds, the car horns from the road, the groundskeeper's cough.

He listened to the difference. Counted nothing. Just: listened.

The city outside was the same city. He was in it differently.

Somewhere in the new quiet, he fell asleep.

In the morning, he would face the throwdown specialist again. He was going to ask for 150.

He had counted the ceiling tiles: forty-eight. Less than the MCA office. More than the storage room, which had no tiles, only the crack. He wondered briefly if the Taj maintenance staff had a River crack equivalent somewhere in the building — some persistent structural marking that came back every time you addressed it. He decided probably not. Probably the Taj just fixed things.

He closed his eyes.

The pendant was warm against his chest. The MI practice ball was at the bottom of his kit bag. The piece of scorebook paper — *I want them to say my name* — was in the drawer of the room, the one piece of furniture he'd immediately commandeered. Tomorrow he would put the birth certificate in the room safe with the combination that was also the number of branches on the River crack. It seemed right to keep those two documents in proximity. Both of them were about the same thing: the gap between where you came from and what you were permitted to claim.
