# Chapter 10: The Last Room

The morning after the auction, the storage room smelled exactly the same.

Vipin lay on the cot and took inventory of this. The smell was concrete and something metallic from the pipes in the wall and the faint sweetness of the groundskeeper's agarbatti that drifted under the door from wherever he burned it in the evenings. He'd stopped noticing this smell somewhere in the second year. It was just air. But now he noticed it.

The River crack: north to south, twenty-three branches. He counted them before he was fully awake, muscle memory.

He was, by the financial reckoning of the IPL, worth one hundred thousand American dollars.

He lay there and let this sit alongside the smell and the crack and the particular texture of the cot's blanket against his cheek. The blanket was old — not threadbare, just old, worn soft from years of use, faded from something dark to something in between. The groundskeeper had given it to him in the second winter. No explanation. Just: it appeared at the door. He had been using it for three years and had never asked where it came from.

He got up. Ran his laps. The park was the park — the morning walkers, the early coaching groups, the dogs making their territorial circuits. Nothing had changed in the geography. He ran his second lap and a man with a stroller gave him the same polite nod he'd given Vipin every morning for six months, a nod that communicated: *you run, I walk, we share the path, this is the social contract.* The man had no idea. None of the morning walkers had any idea. The world ran its morning and Vipin ran his laps and nothing in the air suggested that yesterday a hotel ballroom full of billionaires had said his name.

He stopped at the end of the second lap. Caught his breath. Put his hand in his pocket and felt the rubber ball. Squeezed once. Then went to shower.

---

Ravi called at eight-fifteen.

"Vada pav," he said, instead of hello.

"I told you. My money hasn't arrived."

"Tere paisa ka wait kaun karega?" (Who's going to wait for your money?) "I'll pay. You owe me one vada pav. Write it down. First one you owe me."

They met at the stall near the park's eastern gate — a permanent fixture, the kind of Mumbai vada pav stall that had been in the same spot so long it had become infrastructure. The owner knew Vipin by face; had given him credit twice during the street years and never mentioned it again. Ravi was already there when Vipin arrived, two vada pavs in hand, chutney dripping.

"Mumbai Indians," Ravi said, handing one over. "Bhai. Mumbai. Indians."

"I know."

"Sachin Tendulkar is going to be your teammate."

"I know."

"Do you know what that *means?*"

Vipin bit into the vada pav. It was good. It had always been good. He was struck by the sudden thought that this vada pav was the exact same thing it had been yesterday before the auction and would be tomorrow after and the day after that, and the price was seven rupees, and he had a contract worth forty lakh, and the vada pav did not know the difference and neither did his stomach.

"I don't know what it means yet," he said. "Ask me in a month."

Ravi laughed — his good laugh, the full one that didn't care if people looked. A few people at the stall glanced at them. Ravi with his naturally loud presence, Vipin eating in the focused way he always ate.

"I'm in Kolkata," Ravi said. "Knight Riders. KKR."

"I know. I'm happy for you."

"Shah Rukh Khan's team."

"I know."

"Shah Rukh Khan, Chaudhary. The man from *Dilwale Dulhania.*"

"I know who Shah Rukh Khan is."

"I just want you to appreciate the poetry." Ravi finished his vada pav in three bites. "We started in a park where the boundary rope was someone's old dupatta tied between two sticks. Now we're going to play in actual stadiums." He paused. "With actual boundary ropes. That are not someone's dupatta."

"I remember the dupatta," Vipin said. "It was yellow."

"It was someone's mother's dupatta and she was not happy about it."

They stood at the stall for another twenty minutes. Not talking about anything important. The way the morning birds were noisy in the neem trees. A recent Bollywood song coming from a car stereo. The best sequence of deliveries Vipin had ever bowled and where, in his own judgment, the third ball had gone wrong even if the batsman got out. Ravi had a theory about cover drives that Vipin mostly agreed with but not entirely.

It was the most normal twenty minutes he'd had in three months.

When Ravi left — he had a train to catch, his own camp, his own world now — Vipin stood at the stall alone and ate a second vada pav, which he paid for himself with coins from his training allowance. Seven rupees. He had forty lakh in a contract he hadn't signed yet and he paid for vada pav with coins.

He walked back to the storage room.

---

Bhai's man found him on a Wednesday.

Not Bhai himself — Bhai didn't make direct contact for things like this, he sent someone. A boy Vipin half-recognized from Dongri, maybe twenty now, in the kind of plain clothes that communicated deliberate plainness. He appeared at the park entrance one morning when Vipin was finishing his run.

"Bhai wants to congratulate you," the boy said.

"Tell him thank you."

"He wants your new number. The IPL people will give you a phone, he says."

"When I have one, I'll call him."

The boy looked at him. Trying to read whether this was a delay or a refusal. Vipin gave him nothing to read. He had learned this from Bhai, which was either ironic or fitting.

"He said — he said to remind you. He was there when nobody else was."

"I remember," Vipin said.

The boy nodded. Left. Vipin watched him go and then looked at the park entrance, the familiar gate, the trees beyond it where the morning walkers moved.

He thought about the weight of a sentence. *Remember who was here.* Bhai had said it two years ago. Vipin had been twelve kilometers away from that rooftop when he was sold, and the sentence had been ticking, and now it had activated, and Bhai's version of congratulations was a messenger and a reminder of a debt.

He thought about what forty lakh looked like to Bhai — the man who had taught him to calculate value before it was visible, who had filed *this is an investment* in 2000 when a ten-year-old hit tennis balls over compound walls. Bhai had been patient for eight years. Bhai was now presenting his invoice.

Vipin went back to the storage room and picked up the rubber ball.

He didn't not call Bhai. He just didn't call him yet. He was still figuring out the terms of that conversation. In the meantime, the ball. Forty-two squeezes — one per chapter left in the story of his childhood, a number he arrived at without knowing why.

---

Five days after the auction, he went back to Matunga.

Not to the temple — he wasn't ready for the temple. But to the street outside it. He took the local from Dadar and got off at Matunga station and walked the ten minutes to the street he remembered, which was still the street he remembered, because streets in Mumbai do not change the way people do. The same buildings. The same pattern of shops — medical store, STD booth, a restaurant he had eaten at twice in the temple years when the pandit took him for a treat. The restaurant was still there. He looked at it through the window. Different tables, possibly the same tablecloths in the same shade of institutional orange.

The temple was at the end of the lane. He didn't go in. He stood outside and looked at the entrance — the stone steps, the bell, the marigold garland on the frame that was replaced daily and was currently at about day three, starting to brown at the edges. Two women came out while he was standing there. An old man with a small tray. The new pujari's schedule was different from the pandit's — the evening prayers were earlier, or later, he couldn't remember exactly how it had been.

He stood there for a while. Counted the steps: nine. He had known it was nine. He'd counted them daily for seven years.

He didn't go in. He wasn't sure what he would say or do or feel in there, and he had learned in the last few months that some situations required advance knowledge of your own reaction before you entered them. He didn't have it here. So he stood on the street and looked at the steps and the bell and the brownish marigold and then turned around and walked back to the station.

On the train back, a man with a newspaper sat next to him. The newspaper was the day's edition. On the back page, a small article about the IPL pre-season: the player acquisitions, the franchise strategies. Mumbai Indians were mentioned. He read the section about MI without looking for his own name but his name was there: "...all-rounder Vipin Chaudhary, picked up from the U-19 circuit..."

He folded the paper back. The man hadn't noticed him reading it. He looked like a thousand other passengers on the 5:40 Dadar local.

This was the last time he would be that.

Rao came on a Saturday with two cups of chai and a notebook.

The chai was from the stall near the park, carried carefully, the cups already starting to cool by the time he handed one to Vipin. Vipin drank it. The notebook, Rao put on the cot between them.

"Cricket camp is March 3rd," Rao said. "Hotel check-in the evening before."

"Which hotel?"

"Taj. Marine Drive."

Vipin processed this. He had walked past the Taj on Marine Drive twice in his life, both times by accident, both times registering it as a different species of building than the ones he inhabited. Glass and stone. Doormen in white uniforms. Cars outside that cost more than some buildings.

"I'll figure it out," he said.

"There are things to know before you go." Rao opened the notebook. He had made notes, which was Rao's version of preparing a speech. "The schedule will be fixed. Breakfast at seven, nets from eight-thirty. You don't miss either of these."

"I know."

"You'll be in the same building as Sachin Tendulkar."

"I know."

"You will not treat this casually."

"Uncle." Vipin looked at him. "I know."

Rao turned a page. "Media will approach. Journalists. Not now — you're not famous enough now. But there will be team press events. They will ask about your background."

Vipin waited.

"You can say what you want. But I want you to think about what you want to say before they ask you. Have an answer ready. Don't let them write your story for you."

This was the most Rao had ever said at once about something other than cricket. Vipin heard the weight of it — five years of coaching a boy who had no documents, no parents, no verifiable past, who had been registered by an enrollment bending interpretation of the rules and had played through a system that technically should have caught him at several points. Vipin had been a liability on paper for years. Now he was an asset. The story had switched, but stories didn't switch cleanly.

"What do I say?"

"The truth. Some version of it. You grew up in Mumbai. You trained at Shivaji Park. Coach Rao found you." A pause. "You don't owe them the whole truth. But the truth you tell has to be consistent."

"What about the storage room?"

Rao was quiet for a moment. "If they find out, it's a good story. If you volunteer it, it becomes a symbol before you've played a single match. I'd wait."

Vipin nodded. He had already thought about this — not as strategy, but as the habit of managing information he'd built on the streets. You told people what they needed to know. Nothing extra. Nothing that gave them leverage.

"Bhai sent someone," Vipin said.

Rao looked at him. His face changed in the subtle way it did when something required processing before response. "When."

"Wednesday."

"What did he want?"

"My new number. To congratulate me."

"And?"

"I told him I'd call when I had a number."

Rao looked at the notebook. Turned a page, though there was nothing on it. The reading glasses came off his forehead and he held them in his hand, cleaning them on his shirt. This was his processing habit — the glasses came down when the subject required it.

"Bhai is not a problem you need to solve today," he said finally.

"I know."

"But he's not a problem that goes away."

"I know that too."

They drank their chai. The park sounds came through the storage room door — a coaching session in progress on the outer pitches, someone calling instruction, the thump of ball on bat. The same sounds Vipin had been hearing for four years from this room. He realized he had a count for those sounds somewhere in his head — a running tally of specific sessions he'd listened to through this door when he couldn't sleep or didn't want to be outside. He hadn't counted them intentionally. His brain had just filed them.

"How many chapatis can I eat at the team dinner?" he said.

Rao looked at him. "What?"

"I've never eaten at a hotel. If they have chapatis. How many is normal."

"As many as you want. It's included in—" Rao stopped. Then something passed across his face that Vipin had seen twice in four years — the crack in the composure, the thing underneath. "Eat as much as you want. Take seconds. You've earned it."

Vipin finished his chai. "I know," he said, for the fourth time. But this time he meant it differently.

---

The week before camp, he told Rao about the debt in different words.

Not Bhai specifically — the debt. The thing that lived in the gap between what he owed people and what he could pay. He was trying to think about money in practical terms, which was a new skill. He had never had money before in quantities that required thinking about. Now he was going to have some, and the thinking was going to start, and he wanted to do it correctly before it was required.

"The contract money," he said. "When does it arrive?"

"End of the season, typically. Some advance before the tournament. The agent will handle the disbursement — you'll need an agent, by the way. The MI management will connect you with their preferred representatives."

"I want to give some to the groundskeeper."

"You can't," Rao said.

"Why not?"

"Because Keshav is a government employee and it would create complications and because he would refuse it and you'd both be embarrassed." A pause. "The ticket you mentioned was the right idea. Something that isn't money."

Vipin thought about this. "The pandit's temple. Is it still there?"

"In Matunga? I believe so."

"Can you give something to them? From me." He didn't say how much. He didn't know what was right. The pandit had fed him, housed him, taught him to read. These were things that had prices he couldn't calculate and he didn't want to insult them by trying. "Something that doesn't have my name on it."

Rao looked at him. The reading glasses came down. He put them on the table.

"Yes," he said. "I'll handle it."

They didn't talk about Bhai. Vipin didn't bring him up again and Rao didn't either. Some problems you didn't solve — you just kept them at the correct distance until something changed.

The week before camp, he cleaned the storage room.

Not because it was required — the groundskeeper kept the building, it wasn't Vipin's to maintain. He cleaned it because it was his. Because for four years it had been the only space in the world that answered to him, and he wasn't leaving without acknowledging that.

He swept the floor with the broom that lived behind the door. The dust came up and he let it settle and swept again. The cot he folded and leaned against the wall — it wasn't his to take, it belonged to the park. The bat he wrapped in cloth. The drawer he examined.

He hadn't opened the drawer all at once before — had only ever added things to it. Now he looked at what was in it.

The batting gloves: seven left gloves and six right gloves, worn leather, mismatched, from eleven different senior players he'd been close enough to over the last two years to collect discarded kit from. None of them had known he kept the gloves. He had worn them wrong-handed to the nets sometimes, just to understand how different hands moved through the same grip.

The newspaper clipping from page twelve. Three words: *Vipin Chaudhary — MI.* Already yellowing at the edges, though it was only three weeks old.

The birth certificate: pale green government paper, the specific institutional green that the state of Maharashtra used for things that needed to communicate permanence without being able to deliver it. *Father: Unknown. Mother: Unknown.*

The folded piece of scorebook paper: *I want them to say my name.*

A rubber ball — red, not the one he carried. An older one, split along one seam, from the first real leather ball he'd been given at Shivaji Park. He'd kept it when it went out of use without deciding to. It had just ended up in the drawer.

An India U-19 cap. Not the one he'd been given officially — that one was in his kit bag. This was a spare, unclaimed from a tournament, that the manager had said he could keep.

He looked at all of it. Did not count it. Just looked.

He thought about Bhai's man at the gate. *Remember who was here.* He thought about the pandit's slippers on stone. He thought about Ravi laughing at the vada pav stall with chutney on his shirt. He thought about Rao's cold chai and the way the reading glasses worked as a timer for important thoughts.

Five people in seventeen years. He'd counted them the night before the auction and the number had felt like a small number without a reference point. He had one now. The hotel he was going to had a team of twenty-two. The team had a support staff of thirty. There would be media, management, franchise officials, sponsors, fans. By the end of the IPL tournament, the number of people who knew his name would be in the tens of thousands. He would acquire five new people in the first week.

He didn't know if this was a comfort or a threat. Probably both.

He put the India U-19 cap on top of the kit bag.

Then he packed what was his: the bat in its cloth, the kit bag with the official cap and his two changes of clothes and the pendant — he never took the pendant off, so packing it wasn't distinct from packing himself. The rubber ball went in his pocket. He took the folded piece of scorebook paper and put it in his shirt pocket.

The gloves he left in the drawer. The drawer he left in the room.

He stood at the door for a moment. The River crack ran its familiar line across the ceiling. Twenty-three branches. He didn't count them this time. He already knew.

---

On his last night in the storage room, he didn't sleep.

This was different from the pre-auction insomnia. That had been anxiety — the grinding, unproductive kind that the counting was supposed to manage. This was something else. He lay on the cot and was awake in a clean way, alert, aware of each sound the park made in the dark. The trains. A cat that had been coming around for the last month, its footsteps on the roof of the adjacent building at irregular intervals. The groundskeeper's cough through the wall — forty-three seconds, then sixty-one, then irregular, which meant Keshav was asleep.

He thought about the first night in this room.

Rao had arranged it through the groundskeeper — "he can use the storage room, if that's acceptable" — and the groundskeeper had looked at Vipin for three seconds and said nothing and turned and unlocked the door. The room had had a broom in it, two paint cans, and a length of rope he never worked out the purpose of. He'd moved these to one side and laid cardboard on the floor. This had been better than his construction site overhang because it had a door that locked from the inside. The lock was the luxury.

Now the cot. The blanket that wasn't his. The drawer built into the wall that he had incrementally made his archive. The crack in the ceiling that he had watched change and not change for four years — two of its branches had extended slightly, or he thought they had; he could not be certain because he was the only one who had been tracking them.

He was leaving behind a room that had his shape in it. The particular way he had arranged things — shoes by the door, bat against the left wall, rubber ball always on the specific ledge near the window where it sat best. These arrangements would persist for a little while after he left and then someone or time would rearrange them and the room would belong to itself again.

He thought about this without sadness, which surprised him slightly. He had expected sadness, had allocated mental space for it. But what he felt was closer to: accurate. This is what this was. Four years, twelve kilometers from the streets where he'd learned that survival and cricket were compatible, in a room that held no history before him and would hold none after. He had been in it. Now he was going.

He turned over on the cot. The springs made their familiar sound.

At some point he slept, because the next thing was morning light and the sound of the park opening.

On the last morning, the groundskeeper was at the entrance of the building at five-thirty AM, which was earlier than Vipin had expected.

Keshav was sixty-one. Vipin had never asked but had calculated it from context — the gray in the hair, the years of service he'd mentioned in passing over four years of near-silence. He had the bucket and the mop even at this hour, which was theater; the entrance didn't need cleaning. He was there because he was there.

They stood in the corridor. The morning light came through the small window at the end. A pigeon sounded off somewhere above.

"I left the gloves in the drawer," Vipin said. "The kit room can use them. Or whoever's next."

Keshav nodded.

"Thank you for the newspapers," Vipin said. "And the biscuits."

Another nod. The bucket handle turned slightly in his hand, a small movement.

Vipin had thought about what else to say. He had been thinking about it for three days. What do you say to someone who gave you newspapers and Parle-G biscuits for four years without ever asking anything in exchange, whose silence was a form of kindness that the word *kindness* was too small for? He had counted the things Keshav had left at his door: newspapers (daily, four years, approximately 1,460), biscuit packets (irregular, maybe 200), twice a bottle of Limca on hot days, the blanket, and once — at the start of the second year, when Vipin had been sick and hadn't emerged for two days — a small tiffin of khichdi, left without a knock.

He didn't say any of this. Some things were outside the available language.

"If you want," Vipin said, "after the first match — I'll get you a ticket."

Keshav looked at him. His face didn't change dramatically, but something in it shifted — not surprise, more: *registered.* He turned the bucket handle another small degree.

"The Mumbai Indians stadium ticket is for members," Keshav said. His voice was rougher than Vipin expected from so little use — a smoker's texture, though Vipin had never seen him smoke. "I don't have a membership."

"I'll sort it," Vipin said.

A pause. "Theek hai," Keshav said. Fine.

They stood there for another moment. The pigeon sounded again. Vipin picked up his kit bag.

"The River crack," he said, half to himself. "Twenty-three branches."

"I know," Keshav said. "I painted over it twice. Came back both times."

Vipin looked at him. In four years, he hadn't known this.

"Good crack," Keshav said, with the absolute flatness of someone who had made peace with a ceiling.

Vipin laughed. He didn't plan to. It came out of him the way real laughs did — before the brain could route it through the armor. Keshav's face changed: not a full smile, but the corner of his mouth moved. The first time Vipin had seen his face do that.

He walked out of the building into the morning. Shivaji Park was ahead, the familiar gates, the familiar trees, the outer pitches with their morning dew. He walked through it. Past the boundary bench where Rao had handed him cold chai and not said anything. Past the center pitch where he'd played his imaginary cover drive at two in the morning. Past the spot where, nine years ago, he'd stood on the boundary rope and watched organized cricket from the outside and understood that the game was real and he wasn't in it yet.

He was in it now.

At the park gate, he stopped. Turned back once — not looking for anything in particular. The storage room window was visible from here, a small square of glass. Not glowing. The light was off. The room had returned to what it had been before him: a storage room. In some years, it would be a storage room again. It had just been, for four years, also home.

He turned back to the gate. The city was on the other side.

He walked through.

The Taj on Marine Drive was twelve kilometers away. He took the bus — 37 Limited, which he'd taken exactly twice before and both times in the wrong direction before figuring out the route. He had the correct direction now. The bus was crowded in the particular mid-morning way of Mumbai buses: not rush-hour packed, but full enough that he stood with his kit bag between his feet and held the overhead bar and looked out the window at the city moving past.

Mumbai from a bus window was the city he knew. Not the city from the hotel window that was coming. This city: the narrow gaps between buildings, the laundry on every balcony, the chai stalls and repair shops and the specific density of human activity at street level that meant something was always happening within ten meters of wherever you stood. He had learned to read this city the way a cricketer reads a pitch — the behavioral patterns of specific neighborhoods, which lanes moved freely and which ones clogged, where a shortcut hid, where the danger was.

He didn't know the city he was going to. Not yet.

The bus passed Worli. Passed Haji Ali. The Arabian Sea appeared to his left — he could see it through the buildings, the flat grey-blue of late morning. He'd grown up in the city built on this sea and had looked at it from street level for years. The hotel was on Marine Drive. The sea would be visible from his room.

He watched it through the window as the bus turned onto Marine Drive. The same sea. Different eye level.

The bus stopped. He picked up his kit bag, squeezed past three people, and stepped off onto the pavement. The Taj was ahead — the familiar stone façade, the doormen, the cars. A bellboy was already moving toward him as he approached, eyes on the kit bag.

This time, Vipin let him take it.

He walked in through the doors.

The lobby chandelier caught the light.
