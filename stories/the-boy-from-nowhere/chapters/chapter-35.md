# Chapter 35: The Grandmother

**Timeline**: Late 2017 (age 27)

---

The drive was Shraddha's idea.

Not the visit — that was already in his head, the thought arriving around the time Shakti said *don't make her wait forever* and the monsoon came and went and the West Indies series was behind him and the selectors had called. The visit was inevitable; he'd known it since the night he got back from Haryana in 2016 and lay awake counting the cracks in his bedroom ceiling. Seventeen. There were seventeen. He'd counted them three times and gotten different numbers and then committed to seventeen by executive decision.

What was Shraddha's idea was the going together.

"I want to meet her," she said. It was October, a Tuesday morning. She was cross-legged on the kitchen floor in front of the open bottom cabinet, trying to locate a specific pan — the flat one, not the deep one — and speaking to him with her head half inside the cabinet. "She's family. She's the only family you have."

"I don't want to make it —" He was at the stove. "It's not a thing to make into a thing."

Shraddha emerged from the cabinet with the wrong pan, looked at it, put it back. "I'm not making it into a thing. I want to come. She must be lonely."

"She's not —" He stopped. "She walks three kilometers to the post office by herself. She has a whole village. She's not lonely the way you mean."

"She has a grandson who has visited once."

He had no answer to this that wasn't directly incriminating. He found the correct pan on the second shelf and handed it to her without ceremony.

"Drive?" Shraddha said.

"It's three hours from Delhi. That's four flights and a —"

"I like road trips."

"You've been on one road trip."

"And I liked it. That's the data." She stood up with the pan and was already moving toward the stove. "I'll tell Ravi to clear the schedule."

He looked at his chai. "You can't just tell Ravi —"

"I can and I'm going to."

---

They flew to Delhi on a Friday, and the October air was what Delhi air is in October — dry, sharp around the edges, nothing like Mumbai's thick coastal warmth, the kind of air that makes you pull a jacket out of a bag you'd half-forgotten you'd packed. Shraddha pulled on a light sweater at the airport and then immediately took it off in the terminal and folded it wrong and put it back in her bag. She folded most things wrong.

"You're watching me fold," she said, without looking up.

"I'm observing."

"You're judging."

"There's a difference."

The car he'd arranged was a white Innova, a driver named Sureshji who had the professional Delhi driver's gift for navigating the expressway at speeds that suggested the car's own opinions on the matter. Shraddha sat in the front seat because she got carsick in the back. She asked Sureshji about his family within the first ten minutes — his children, whether his wife had come with him to Delhi or stayed in Meerut. Sureshji had two daughters, one in engineering and one still in school. Shraddha asked after the daughters by name for the rest of the drive.

Vipin sat in the back and watched the city thin out. The same transformation as before: buildings dropping from ten stories to five to two to scattered, the highway opening up, the October sky over Haryana wide and cloudless in a way that Mumbai sky never was — the Arabian Sea always making Mumbai's sky feel conditional, as if the weather were still negotiating. This sky had decided.

He'd been here once. Alone, hands on the wheel, the PI's report in his pocket, the specific fear of a man about to find something he's looked for and not knowing what it would do to him. That version of the drive was nothing like this.

He'd called Shraddha from the service station on the way back: *I found her.* She'd said: *Come home.* He'd driven to Delhi, flown to Mumbai, gone directly to the flat, and sat on the kitchen floor with his back against the refrigerator for an hour. Shraddha had sat on the floor next to him. She hadn't asked him to get up. She'd made chai eventually, using a very long reach to the stove, and handed him the cup without making it significant. That hour was one of the things he was carrying from that year that had weight but not bitterness — present, solid, part of the structure.

"How are her hands?" Shraddha asked from the front seat. She was looking at the road ahead. "The arthritis."

"I don't know. Last time she had —" He thought. "Some stiffness. She managed."

"I brought her something. From Mumbai."

He looked at the back of her head. "What did you bring?"

"Cashew barfi from that shop near your place. The one you like. She's from Haryana — she'll like sweets."

He thought about this. "She might have given up sweets. Older women sometimes —"

"She'll like sweets," Shraddha said, with the certainty of a person who has an accurate empirical record. "Everyone likes sweets. It's basic."

Sureshji made a small sound that was not quite a laugh but was not a cough either.

---

The village arrived the same way it had arrived in June 2016: suddenly, from the flat distance, without preamble. The temple first, then the structures clustering around it, then the lanes that the Innova wouldn't fit down.

"We walk from here," he said.

Shraddha got out and looked around with the frank curiosity of someone who has not spent time in villages and is finding the geography interesting rather than uncomfortable. She looked at the water tower with the village name in Hindi, the hand-pump, the man on a tractor in the near distance moving at the tractor's own considered pace. Then she looked at him.

"Okay," she said. "Lead the way."

He carried the cashew barfi. She carried the bag. The lane was the same — the green gate visible at the third house past the temple, the rust at the lower edge where the October damp had reached. The tulsi plant in the painted pot. The ceiling fan just visible through the open door.

He called from the gate. "Sunita Devi ji?"

A pause. Then movement inside.

She appeared in the doorway in a cotton salwar-kameez, blue this time, the dupatta around her shoulders. The same assessment of an arriving stranger — the frank, unhurried kind, that had probably been her default since before he was born and would remain her default until it wasn't needed anymore.

Then she saw him. The assessment became something different.

"Vipin." Not a question. The same way she'd said it the first time — the name alone, with everything the name contained.

"Amma," he said. He'd called her that once before, on the phone, in the letter she'd written during his crisis, the letter that had arrived at his Mumbai flat in careful handwriting that shook at the corners. She'd called him *beta* in the letter. He'd called her *Amma* in the letter back. Some things happened in letters first because letters gave you time to try the word privately before you committed it to air.

She came across the courtyard. She stopped in front of him, assessing, and then her gaze moved to Shraddha.

---

Shraddha had the particular quality — he'd noticed this many times over the six years — of creating stillness in rooms. Not silence, not formality: stillness. People went calm in her presence. She didn't perform it; it was just something she had, the way some people have an easy voice or a walk that doesn't announce itself. Sunita Devi looked at her for a long moment, the same direct appraisal she'd turned on him a year and a half ago, and then she reached up and took Shraddha's face in both hands.

The hands were rougher than they'd been. Or he was noticing them more carefully, because he'd been told about the arthritis and was looking for it. The knuckles were more prominent, the skin across the joints drier. They held Shraddha's face with a careful steadiness, the grip adjusted for the imprecision — the way a skilled person compensates for a tool that's changed.

She studied Shraddha's face. Whatever she was looking for, she apparently found.

"You're beautiful," she said in Hindi, matter-of-fact. "He married well."

"We're not married yet," Vipin said.

Sunita turned her head toward him without releasing Shraddha's face. Her expression was the same expression his coach made when he was stating something so obvious the words were wasted air.

"*Yet,*" she said.

Shraddha's expression — the expression of a woman currently having her face held by a seventy-four-year-old Haryanvi grandmother — was doing several things simultaneously. Some of them were visible. The rest of them made her cheeks go warm in a way that started at the ears and moved inward. He had been present for many things in six years and had never seen Shraddha Kapoor blush. He filed this carefully, in the mental drawer alongside other things he would keep.

It was also, he noticed, something happening to his own face. Some interior heat rising. He looked at the tulsi plant and counted its branches. Nine. The main stalk plus eight.

Sunita released Shraddha and said: "Come in, come in, it's October but it's still hot at this hour. I'll make chai."

"I brought —" Shraddha held up the sweets box. "From Mumbai. Cashew barfi."

Sunita looked at the box, then looked at Shraddha, and the expression that crossed her face was so pure and unexpected — the genuine delight of a woman whose guest has brought exactly the right thing — that he felt the particular ache of having arrived late to something that had been going on without him.

---

Inside, the red oxide floor was cool. The ceiling fan turned at medium speed. On the steel cupboard there was a framed photo he hadn't seen on his previous visit: a boy of maybe seven or eight, squinting into the sun, large-framed, standing in a field with one hand blocking the glare. The frame was plain wood. The photograph was slightly yellowed at the edges, the way photographs yellow in North Indian homes where the heat and the dry air work on everything over decades.

He sat on the charpoy while Sunita moved to the kitchen area. Shraddha moved with her, not by invitation — she simply went, the natural gravity of a person who helps, who reads what's needed. He could hear them negotiating the kitchen: Sunita directing in short Hindi sentences, Shraddha asking clarifying questions, a small sound of something being located in a cupboard.

He looked at the photograph.

The boy was big-framed in the way that Jaat boys were big-framed — not fat, but dense, the frame built for a life that would make demands. Wide at the shoulder even at seven or eight, the jaw already showing its intention. One hand raised to block the sun, which meant the face was partially obscured, but what was visible was what was always going to be visible from the first time Sunita looked at him at the gate: the jaw, the brow, the particular angle of the eyes.

He looked at the photograph. The photograph looked back.

The sound of chai being prepared in the kitchen — the specific sequence, the water and then the tea leaves and the milk — came from behind him. He was aware that this was probably what it had sounded like in this room thirty-seven years ago, when the boy in the photograph was making demands on this kitchen, this house, this woman's time.

Sunita came in with two chai cups. Shraddha came with the rest — the small steel plate she'd found somewhere, the cashew barfi on it already arranged in a pattern that followed the natural geometry of diamond-cut pieces. She'd arranged them by size, which she did automatically, always.

He pointed at the photograph. "Is that —"

"Rajveer." She didn't elaborate. She sat on the stool, distributing chai. "Eight years old. Maybe nine. That's the field behind his uncle's house. He spent the whole summer there that year — chasing the water buffalo, his uncle said. The buffalo was not interested in being chased. But Rajveer was also not interested in stopping." She picked up her chai cup. "He looked like you. Exactly like you."

He heard this the way you hear a fact that has been true for your entire life but has only just been named. Rajveer was twenty-five when he died. He was twenty-seven. He'd been taller than his father since the age of twenty-one or so, by simple mathematics — the generation that gets regular food becomes bigger than the generation that ate whatever was available. But the structure was the same. The jaw was the same.

*Exactly like you.*

Sunita was watching his face while he looked at the photograph. "I'll find the other ones," she said. "After chai."

---

The photographs were in a metal box in the steel cupboard, which needed a small key that Sunita kept on a ring with four other small keys. The box had the quality of an archive — organized, but organized by its owner's system, which was not visible to anyone but her. She sat on the charpoy with the box on her lap and went through it with the arthritis-adjusted precision of a woman who had done this before, who knew what was in here and where, but whose hands now required more time than they once had to communicate with the paper.

He sat beside her. Shraddha sat on the floor near the charpoy with her chai cup, which was her way of making herself not-intrusive — sitting lower, taking up less vertical space, present but not positioned as a witness.

Rajveer at five, standing at the gate in a kurta with a button undone at the collar. Rajveer at perhaps thirteen, on a bicycle, face turned toward the camera with an expression of someone who had been interrupted mid-motion and was mildly annoyed about it. Rajveer with a group of young men — a wedding, the flowers visible, late 1980s in the quality of the print, some synthetic dye at the border of the page that print technology of that era reliably produced. He looked at the group photograph for a long time.

"He was twenty-three here," Sunita said. "Harish's wedding. Six months before he married Kamla."

In the group photograph, Rajveer was standing at the back left, which was where the tall person stood. His jacket was from the late-eighties style — wide lapels, a collar too large for the period it had actually entered — and it fit him badly because he was wider in the shoulder than the jacket assumed. He was laughing at something outside the frame. The laugh was open, uninhibited, the kind that didn't arrange itself for the camera. He looked like a man who knew everyone in the room and was at ease with all of them.

Then Sunita said: "And this is Kamla."

The photograph she produced was smaller than the others. Credit-card-sized, almost, the kind cut from a larger photograph. The cut edges were scissor-clean — trimmed with intention, not torn. A woman of perhaps twenty-two or twenty-three, small and sharp-featured, her hair loose past her shoulders. She was not looking at the camera. She was looking at something to the left — a look of concentration, or recognition, or some calculation being made. Her eyes were clear and focused and her mouth was in the particular expression of someone who is about to say something.

*Kind eyes*, the instruction had said. But that wasn't quite right. They were intelligent eyes. They were eyes that were paying attention.

He held the photograph. The quality of the cut — careful, deliberate — told him this had been trimmed from a larger image, someone else removed from the frame. He thought of the anonymous photograph in his drawer in Mumbai, the one with its right edge cut clean. He thought about who cuts the edges of photographs and what the cutting means.

He didn't ask. Sunita's face, watching him look at Kamla, had the quality of a face holding something steady. He didn't push on it. Not today.

"She sang," Sunita said. Unprompted. As if she'd been saving this one. "Always singing. In the kitchen, in the courtyard, walking from the house to the road. She had a voice — not trained, but the kind that doesn't need training. The kind that's just in you." A pause. "Rajveer said he fell in love with the voice first. Heard her singing from two houses away. Went to find the source." A small compression of her mouth. "That was Rajveer. The source of a thing was always more important to him than the thing itself."

He thought, very briefly, of Shraddha. Of the shower on Sunday mornings when she thought he was asleep — the specific unconscious singing, half-remembered old film songs, the ones from before she was born that she'd grown up hearing her mother hum. He thought of the pandit humming during puja, early mornings in the temple quarter, the sound arriving before full consciousness in a boy who'd sleep through everything except that sound. He thought of the connection that might not be connection — might just be coincidence, the universe's tendency to rhyme rather than repeat — but sat in him as pattern, as some line running through time that he was only now learning to see.

Shraddha, from the floor, said nothing. She was looking at her chai cup. But her ears were working.

---

The gloves were in the metal box, at the bottom, wrapped in a piece of cotton cloth that was faded from white to the color of old newspaper. Sunita lifted them out with the same careful adjustment for the arthritis in her hands, unwrapping them on her lap, and handed them to him.

Cricket gloves. Batting gloves. The leather was cracked at the knuckles, dried and re-dried by years in storage, the stitching at the base of the thumb visible where the thread had once repaired a small tear. The padding had compressed into flatness — whatever cushion they'd once offered had long since conceded to time. The fingers were shaped by a specific pair of hands that had worn them regularly enough to make impressions.

His father's hands. Too big for the bat, Sunita had said. He could see it now: the gloves were not particularly small — they'd have been a reasonable fit for an adult man — but they were too small for his own hands. The width at the palm was wrong, the fingers of the glove ending a centimeter short of where his actual fingers ended.

He slid his right hand in. It went far enough for the glove to sit on his knuckles, stretched taut across his palm, the wrist strap unable to fasten because the diameter was different.

"He played gully cricket," Sunita said. She was watching his hands with the gloves. "Your father. Not well, like I told you before — he was bad. He would have argued with you that he wasn't bad, but he was bad. He loved it, though. He loved the game." She paused. "He would have been so proud of you. Beyond what any words could hold." She said this without performance — flatly, as fact, as something she had calculated over a long time. "The boy who played badly and loved it — what he would have felt watching you —" She stopped. Didn't reach for the rest of the sentence. The rest of the sentence wasn't available.

He held the gloves. They were shaped by another pair of hands and too small for his. His father had stood at the batting crease in a Haryana gully with these gloves on and played badly and loved it. His father was twenty-five years old when he died in a charitable hospital corridor in Dharavi and had never seen him. His son was twenty-seven and had played a Ranji innings of 167 and had arms built for fast bowling and had found, this late, this particular artifact of a man who would have been proud.

The correct emotional response was available to him. He knew what it was. He did not perform it.

He removed the glove carefully and rewrapped both in the cotton cloth. He held the small package in his lap.

"I'll take care of them," he said.

"I know," she said. "I know you will."

---

The dinner was dal and roti and a dry potato sabzi that Sunita made with a particular combination of cumin and dried mango powder that he could not identify precisely while eating it but would identify later in the week by smell in a restaurant, the smell landing before the name did. The roti was made on a flat tawa over a gas flame, each one coming off the heat with a slight char at the edge that added exactly the right contrast. She cooked with the authority of someone who had cooked this food ten thousand times and had stopped thinking about how and was now only doing.

Shraddha ate everything on her plate. This was notable, for reasons she didn't explain, but which he understood: she was in someone's home and she had been fed and she was going to eat it properly. He ate everything on his plate because he always did — the pandit had made eating a practice, not a performance, and the practice had never left him.

Sunita noticed. Not with words — with the particular attention of a woman who measures warmth by whether her food gets eaten. She looked at Vipin's empty plate and then at Shraddha's empty plate, and a brief expression crossed her face that he could not have described precisely but recognized as the face of a person whose offering has been accepted.

"More roti," she said. It was not a question.

They talked across the dinner with the randomness of three people who had never all been in the same room before and were now finding the shape of it. Sunita asked Shraddha about her films — carefully, respectfully, as someone asking about a profession she had seen but not been part of — and Shraddha answered without the performance she sometimes applied to these questions, the deflection she deployed in rooms that wanted her to be an image. She told Sunita about the early auditions, the years of not quite working, the specific grinding quality of being Shakti Kapoor's daughter in rooms full of directors who had already decided what that meant.

"My father is very large," Shraddha said. "As a personality. You might have seen him in films — he plays villains, usually."

Sunita said, with great dignity: "I have seen many films."

"Then you've seen him. He's the one who is frightening."

"The frightening one is always loved by his family," Sunita said. "Outside they are frightening. Inside the house they are just a person."

Shraddha looked at Vipin. He looked at his roti.

"He is exactly that," she said.

They talked about the post office — Sunita's three-kilometer walk, the letters that still came occasionally addressed to Rajveer that she no longer forwarded anywhere. The postmaster, an older man who had been there for decades, always asked after her health with what she described as professional concern. She had timed both routes: the canal road was nineteen minutes, the paved route was twenty-six, and the canal road became impractical in monsoon so she had the calculation ready in both seasons.

"Why do you go yourself?" Shraddha asked. "Your hands —"

"My hands hurt," Sunita said. "They've hurt for three years. But they work." She held them up briefly — the prominent knuckles, the dry skin at the joints, the slight curve inward at the index fingers. "Walking works them. If I stop walking, they'll stop working sooner." She lowered her hands. "Also, the postmaster is good company."

He counted the seams in the ceiling. Eleven panels. The fan blade made five complete rotations in four seconds at this speed. He stopped counting and ate his second roti.

---

She gave him the gloves at the door when they were leaving. She'd wrapped them again in the cotton cloth and tied the cloth with a length of cotton string, the knot adjusted slowly to work with the arthritis. He took the package with both hands, which was the right way to receive something.

She held his face again — the same gesture as the first time, her palms rough and warm, the careful grip. He was ready for it this time and it still did what it did, the thing that had nothing to do with preparation.

"Come back," she said. Not a plea. An instruction.

"We will," Shraddha said from beside him, before he could answer. She said it with the particular authority of a person who has decided something and is not asking for confirmation.

Sunita looked from Shraddha to him. Some assessment completed itself behind her eyes.

"Good," she said. She released his face. She looked at Shraddha. "Take care of him."

"He takes care of himself mainly," Shraddha said.

"Yes," Sunita said. "He learned it too well. That's the problem."

He had no appropriate response to two women discussing his management of himself while he stood between them holding a parcel of cricket gloves. He held the parcel and said: "We should get to the car before it's dark."

Sunita made the gesture that was the universal North Indian grandmother's gesture for *you and your practical concerns.*

They walked back through the lane to where Sureshji had the Innova idling. Shraddha got in the front. He sat in the back with the cotton-wrapped parcel on his lap. As Sureshji pulled out and the green gate receded in the side mirror, Shraddha turned in her seat and looked at him.

She didn't say anything.

He looked at the parcel.

"She noticed you eat everything," he said.

"Everyone should eat everything when someone cooks for them."

"Not everyone thinks that."

"Then they're wrong." She turned back to the road. "She's good."

He looked out the window. The village was already becoming what the distance made it — water tower, temple shikhara, eucalyptus trees. The same landscape he'd driven through before, now different. Not because the land had changed. Because he was not the same man who'd driven through it the first time, alone, counting eucalyptus trees.

Eleven going in. He'd counted nine going out. Sureshji had taken a slightly different route to turn the car around.

---

The parcel went into the drawer that night.

Not that night exactly — they didn't get back to Mumbai until the following afternoon, and the following afternoon was management and Shraddha on three separate calls and the physio appointment he'd rescheduled twice. But when the drawer opened, the gloves went in. He unwrapped them first, laying them flat on the drawer floor next to the tennis ball — Bhai's taped tennis ball, from the years when the game was played on concrete — and the pendant copy, and the newspaper clipping and the India cap photograph and the boarding pass and the rest of the accumulated record of a life that had tried to verify itself.

Rajveer's gloves. Too small for Vipin's hands. His father had played badly and loved it.

He closed the drawer.

He went to the kitchen. Shraddha was at the table with her phone, the Indira Gandhi biography open beside her that she'd dog-eared at Emergency and not returned to. The cardamom smell of her face cream was in the air. The flat had its evening quality — the light going amber over the strip of sea visible through the kitchen window, the city below doing what it did without requiring acknowledgment.

"When you find your family," she said, without looking up from her phone. She said it the way she sometimes started sentences — in the middle, as though the beginning had been happening in her head and she was letting him in at the appropriate point. "When you find your family, what do you do with the search?"

He was quiet. He opened the refrigerator and looked into it for no particular reason, the cold air coming out, a habit from years of thinking with the fridge open that Shraddha found actively irritating and had mentioned three times. He closed it.

He went to the drawer. Not the kitchen drawer — the other one, the real one, in the bedroom. He opened it and looked at what was in it. The gloves were still warm from his hands. They wouldn't be warm tomorrow. They'd be part of the drawer, part of the archive.

The pendant copy was next to them. The tennis ball to the left. The boarding pass from his first international flight, the paper soft now from years of existing in a drawer with other things.

He thought about what she'd asked.

He came back to the kitchen and sat down across from her.

"You keep looking," he said. "But you stop needing to find."

She looked up from her phone.

"There's a difference?" she said.

"Yes." The search was still running — Krishnamurthy was still running it, following whatever threads remained. His mother might be alive. The photo with the cut edge might have a story he'd never access, or would eventually. These were open questions. He hadn't closed them. He wasn't going to.

But the quality of his relationship to the opening had changed.

"When I went the first time," he said, "I went because I needed to find something. Like there was a specific absence and the search was the only thing that might fill it."

"And now?"

He thought about the gloves. Too small. His father played badly and loved it. His grandmother walked three kilometers with arthritic hands because the postmaster was good company and the walking worked the joints. His mother had a voice, always singing, the kind that doesn't need training. There was a boy in a field at eight years old with one hand raised against the sun and the jaw Vipin saw in the mirror.

"Now I know what's there," he said. "I know who they were. I know there's a woman in Haryana who held my face and knows my name." He put his hands flat on the table. "The question is still open. But I'm not searching from the place of *I'm not enough without the answer.* I'm searching from —" He couldn't find the word. "From wanting to know. Not from needing to know in order to be real."

Shraddha was quiet. She closed her phone.

"When did that happen?"

He thought about it. "Dinner," he said. "Her empty plate."

She looked at him for a moment. Then she picked up the Indira Gandhi biography and found the dog-eared page. "I'm going back to Emergency," she said.

"It gets better," he said.

"You've read ahead."

"I read when you were asleep."

"You have no loyalty," she said. But she was already reading.

He sat there. The amber light moved across the wall as the sun went down over the sea, and the city made its evening sounds — the traffic, the distant train, the compound speaker from the building down the road that played devotional music between seven and seven-thirty every evening without fail, a fact he'd absorbed over four years of living here the way you absorb all the small regularities of a particular place, the way you stop hearing them and start being held by them.

Forty-seven rotations of the ceiling fan per minute. The fan had been forty-seven for as long as he'd lived here. He stopped counting at forty-seven and left the fan to itself.

Tomorrow the green gate in Haryana would still be there. Sunita Devi would walk to the post office and the postmaster would ask after her health and she would tell him precisely how she was, because she was not a woman who said *fine* when fine was not accurate.

Somewhere, possibly, Kamla was in a city he knew or didn't. That question stayed open. He was learning to let the open things stay open without falling into them.

He looked at Shraddha reading across the table, the book held at the angle she always held it, her feet tucked under her on the chair in the way chairs were not designed for. He thought about the word *yet.* Said with the authority of a seventy-four-year-old woman who had been waiting long enough to know that *yet* was just a door the time hadn't opened.

He thought he might tell her, when the time was right, what Sunita had said. Not tonight. Tonight was good as it was.

The devotional music started, seven o'clock.
