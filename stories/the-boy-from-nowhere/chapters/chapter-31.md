# Chapter 31: Haryana

**Timeline**: Mid-2016 (age 26)

---

He bought the flight ticket at twelve-forty in the morning, on his phone, in bed, without telling anyone.

Mumbai to Delhi, 6:15 AM departure, which required a four AM departure from Bandra and meant he would be in a taxi before the city had fully committed to being awake. He booked a return for the evening. He didn't book a hotel. Whatever this was, it would not take more than a day — either he would find what he needed to find or he would find a closed door, and both outcomes would be resolved by nightfall.

He set his alarm for three-thirty and lay in the dark and did not sleep.

He'd flown for cricket since he was seventeen. The first flight had been to Bangalore for an India Under-19 tournament, which he'd approached with the focused terror of someone who had grown up moving by train and bus and on foot and had never considered flight as a realistic mode of transport — it was a thing that existed for other people, for rich people, for Bollywood stars and businessmen and the politicians whose faces appeared on walls during election season. Getting on that Bangalore flight at seventeen had felt like inhabiting someone else's life. Within a year it was routine. He'd flown to every major Indian city, to South Africa, England, the West Indies, the subcontinent circuits. He'd flown business class and economy and chartered and once in a small prop plane between two Caribbean islands in weather that suggested the prop plane had not been consulted on the matter. Flights were transit. Flights were the space between games.

This flight was different. He understood this when he sat down in the window seat at 6 AM and the city was still dark below and there was no cricket kit in the overhead locker, no teammates around him, no match at the other end. He was going to a village near Hisar because a woman named Sunita Devi had filed a missing-person report in 1991 and might know the name of someone who might be his father, and nothing about this journey was transit.

He wore jeans and a plain shirt, no team gear, nothing that identified him. The pendant, as always, against his chest. He had Krishnamurthy's report folded in his back pocket, specifically the page with the village name and the last known address. He had two photographs — one of the pendant, printed, and the original anonymous photograph with its cut right edge.

The flight was fifty minutes. He looked out the window. Mumbai disappeared and below was the patchwork of the Deccan plateau and then Rajasthan's edge and then the flat brown of the north Indian plain in June, heat already haze-bright at this hour, the fields a particular parched yellow-green that was nothing like the Mumbai landscape he'd grown up in. He'd been in Delhi for cricket. Cricket Delhi — the Feroz Shah Kotla, team hotels in Connaught Place, the specific Delhi experience of a touring player, which was organized and air-conditioned and insulated from the city's actual texture. This was not cricket Delhi.

---

At Indira Gandhi Airport he rented a car. He had a license — had gotten one at twenty-three, late by civilian standards but he'd never needed one before, everything in Mumbai was train or taxi or team bus. He'd driven in Mumbai traffic a handful of times, which was the equivalent of having survived multiple near-death experiences while also learning to drive. Delhi traffic was its own species. He managed. The rental car had a GPS and he'd entered the village name, and the GPS was calm and confident in the way of machines that have no stake in the outcome.

The city thinned after Gurgaon. The buildings dropped from fifteen stories to five stories to two stories to the scattered structures that mark where a city concedes to the land around it. Then the highway: National Highway 9, flat Haryana road, two lanes becoming four and then back to two, the landscape opening into the expanse that North India does in a way that Mumbai — vertical, compressed, built on an island that had no room to be horizontal — never does.

The land was flat in a way that felt deliberate. Not the flatness of a plateau or a coastal plain but the flatness of a landscape that had decided on its own terms and committed fully, horizon to horizon, no apology. Wheat stubble fields from the winter harvest, the stalks cut short and dry in the June heat, the topsoil pale with drought-dust between irrigation rows. Eucalyptus windbreaks along the road's edge — the trees in long straight lines, planted with government precision, their narrow crowns making a broken vertical rhythm against the sky. Water towers in the distances, painted white with the names of villages in Hindi. *Chaudhary ki Dhani. Rampur. Sector 12 Phirni Road.* He read the signs as they passed. His Hindi was functional — he'd learned it properly in school, had the advantage of speaking it rather than having it formally schooled out of him by an English-medium education, and in this landscape it had the particular quality of being the right language for the place, the only language that fit the proportions of this much flat sky.

He passed a man driving a tractor in the opposite direction, moving at a pace the tractor thought was appropriate regardless of any other traffic's opinion on the matter. He passed a government school, closed for summer, its flag limp on the flagpole. A group of women in bright salwar-kameezes walking in single file along the road's edge, carrying water pots on their heads with the easy balance of people who had been doing this since childhood and had never considered it remarkable. He passed a tea stall that had a hand-painted sign in Hindi: *Chaudhary Tea House. Milk extra.* He noted this. The first of them.

He drove for three hours. The GPS took him off the highway at Hisar and onto a state road, then a district road, then a road that the GPS seemed optimistic about characterizing as a road — more a paved intention, the edges crumbled away by monsoon and traffic and the general entropy of roads that exist primarily for agricultural vehicles. The landscape was Haryana in the summer: flat, scorched around the edges, irrigated in patches by canal water, dusty where it wasn't irrigated.

Jaat country. His country, the pendant said. He'd grown up on Mumbai's pavement and eaten the pandit's temple food and learned cricket from Rao and navigated the city by train and autorickshaw, and he was genetically from this — from this flat, dry, no-nonsense landscape where everything was agriculture and hard work and the particular Jaat stubbornness that he'd been told from age fourteen by everyone who'd tried to argue him out of a decision manifested in him in a way that suggested the descriptor was accurate.

He thought, driving: what does it mean to be from a place you've never been. He'd felt Mumbai in his bones since he was six years old — the trains, the salt wind off the creek at Bandra, the particular quality of December mornings at Shivaji Park, the heat that was moist rather than dry, the city that never truly went quiet. He had acquired Mumbai. But he hadn't acquired this. This was something prior to acquisition. He looked at the fields and the sky and the water towers and felt the odd sensation of looking at a landscape that was not home and might have been home and was now neither.

---

The village arrived the way small villages arrive when you've been driving through flat country: suddenly, from a distance that makes distance irrelevant. Thirty or forty houses in the immediate cluster, more spread along the road's continuation, a primary school with a faded government sign, a temple — not the grand kind, the neighborhood kind, white walls, a small shikhara, a bell visible from the road. A tractor parked. Two dogs asleep in a patch of shade that was not adequate shade but was the best shade available.

The Chaudhary name was everywhere. Krishnamurthy had noted this in his report — that in Jaat country the surname was ubiquitous, not a distinguishing mark but a communal fact — and Vipin had understood it intellectually. But understanding a thing intellectually and standing in it were different experiences. Every third structure had the name somewhere. *Chaudhary Ramkishan, Hardware.* A gate with the initials *R.C.* and below it, lettered differently, as if added later: *Chaudhary Dairy and Farm Supplies.* A hand-pump with a small painted board: *Donated by Chaudhary Bhoop Singh Panchayat Fund 2009.* He parked the car outside the primary school and stood in the heat and read the signs and felt the name detach from its personal weight and become simply a word — a regional fact, a community identifier, the name on every wall and gate in a village that had been Chaudhary for generations before there was a pendant with the name on it and a boy on a temple step.

He had imagined, without acknowledging the imagination, that the name would be a key. That it would orient the people here, that they would see the pendant and understand immediately what it was. But you couldn't walk into this village and say "I'm looking for a Chaudhary" and expect that to narrow anything. Everyone here was a Chaudhary.

He got out of the car. The heat was immediate and total — the kind that doesn't arrive gradually but is simply present the moment you leave conditioned air, pressing down from above and radiating up from the ground simultaneously. Dry heat. Haryana summer, no humidity, just sustained thermal pressure that he'd never experienced growing up in Mumbai's coastal moisture. He stood in it for a moment, adjusting. A crow on a nearby wall regarded him with the professional indifference of a bird that had seen many strangers and found them mostly unremarkable.

He asked a woman near the school — older, carrying a plastic bag of vegetables, giving him the frank appraisal that Haryana women give unknown men in their village — whether she knew a Sunita Devi. Not Chaudhary; just the first name and title.

"Whose wife?" the woman asked.

"Widow," he said. "Rajveer Chaudhary's family."

The woman's appraisal shifted slightly — not suspicion exactly, more recalibration. "Rajveer Bhura's mother?"

He didn't know if "Bhura" was a nickname for Rajveer or a different person entirely. "Possibly. She filed a missing-person report, some years ago, for her son who had gone to Mumbai."

The woman looked at him more carefully. The mention of Mumbai had registered. "Mumbai wala hai tu?"

"I live in Mumbai, yes."

A pause. She repositioned the plastic bag on her arm. He could see she was calculating something — not suspicion, something more prosaic, the rural calculation of where a Mumbai man fits in the ledger of the village's business. "Go to the third house past the temple. The green gate." She was already looking away, reorganizing her bag. Not unfriendly — just efficient. This was a village. You answered questions and you moved on.

He walked toward the temple. Past the pump with the Chaudhary Bhoop Singh board. Past a house with a hand-painted *Chaudhary Provision Store* sign on its outer wall and a curtain across the doorway instead of a door. A boy of perhaps ten came around the corner of a house on a bicycle, saw him, braked, stared with the open curiosity of a child who had not yet learned to pretend not to stare. Vipin looked back. The boy pedaled away.

He found the house. Green gate, as described: a painted metal gate, the green faded and showing rust at the lower edges, with a bolt rather than a lock. Inside the gate, a small courtyard — packed earth, clean-swept, a tulsi plant in a painted pot near the entrance to the house itself. A charpoy in the shade of the courtyard wall, unoccupied. The house was pucca construction — brick and plaster, painted cream, the lower foot of the wall painted dark blue in the way that Haryana houses often were. A ceiling fan visible through the open door, turning at low speed.

He called out at the gate in Hindi that was cleaner than his usual Mumbai patois. "Namaste? Sunita Devi ji?"

A pause. Then a movement inside. Then a woman appeared in the doorway.

---

She was perhaps sixty-two, the age Krishnamurthy had estimated. Short, stout in the way that rural North Indian women often are in their sixties — not fat, but solid, weight earned over decades. A cotton salwar-kameez, printed, the dupatta around her shoulders rather than her head in the manner of a woman alone at home. Grey hair pulled back without ceremony. The face: weathered by forty years of agricultural life in a place with no shade, with deep lines around the eyes and mouth, the skin a rich tobacco-brown from decades of Haryana sun. Her eyes were dark and direct, and they assessed him with the frank speed of someone who had no time for indirect assessment.

"Who are you?" she asked. Not unfriendly. Flat.

"My name is Vipin," he said. Not Vipin Chaudhary the cricketer — just Vipin, which was the truth stripped to its minimum. "I'm looking for information about Rajveer Chaudhary. I was told his family is here."

Something crossed her face. Not pain — that would come later. This was the look of a word that lands in an old wound, the reflexive flinch that doesn't announce itself as flinch. "Rajveer was my son," she said. Past tense. "Come in."

The inside of the house was clean and spare, which was its own kind of wealth in this climate — the wealth of organization, of things being where they should be. A main room with a ceiling fan, a steel cupboard against one wall, a framed photograph on another wall: a man in his forties, a formal portrait, who was not Rajveer because the age was wrong but might have been her husband's brother or another male relative, identified by the marigold garland that someone had draped over the frame and left to dry. A gas stove in the kitchen area visible through a narrow opening. The floor was red oxide — smooth, slightly cool underfoot, the specific temperature of red oxide floors in mid-morning that is one of the most distinctive physical facts of a certain kind of Indian interior.

She gestured at the charpoy indoors — a second one, strung with cotton rope, pushed against the wall — and he sat on it and she sat on a plastic stool across from him. On the low table between them she put, without asking, a steel glass of water. He drank it. The water was cold from an earthen pot in the corner — he could see the matka, the traditional unglazed clay pot, sweating in the heat. He drank it and thought: *there are things you are offered in houses that tell you everything about the house. This water is the right thing.*

"You said Rajveer," she said. Not a question.

"Your son. Who went to Mumbai."

Her hands were in her lap, and they were still, which was the quality that distinguished this from a relaxed posture — not stillness from ease but stillness from long practice of holding a thing steady. "He went in 1989. He was twenty-four." A pause. "He was always going to go. These boys, that generation — Mumbai was where the money was, Rajveer said. He had — he had ambition. You know the word I mean. Not just wanting. Needing. To do something more than this." Her chin indicated the house, the courtyard, the village beyond it. Not dismissive — accepting. "From the time he was twelve, thirteen — always with some plan. He would tell me: *Amma, this village is too small for me.* I used to say: *All right. But there are people in this small village who love you.* He would say: *I'll come back with money and build you a pucca house.* He looked at this house, this house he grew up in, like it was a temporary situation." A compression of her mouth, fleeting. "He was a good boy. He was not unhappy. He just had that thing — the thing that makes a person need to go further."

"He married," Vipin said. "Before he went."

"Kamla." She said the name with a specific weight, the weight of a name that had been held carefully for many years. "Yes. Love marriage — there was family friction, but Rajveer was not a man who waited for permission. Never was. He wanted Kamla, so he married Kamla, and the family could either be at the wedding or not." A pause. "His aunt didn't come. His uncle came late. But his cousins came, and his friends, and it was a good wedding. Kamla was — beautiful is the easy word. Direct. She spoke her mind. Rajveer loved this about her. His family didn't love this about her."

Sunita shifted on the stool. She was looking at a point on the floor between them, not avoiding him but moving through something. "She had a kind of force to her. A certainty. Like she'd decided who she was and the world could either accommodate it or not. She would say things that — frank things. Not cruel. Just true. She would say true things in rooms where people preferred comfortable things, and Rajveer would watch her do it and he would be — proud. You could see it." A brief smile, not for him. For the memory. "They were very matched. Different in every surface way, same underneath — both people who needed the world to be real. Who couldn't pretend."

"What was he like?" Vipin said. "Rajveer. When he was young."

She looked up. The question had shifted something.

"Stubborn," she said. "Stubborn before he knew the word. When he was five, six — he would decide a thing and that was the end of the discussion. You could shout, you could punish, you could reason with him. None of it moved the decision. He would sit there with his small serious face and listen to whatever you had to say and then continue to do the thing he'd decided. His father — his father used to say: *I don't know where this boy gets it from.* And everyone would look at me." She paused. "So it came from me. I know where he got it from."

"Did he play cricket?" Vipin said. He heard the question come out and wasn't certain what he was doing.

"Not well." A genuine laugh, short. "He tried. All the boys tried. He was fast on his feet but his hands were wrong for it. He used to say: *Amma, I'm a farmer's son, my hands are too big for the bat.*" She mimed large hands, the gesture carrying the memory of a specific person. "He played carrom better. He was good at things that required patience and precision. Bad at things that required you to relax."

The ceiling fan turned. Outside, a goat made its flat complaint. Vipin looked at his hands — his bowler's hands, the right hand slightly larger from years of grip and release, the seam callus on the index finger. His father's hands had been too big for the bat.

He reached under his shirt and lifted the pendant over his head. He held it out across the low table, not aggressively — just held it out, for her to see.

She looked at it for a moment without touching it. Then she reached forward and took it. She held it in both hands, which were the hands of someone who had worked the land and cleaned houses and managed a family and a grief for thirty years — thick at the knuckle, the skin roughened at the fingertips. She read the engraving. Her hands shook. Not violently — the faint tremor of something that is being absorbed at the cellular level.

"Vipin," she said. The name, just the name, in a voice that had found its way to a register it hadn't used in a long time.

"I was left on temple steps in Mumbai," he said. "In June 1990. With this pendant. And money. And no other information."

She didn't look up. She was still looking at the pendant in her hands. "Rajveer's wife was named Kamla," she said, which was a thing she'd already told him, and which she was apparently telling him again because she needed to say it in proximity to the pendant. "If they named a son Vipin —" She stopped. The sentence did not need to be finished.

"Did he ever mention the name? If they had a son, what they would name him."

A long pause. She was still holding the pendant. "Once. When they left — the last time I saw them, when they left for Mumbai with Kamla — I asked him: *What if you have children there?* He said something like: *A boy we'll name Vipin, after Kamla's grandfather.* I didn't — I remember it now. I didn't register it at the time as important." She looked up. "He said Vipin after Kamla's grandfather."

The name on the pendant was not an invented name. It was a chosen name. Someone had chosen it before he was born, from a family line that now had a grandmother in a Haryana village and a grandfather in Kamla's history. The pendant wasn't a label. It was a preparation.

He sat with her in the spare clean room, red oxide cool beneath his feet, the ceiling fan turning at low speed, and the question that had organized his entire emotional life acquired, for the first time, something other than its own absence. Not an answer. Texture. His father's name was Rajveer Chaudhary. His father was a man who had left his village with ambition and love and gone to Mumbai. His mother's name was Kamla and she had certainty and force, a kind of knowing who she was. These were not abstractions anymore. These were people. People who had existed in this world, who had a mother who filed missing-person reports, who had left with hope and disappeared somewhere in the city's machinery in 1990.

"Do you know why?" he said. His voice was steady, which cost him something he didn't name. "Why they left me. Why I was left at the temple."

"No." She shook her head slowly. "I don't know what happened to them. I only know they disappeared. Maybe — something bad happened. Or —" She paused. "Or something else. I don't know."

The phrase *something else* hung in the room. He didn't push on it. It meant: the possibility that they had left him deliberately, for reasons she didn't have. It meant the worst version of the question. She didn't elaborate and he didn't ask her to.

"I filed the report in 1991," she said, unprompted. Her hands had lowered the pendant to the table, though she hadn't let go of it entirely — her fingertips still rested on it. "When a year had passed and no letter had come. I went to the police station in Hisar and the officer wrote it down and then I never heard from them again. I went back twice. They said they were looking. A Haryana man going to Mumbai in those years — they looked at me like I was asking them to search for rain." A pause. "After that I — you live your life. You don't stop waiting but you stop expecting. You make a kind of accommodation with the absence." She looked down at the pendant again. "I told myself he was alive somewhere. I told myself sometimes the alternative. I told myself nothing, on the days when nothing was the only thing that didn't hurt."

He said: "I'm sorry."

She looked at him with a directness that had nothing performative in it. "You don't apologize for being found," she said. "You're here. You came."

Outside, a two-wheeler passed on the road. The ceiling fan turned.

He stood to go. She stood too, and she walked with him across the courtyard to the road, and at the road she did something that he had not anticipated and that he would carry in his body for the rest of his life: she reached up and took his face in both hands.

Her palms were rough and warm. She held his face in the manner of someone who has known what it is to hold a face this way, who has held a child's face this way and measured the face against love. She looked at him closely, the way you look at something you've been waiting for.

"You look like him," she said. "Rajveer. The jaw. The eyes." Her thumbs at the edges of his cheekbones. "He had your jaw exactly. Your father." She said *your father* in Hindi — *tere baap* — without hedging, without *possibly* or *maybe,* as if the pendant had resolved it and now there was only fact.

He couldn't speak. He nodded. She held his face for one more second and then let go.

He walked to the car. He got in. He sat with his hands on the wheel and didn't start the car for perhaps five minutes, looking straight ahead at the road that turned left after the temple and went back toward the highway. He counted the eucalyptus trees visible from where he'd parked. Eleven. Two dead ones near the far end, gray and bark-stripped, held vertical by habit.

*Tere baap.* Your father.

He thought about Rajveer's hands. Too big for the bat. He thought about Kamla's certainty — a woman who said true things in rooms that wanted comfortable things, a woman who had been described by her mother-in-law with the pride of a woman who had liked her daughter-in-law despite the family friction. He thought about Sunita holding his face, the specific pressure of her palms. She had been holding that gesture in reserve for twenty-six years.

He started the car.

---

The drive back to Delhi was three hours of flat highway and the heat coming down and the GPS calm and consistent with its directions. He drove through the eucalyptus corridor back to the district road, back to the state road, back to the NH9, and the landscape repeated itself in reverse: the water towers, the wheat stubble fields, the tractor traffic, the tea stall with its hand-painted sign. He passed the *Chaudhary Tea House* going the other direction now and it meant something different than it had meant going in. Or not different. More specific.

He stopped at a service station outside Rohtak for water and to let the car's AC recover from thirty minutes of direct sun while he'd briefly stood outside at the pump. The tarmac was heat-shimmer, the kind that makes the near distance unreliable, wavering. He drank a bottle of water in one long sequence and stood there. A truck driver at the adjacent pump watched him with the mild curiosity of a man who found it unusual that a person would drink that fast.

He turned the Haryana trip in his mind the way you turn a stone — the same stone from multiple angles. Rajveer's ambition. Kamla's certainty and force. The love marriage, the family friction. The pendant prepared before a birth, a name chosen from Kamla's grandfather, an intention made. They had planned for him. They had not arrived in Mumbai and improvised a name — they had come with a name. And then something had happened in 1990 and the baby was on the temple steps and Rajveer Chaudhary was never heard from again.

*What happened to you?*

The question had a different shape now. Not formless. Not just an absence demanding to be filled. The question had edges — it had people in it, specific people, Rajveer with his hands too big for the bat and Kamla who couldn't pretend and the year 1990 in a city that was doing many things at once, most of them having nothing to do with a Haryana man with a baby and a love marriage and a plan.

He called Shraddha from the service station.

"I found her," he said, when she picked up. "Rajveer's mother. She confirmed the names. She held the pendant."

A pause on the line, the specific pause of someone receiving information whose weight they'd been anticipating. "Are you okay?" Shraddha said.

"I'm —" He looked at the service station's tarmac, the heat-shimmer rising from it, a truck filling up at the adjacent pump. "I don't know yet. I think I need to be somewhere quiet to know."

"Come home," she said.

"Yes."

"I'll be at yours when you land."

He thought about saying he'd be fine, he didn't need — and then did not say it. "Okay," he said. "Yes."

He drove to Delhi. He flew back to Mumbai. The flight attendant brought him orange juice that tasted of concentrate, and he drank it and looked out the window at the Deccan below and thought about the specific way Sunita Devi had said *tere baap* — not tentatively, not *possibly your father*, but *your father*, committed fully, the pendant having been enough and his face having been enough and twenty-six years of waiting having turned out to require only this: a person who showed up with the right object and the right jaw.

His father had her stubbornness, she'd said. And where had Rajveer's stubbornness come from? From Sunita. Which meant that the quality in Vipin that Rao called resolve, that Venkat called competitiveness, that the press called Vipin Chaudhary's famous refusal to accept defeat — the quality that had gotten him through eight months of rehabilitation in a white room and out the other side with more pace and more intelligence than he'd gone in with — that quality traced through Rajveer to a woman in a Haryana village who had held his face in her hands and named his jaw.

It had always been his. He'd never needed to earn it. It had been in the inheritance.

*What happened to you?* Not *who were you?* He knew who they were now. A little. Enough to know they'd been real. The question had moved.

At the Mumbai airport, waiting for his bag, his phone buzzed. Krishnamurthy.

*I have been doing preliminary cross-referencing since you initiated your trip. Have located one more possible lead from the Mumbai end — hospital records, 1990, incomplete but partial. Need to verify. Will report within the week.*

He read this twice. Then put the phone in his pocket.

The baggage carousel turned. His bag appeared. He picked it up and walked toward the exit and the city and Shraddha's car, already waiting.

One more lead. One more thread.

*What happened to you?*

The question was going somewhere. He wasn't sure yet where, and he wasn't sure the destination would be kind, but the question was going somewhere and it was going toward an answer, and the man walking through the Mumbai airport with a pending lead in his pocket was not, despite everything, afraid of what the answer might be.

He was almost afraid. But not quite.

Not yet.
