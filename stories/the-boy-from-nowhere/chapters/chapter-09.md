# Chapter 9: The Price of a Name

"You're not eating," Ravi said.

It wasn't a question. Vipin had the phone wedged between his ear and shoulder, sitting on his cot in the storage room at Shivaji Park, looking at a plate of dal-chawal the groundskeeper had left outside his door thirty minutes ago. The dal had a skin forming on it. He knew this because he'd been watching it form for twenty-five minutes.

"I'm eating," he said.

"You're lying. I can tell from your voice."

"What does lying sound like?"

"Like you, but slightly slower." A pause on the other end. A TV in Ravi's background — sounds from a cricket match, commentator's voice rising over crowd. "It went through, Chaudhary. The registration — they confirmed it today. Both of us."

He knew this. Rao had told him two hours ago, calling from the landline at his flat in Dadar, his voice careful in the particular way it got when he was managing information. The registration had been submitted, approved, and processed. Vipin Chaudhary, uncapped Indian cricketer, seventeen years old, right-hand bat, right-arm fast, base price thirty thousand dollars, was in the IPL player pool.

"Haan."

"*Haan?* That's all you — I've been training since I was nine years old. We just made the player pool for the biggest cricket tournament ever created and you're giving me *haan?*"

The dal skin was now complete. A pale layer, unbroken. He had counted the ripples in it: seven, radiating from where the steam had escaped.

"I'm happy. I'm very happy."

"You sound like a man filling out a tax form."

"I've never filled out a tax form."

"Okay." A beat. "You sound like a man *watching* someone fill out a tax form."

He almost smiled. Ravi was a good person to talk to, which was one of the things he'd learned about himself in the last two years — he had preferences about people, actual preferences, which was different from having categories of useful versus not useful. Ravi fell into a category he hadn't known existed before Ravi: people who made silence companionable rather than pressured. They could be on the phone saying nothing and it didn't demand filling.

"I'll eat it," he said, reaching for the dal-chawal.

"Eat it warm. The groundskeeper makes good dal."

"It's not warm anymore."

"Then eat it cold. Your body doesn't care."

He ate it cold. The skin broke under the spoon and folded over and he ate it in four minutes without tasting much, phone still wedged at his shoulder, while Ravi narrated something happening in a cricket match on his TV. The commentator's voice came through in fragments — *beautiful timing, textbook cover drive* — and Vipin thought about cover drives for a moment, the specific weight transfer he'd been working on since the Ranji camp, and then thought about the fact that the auction was in February and it was now late November and there were eleven weeks between now and the thing he wasn't thinking about.

He set the empty plate outside the door. "Okay," he said.

"You're very strange, Chaudhary," Ravi said, not unkindly. "But also, good luck. Not that you need it. Luck finds people who move quickly enough."

He'd said something like that before. A version of it. He was the kind of person who had sayings, which Vipin respected even while finding it slightly alien — to have language ready-made for the large moments, as if you'd prepared for them. Vipin had never prepared for large moments. Large moments arrived and he dealt with them or he didn't.

He put the phone down. Picked up the rubber ball. Squeezed.

The registration form was in Rao's briefcase. A PDF, printed on A4, with a passport-size photograph of Vipin stapled to the upper right corner. The photo had been taken at a studio in Dadar that charged forty-five rupees for four. Rao had waited outside on the pavement. Inside, a man with a flash attachment and the particular world-weariness of someone who has taken forty-five-rupee photographs for twenty years had told Vipin to look at the camera and not smile.

He had not smiled. He'd worn a borrowed shirt — white collar, striped — that was half a size too large in the shoulders, and he'd looked at the camera with his jaw set in the way it got when he was deciding things, and the man had said *theek hai, done* and handed him a strip of four identical faces.

Rao had looked at the photo and said nothing for a moment. Then: "Good. You look determined."

He had not felt determined. He had felt the way he always felt in rooms where things were decided about him without his full understanding of the machinery — the same way he'd felt in the MCA meeting when the registration question came up, and in the juvenile records office when the birth certificate application was being processed. A specific quality of waiting that had nothing to do with patience and everything to do with the recognition that other people were operating levers he couldn't reach.

On the registration form, under "Next of Kin / Emergency Contact," there was a blank line.

Rao had written his own name and number.

---

The weeks after registration were the longest of his life, and he'd lived through some genuinely long weeks.

On the streets, time had moved by event. Hunger marked one interval, a meal marked another. Bhai's schedule structured the working hours. The heat and the monsoon divided the year into bearable and less bearable. He had never experienced time as abstract before — had never had a specific future date so far ahead and so fixed that every day was simply the space between now and then.

The auction: February 20, 2008. He knew the date the way he knew the River crack in his ceiling — precisely, involuntarily, impossible to look away from.

He trained. He trained the way he'd always trained, which was with a totality that Rao had twice told him was excessive and once told him was necessary, contradicting himself in a way Rao considered wisdom and Vipin considered imprecision. In the absence of matches — the Ranji season had wound down, the age-group season paused — he trained with the intensity of someone filling a gap. At seven in the morning, laps of the park. From nine to twelve, technical work: Rao had arranged a club bowler three mornings a week, and the other two Vipin faced a bowling machine that the MCA had at their facility in Wankhede.

The bowling machine had no read on a batsman's anxiety. It released the ball at a specified speed and a specified length with perfect mechanical indifference, over and over, for as long as Vipin wanted. He spent two and a half hours in front of it one afternoon in December, alone in the facility, until the operator who ran the machine came and said they were closing and could he please leave.

He was twelve kilometers and two bus rides away from where he grew up. He thought about Bhai sometimes, in December. Not with affection exactly, not with resentment exactly. More with the recognition of a debt whose terms he didn't fully understand — *remember who was here* — and the awareness that the auction, if it produced what it might produce, would make the terms clearer by making the stakes larger. Bhai was strategic. Bhai had known this was an asset the way he knew everything about value: before the value was visible to anyone else.

He hadn't gone back to Dongri since October.

He stopped sleeping well in January.

It came on gradually and then suddenly, the way things that had no physical cause arrived — not a bad night, but a pattern of bad nights that accumulated until by the third week of January he was waking at three and four in the morning with his mind already running. Not nightmares. Just: running. The catastrophizing was new. On the streets, fear had been actionable. You ran from a thing, or you fought it, or you found a way around it. You did not lie in the dark thinking about things that had not happened yet and might not happen.

He'd think: what if nobody bids.

He'd think: what if it goes to base price and then stops.

He'd think: what if the highlight reel looks amateur next to the actual players. He'd seen the IPL player list — the names on it, the international names, Hayden and Symonds and Malinga and Muralitharan — and then his name somewhere in the uncapped Indian section of a spreadsheet, seventeen years old, and the gap between those entries was the gap between his life and theirs.

He'd think: the form says Father Unknown, Mother Unknown. Did franchises look at that? Was there a committee that read background on the players they were considering, and would the committee see *unknown* twice and make a calculation about risk?

He didn't know. This was the central problem. He had always functioned on incomplete information — had built a system for operating under uncertainty, for treating incomplete data as workable rather than paralyzing. But this uncertainty was different because it had a specific date attached to it and he couldn't do anything between now and that date to alter the outcome. He could train until the bowling machine operator asked him to leave and it wouldn't change what happened in a hotel ballroom in February. The auction didn't care how hard he hit the ball. The auction was about something else — about what men in suits decided his talent was worth, and that decision was already made in all the ways that mattered, the information already in their hands, the bids already forming in their heads, and there was nothing to do but wait.

He counted things.

The groundskeeper's cough: irregular, sometimes forty seconds between, sometimes ninety. Unpleasant to count because it gave no stable rhythm. The car horns from the road beyond the park: also irregular. The trains from the distant track, if the night was quiet enough: better. A gap of approximately twelve minutes between locals running late at night, reasonably consistent. He'd fall into the rhythm of the trains and sometimes find himself on the other side of consciousness without knowing it, not sleeping exactly, something adjacent to sleeping that left him groggy in the morning.

Rao noticed.

He did not notice loudly. Rao's noticing was quiet, an adjustment of schedule without announcement. One morning in late January he called and said they were not training that day. Instead: Vipin should meet him at Shivaji Park at seven. Bring the rubber ball, not the bat.

Vipin arrived to find Rao sitting on the boundary bench — the wooden one, painted green, that faced the main pitch — with two steel cups of chai. He handed one to Vipin and said nothing.

The park was waking up. The morning walkers in their particular circuits, the dogs who moved through Shivaji Park with the territorial confidence of residents, the first of the coaching groups setting up on the outer pitches. A boy who couldn't have been more than eight was being coached by an older man — father or uncle, not professional — on a rough stretch near the boundary, the boy's stance too wide and his bat too heavy for him, swinging with the full-body commitment that kids used before anyone taught them to economize.

Vipin watched the boy swing and miss for the third time.

The kid didn't get upset. Just reset his stance and waited for the next ball. The sort of reset that had not yet been made complicated by the awareness of being watched.

"The game doesn't know about auctions," Rao said eventually. He had finished his chai. The cup was empty. He still held it. "The game knows what you do with the ball. That's the only thing it's ever known."

Vipin looked at the boy, who had just made contact — a mis-hit, the ball squirting off the bottom edge into the dirt — and the kid's face changed with the contact, lit up at the information of bat on ball even when the shot was wrong.

"I know," Vipin said.

"You're not sleeping."

"I'm sleeping."

"Chaudhary."

He drank his chai — cold by now, he'd forgotten to drink it while Rao wasn't looking. "I'll sleep better in February. When it's over."

Rao set down his own cup. He didn't push. He sat with him for another forty minutes, watching the park fill up, not coaching anything, not giving advice. When Vipin's chai was long cold he drank it anyway, which was a habit he'd had since the street years when you drank whatever was in the cup when you could because the thing in the cup did not keep.

It helped. For the rest of that day, it helped. The next morning at three AM he was awake again, counting the trains.

---

He went to a temple in the second week of February.

Not his old temple — his temple was in Matunga, and he hadn't been back to it since the year of the pandit's death, hadn't been near it by anything more than transit, the particular avoidance of places that held a shape of grief the body remembered without the mind's permission. A different temple, nearby, the kind that existed in every city neighborhood without demanding anything specific of its visitors.

He went in the early evening, after training. Still in his training clothes, the rubber ball in his pocket. The temple was in the middle of its evening rush — worshippers who came after work, the particular energy of people who had spent a day doing other things and were now redirecting briefly. An old woman near the entrance had a plastic bag of marigolds and was distributing them to people who wanted to offer flowers. He took one, not knowing why, and then didn't know what to do with it.

He found a space at the back. The kind of space you could occupy without participating — far enough from the main shrine that the rituals were at a distance, not a demand.

He did not pray. He had no architecture for prayer — the pandit had taught him the words and the forms and the meanings, but those things had always felt to Vipin like a language he could pronounce without being fluent in. He understood what prayer was for, understood the function of it, the way it gave a shape to the uncontrollable, made a container for the things that couldn't be contained. He had never been able to make the container. His mind didn't stay inside it.

He sat and watched other people pray.

The old woman who'd been distributing flowers had found a spot at the front and was chanting, her voice underneath the general sound of the temple — a continuous undertone, word after word with the rhythm of something said ten thousand times and still meant. And the cadence of it was — the cadence was —

The pandit had chanted in the early mornings. He hadn't thought about this in years, consciously. The pandit's voice at dawn, the specific frequency of it in the stone room of the temple, coming through the wall into wherever Vipin had been sleeping. He'd heard it every day for seven years without registering it as something he was hearing, and then it had stopped and he'd registered its absence for one week before stopping that too, because on the streets there was no mechanism for carrying absences.

The chanting cadence was the same. Old women chanting in old temples used the same cadence across time and geography, apparently. His hands started to shake.

Not violently. Just a small tremor in the fingers holding the marigold, the petals pressing slightly against his palm. He looked at his hands — the specific calluses of a batsman, wrong on one or two points since Rao had corrected his grip, still carrying the evidence of the uncorrected years underneath the corrected ones. He closed his fingers around the marigold.

He got up. He did not touch the pendant. He placed the marigold at the edge of the shrine, not in the center where it would require more action. He walked out.

On the street, the evening traffic was already beginning to clog. An autorickshaw negotiated a gap that didn't exist and found it anyway. He stood on the pavement for a moment with his hands in his pockets.

He thought about going back once, two Sundays later. He was already on the bus when he decided not to, without knowing exactly why. He got off two stops early and walked the rest of the way to Shivaji Park along the road that smelled of bread from the bakery that opened at five.

---

The night before the auction, February 19, the storage room was the same room it had been every night for four years.

He'd thought about this. He'd thought: the room doesn't change, and I'm going to be in it the night before a thing that might change everything, and the room doesn't know the difference. The River crack in the ceiling was the same crack. Twenty-three branches, the main line running north to south. The cot was the same cot. The bat was across his lap the way it was when he needed something solid.

He picked up the rubber ball. Put it down. Picked up the pendant and held it outside his shirt, feeling the weight of it. The engraving was too fine to read by touch — he'd tried this many times, could never make out the letters. He knew what it said. He didn't need to feel it.

He thought about the pandit. About the specific sound of the pandit's slippers on the stone floor at dawn — a shuffle, then a pause, then a shuffle. He hadn't known he remembered this until just now.

The gully game in June 2000: the bowler who'd thought he was quick, the ball leaving the hand early, the bat finding it before the decision was fully made. The way the game had gone quiet. He'd walked home afterward touching the pendant without knowing it.

Bhai on the rooftop.

Then Rao — the borrowed bat, the ball back past the bowler, Rao's face doing something complicated with its composure that it had never quite fully recovered from.

He thought about the document in Rao's briefcase. *Father: Unknown. Mother: Unknown.* A green piece of government paper that explained him in the terms available to it, which were the terms of absence.

He counted. Five people in seventeen years who had, in various degrees and various ways, been present. The pandit. Bhai. Rao. Keshav the groundskeeper. Ravi. Five. He was not sure this was a small number or a large number — he had no reference point for how many people accumulated in an ordinary life by the age of seventeen. He suspected more. He wasn't sure what to do with this thought so he set it aside in the file where he put things that were true but not actionable.

He picked up a pen from the ground beside the cot. A blue pen, the cheap kind, the kind that arrived in packs of ten from a stationery shop. A piece of paper, torn from the back of a scorebook he'd kept from the last U-19 tournament.

He'd never written anything personal before. Letters were for people who had people to write to. Journals were for — he didn't know what journals were for. Cricketers kept scorebooks and coaches kept training logs. He kept a rubber ball and a pendant and a box of clippings in a drawer.

He wrote one sentence.

He read it back. It was correct. It was the thing. He folded the paper in half and put it under the pillow.

He lay on the cot. The bat to one side. The ball in his right hand. Outside, the park was dark and quiet — the specific quality of quiet that came after eleven at night when the last of the evening walkers had gone home and the sound was just the road, distant, and whatever the trees were doing.

He should sleep. He knew he should sleep. He also knew with total certainty that he wasn't going to sleep, not properly, not for more than fragments.

At half past two he gave up the effort.

He pulled on his training shoes and went outside.

Shivaji Park at two in the morning was a different geography. The same grass, the same trees, the same painted boundary rope, but all of it drained of the daytime activity that gave it meaning — the coaching groups and the matches and the walkers — and left as just: a space. Open under the moon. The city behind the trees still making its sounds but at a distance, like something that could be turned off.

He walked to the center pitch.

The pitch covers were on — canvas sheets over the square, held down at the edges by sandbags. He didn't disturb them. He walked around to the far end of the square, where the batting crease would be, and stood on the grass beside it in the dark.

Tapped three times with an imaginary bat.

It was the ritual. He'd had it since he was eleven and didn't understand where it came from — three taps, the bat on the crease, before he took guard. The connection to the pandit's three steps from the cooking area to the temple door had occurred to him exactly once, when he was fourteen, and he'd immediately stopped thinking about it because it was the kind of thing you didn't want to pull apart.

He looked at the far end of the pitch where the bowler would run in from.

He played the shot — the imaginary cover drive, weight forward, bat face coming through square, the follow-through up toward gully. Clean. He watched it go to the imaginary boundary. He watched the imaginary crowd — not cheering exactly, just present, the specific mass of expectation that an actual crowd generated — process it.

He stood there for a moment.

Then he walked back to the storage room, took off his shoes, and lay on the cot.

The paper was under his pillow.

He didn't sleep, not really, but the counting stopped. He was just there, in the room, in the dark, with the pendant against his chest and the auction in six hours and the sentence under his pillow, and for once in a very long time the anxiety was not amplified by the counting but only by itself, which turned out to be a manageable amount.

---

He opened his eyes at six. Not woken — just: eyes open. The room was the same room.

The paper under the pillow. He pulled it out, unfolded it. Read the sentence.

*I want them to say my name.*

He folded it and put it in his shirt pocket.

He ran two laps of the park. Not because it was a training day — it wasn't. Because his body required it. The park was in its morning mode, the early walkers already out, one group of coaching kids setting up at the far pitch for a Saturday session. He ran at half pace, not pushing, and when the two laps were done he stopped and let the morning light and the sweat do the work they did.

He showered at the park facilities. He put on a clean shirt — he had three, and he chose the best one, which was a white one that Rao had given him the previous year for a tournament photograph, and which was still white because he'd washed it carefully every time.

In the small mirror on the storage room wall, he looked at himself.

The scar above the left eyebrow: permanent, a thin pale line against the skin. The jaw: set. The pendant: under the shirt. The paper: in the shirt pocket, against the left side of his chest.

He heard Rao's Maruti in the lane outside.

He picked up the rubber ball. Put it in his other pocket. Went out.

Rao's car smelled of the chai he'd spilled last week on the dashboard — a stain he'd addressed with a cloth and failed to fully remove, a faint discoloration that the morning heat brought out as a sweetness. The radio was on: a Hindi film song, something from the late nineties that Vipin didn't recognize but that Rao was quietly humming to without appearing to know he was doing it. They drove through the Dadar traffic in silence, which was the right kind of silence — not the taut kind, just the kind of silence that fit the morning.

At a signal on Senapati Bapat Marg, a kid — maybe ten, maybe eleven — was selling newspapers between the stopped cars. Scrawny, quick, moving car to car with the efficiency of someone who had worked out the timing of a forty-five-second red light down to the movement. He looked up once into the Maruti and his eyes met Vipin's through the glass. Neither of them moved. The signal changed. The kid turned to the next car. Vipin watched him in the mirror until the traffic closed around him.

He put his hand into his shirt pocket and felt the folded paper.

The MCA office was small and already crowded. There were six people in a room built for four: two senior MCA officials, a coach from the Mumbai U-19 setup, another coach he didn't know, Rao, and him. Someone had arranged chairs in a rough arc facing a television on a metal stand. The television was already on — a test pattern, then the broadcast feed cutting in, the IPL auction logo appearing on screen.

Someone had brought samosas. They were on a paper plate on the corner table, still warm from the vendor. Nobody touched them.

The auctioneer's voice filled the room.

For six hours, Vipin sat in a plastic chair and watched other men decide things.

He thought: this is what it is. This is the machinery. He watched franchise representatives in expensive suits raise paddles while their advisors leaned in to murmur numbers. He watched the auctioneer move through the names with professional rhythm — *going once, going twice, sold* — and felt the specific unreality of watching cricket reduced to commerce, the way something you'd built your entire understanding of the world around was being processed by people who measured it in dollars.

MS Dhoni went to Chennai for a number that had no relationship to anything Vipin had encountered.

Andrew Symonds. Sanath Jayasuriya. Zaheer Khan.

Ravi's name was called at lunchtime. A modest bid. A quick resolution. *Sold.* The room clapped lightly. Rao looked at Vipin. Vipin clapped.

The samosas got cold.

He counted the ceiling tiles. Thirty-four.

Outside the window, a crow was sitting on the ledge. It looked in once, then turned away.

The afternoon session started. The uncapped Indians. The names called quickly now, some going for base price, some generating brief bidding wars, some — and the silence that followed an unsold was a specific quality of silence, flat and clinical — going nowhere. *Unsold.* The auctioneer moved on before anyone could dwell on it.

A bowler from Vipin's U-19 cohort: talented, academy-trained, solid technique. His name came up. Vipin watched the franchise tables. One paddle. *Sold, base price.* The room exhaled slightly.

*Going well,* Rao said, very quietly, to no one in particular.

There was a segment break on the broadcast. The TV showed a studio panel — analysts discussing the morning session, numbers scrolling in a banner underneath. The room shifted slightly in its chairs. One of the senior officials went to use the phone in the corridor.

The samosas were cold by now and someone ate one anyway, and the sound was loud in the room.

Vipin put his hand in his shirt pocket and felt the paper folded there.

The broadcast resumed.

The auctioneer consulted his list. The next lot number appeared on the screen.

"Lot number forty-seven," the auctioneer said. "Vipin Chaudhary. Right-hand batsman, right-arm fast-medium. Mumbai. Seventeen years of age. Base price: thirty thousand United States dollars."

The screen cut to the highlight reel. Six seconds. The cover drive from the U-19 final — the ball traveling so fast the fielder at cover didn't react. The yorker in the second clip, the off stump cartwheel. The catch at deep midwicket, the one that defied the angle.

Rao did not move.

Vipin did not move.

The room at MCA was not breathing.

A paddle went up at the Mumbai Indians table. Quiet, authoritative. *Thirty thousand.*

He was in the auction.

Another paddle — Rajasthan Royals. *Forty thousand.* The auctioneer's rhythm picked up.

Mumbai Indians: *Fifty.*

A third franchise entered — Delhi Daredevils. *Sixty.* Three paddles for a kid from a storage room at Shivaji Park.

Mumbai Indians came back without hesitation: *One hundred thousand.* A jump bid. The number moved through the MCA room before the auctioneer had finished saying it. Rao's hand tightened on his knee. Vipin did not look at him.

Delhi dropped. The room at MCA inhaled.

Rajasthan held the paddle for one second — Vipin counted the second — and then lowered it.

"One hundred thousand dollars, going once —"

The silence in the hotel ballroom came through the television speakers like a held breath. The auctioneer looked across the room. Nobody moved.

"Going twice —"

"*Sold.* Mumbai Indians."

The MCA room erupted. Rao grabbed Vipin's arm — the physical contact startled both of them, because Rao didn't touch — and the room was on its feet and someone was saying his name and the TV was too small and the sound was wrong and the rubber ball was in his pocket, unsqueezed, the first time in six hours he'd forgotten it was there.

He stared at the screen. His name next to the Mumbai Indians logo. Below Sachin Tendulkar's. Below Harbhajan Singh's. Below Lasith Malinga's.

For ten seconds, Vipin Chaudhary was completely, entirely still.

They had said his name.

---

He did not sleep that night.

That part was the same as all the other nights. The difference was that when he was awake at two in the morning, hands folded on his chest in the dark, the thing he was awake about had already happened.

Mumbai Indians. One hundred thousand dollars.

He'd done the math in the car — thirty-eight point two lakh rupees, approximately, at the current exchange rate, minus tax, minus agent fees if he had an agent which he didn't, which was a question for a later date. The number was inert to him. Too large to feel. He'd grown up counting seven-rupee meals and the number had no floor to rest on, no scale. He could repeat it. He couldn't inhabit it.

Rao had parked at Shivaji Park afterward, and they'd sat in the Maruti with the engine off, the windows fogging slightly in the evening air.

"I don't know what to do now."

He hadn't planned to say it. It was simply the thing that was true.

Rao looked at the pitch covers in the dark for a moment. The engine ticked as it cooled.

"Training camp starts in three weeks. You meet the squad. You play cricket."

"Yes."

"That's all it's been. That's all it stays."

He'd gotten out of the car. Walked to the storage room. The groundskeeper had left the evening newspaper at his door. He'd found his name on page twelve, inside a list of forty-three players sold during the afternoon session. Three words: *Vipin Chaudhary — MI.*

He cut it out with the groundskeeper's scissors. Put it inside the folded paper from his shirt pocket. Put both in the drawer.

In the storage room that night, lying on the cot with the bat beside him and the pendant against his chest, he heard through the wall the groundskeeper's radio — a cricket roundup, the presenter covering the auction results in summary. The presenter's voice, quick and professional: *"...Mumbai Indians also picked up a young all-rounder from the U-19 circuit, Vipin Chaudhary, seventeen, seen as a long-term prospect..."*

A click. The groundskeeper turned the radio off.

A pause.

Then a single knock on Vipin's wall. Not loud. Not demanding. A specific knock: two knuckles, once, solid.

He lay still for a moment.

Then he turned over and knocked back. Once. Same weight.

Nothing else was said. Through the wall, the sound of the groundskeeper settling in for the night — the creak of a chair, the click of a lamp. And then quiet.

Vipin closed his eyes.

Tomorrow, everything changed. Tonight, the storage room was the same. The River crack ran north to south across the ceiling, twenty-three branches, exactly where it had always been.

He touched the pendant.

Slept.
