# Chapter 18: The Space Between

*[Shraddha POV]*

The restaurant was exactly as advertised: fluorescent lighting, four plastic tables, a blackboard menu with three items, and an owner who looked at Shraddha and Vipin as if their arrival was a personal inconvenience he was prepared to manage but not enjoy. The food smell hit them before they reached the door — oil at the right temperature, the specific warmth of something made with attention rather than process.

"The owner is Gajanan." She dropped her voice before they went in. "Don't ask his name. He doesn't like being asked his name by new people."

"How do you know his name?"

"My cousin comes here. She gave me a briefing."

Vipin stopped at the door. "There was a briefing."

"There was extensive preparation." She pushed the door open. "This is important batata vada."

They took the table nearest the wall — only table available, since two of the four were occupied by people eating with the focus of people who had been coming here for years and didn't need conversation to justify the meal. Gajanan appeared at their table and looked at them.

"Two?" Shraddha said.

"How many do you think you can eat?" Vipin said, to her.

"Four," she said. Then, to Gajanan: "Eight total."

Gajanan wrote this down and left without comment. Shraddha watched Vipin look after him. "He won't say anything else until the food comes," she said. "He has a system."

"I respect a system."

"You'd fit in here."

He smiled at this. She noticed the smile: not the public one she'd first seen across a crowded room, not the deflection smile that came with a joke. This was wider, less managed. She'd seen it once before, at the charity dinner, when the England-paneer story finished. It was the smile that showed up when he wasn't thinking about the smile.

She looked at the table instead.

She'd been looking at things instead of him since they'd sat down, because looking directly at him in a non-event context was — she would have said it was just unusual. It was slightly more than unusual. She'd been to enough media events and film sets and industry lunches to understand the mechanics of physically attractive people in rooms, the way the eye moved toward them and you had to redirect. With him, the pull had a different quality. Not just the face or the build — something about the attention he brought, the way he looked at things with the full weight of his focus, the way you felt the focus directed at you.

She was not thinking about this. She was looking at the menu board, which she already knew by heart.

---

The food came in twelve minutes. He ordered a chai without being asked how he wanted it. He ate the first batata vada with the methodical attention she'd predicted — not fast, not rushed, working through it as if the task required full commitment. He ate the way he moved, she thought: like someone who had learned not to take available resources for granted.

"Well?" she said.

"The oil is correct." He finished the first. Started the second. "The outside is right — it's not over-fried. The inside is still moist."

"That's the critical thing."

"Most places sacrifice the inside for the—"

"—afraid of the inside staying wet." She'd finished his sentence before he got there. He looked at her for a moment. She picked up her chai and set it down slightly too hard — not a crash, just a single thud, the table shifting once. Neither of them commented on it.

"If you're afraid of something going wrong, you overcorrect and it goes wrong anyway in a different direction." He looked at the second vada. "This person is not afraid."

She laughed. "You've just described Gajanan's entire philosophy."

"I've described batting."

She watched his hands while he talked — she couldn't help it, they were right there on the table, large and oddly articulate, the way hands got when they were used for something that required precision. She'd shaken hands with cricketers at events before, knew the general template: thick-wristed, strong-gripped, the kind of hands that existed at the far end of physical capability. His were that, plus callused in patterns she didn't recognize as standard — not just the ridge of the bat-grip, but something on the inner fingers that spoke of a different kind of work. There was a scar on his left thumb, a small pale line she hadn't noticed at any distance. His nails were short and she was fairly sure they'd been bitten recently, and equally sure he would deny this if asked.

She was not going to ask.

He was talking about batting technique — she'd asked about the World Cup squad announcement, which had come the previous week, and he'd given her the public answer (standby list, reasonable, one injury away, etc.) and she'd pressed, lightly, once, and he'd shifted into cricket talk as deflection, which she now recognized as his standard move. The cricket talk was genuine — he wasn't performing interest — but it was also a redirect. She let it happen.

She told him about the auditions.

She hadn't planned to. They were talking about the industry generally — she'd asked about his endorsements after the cricket diversion, and he'd mentioned the protein supplement he'd declined, which had opened into a conversation about what you did and didn't put your name on, and somehow that arrived at what she'd said at the charity dinner about her film being fine. He'd heard the "fine" and filed it and then six minutes later came back to it: "What actually happened with the film?"

Nobody from outside the industry had ever asked that. People either already knew or didn't care to know.

She told him about the auditions. The specific rejections. The casting director who'd told her she didn't have "the Kapoor look" — which was baffling, since she was literally a Kapoor — meaning the dark-eyed, intensely glamorous version of the name. Her own look: warm, accessible, the kind of face that invited people to feel comfortable. Not wrong. Just different from what the 1990s had decided was the correct face for a film heroine.

"You nearly quit," he said. He'd been listening without interruption — she'd noticed this, the absence of interruption, which was unusual.

"I nearly quit." She pushed a vada piece around her plate. "There's a version of me that did. She's probably doing something sensible."

"Like what — the psych degree, or the sensible decisions in general?"

"Psychology. I studied psychology for a year before I decided to try auditions seriously. I still read it."

He nodded, slowly, the way he processed information she was learning — taking it fully before responding. "You think about why people do what they do," he said.

"Constantly." She looked at him. "It's useful and occasionally very annoying."

The corner of his mouth went up. "For you or the people you're analyzing?"

"Both." She held his gaze for a second. "You don't like being analyzed."

"I don't like being reduced."

"There's a difference?"

"Analysis respects the complexity. Reduction replaces it with a simpler version." He picked up his tea. "Most analysis I've received is reduction."

She thought about the article she'd read. The one Anjali Mehta had written. She hadn't told him she'd read it — she'd googled him the night after their restaurant reservation was made, two days ago, and found the article in the first page of results, and sat with her laptop for a long time reading about the boy from nowhere. The "Father: Unknown, Mother: Unknown" of the birth certificate. The temple stairs. The pandit's death. The streets. She'd closed the laptop and sat still and looked at the ceiling and thought about the fifteen-minute conversation at the charity dinner and what he hadn't said in it.

She'd already decided she wasn't going to bring this up. He hadn't chosen to tell her. The article existed, but the article wasn't hers to use.

"We're the same kind of different," she said.

He looked at her. Something shifted.

"I mean—" She caught herself. The comparison was there before the filter. His different (she now knew) was temple stairs and streets and a pendant that was the only trace of a family that had vanished. Her different was Bollywood rejection within a family structure that cushioned every fall. The gap between them was vast. "That wasn't—"

"Different is different," he said. Quiet. "You don't have to rank it."

She stopped.

"Being counted out is being counted out. The size of the fall doesn't matter." He looked at the table. "You fell. You got up. That's what happened."

She didn't say anything for a moment. She arranged the condiment jars on the table — by color, which she always did, the habit older than conscious memory. Green chutney on the left, red ketchup in the middle, white salt on the right. She did this while thinking about what he'd just said and not said.

He noticed the arrangement. She saw him notice it. He said nothing.

She decided she liked him very much.

They stayed another forty minutes. Gajanan refilled the chai without being asked, which she interpreted as the highest form of acceptance available in this establishment. Vipin noticed and filed it — she could see him absorbing it, storing it somewhere interior. He had this habit: taking in information without commenting, then producing it later, precisely, at the moment it would mean something. She was beginning to find this both reassuring and slightly alarming.

When they got up to leave, he paid before she'd found her wallet. No performance to it — no glance at her while handling the transaction, no gesture toward generosity. Just a fact accomplished quietly. She decided not to say anything about it. Some things became smaller when you narrated them.

On the footpath outside, the evening traffic had thickened. She'd parked two lanes over and they stood for a moment while a family with a pushchair navigated around them. His shoulder was beside her shoulder. Not touching. The exact distance of two people standing near each other without having decided anything about it.

"Same time next week?" she said. She'd meant to phrase it differently — something lighter, something with more room. It came out direct.

He looked at her for a beat too long. "Yeah," he said.

She walked to her car. She didn't look back. She knew he'd watch — not anything she could justify with evidence, just the quality of the air behind her. At the car, she put the key in without looking and managed it right on the first try. This felt like a personal victory.

She sat in the car for a moment before starting the engine. Outside, the street did its ordinary evening work: an autorickshaw cut across traffic, a man on a bicycle carrying two stacked tiffin containers navigated the gap with the calm of someone who had done it a thousand times. She turned the radio on. She turned it off. She started the engine.

*[Vipin POV]*

The texts had started as functional. Scores, match results, the kind of cricket-world information he sent out of habit to people who were adjacent to cricket through him. She sent back film things: trailers she'd found interesting, a piece about an actor whose process she was studying, once a photograph of a sunset from a rooftop where they were shooting that he looked at for longer than the content required.

They migrated to other things without announcement.

She discovered he read — the groundskeeper's newspaper habit had become a book habit around age sixteen, which was something he hadn't mentioned to anyone because it didn't fit the template people had for him. He was currently reading Shantaram, which was set in Mumbai and was massive and slightly ridiculous and he was four hundred pages in and committed despite himself. She was reading a psychology book about attachment theory. They sent each other passages.

*What's your attachment style?* she texted.

*Whatever style avoids answering that question.*

Three dots. Then: *Avoidant. I know, I know.*

He read this twice. *What's yours?*

*Anxious, officially. But I'm working on it. We make a terrible pair theoretically.*

He looked at the word "pair." He put his phone face down on the table.

He picked it up. *Theoretically.*

Three dots for a long time. Then: *Theoretically.*

He smiled at his phone in his flat in Dadar at eleven at night and felt twenty years old, which he was.

She sent a voice note of herself laughing at something on set — she hadn't explained what, just the laugh, running for about six seconds. He'd played it on his headphones during the bus to Nagpur and Ravi had said "what?" and he'd said "nothing."

*You laugh like something surprised you every time,* he texted.

*Is that good?*

*I don't know. I keep listening to it.*

Three dots. Then three more dots. Then: *I'm going to pretend you didn't say that so I don't have to figure out what to do with it.*

*Good plan.*

He was, technically, being responsible. The word "theoretically" stayed in his head for three days without his consent.

She told him about her mother in increments. The way her mother was the quiet correction to her father's volume — the person she called when something was real, not the person she called for advice, but the person whose voice reorganized her thoughts when she was spinning. He listened to this description and understood it the way you understood things that were true without having experienced them. He told her about Rao, carefully: the coach who had found him, the technical rigor, the cold chai, the morning sessions. He described Rao as a coach. Not a father figure. The distinction mattered to him.

She heard the distinction. She didn't comment on it. He was grateful for the not-commenting.

He went silent for four days in November.

He hadn't planned to. A Ranji match: he'd batted at four, there was a partnership he'd needed to protect, the ball was doing things in the second session, and he'd come down the track at a medium-pacer who had no right to bowl him and been bowled for a duck. Clean bowled. The stumps hit in a way that felt specific. He walked off. He showered. He got on the bus. He went to his flat.

He didn't train the next morning. He sat in the flat and ate dal from the dhaba and watched MasterChef Australia and didn't talk to anyone.

The dhaba packaged its containers in a specific way — foam box, thin plastic bag, a rubber band. The rubber band had a particular smell when you stretched it: synthetic, slightly chemical, the same as the cleaning products the municipal workers had used in the temple the morning after the pandit died. He'd been seven. He'd sat in the corner of the room and watched them work, not sure what he was supposed to do with himself, and the smell had filled the space where the incense used to be. He ate his dal. He didn't know why he remembered this. He watched two contestants argue about the correct temperature for tempering chocolate and eventually the rubber band smell went away.

She texted on day three: *You okay?*

He looked at the text for a long time.

He responded on day four: *Yeah. Training been intense. Sorry.* He added a joke — something about the MasterChef contestants misunderstanding the role of spice in Indian cooking — and it didn't quite land because it was pitched at the wrong level, too broad for someone who knew him, a public joke sent to a private person.

She replied: *Okay. I'm here if you want to talk. No pressure.*

He knew she knew something had happened. He also knew she wasn't going to push. He filed this — the texture of her not-pushing — as information about her, and also as something he didn't know what to do with.

The shame wasn't about the duck. It was about the fear that he was only interesting to people when he was performing. The cricket, the jokes, the quick reading of every room. Take away the performance and there was — what? A man who watched cooking shows in a flat in Dadar and had a drawer full of things that constituted his entire history. He was afraid, specifically, that this was insufficient for her.

He didn't say this. He never said this.

What he thought about instead, lying on the flat's single mattress with the fan running at three, was a conversation she'd referenced once — something about watching her father receive an award at a ceremony, how the room had looked at him with the particular warmth reserved for people the industry claimed as its own. She had been in that room too, unknown then, and had watched and filed it and gone home and practiced the same monologue for the fortieth time. She'd told him this as illustration, not complaint. But underneath the illustration was the thing itself: the experience of being in a room where everyone else had been recognized and you hadn't been yet. He knew what that room felt like from the inside. He had been sitting in it for two years.

He understood her. He was afraid she would understand him back and find insufficient material.

He went back to training the next morning and bowled until his shoulder ached and worked the problem out through the body the way he'd always worked problems out.

---

The coffee shop in Bandra was her territory and he was aware of it from the moment he arrived.

Not unwelcome — she'd chosen it deliberately, he understood, because it was comfortable for her, because comfort was what she offered. But the coffee shop was full of film people in their natural habitat: mid-morning, the specific Bandra industry crowd of late risers and people between shoots and assistants with laptops who came here to work and be seen working. She knew most of them. He knew none of them. People recognized him — the IPL had made his face part of the city — but they looked at him with the mild surprise of a cricketer found outside his usual context.

She got a table near the window. They talked for two hours. He lost track of the time, which didn't happen to him — he always knew approximately where he was in an hour. She touched his arm when she was making a point and his brain produced a full second of empty, which was a second he'd never experienced outside cricket's time-stretching. She noticed. She didn't comment. She moved the conversation on.

She talked about her next project — a film her mother had forwarded to her agent, which she hadn't read yet but probably would, since her mother had a reliable instinct for what was worth her time. She talked about this with a casual certainty that interested him. The casual certainty of someone who had family in their corner, a net beneath the trapeze. He was not resentful of this. He just catalogued it — the way she moved through the industry as someone who belonged there, even when it had rejected her, the familial architecture around her that made the rejection temporary instead of permanent. He had had Rao. That was something. He didn't rank them. He just noticed the difference.

When they walked out, the evening was starting — six o'clock, the Bandra light going golden, the street crowded with the early-evening density of people moving between places. She was beside him. The crowd required proximity. Her shoulder was close enough to his that the gap between them was negligible.

Her hand was right there. His hand was right there. The space between them was a centimeter, not a decision, just the arithmetic of being in a crowd together.

He made the decision.

The gap stayed. He didn't take it. The catastrophizing did what it did: *she grew up in Juhu, she has a career, she knows everybody, this ends with her discovering there's nothing behind the jokes and nothing will be more embarrassing than that.* His hand stayed at his side. The centimeter became three centimeters. He registered the moment he'd created.

She didn't say anything. She kept walking. But her walking shifted — a fraction of a degree, a posture adjustment so small that only someone paying the kind of attention he paid to small things would have noticed. She'd felt the decision happen. She hadn't pushed.

At the intersection, she got her car. He got an auto. They said goodnight — warm, friendly, the specific warmth of two people who had been laughing ten minutes ago and were now navigating the cooling-off of an evening that had arrived at a threshold and passed through it without acknowledging it. The distance in the goodbye was larger than the distance in the hello.

In the auto, he watched the city do its evening thing. The odometer on the auto's meter read 47,000 when they set off. Forty-seven was his jersey number. He noticed this and dismissed it — signs were for people who believed in signs, and he didn't believe in signs, he believed in numbers that appeared and stayed in your head all night whether you'd agreed to let them stay or not.

---

The World Cup squad was announced in January, three months after Bandra.

He heard it from Ravi, who called at noon and said: "I saw the squad" in the voice of someone calling because they wanted to get there before the news did.

He was not in the squad. He was on the standby list — one injury, one unexpected form dip, one medical report away from selection. The selectors had cited his "limited international experience." Rao's reading, later: the real constraint was the older guard protecting the squad composition, the BCCI politics he'd always been warned about but had never fully believed would apply to him specifically.

He read the squad list twice. He knew every name. Six of the bowlers in the first fifteen had played against him in domestic cricket, and he had the numbers on all six — economy rates, average wickets per match, the specific deliveries each one struggled with under pressure. He was better than two of them. He was aware this was not how squad selection worked and he was aware of it anyway, the arithmetic sitting in his chest like a stone he hadn't agreed to carry.

The standby list had four names. He was one of four people who might be called. He held on to this the way he held on to things that were technically true but practically insufficient.

He went to the nets that afternoon and bowled. He bowled at the stumps — the single stump he placed at a good length, the stump with dents from months of repeated accuracy work. He bowled fast. Faster. The stump went down and he set it back up and bowled again. His shoulder started producing a sound he recognized as the precursor to a different sound he'd learned to avoid, but he bowled three more balls before Rao, who had been watching in silence from the boundary cot, walked out to the pitch and stood at the crease.

Rao didn't say anything. He just stood there, which was the whole point. You couldn't bowl at someone standing at the crease without bowling at them, and he wasn't going to bowl at Rao. He stopped. He walked back to his mark. He picked up his kit.

"Tomorrow," Rao said.

"Tomorrow," he agreed.

Shraddha texted that evening: *I saw the squad. I'm sorry.*

He looked at the text. He typed: *Standby. One hamstring away from the World Cup.*

She replied: *You'll get there. Not because of luck. Because you're you.*

He read it four times. He had no response to that. He put the phone on the table and drank his chai and read it one more time.

He had wanted the squad. He had wanted it for eleven years — since the first time he'd understood what an India shirt meant, since Rao had told him once that the final measure of a cricketer was the cap with the map of India on it. He was not in the squad. He was on a list of people who would be called if the first fifteen couldn't be. This was not the thing.

But the World Cup final was at Wankhede. His stadium. The one where 35,000 people had chanted his name two years ago. He was going to be in Mumbai in April when the final happened, and he was going to be watching from outside the boundary rope, and this was a fact he could not change and was going to live with anyway.

He put the phone down. He opened his book — 416 pages into Shantaram, the part where the city started exerting its specific gravity — and read until midnight. His phone's screen lit up once, twice, while he read. He didn't check it until he was done with the chapter.

She'd sent two more texts. The second one was a voice note. He pressed play.

She was humming. Just humming — a film song he recognized but couldn't name, half a minute of her in a car somewhere at night with the windows down, the Mumbai traffic noise underneath, the note carrying cleanly above it. The sound of someone who didn't know they were being recorded, or had decided not to mind. She'd sent it because she'd been humming and he'd come to mind, which was a thing she probably hadn't planned to tell him but had told him anyway.

He played it three times. He put his phone face down on the bedside table.

He lay in the dark. The squad. The stump with the dents in it — he'd been staring at those dents all afternoon, each one a ball he'd placed correctly, and it hadn't been enough. Rao standing at the crease and not saying anything, which was worse than anything he could have said. The World Cup final at Wankhede in April.

A woman who arranged condiments by color without appearing to notice she was doing it. The way she'd finished his sentence. The specific silence after she got in her car.

The hand he had not taken.

He was twenty years old and it was the middle of January in Mumbai and the cricket season was continuing and the World Cup was coming. And something else was either beginning or ending, and he couldn't tell which, and the uncertainty had its own particular shape.

He counted the ceiling tiles. Forty-seven.

He put the light on, looked at the ceiling. Counted again. Forty-seven. He counted a third time, moving his finger slightly along each tile to keep track, the way he counted deliveries in a spell when the umpire's count slipped his mind. Still forty-seven.

He was aware this meant nothing. The flat had forty-seven ceiling tiles because whoever had installed them had installed exactly that many. The odometer on the auto had 47,000 kilometers because it had covered exactly that distance. These were numbers. Numbers appeared. He happened to have forty-seven on his back, which was itself a number chosen by a kit coordinator who'd given him the last available jersey two years ago.

He turned the light off. He did not check whether this was a sign. Signs were for people who believed in signs. He was not going to think about it.

He thought about it. He thought about the voice note. He thought about the hand he hadn't taken. He thought about Rao standing at the crease in the afternoon light without saying a single word, which was somehow the most precise thing anyone had said to him all month.

Eventually he slept. The tiles were still there in the morning, forty-seven of them, indifferent.
