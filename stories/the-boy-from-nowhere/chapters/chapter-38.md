# Chapter 38: The Wedding

**Timeline**: 2020 (age 30)

---

The lockdown arrived in the third week of March like a word someone said wrong at a party — everyone stopping, the room going quiet, and then the slow rearrangement of all the furniture.

Vipin's first experience of it was practical: the IPL postponed, the MI WhatsApp group filling with links to government advisories, a text from the team physio about maintaining fitness with bodyweight exercises in whatever space was available. His flat in Bandra was 900 square feet. He had done the arithmetic: enough room for a short run-up, nowhere near enough for a proper sprint. He moved the coffee table on day two and did lengths of the living room at six in the morning while Shraddha was still sleeping, which she found out about on day four when she woke early and stood in the bedroom doorway watching him with an expression that suggested she was calibrating whether to find this endearing or alarming.

"You're running," she said.

"Length work."

"In the flat."

"The coffee table's moved. I have eight meters."

She looked at the coffee table against the far wall, at the small rug now rolled and propped beside it, at him in his running shoes standing on bare floor. "You moved the furniture," she said. "At six in the morning."

"The furniture was in the way."

She went back to bed. When he came in from the living room an hour later she was awake and reading, and without looking up she said: "Next time move the furniture the night before. It sounds like someone dropping a car."

He had not realized the coffee table made that much noise. He moved it the night before, after that.

---

The thing about stillness — enforced, total, the kind that came from all directions at once — was that Vipin had no practice with it.

His adult life had been motion. Training camps, IPL seasons, India tours, the particular velocity of a career that had never offered a genuine pause. Even the injury had come with its own motion: physios, rehab, the Ranji circuit, the long hard road back. There had been nights of stillness — the bathroom floors, the drawer, the pendant in hotel rooms — but those were stillness inside motion, punctuation in an ongoing sentence. This was different. The sentence had stopped.

He found himself, in the first two weeks, doing strange things. He reorganized his kit bag twice. He read the entire back-of-the-packet text on Shraddha's bottle of shampoo one morning in the bathroom out of pure ambient attention. He counted the tiles in the flat twice — once by row, once by column — and arrived at different totals both times, which meant he'd made an error, and the error bothered him for a day in a background, persistent way before he accepted that the tiles were not going to resolve this.

He also discovered that Shraddha, at home in a way she never quite was when she was in the middle of a shoot, was different. Not different in quality — the same person, the same reading glasses, the same half-finished bowls of fruit — but expanded somehow, like a word you've only seen in text finally heard spoken aloud. She cooked badly and with great enthusiasm, made herself available for film script discussions via phone at inconvenient hours while simultaneously making him chai, fell asleep on the couch in the afternoons in the specific position of someone who does not plan to fall asleep, and talked, more than usual, about things that were not the news or the industry or the pandemic numbers. About her grandmother's house in Pune. About a film she'd seen in 2009 and still thought about. About what she would do, she said one evening, completely unprompted, if she had not become an actress — the question arrived while he was watching her peel an orange at the kitchen table and she held the orange peel mid-strip and said: "I think I would have been a translator."

"Of what?"

"Languages. I'd have liked to know more of them. I know four if you count the Gujarati I can understand but not really speak, which I don't think counts." She resumed the peel. "I like the idea of being the person who sits between two languages and makes it work."

He thought about this. "That's basically what you do."

She looked at him.

"You sit between the story and the audience," he said. "You make the translation."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she pointed the orange peel at him. "That's a good one, Chaudhary," she said. "Don't make me think you're clever, I'm not ready."

He was. He'd been clever for thirty years. She knew this. But she gave him the moment anyway.

---

The wedding had been planned — mostly in outline, everything TBD — for sometime in late 2020. The venue question had produced three separate family group chats, each operating at full volume, which Vipin had observed from a careful remove. The Kapoor side had a shortlist. The MI contingent's unofficial contributions had been enthusiastic and structurally chaotic. Rao had said, when asked, that the venue should have good grass and then clarified, when pressed, that by this he meant the natural kind, not the artificial turf they were putting everywhere now, which nobody had been asking about but was a real concern.

The pandemic simplified everything.

The law said fifty people. The world said no large gatherings. The logic of it was brutal and clarifying in the way that constraints often are: if you could only have fifty, you had to choose. The guest list, when they made it together one evening in April — her laptop open on the coffee table she'd helped him move back to its regular position — was not a negotiation. It was a distillation. They went through each name with the same question: *is this someone who has actually been there?* Most of the list resolved itself quickly.

"Ravi and Priya," Shraddha said.

"Obviously."

"Rao."

"Yes."

"Kavita Aunty."

"Yes."

She typed. "MI teammates. How many?"

"Four. Karthik, Mohit, the two Pandyas."

"Baba and Maa. Sunita Devi."

"Sunita will need the full day's travel from Hisar accounted for. She'll need to come the day before." He paused. "She'll complain about the travel. And the humidity. And the hotel."

"She can complain about everything she wants," Shraddha said. "She's the grandmother." She looked at the laptop screen. "Keshav?"

He had not thought about Keshav.

"The groundskeeper," she said. "From Shivaji Park. You've mentioned him."

"He's — I'd have to ask Rao. He doesn't have a phone."

"Then ask Rao." She typed something. "He should be there."

He looked at her. She was in her reading glasses, properly on her face now rather than pushed to the top of her head, which was the version she wore when she was actually concentrating. The evening light from the window cut across the table. Outside, the Bandra street had the specific quality it had developed over lockdown weeks — quieter than itself, the usual register of auto-rickshaws and building voices reduced to something more skeletal, like a song heard without the percussion.

"You know," he said, "I thought we'd be upset about the smaller wedding."

"Are you?"

He considered it honestly. "No."

"I'm not either." She looked at the screen. "I think I would have spent six months planning something enormous and the morning of I would have looked out at five hundred people and known that half of them came because my father is who he is, not because they love me." She paused. "This list is the people who love us."

He looked at the list. Thirty-eight names. They had space for twelve more.

"It's enough," he said.

"It's exactly enough," she said, and closed the laptop, and that was the wedding planning done.

---

The IPL happened in September, relocated to UAE, three teams in bio-bubbles in Dubai and Abu Dhabi and Sharjah, daily COVID tests, empty stadiums, the particular strangeness of cricket played without crowd noise — the clean thwack of ball on bat carrying in a way it never did in full grounds, the players' voices audible from the boundary, the whole thing feeling like a practice match that had accidentally become real.

MI won. Fifth title. Vipin was part of it — captain's innings of 58 not-out in the final, two wickets in the last three overs, the numbers correct, the execution there. The celebration in the dressing room afterward was contained. The trophy was there. The COVID protocols meant masks at photo time, some of the boys in face shields, the usual dressing-room chaos replaced with sanitizer bottles and two-meter spacing attempted and largely abandoned in the joy of the thing.

He held the trophy and thought about what Shraddha had said, early in the pandemic, about the translator's job — sitting between two languages and making it work. He was doing that now. Between the version of this that was real joy, earned and genuine, and the version that felt strange and somehow borrowed — winning a cricket tournament while the world was doing what the world was doing. Both were true. Neither cancelled the other. He held the trophy and was glad and was also glad to put it down.

Later, in his hotel room, he called Shraddha.

"How is it?" she said.

"Good. Strange. Both."

"Both is allowed," she said. "Come home soon."

He did.

---

The morning of the wedding arrived in December with the particular insolence of Mumbai winter — which was to say not cold, not truly, but cool enough in the early hours that it had the quality of winter without the inconvenience of it. Six degrees, maybe seven of relief from the humidity. The jasmine garlands on every doorway of the venue, a farm property outside Alibaug, made the whole place smell like the inside of a temple, which was appropriate.

He was in a room on the ground floor getting dressed when Rao arrived.

Rao arrived the way Rao did everything: fifteen minutes early, in a white kurta-pajama he had clearly bought specifically for this occasion because Vipin had never seen it before, his reading glasses on his forehead. He stood in the doorway and looked at Vipin, who was in the white sherwani, the dupatta not yet arranged, the turban on the table because he had not worked out the turban.

"The turban," Vipin said.

"Give it here."

Rao took the turban, assessed it with the expression he gave to difficult field settings, and began to fix it. Vipin stood still. This was a domestic activity outside his experience and apparently within Rao's, which surprised him until he remembered that Rao had been at various weddings over sixty years of life and had presumably been this specific kind of useful on more than one occasion.

"Hold still."

"I'm holding still."

"You're not." Rao made an adjustment. "Your left shoulder is up. Stand normally."

He adjusted the shoulder. Rao worked. The room had the morning smell of the farm — jasmine from outside, something earthier underneath, the dew still burning off the grass. Through the window, Vipin could see a corner of the courtyard where the mandap had been set up, the red and gold cloth bright in the early light.

"Keshav's here," Rao said, without looking up from the turban.

"I know. I saw him at breakfast."

"He told the venue's grounds staff that their lawn maintenance was inadequate." Rao's voice was neutral. "He spent approximately twenty minutes explaining this. The grounds staff took notes, I think out of courtesy."

Vipin absorbed this. "Did he wear the kurta?"

"He is wearing the kurta." A pause. "He is wearing it with some distress. But he is wearing it."

"That's all I wanted."

Rao finished the turban. Stood back. The reading glasses were still on his forehead, which was where they lived — Vipin had never, in fifteen years, seen them sit anywhere else. Rao looked at him the way Rao looked at things he had been working on for a long time and that had arrived at the point they were always going to arrive at. Not surprise. Recognition.

"Good," Rao said.

The word had the weight it always had when Rao used it this way — compressed, complete, everything requiring no elaboration.

"Sunita complained about the humidity," Vipin said.

"How does she know? It's barely eight o'clock."

"She's from Haryana. She knew the moment the car crossed the Maharashtra border."

Rao almost smiled. "She'll enjoy herself."

"She'll enjoy herself while telling everyone what's wrong with everything." He looked at himself in the mirror. The sherwani was right. The turban was right. He looked like a man getting married, which was what he was. "She told me this morning that the marble on the venue floor was inferior to Rajasthani marble and she'd been to enough weddings to know the difference."

"Has she?"

"I genuinely don't know. She may have looked it up specifically to say this." He looked at his reflection. "She was up at four in the morning. She's been up since four."

"First time in Mumbai," Rao said.

"First time in Mumbai." He thought about Sunita Devi, seventy-four years old, arthritis in both hands, first time leaving Hisar for anything south of Delhi, and the sheer weight of will it had taken for her to come. She'd called him twice in November saying she would come and twice saying she wasn't sure she could manage the journey, and he'd said each time: only come if you want to, no obligation. She'd shown up at the venue yesterday afternoon with a steel trunk and a list of complaints about the auto-wallah's driving from the station that had lasted forty-five minutes and been the funniest thing that had happened to him all month. "She cried when she arrived," he said. "She saw the jasmine and she cried. Then she told the front desk their check-in system was slow."

Rao picked up his cold chai from the table and took a sip. Even today, somehow, it was cold. Some constants were architectural.

"You're ready?" Rao said.

Vipin looked at himself one last time.

He was thirty years old. The pendant was against his chest, under the sherwani — he had put it on first, before the sherwani, and adjusted the sherwani over it. It made a faint shape beneath the fabric if you knew it was there. He touched it once, through the cloth.

"Ready," he said.

---

The horse was a grey, approximately fifteen hands, with the stoic expression of an animal that has been involved in many baraats and found them acceptable but not inspiring. Someone had put marigolds in its mane, which the horse bore with professional dignity.

Vipin had been on a horse exactly once before, age seventeen, at a camp in Pune where the idea had been that cricket players would benefit from equestrian activities for balance and they'd lasted forty minutes before the coach running the camp admitted this had been a poor theory. What he remembered about horses was that they were larger than they appeared in photographs and you could feel the muscle in their back through the saddle in a way that made them feel like a controlled geological event.

He stood at the mounting block — a wooden step the venue had provided — and looked at the horse.

"It's a horse," Ravi said, from somewhere behind him. Ravi was in a navy blue sherwani and had his sunglasses on despite it being December and overcast. He had also been crying since approximately seven AM, which he was managing by making observations about everything in a voice slightly louder than necessary.

"I know it's a horse."

"Then get on the horse."

"I know how horses work."

"You're standing next to it like you're waiting for an introduction."

Vipin put his foot in the stirrup, swung up, found his seat. The horse adjusted under his weight, took a step sideways, decided this was acceptable, and became still again. From up here, the courtyard was a different geography — the guests assembled in a loose crowd, the marigold archway visible at the far end of the path, the mandap beyond. He could see Priya pulling at Ravi's sleeve about the sunglasses, which was correct, the sunglasses were wrong.

He looked at the assembled crowd: the MI boys in their sherwanis, talking loudly and pointing at something near the catering setup. Kavita Aunty in a dark green saree talking to one of Shraddha's film friends. Sunita Devi in a red dupatta seated in a chair that someone had very wisely provided, her arthritis hands in her lap, watching everything with the sharp attention of a woman who would have opinions about it later. Keshav beside her, in the kurta, looking deeply uncomfortable and periodically glancing at the lawn with the expression of a man forming a specific assessment.

And Rao. Who was standing where a father would stand.

Not next to the horse. Not on the dais. In the place beside him, on foot, where the baraat walked — the space that in a regular wedding would be occupied by the groom's father, who would walk beside the horse, and who in this wedding was occupied by a sixty-nine-year-old man in a white kurta with reading glasses on his forehead and cold chai somewhere and fifteen years of cold chai and nets and corrected shoulders and the one word *good* used with all the weight in the world.

Rao looked at him.

Vipin reached down and tapped the horse's flank once. Then, without quite deciding to, he put three fingers to the mounting block and tapped it three times — the tap he did before his run-up, the three-beat rhythm that meant *I'm here, I'm present, this is real.* He'd been doing it for twenty years. He did it now.

Rao saw it. He didn't comment on it. His face acknowledged it with something that wasn't quite a smile and wasn't quite not one.

The shehnai started.

The baraat began.

---

The mandap was red and gold, the fire in the center already lit, the priest waiting with the patience of a man who had done this many hundreds of times and found it each time containing something he hadn't expected. Shraddha arrived from the left entrance of the venue on her father's arm.

Vipin, from where he stood at the mandap, watched her come.

She was in a red and gold lehenga, the kind of red that photographed poorly because photographs couldn't hold it — the red you had to be in the same room as, the red that in the December light from the venue's open sides looked like something older than color. Her hair had been done by someone who knew how to make elaborate things look simple. She had the gold ring on her left hand, which he could see from this distance because he was looking for it, because it had been there since November 2019 and would be there past whatever came next.

She was not looking at him yet. She was looking at the path, the particular focus of someone walking in formal clothes who does not want to catch their heel on the stone. Then she looked up.

She found him at forty feet and the expression was the specific one — not the camera smile, not the public warmth she deployed correctly, but the direct look, the one that was just for him, the one she'd been giving him since she was twenty-four years old in a coffee shop explaining a misquoted interview. It was the look that said: *I know exactly who you are, including the parts you think you're hiding, and I'm still here.*

Shakti, walking her in, had his head up, chest out, the walk of a man doing the most important version of a thing he had done in many films but never in his own life. His face was doing the thing Shraddha called the Shakti cycle, but slowly, the cycle extended, the theatrical surprise long past, the assessment done, and what remained on his face was just the sentiment, just the man.

He was crying. Shakti Kapoor, opening scene of three hundred films, the man who had made audiences cry and laugh and hold their breath for forty years — he was crying at his daughter's wedding without any attempt to be otherwise. Shivangi, two steps behind, was not crying but had the compressed expression of someone not crying through technical effort.

Ravi, behind Vipin, made a sound.

"I'm not crying," Ravi said, to no one in particular.

"Nobody said anything," Priya said.

"Pre-emptively."

Shraddha arrived at the mandap and Shakti placed her hand — her right hand, gold ring on the left, the ring he had put there in a kitchen with suds and November light — and put it in Vipin's, and Shakti looked at him for a moment with the eyes that had done so many things across nine years, and said nothing, which was everything.

The ceremony began.

---

The saat phere took approximately an hour.

The priest spoke in Sanskrit with periodic Hindi explanations, his voice the steady cadence of someone who had done this hundreds of times but had not let repetition flatten the thing itself. The fire was real fire, jasmine-scented wood that gave off more smoke than expected, which made Vipin's eyes water in the second phera, which he was grateful for because it gave cover for what was happening to his eyes anyway.

The first phera: prosperity. The second: strength. The third: growth.

The fourth phera was for happiness and family.

They came around the fire, the dupatta tied to her lehenga pulling slightly at the moment he turned, and the priest said the Sanskrit words for family — *grihastha, parivaar, the making of home* — and Vipin's eyes went full.

Not overflow — he blinked. Once. The controlled version, the version he'd been using since he was old enough to understand that there was a controlled version. But the fullness was there, the particular combination of things that had arrived in this moment from the specific distance they'd traveled: the temple stairs, the storage room ceiling with its twenty-three cracks, the pandit's dry warm hands, the pendant, Rao at Shivaji Park with cold chai, the drawer, the note that said *I want them to say my name,* the photograph of Rajveer and Kamla with their held-back smiles and large hands, Sunita Devi's chai with the unidentified spice, Shakti's arms in the garden. The ring on her soapy finger in the kitchen.

Shraddha, moving beside him around the fire, was looking at the ground with the care of her feet in the lehenga, but she looked up in that moment — as if she'd felt the shift in him through the dupatta and the phera and the smoke — and looked at his face and saw.

She squeezed his hand.

Once. The specific once.

Her eyes were full too.

Behind them, from somewhere in the assembled fifty, Shakti made a sound that was definitively crying. Shivangi said something low that was probably *I told you to bring an extra handkerchief.* From his right, he could hear Ravi's breathing doing something complicated that Ravi would describe later, and for weeks afterward, as sinusitis.

They completed the fourth phera.

The fifth, sixth, seventh.

The priest said the final words. The fire did what fire does at the end of things — dropped lower, the immediate intensity past, the steady burn that would continue.

The fifty people in attendance made the kind of noise that fifty people make when they are precisely the right fifty: not the roar of five hundred strangers, but the warmer and more particular sound of people who knew exactly who this was, what it had cost, what it meant.

---

He found the moment between the ceremony and the meal.

The guests had moved to the dining area, the Kapoor and MI contingents having established a natural boundary that would dissolve in twenty minutes as Ravi introduced himself to everyone on the wrong side of it. Sunita Devi had been installed in a comfortable chair with a plate of food and a grandchild-surrogate in the form of Kavita Aunty, who had sat beside her with the specific tact of someone who understood exactly what this old woman had traveled for and was going to make sure it was received properly. Keshav had been located near the catering area telling one of the caterers something about the serving temperature of the dal.

Vipin found a corner of the venue near the far wall, where the jasmine had been trained up a lattice and was doing its job aggressively, the smell of it close and specific. He stood there for a moment. Not hiding — he could see the dining area, the white lights strung between the walls, the sound of it carrying. He just needed sixty seconds of his own.

He reached inside the sherwani.

The pendant was warm, the way it always was. He held it in his hand and looked at the jasmine on the lattice — there was a section where the vine had grown into itself and produced a double layer of flowers, which was not the plan but was better than the plan, which was an argument about planning that he was too happy to make right now.

"Rajveer," he said.

Aloud. The way he knew how to do this now — not the first time at Wankhede, pendant held and silence doing the work, not the second time in the bathroom corridor after the IPL, the sweat still on his face. The third time. A different register. He knew what he was doing with this.

"Kamla."

He held the pendant.

"I got married today." He paused, because the next thing was the thing he wanted to say correctly. "She said yes to the kitchen proposal. Her caption was 'kitchen proposal' because she thought it was funny and true." He looked at the jasmine. A bee was working the double section, unhurried, December being apparently no obstacle for bees at this venue. "Her name is Shraddha. She's been saying my name like it belongs to me since I was twenty. She has reading glasses she loses everywhere and she approaches washing up with the enthusiasm of someone who doesn't fundamentally believe in washing up." He stopped. Started again. "She knows about the drawer. She knows about you. She knows and she stayed."

The pendant was warm in his palm.

There was no answer. There never was. He had stopped waiting for one somewhere around the second pendant conversation, had accepted that the conversation moved one direction only — him, outward, into the space that held the names of two people who were both the beginning and the permanent unanswered question. The mystery was still there. Sunita had told him what she knew — Rajveer's hands, Kamla's laugh, the field outside Hisar, the year they'd left — and the rest remained what it was. He would probably never know the rest. He had decided, somewhere in the years of living with this, that the not-knowing was the shape of the thing, not a gap in it. Some things were made of their own mysteries.

"Rao walked beside me," he said. "On the horse. In the place where a father would be." He held the pendant. "You'd have liked him. He's unreasonably certain about yorkers."

The sounds of the wedding carried from the dining area. Ravi's laugh — the big version — audible over everything else.

"The fifty people in there," he said, "are the right fifty." He looked at the jasmine. "You couldn't be here. I know that. But you're here." He touched the pendant. "You've always been here."

He stood in the jasmine corner for another moment.

Then he tucked the pendant back inside the sherwani and straightened his turban, which had gone slightly crooked in the hour since Rao had fixed it, and turned back toward the wedding.

---

The meal was everything. The dal was at the correct temperature, Keshav's consultation apparently having worked. The MI boys had formed an alliance with Shakti's film industry friends on the grounds of a shared interest in cricket data, which had not been predicted. Ravi had made three toasts, each one longer than the last, each one making Shraddha laugh in the involuntary way, the pre-decided laugh, the one that meant something was actually right. Priya had at some point ended up in conversation with Sunita Devi and they had been there for forty minutes, which Vipin watched from across the room with something approaching awe — the internal logic of those two women finding each other across the room was not immediately apparent and might never be explained.

Sunita Devi, in her red dupatta, had eaten well and complained about the dessert options. She had also held Shraddha's hand for a full two minutes at the table, saying nothing, which Shraddha had received with the complete stillness of someone receiving something that was being given at full weight.

Keshav ate three plates and said little and looked at the lawn twice and appeared, overall, to be having an adequate time, which from Keshav was high praise.

Rao sat beside Kavita and drank two cups of chai that arrived, improbably, at the correct temperature for once, which he mentioned once to Vipin in passing as if this were a minor administrative anomaly worth recording.

Vipin moved through the evening in the specific way you move through the best things: unhurriedly, with the awareness that the thing is happening and will not happen again in exactly this form and the awareness itself is part of it. He talked to everyone. He ate three portions of paneer because the paneer was exceptional and he was not going to be shy about it. He stood with Shraddha at the center of rooms and at the edge of rooms and in one brief moment between courses on the empty dais where the mandap was now cooling, just the two of them, her hand in his.

"How's the paneer," she said.

"The paneer is exceptional."

"You've had three plates."

"I'm aware." He looked at her. The red and gold of the lehenga in the evening light. The ring. The reading glasses, for once, not on her head — they were in a bag somewhere, which was the correct call for the lehenga. "Did you know Priya and Sunita have been talking for forty minutes?"

"I know." She looked across the room at the two of them. "Priya speaks a little Haryanvi, apparently. From her mother's side. Sunita Devi nearly fell off her chair."

"That explains it."

"It explains nothing, actually. They'd have talked anyway." She looked at him. "Sunita looked at me at the table and I—" She stopped. Not the emotional stop — the choosing stop. "She looked at me the way your grandmother looks at you," she said. "Like she was trying to memorize something."

He was quiet.

"I think she was," he said.

Shraddha squeezed his hand once and he squeezed back and they stood on the cooling dais while the wedding moved around them and the jasmine continued its assault on the night air.

---

Later — much later, the meal done, the guests dispersing in the organized chaos of late-night departures, Ravi and Priya in the car to the hotel Ravi had already named "the best wedding hotel I've ever stayed in," Sunita Devi escorted by Kavita with the steel trunk and the catalog of complaints she was already preparing for the morning, Keshav walking to his car with Rao and saying something about the lawn that Rao was receiving with the patience of a man with more important things on his mind — Vipin and Shraddha were in the room that had been set up for them.

She was at the mirror, taking the elaborate things out of her hair with the specific focus of someone who has been in formal clothes for eight hours and is now conducting a return to self. He was sitting on the edge of the bed in the sherwani, the turban off, the dupatta folded on the chair beside him, the pendant against his chest.

"Ravi cried during the fourth phera," she said, without turning from the mirror.

"He announced he wasn't crying. Pre-emptively."

"Pre-emptively."

"Before anyone could say it." He looked at the turban on the chair. "He cried at your wedding to Aditya in *Dhadak*, he told me. He was at a screening and he—"

"He cried at a film."

"He cried at a fictional wedding. In a cinema."

She laughed. "That's extremely Ravi."

"He's consistent."

She took out the last pin and her hair came down, and she looked at herself in the mirror for a moment, and then at him in the mirror, and the expression was the quiet register, the one only for the things that mattered.

"Chaudhary," she said.

"Yeah."

"Come here."

He crossed the room.

The night outside the window was the particular quality of December near the coast — cool enough, the jasmine still working, the last sounds of the departing wedding audible as a diminishing register of car doors and voices and then the farm's actual sounds underneath: something moving in the grass, a cricket (the insect, he noted to himself, not the sport, which was a joke only he was making), the faint creak of the lattice.

She was looking at the ring on her hand, then at him.

"Today was right," she said.

"Yeah."

"The fifty people."

"Yeah."

"The paneer."

He almost smiled. "Three plates was not excessive."

"It was a little excessive." She wasn't arguing. She was — he'd learned this over three years — categorizing. Making the record accurate. "Sunita Devi," she said. "At the table. When she held my hand."

"I saw."

"What was she saying? She was saying something in Haryanvi. Quietly."

He thought about this. He didn't speak enough Haryanvi to catch all of it — Sunita's Haryanvi was the particular fast variant of a woman who had spoken it for seventy-four years — but he'd caught a phrase. "I think she was saying: *tu ghar aayi.*" He paused. "You came home."

Shraddha was quiet.

Outside, the night continued. The jasmine. The cricket-insect. The cooling farm.

He looked at his wife.

The word was new. Not the person — the person had been there since 2011, since the coffee shop, since *do you want to find out,* since three years of 1 AM calls and kitchen proposals and the ring on the soapy hand. The word was new, and it fit the person the way words sometimes do, the way the right name lands on the right thing and makes the thing more itself.

His wife, who had red and gold on her lehenga and reading glasses in a bag somewhere and an inexplicable forty-minute connection with his grandmother and three years of knowing about the drawer and staying anyway.

She put her hand against his chest, over the pendant, and he covered it with his.

"I came home," she said. "For good."

He believed her.

---

Three months later, in March 2021, Shraddha stood in the bathroom of their flat in Bandra at seven in the morning and looked at the test in her hand for a long moment before she came to the doorway of the bedroom and said his name.

He was awake. He was always awake before seven.

She held up the test.

He sat up. Looked at it. Looked at her. Her face in the doorway, the morning light at the bathroom window behind her, the reading glasses on top of her head at the precise angle they always achieved on their own.

He didn't say anything.

She didn't say anything.

Outside, Bandra was being itself — the auto-rickshaw, the building voices, the city that had put him on its stairs and taken him back and never apologized and never needed to.

He got up.
