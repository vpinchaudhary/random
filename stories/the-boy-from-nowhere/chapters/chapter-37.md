# Chapter 37: The Question

**Timeline**: Late 2019 (age 29)

---

The flat had curtains now.

This was Shraddha's doing — not her imposing her taste, exactly, but the kind of gradual accretion that happens when two people spend enough time in the same space and one of them has opinions about what happens to natural light in a room. The curtains were mustard yellow, which Vipin would not have chosen and could not now imagine the flat without. They had arrived in a bag one Saturday in 2017 along with a bookend shaped like an elephant that still sat on his shelf without any books leaning against it. He used it as a paperweight for IPL documents, which was not what bookends were for, and which Shraddha had pointed out twice without real conviction.

The flat itself had changed in the way flats change when life settles into them: not renovated, just occupied. A second coffee mug on the hook beside his. Her reading glasses — the reading glasses she was always leaving places — left on the kitchen counter. A framed photograph of the two of them on the shelf beside the elephant bookend: Goa, 2017, her in a white kurta, him with that particular squinting half-smile he was never aware of making. He'd found it there one weekend and not moved it. Some things become furniture without a formal decision.

He was twenty-nine years old, standing in his own kitchen doorway in November 2019, watching Shraddha Kapoor do the dishes.

She was, by her own frank assessment, terrible at dishes. She approached washing up with the abstract enthusiasm of someone who did not fundamentally believe in the enterprise — she'd start, get distracted, scrub the first plate with real commitment, set it down, and then have a thought about something entirely unrelated that required her immediate mental attention. The second plate would get approximately half the attention of the first. By the third she'd be talking. He'd watched this pattern for three years with the specific enjoyment of a person cataloguing something they find endearing against their own better judgment.

She was currently on the second plate and talking about a co-star who had committed some offense whose nature Vipin had not fully caught — something about the blocking in a rehearsal scene, something about the light — and she had the dish soap in her left hand and was making a point with it in the direction of the kitchen window.

He was standing in the doorway with a ring in his right hand.

He'd picked it up that morning from Maheshwar Jewellers on Hill Road, the place where he'd seen it in the window three weeks ago and then gone back twice to look at it without going in and then come back a third time with Ravi, who had been characteristically chaotic in his assistance. Ravi's advice on the ring had occupied twenty-four minutes and covered: the importance of the gold-to-gemstone ratio, a story about his cousin's engagement ring that had inexplicably involved a cricket umpiring dispute, a theory about the width of the band as a predictor of marital satisfaction that Ravi had arrived at himself and appeared to believe completely, and the observation that the ring in the window was "simple but it has something about it, yaar, look at the setting, it's not trying to be anything other than what it is." That last part had landed.

It was a simple gold ring. The setting was a small spiral — not floral, not modern-geometric, something between. He'd held it in his palm for a moment in the shop and the jeweler had said something about it being handmade and he'd said he'd take it and the jeweler had asked what size and he'd looked at his own hands — too big, always too big — and said he wasn't sure, it was for someone else, and had held up his little finger in demonstration, and the jeweler had named a size and he had no framework for understanding ring sizes and had trusted the jeweler entirely.

It had sat in his kit bag for three weeks.

He'd moved it to his jacket pocket the previous Monday after a team training session where he'd spent forty minutes in the nets thinking about something that wasn't cricket, which was unusual for him. He'd moved it back to the kit bag on Tuesday. He'd moved it to his bedside drawer on Thursday. He'd picked it up this morning without planning to and put it in his jeans pocket and gone through the day like that, aware of it in the way you're aware of something you know is there — a low, constant note.

He had not told anyone except Ravi. He had not told Rao, and he and Rao told each other most things. He wasn't sure why he hadn't told Rao except that some things need to be yours before they become a sentence, and he hadn't been ready for it to be a sentence.

Shraddha put the second plate in the drying rack and picked up the third.

"—and I told him the blocking doesn't work because you can't have the light source coming from that direction if the emotional center of the scene is supposed to be the other person's face," she said. "It's not complicated. It's just geometry."

"The scene has a geometry," he said.

"Everything has a geometry. That's my point." She rinsed the plate. "Are you listening?"

"I'm listening."

"You have your listening face but your eyes are doing the thing they do when you're thinking about something else."

"What thing?"

"The very still thing." She set the plate in the rack. "What are you thinking about?"

He came out of the doorway.

She heard him cross the kitchen and turned slightly — the reflex of a person checking whether they need to move to let someone through — and then she turned fully when she saw he was standing still, not coming to help with the dishes, not reaching past her for a glass. She turned and looked at him and he was holding the ring, which he'd taken from his pocket without quite deciding to, just his hands doing what they did before his brain caught up.

The dish soap was still in her left hand. Her right hand had suds on it from the plate.

She looked at the ring. Then at him.

She looked at the ring again.

"I've been thinking about this for a while," he said. His voice came out approximately normal, which he registered as a minor administrative achievement. "The answer to the question you asked me. In 2011." He paused. He knew she knew which question. "Do you want to find out. The answer is yes. To all of it." He looked at her. "Including this."

He was not good at speeches. He had known he would not be good at the speech, and he had not prepared a speech because preparation for something like this felt like it would make it smaller rather than larger — like wearing the wrong size clothes for a thing that needed to fit. What came out was the short version, which was also the real version, and he watched her face do the thing faces do when they receive something they've been waiting for long enough that the waiting has become the texture of the room rather than the furniture.

Her eyes went full.

Not overflow — she blinked. Once, deliberately. The way she blinked when she was deciding whether to perform a feeling or just have it. She chose to have it.

She looked at his face for three seconds in the particular way she looked at him sometimes — not watching, not reading, just being in the room of him. Then she looked at the ring again. Then back at him.

"Chaudhary," she said, and her voice had the quiet register, the one she'd had since the beginning — not the teasing one, not the point-making one, the one that only came out for the things that mattered. "I've been waiting for you to ask for three years."

He stepped forward. She lifted her right hand — the soapy one — and he put the ring on her finger and it went on with a faint resistance from the suds and then it was there.

She looked at her hand. The ring sat on her wet, soap-filmed finger under the kitchen light that always needed a replacement bulb and he kept forgetting to replace it. She laughed. It was not a camera-ready laugh — it was the involuntary kind, the one that arrives before you know you're going to laugh, the kind that means something was perfect in the imperfect way that perfect things usually are.

He laughed too. He hadn't been expecting to laugh.

She held up her hand between them with the ring on it. "I look like I'm washing up," she said.

"You were washing up."

"I was in the middle of washing up when my boyfriend—" She stopped. A beat. "Fiancé. Proposed." She looked at the ring. "The timing is extremely you."

"I could have waited until you finished."

"You absolutely could not have," she said. "You would have spiraled." She lowered her hand and dried it on the kitchen towel with a competence that the washing up itself had not received, preserving the ring from the towel's edge in a small careful way, and then she looked at him with her reading glasses slightly crooked on her head — she'd pushed them there twenty minutes ago and they'd been sliding since — and said: "Yes, obviously."

"I didn't actually ask yet."

"You held up a ring in a kitchen and said the answer is yes to all of it. That's a proposal." She leaned up and pressed her forehead to his jaw, which was the specific height differential between them when she was in flat shoes, which meant she stood slightly into him and her forehead found the angle below his ear. Her hands found the front of his shirt. He put his arms around her. "Three years," she said into his collar. "Almost exactly."

"Ravi says the ring has something about it."

"Tell Ravi I agree."

"He'll be impossible about this."

"He's earned it. He helped pick it."

"He also had a theory about band width predicting—"

"Do not tell me about Ravi's band width theory," she said. "Not right now." She pulled back slightly to look at the ring on her finger. The suds had dried a little, leaving a faint film that would rinse off and wasn't the point. She looked at the ring the way you look at something that has just been named — the way the name makes the thing more itself, somehow, which is the mechanism of all significant namings. "It's right," she said, not to him exactly. To the room.

He looked at her.

She looked up.

Outside the kitchen window, the Bandra street was doing its evening thing — the particular honk-register that arrived at seven o'clock, an auto-rickshaw, someone calling from the building next door in the carrying voice of someone who doesn't carry a phone. Mumbai performing itself, as it did always, indifferent to what was happening in this kitchen. He'd lived in this city since before he could name things. The city had left him on its stairs and let him find his own way in and had never apologized for the leaving, which was correct, because cities don't apologize, they just accommodate or they don't, and this one had accommodated him despite everything. He'd found this flat in 2013 and paid for it with money he'd earned by reading fields and bowling balls at speed and sitting in cold early-morning nets. He'd put the curtains up himself, before Shraddha arrived with the right ones.

He was here. The flat was his. The ring was on her finger.

The thing he'd written at fifteen — *I want them to say my name* — had been about the world, the crowd, the audience that would confirm he existed. He understood now that wasn't quite right. It wasn't the world he'd needed. It was more specific than that. A name becomes yours when someone says it like it belongs to you.

She'd been saying it like it belonged to him since 2011.

---

He went to the Kapoor house on a Tuesday.

Not because Tuesday had significance — just that the IPL commitments made Tuesday the available day, and he'd decided he was doing this before the end of November, and he went. He didn't tell Shraddha he was going, which she knew anyway because she and her mother had a communication infrastructure that operated below the official level of exchange, and Shivangi Kapoor had been more welcoming than usual on the phone when he'd called to confirm.

He went alone.

Shakti was in the garden when he arrived, which was unusual — Shakti was a man of interiors, of rooms with food and conversation. The garden had a quality of recent discovery about it, as if someone had mentioned the garden's existence and Shakti had decided to investigate. He was sitting in one of the garden chairs with a glass of something, doing nothing visible, which was an activity Vipin had not previously witnessed him perform.

"You called," Shakti said, looking at him over the glass. "You never call directly."

"I do now."

"Sit."

He sat in the other garden chair. The garden was November Mumbai: warm but not oppressive, the specific gold of late afternoon cutting through the trees at an angle. The Kapoor property's garden had a bougainvillea along the east wall that had been growing for at least fifteen years, the kind of plant that becomes part of a place rather than an addition to it.

Shakti looked at him. Shakti had the same eyes he'd always had — expressive, theatrical, capable of real gravity when the performance dropped — and they were doing the reading they'd done at their first dinner in 2011, when Vipin had understood that Shakti Kapoor was more perceptive than the volume suggested.

"You're nervous," Shakti said.

"No."

"You counted the steps from the gate." Shakti looked pleased with himself in a small way. "You do that sometimes. I've watched."

He had, in fact, counted the steps. Nine from the gate to the garden chairs. He didn't dignify this with a response.

"Shraddha," Shakti said. Not a question. The word as a container for whatever he wanted to say.

"I asked her yesterday," Vipin said. "She said yes."

Shakti's face did the thing Shraddha called the Shakti cycle. He'd heard her describe it twice and had witnessed partial iterations over the years — the theatrical shock first, eyebrows ascending, the mouth opening slightly, the quality of a man performing surprise while also genuinely experiencing it. Then the shift: the assessment coming in underneath, the paternal machinery working, the deep-cut consideration of whether this was good or bad, a man weighing his daughter's life. Then, in the third movement, the sentiment — barely held, arriving at the edges of the eyes, the specific look of a father who has been building toward something for eight years and has arrived.

He was working through the cycle in real time.

"You want to marry my daughter," Shakti said.

"Yes."

Shakti put his glass down. He looked at the bougainvillea. He had the quality of a man who needs a moment before the next sentence, and Vipin let him have it. He'd learned that Shakti's silences were part of the conversation, not pauses in it.

"She'll take care of herself," Shakti said. This was not quite a question.

"She'll take care of herself," Vipin said. "I'll be there for whatever she needs."

Shakti turned back from the bougainvillea. He looked at Vipin for a moment with the direct look — not theatrical, not performing anything, just the man himself, which Vipin had seen only a handful of times across eight years: in the garden in 2011 when he'd talked about Rao; at the Hyderabad stadium with the trophy, sitting very still; at the restaurant in Bandra in 2017 when he'd said *don't make her wait forever.*

"I told Shraddha in 2011 that you were from nowhere," Shakti said. "You know I said this."

"She told me."

"I said it as a concern. I was wrong. Not about the fact — you were from nowhere, you've always known that. But the framing. I made it a deficit." He paused. "It isn't one."

"I know that."

"I know you know." Shakti's voice had a different register now — not the volume he used in rooms, not the warmth he deployed for effect, but the plain one, the one that had no technique. "You came from nowhere and you built the whole thing. The cricket. The flat in Bandra. The — all of it." He stopped. The sentence had arrived somewhere that required more than it had. "My daughter loves you. Not the Chaudhary they write about. Not the IPL captain." A pause. "You. The one who counts steps and sits on floors and makes chai before he feels things."

He'd known about the floor-sitting. Of course he'd known.

"I'd like to—" Vipin started.

"Welcome to the family," Shakti said.

He stood up at the same time as Vipin, which was slightly awkward in the garden chairs, and then he did something he had not done in eight years, in all the dinners and the conversations and the garden tests and the stadium nights — he put his arms around him. A proper embrace. The kind Vipin had not been expecting and had no framework for in this specific configuration — Shakti Kapoor, who was larger than expected even when you knew his films, whose embrace had the quality of someone who'd been building up to it — and for a moment Vipin stood in it the way you stand in rooms you haven't been in before.

Then he put his hands on the man's back. And it was there. The hug.

Neither of them said anything for a moment.

Shakti stepped back. His eyes were wet — the same eyes that had assessed him over dal in 2011, that had gone still at the trophy ceremony, that had offered him "Shakti" instead of "sir" across a restaurant table. Full eyes now. He blinked once with theatrical efficiency, which was the Shakti way: the feeling real, the performance of containing it equally real.

"Don't make me cry," Shakti said. "I'm terrible when I cry."

"You're dramatic when you cry."

Shakti pointed at him. "You see? This is what she loves. Terrible timing. The jokes." He picked up his glass. Drank. Put it down again with more force than necessary, which was also very Shakti. "Sit. The tea is coming."

The tea came from Shivangi, who appeared with it and three cups and looked at Vipin with an expression that suggested she'd known about this conversation's outcome before it started and was confirming the information rather than receiving it. She squeezed his shoulder when she put the tray down, which was not a thing she usually did, and then she said "eat something, I made chikki" and went back inside.

The chikki was good. He ate three pieces. Shakti ate four and did not comment.

They sat in the garden while the light continued its November descent, and Shakti told a story about the first time he'd met Shraddha's mother's family — theatrical, extended, involving a misunderstanding about a wedding gift that had somehow involved a second household item being produced at the wrong moment — and Vipin listened and counted the tiles on the garden path (sixteen) and thought about the word that Shakti had just used.

Family.

---

Ravi found out the way Ravi found out everything that happened in Vipin's life: through Shraddha's Instagram, which he followed with greater attentiveness than any press outlet, and which on that Thursday afternoon had shown a photograph of her hand holding a cup of chai, ring visible on the third finger, caption: *said yes to the kitchen proposal. Yes, kitchen.* Posted at 2:47 PM. Ravi's call arrived at 2:49 PM.

"Kitchen proposal," Ravi said, before Vipin could say anything. "I specifically suggested the sunset on Marine Drive. I outlined a whole—"

"I know you did."

"I had three backup locations."

"I know."

"The kitchen, yaar." He sounded genuinely aggrieved in the way Ravi was genuinely aggrieved when events deviated from his planned versions of them. "The dishes were happening."

"She was doing the dishes."

"That was the moment you chose."

"It was the right moment."

A pause. Then Ravi laughed — the big version, the Ravi Deshmukh laugh that had not changed since they were fifteen years old and winning under-17 matches, the one that meant he was actually delighted and was complaining about it because the delight needed somewhere to go. "The ring looks right, though," he said.

"You said it had something about it."

"I said that. I stand by that. The setting." A pause. "Did she actually say it took you three years?"

"She said she'd been waiting three years."

"She was right." Another pause. "Bhai." He said it the way he'd said it at the under-17 finals, at the IPL debut, at the Ranji centu­ry, at the points in between — not just the word but the exact weight Ravi put in it, which was the whole of it, which was everything he couldn't say with sentences and didn't need to. "Congratulations. Actually, properly. I'm so happy for you."

"I know you are."

"Give her my love."

"She's right here. Give her your own love."

He handed the phone across. He could hear Ravi from three feet away delivering what appeared to be a prepared congratulatory speech to Shraddha, which she received with the level of ceremony it deserved, which was to say not much. She gave the phone back after two minutes and mouthed *he had notes* at him.

He returned to the call. Ravi had not noted that she'd taken the phone. He was still mid-sentence.

"—and honestly the Marine Drive sunset would have been more photogenic but you can't argue with the Instagram caption, 'kitchen proposal' is very much—"

"Ravi."

"Yeah."

"Thank you. For the ring."

A pause that was different from the others.

"Yaar," Ravi said. Then: "Obviously." Then: "Buy me dinner."

"I'll buy you dinner."

"Somewhere good."

"Within reason."

"That's all I ask," Ravi said. "Within reason. Since day one."

He'd known Ravi Deshmukh for fifteen years. Since the under-15 camp where Ravi had batted against him in the nets and immediately named him Chaudhary and the name had stuck because Ravi was someone whose names stuck. Ravi had been at the debut. At the Wankhede. At Hyderabad. Ravi had been at the Andheri room when the spot-fixing conversation had happened, and had stood with him afterward without asking questions, which was the correct response and the one not everyone managed. Fifteen years of someone being at the place where the things happened.

He thought about this after the call ended. The flat was quiet. Shraddha had taken the ring off to properly wash her hands — she'd been annoyed by the residual soap under the setting — and had put it back on and looked at it twice more since, which she was doing less now that it was officially on her finger and not a new fact. She was in the other room with her reading glasses back on her head, in the position she took when a phone conversation had just given her material she needed to process.

He stood in his kitchen and looked at the curtains.

Mustard yellow. Elephant bookend on the shelf. Photograph of the two of them in Goa. A coffee mug beside his. The flat that had been his since 2013 and was now, quietly, theirs.

He still had a drawer.

The gloves were in the cotton. The pendant was against his chest — it had never not been against his chest. The note was folded, still: *I want them to say my name.* The photograph of Rajveer and Kamla outside the institutional building, slightly grainy, the kind of photograph taken without the subjects knowing they were the point of it.

Sunita Devi was in Hisar. He visited three times a year now, the way you visit someone who has become, slowly and without formal decision, your family — not the blood you were given but the blood you found. She still called him beta with the specific intonation she'd found the second visit, when the word had stopped being careful and become ordinary. She had a way of making chai that involved a spice he couldn't identify and had not asked about and suspected she wouldn't tell him even if he did. The chai just was what it was. Some things you don't need explained.

He thought about the ring on Shraddha's finger.

He thought about Shakti's arms around him in the garden.

He thought about Ravi's fifteen years of showing up.

He thought about Rao at Shivaji Park in the morning, the cold chai, the reading glasses on the forehead, the two hours in the net where nothing was said except what needed to be said.

He thought about the pendant. About Rajveer, who had had the same hands. About Kamla, who had brought those hands together to make him. About the stairs of the temple where he'd been left with a name and the name had been all he'd had for the first years, and the years after, until the name had accumulated — the numbers behind it, the cap, the armband, the people who said it like it was something.

He'd thought, for a long time, that belonging was the thing you were born into or you weren't. That the city owed him nothing and the family he didn't have was an absence that stayed. He'd carried the pendant against his chest for twenty-nine years as evidence that he came from somewhere specific — two names on gold, two faces in a photograph — and the mystery of why they'd left had sat in him like a question that had no end.

The question was still there. He understood now that some questions stayed. That the drawer would always have what it had in it. That Rajveer and Kamla would remain what they were — the beginning he couldn't go back to, the names he said in bathroom mirrors before the important nights. He would always be the boy from the temple stairs. The city had put him down and the city had taken him back and there would always be the gap between those two facts.

But he was standing in his own kitchen. In his flat, with the mustard curtains and the elephant bookend and the photograph from Goa. Shraddha was in the next room with her reading glasses and the ring he'd put on her soapy hand. Shakti had called him family that afternoon. Ravi had said *bhai* in the weight that held everything. Rao would be at Shivaji Park on Thursday morning with the cold chai.

Belonging wasn't something you were born into. It was something you built. Out of what you had available. Out of the people who stayed.

He'd been building it for twenty-nine years.

He looked at the curtains.

Mustard yellow. He would not have chosen them. He could not imagine the flat without them.

---

He called Rao at nine-thirty.

Rao picked up before the second ring, which was always the case, which had always given Vipin the information that Rao sat near the phone in the evenings — or slept near the phone, or kept the phone in the room where he sat at night — and had never directly confirmed or denied this.

"I asked Shraddha to marry me," Vipin said.

A pause.

"Yesterday," Vipin added.

"I know," Rao said.

"You knew."

"Kavita told me."

"Kavita Aunty — how did Kavita Aunty—"

"Shivangi Kapoor called her this morning." A pause in which Rao appeared to let him sit with this for a moment. "Women have a communication system that operates below the level we understand. I've accepted this."

He absorbed this. "You could have said something."

"I was waiting for you to tell me."

"I'm telling you."

"I know." A different pause — the kind Rao kept for the things that mattered. "You're happy?"

"Yes."

"Not the cricket answer. The actual answer."

He looked at the curtains again.

"Yes," he said. "The actual answer."

He could hear, in the quiet on Rao's end, the specific sound of the Rao house at nine-thirty in the evening — the television in the background at the volume Kavita kept it, the ceiling fan the old man refused to turn off regardless of season, a dog somewhere in the lane outside. He'd heard this background for fifteen years. It was as familiar to him as the tiles on his ceiling.

"Good," Rao said. Then: "Bring her to the nets on Thursday."

"She doesn't watch nets."

"She can watch or she can sit outside and do something else. But bring her."

This was, for Rao, a significant statement. He brought people to the nets when he wanted to introduce them to something that was essentially himself, which was to say Shivaji Park on a weekday morning, which was where he'd made everything. Kavita had been there twice. Ravi had a standing invitation he exercised annually. Rao had brought nobody else in the twenty years Vipin had known him.

"Thursday," Vipin said.

"Thursday," Rao said. "Come early. I want to work on your yorker before she arrives."

Of course. The yorker. He almost laughed.

"Rao."

"Mm."

"Thank you."

The pause this time was the short kind — the kind Rao used when he'd already accepted the thing being said and there was nothing to add.

"Sleep," Rao said. "You have training in the morning and you'll run the arithmetic until two if I don't tell you to sleep."

He was not going to run the arithmetic. There was no arithmetic to run. But this was also Rao's language for *I'm glad, go be glad* — the practical instruction that contained the feeling the way cold chai contained everything important about the two of them.

"Goodnight," he said.

He put the phone down.

Shraddha appeared in the kitchen doorway — her turn, apparently, to stand in doorways tonight — with her reading glasses on her face now, properly, which meant she'd been reading rather than thinking. She looked at him. At the phone on the counter.

"Rao?" she said.

"He already knew."

"Shivangi called Kavita Aunty this morning." She said this without surprise. "She was very pleased with how the kitchen proposal was received."

"My proposal was—"

"Your proposal was perfect," she said. "I said kitchen proposal because it was funny and it's true. Not because it was wrong." She crossed the kitchen. Leaned against the counter beside him. Looked at her hand. The ring catching the overhead light — the one he kept forgetting to replace. "It was right," she said, for the second time. "The timing and the words and the whole of it."

He looked at her.

She was thirty-two years old and she had suds on her hand when he'd put the ring there and she'd been waiting three years and she'd said *obviously* and then she'd laughed. She'd been doing this — the laughing at the right moments, the quiet register, the reading glasses and the half-eaten bowls of fruit — since 2011, since the call about the England tour, since *do you want to find out.* Since she'd been the person on the other end of the line on the nights when the answer to any ordinary question was *yeah* and the deeper question was the one neither of them said out loud.

He'd been finding out. For eight years. The answer had been yes the whole time.

"I told Shakti you'd take care of yourself," he said.

"I heard." She looked at him sideways. "Shivangi called Kavita Aunty about that too."

"What did—"

"She said it was the right thing to say." She paused. "Baba called me after you left. He was very quiet about it. He said—" She stopped.

"What?"

She looked at the wall for a moment. The specific look she had when she was deciding whether to give him a thing that would matter. She gave it.

"He said he used to think you were from nowhere," she said. "And now he understands that's not right. He said you're from everywhere you built."

He was quiet.

Outside, the Bandra street did what it did. The city continuing regardless. The auto-rickshaw honk, the neighbor's calling voice, the specific urban texture of a place that does not pause for anything occurring in any particular kitchen.

She put her hand in his. The ring was there between their palms, warm, the small spiral setting against his fingers.

"Chaudhary," she said.

"Yeah."

"What are you thinking?"

He thought about the temple stairs. The pandit's hands. The storage room ceiling with twenty-three cracks. The pendant against his chest. Rajveer and Kamla in the photograph, returned to him by a welfare officer's archive and a retired inspector's persistence. Sunita Devi's chai with the unidentified spice. Shakti's arms in the garden, the first time. Rao and the cold chai, fifteen years of cold chai that meant everything.

The mustard curtains.

"That I want this," he said.

She squeezed his hand once.

"I know," she said. "I always knew."

He believed her.
